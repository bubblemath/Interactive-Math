Measurement Systems Overview

U.S. Customary vs. Metric — two systems, one world

🌍 Why Two Systems?

The U.S. Customary System evolved from English units based on body measurements (foot, hand). The Metric System was designed in France (1790s) as a logical, base-10 (decimal) system. Today, only the U.S., Liberia, and Myanmar have not officially adopted the metric system.

⚠️ Key Observation

Notice how the U.S. system has no consistent pattern: 12 inches in a foot, 3 feet in a yard, 5,280 feet in a mile. Each conversion factor must be memorized individually.

The metric system is built on powers of 10. Every prefix represents a multiplication or division by 10. This means converting between units is as simple as moving the decimal point!

🔑 Special Metric Relationships

  • 1 mL = 1 cm³ (volume and capacity are linked!)
  • 1 L = 1,000 cm³ = 1/1,000 m³
  • 1 hectare (ha) = 10,000 m² (1 hm²)
  • 1 are (a) = 100 m² (1 dam²)

👩‍🏫 Teaching Tip

When introducing measurement to elementary students, start with non-standard units (paper clips, hand spans) before moving to standard units. This builds the concept that measurement is about comparing to a reference unit—the same principle behind both systems!

Unit Fractions & Dimensional Analysis

The universal method for converting any unit to any other unit

💡 The Big Idea: Multiplying by "1"

A unit fraction is any fraction that equals 1, where the numerator and denominator represent the same measure in different units. Since multiplying by 1 doesn't change a value, we can use unit fractions to convert between units without changing the actual measurement.

📝 What is a Unit Fraction?

Since 1 foot = 12 inches, both of these fractions equal 1:

12 in1 ft
and
1 ft12 in

The key is choosing the fraction that cancels the unit you want to eliminate and keeps the unit you want in the numerator.

The Cancellation Method

Example 1: Convert 5 feet to inches

5 ft ×
12 in1 ft
= 60 inches
✅ Why it works:

The "ft" in the numerator cancels with the "ft" in the denominator (just like cancelling common factors in fractions). The only unit remaining is inches.

✅ Chain Calculation:

3 × 4 × 2 × 2 = 48 cups. Each intermediate unit cancels, leaving only cups.

⚠️ Area & Volume Conversions — The Squaring/Cubing Trap!

When converting area units, you must square the linear conversion factor.
When converting volume units, you must cube the linear conversion factor.

  • 1 ft = 12 in → 1 ft² = 12² = 144 in²
  • 1 ft = 12 in → 1 ft³ = 12³ = 1,728 in³
  • 1 yd = 3 ft → 1 yd² = 3² = 9 ft²
  • 1 km = 1,000 m → 1 km² = 1,000² = 1,000,000 m²

👩‍🏫 Teaching Tip

Dimensional analysis is one of the most transferable skills in mathematics. It's used in chemistry (mole conversions), physics (unit analysis), nursing (dosage calculations), and cooking (recipe scaling). Teaching students to "follow the units" builds a problem-solving habit that extends far beyond math class.

Perimeter Formulas

The distance around a figure — always measured in linear units

📏 What is Perimeter?

Perimeter is the total distance around the outside of a figure. Think of it as the length of fence needed to enclose a yard, or the length of ribbon to wrap around a box lid. Perimeter is always measured in linear (one-dimensional) units: inches, feet, meters, etc.

▪️ Square

P = 4s

s = side length. All four sides are equal.

▬ Rectangle

P = 2l + 2w

l = length, w = width. Two pairs of equal sides.

△ Triangle

P = a + b + c

a, b, c = the three side lengths. Just add all sides!

⬡ Regular Polygon

P = n × s

n = number of sides, s = side length. All sides equal in a regular polygon.

⭕ Circle (Circumference)

C = 2πr = πd

r = radius, d = diameter. π ≈ 3.14159...

◗ Semicircle

P = πr + d

Half the circumference plus the diameter (the straight edge).

Worked Examples

Example 1: Square field with side 187 yards

P = 4s = 4(187) = 748 yards

Example 2: Rectangle with P = 400 ft and width = 73 ft. Find the length.

✅ Solution:

P = 2l + 2w
400 = 2l + 2(73)
400 = 2l + 146
254 = 2l
l = 127 ft

Example 3: Circle with radius 5 cm

✅ Solution:

C = 2πr = 2(3.14)(5) = 31.4 cm

Example 4: Regular hexagon with side 4 cm

✅ Solution:

P = ns = 6(4) = 24 cm

👩‍🏫 Teaching Tip

Students often confuse perimeter and area. A powerful physical demonstration: have students walk around the edge of a shape taped on the floor (perimeter) vs. covering the inside with sticky notes (area). The kinesthetic experience makes the distinction memorable.

Area Formulas

The space inside a figure — always measured in square units

📐 What is Area?

Area measures the amount of surface a figure covers. Think of it as the number of unit squares needed to tile the inside of a shape. Area is always measured in square units: in², ft², m², cm², etc.

▪️ Square

A = s²

s = side length

▬ Rectangle

A = l × w

l = length, w = width

▱ Parallelogram

A = b × h

b = base, h = perpendicular height (NOT the slant side!)

△ Triangle

A = ½bh

b = base, h = height. A triangle is half a parallelogram!

⏢ Trapezoid

A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h

b₁, b₂ = parallel bases, h = height between them

⬡ Regular Polygon

A = ½ × a × p

a = apothem (center to midpoint of side), p = perimeter

⭕ Circle

A = πr²

r = radius. Don't use diameter — divide by 2 first!

◔ Sector

A = (θ/360)πr²

θ = central angle in degrees. A sector is a "pizza slice" of a circle.

Worked Examples

Example 1: Trapezoid with bases 5 cm and 7 cm, height 17 cm

✅ Solution:

A = ½(b₁ + b₂)h = ½(5 + 7)(17) = ½(12)(17) = 102 cm²

Example 2: Regular pentagon with sides 7.2 cm and apothem 6.2 cm

✅ Solution:

First find perimeter: P = 5 × 7.2 = 36 cm
A = ½ap = ½(6.2)(36) = 111.6 cm²

Example 3: Partial circle (¾ circle) with radius 5 units

✅ Solution:

Central angle = 360° - 90° = 270°
A = (270/360)πr² = (¾)(3.14)(25) = 58.875 units²

🧠 Why Does A = ½bh Work for Triangles?

Every triangle is exactly half of a parallelogram. If you duplicate a triangle and rotate the copy 180°, the two triangles form a parallelogram with area = bh. Since the triangle is half of that: A = ½bh. This derivation is a powerful Level 2 (Informal Deduction) activity for students!

👩‍🏫 Teaching Tip

The apothem formula (A = ½ap) is actually a generalization that works for ALL regular polygons, including circles! As the number of sides approaches infinity, the polygon becomes a circle, the apothem becomes the radius, and the perimeter becomes the circumference: A = ½ × r × 2πr = πr².

Composite Figures

Breaking complex shapes into simpler ones

🧩 The Decomposition Strategy

A composite figure is a shape made up of two or more basic geometric figures. To find its area, use one of two strategies:

  • Addition Method: Break the figure into basic shapes, find each area, and add them together.
  • Subtraction Method: Start with a larger simple shape and subtract the area of the removed portion.

Interactive Composite Figure Examples

Select a Composite Figure Type

Worked Examples

Example 1: L-Shaped Room (Addition Method)

An L-shaped room has a 60 × 20 section and a 30 × 40 section.

✅ Solution (Addition):

Rectangle 1: A₁ = 60 × 20 = 1,200 sq units
Rectangle 2: A₂ = 30 × 40 = 1,200 sq units
Total Area = 1,200 + 1,200 = 2,400 sq units

Example 2: Garden with Circular Fountain (Subtraction Method)

A 20 m × 15 m rectangular garden has a circular fountain with radius 3 m.

✅ Solution (Subtraction):

Rectangle: A = 20 × 15 = 300 m²
Circle: A = π(3)² = 28.27 m²
Garden Area = 300 - 28.27 = 271.73 m²

Example 3: Window (Rectangle + Semicircle)

A window is a 4 ft × 6 ft rectangle with a semicircle on top (diameter = 4 ft).

✅ Solution (Addition):

Rectangle: A = 4 × 6 = 24 ft²
Semicircle: A = ½π(2)² = ½(3.14)(4) = 6.28 ft²
Total Area = 24 + 6.28 = 30.28 ft²

Example 4: Shaded Region (Square with Quarter Circle)

A 10 cm square has a quarter circle (radius 10 cm) removed from one corner. Find the shaded area.

✅ Solution (Subtraction):

Square: A = 10² = 100 cm²
Quarter Circle: A = (¼)π(10)² = (¼)(3.14)(100) = 78.5 cm²
Shaded Area = 100 - 78.5 = 21.5 cm²

✅ Step-by-Step Strategy for Any Composite Figure

  1. Identify the basic shapes that make up the figure
  2. Decide whether to add or subtract areas
  3. Label all dimensions (you may need to calculate some from given info)
  4. Calculate each individual area
  5. Combine the areas (add or subtract)
  6. Include units — always square units for area!

👩‍🏫 Teaching Tip

Composite figures are where students truly demonstrate Van Hiele Level 2 (Informal Deduction) thinking. They must analyze a complex shape, reason about how to decompose it, and justify their approach. Encourage students to find multiple valid decompositions for the same figure — this builds flexible geometric reasoning!
