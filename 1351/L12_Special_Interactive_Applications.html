Gift Wrapping Calculator

Calculate the minimum wrapping paper needed for a rectangular box (surface area + overlap)

📦 The Scenario

You're wrapping a gift box. You need to find the total surface area, then add a 15% overlap allowance for folding and tucking. Enter your box dimensions below.

Remember: SA = 2LW + 2LH + 2WH. Calculate each pair of faces separately, then add them. The overlap is just multiplying by (1 + overlap%/100).
Step 1

Calculate the area of the top and bottom faces

Formula: \(2 \times L \times W\)

2 × L × W = in²
Step 2

Calculate the area of the front and back faces

Formula: \(2 \times L \times H\)

2 × L × H = in²
Step 3

Calculate the area of the left and right faces

Formula: \(2 \times W \times H\)

2 × W × H = in²
Step 4

Add all face areas for Total Surface Area

Formula: \(SA = 2LW + 2LH + 2WH\)

Total SA = in²
Step 5

Add the overlap allowance

Formula: \(SA_{total} \times (1 + \frac{overlap\%}{100})\)

Paper needed = in²

🎉 Great Work!

Teaching Tip

Ask students: "Why do we need overlap?" Connect to real-world experience — paper doesn't fold perfectly at edges. For advanced students, ask them to calculate the dimensions of a single rectangular sheet that could wrap the box (requires optimization thinking).

Pool Fill Calculator

Calculate pool volume, convert to gallons, and determine fill time

🏊 The Scenario

You're filling a pool with a garden hose. First find the volume in cubic feet, convert to gallons (\(1 \text{ ft}^3 ≈ 7.48 \text{ gallons}\)), then calculate how long it takes at a given flow rate.

Rectangular: V = L × W × D. Cylindrical: V = πr²h. Then multiply cubic feet by 7.48 to get gallons. Divide gallons by flow rate for minutes.
Step 1

Calculate the volume in cubic feet

Formula: \(V = L \times W \times D\)

Volume = ft³
Step 2

Convert cubic feet to gallons

Conversion: \(1 \text{ ft}^3 = 7.48 \text{ gallons}\)

Gallons = gal
Step 3

Calculate fill time in minutes

Formula: \(\text{Time} = \frac{\text{Gallons}}{\text{Flow Rate}}\)

Time = minutes
Step 4

Convert to hours and minutes

Divide minutes by 60 for hours, use remainder for minutes

hours minutes

🎉 Pool Calculation Complete!

Teaching Tip

This is a perfect example of dimensional analysis in action! Have students write out the full unit-cancellation chain: ft³ × (gal/ft³) = gal, then gal ÷ (gal/min) = min. Connect back to Lesson 11's dimensional analysis concepts.

Ice Cream Cone Challenge

Calculate the volume of ice cream (hemisphere + cone) and the surface area of the waffle cone

🍦 The Scenario

You have a waffle cone topped with a perfect hemisphere of ice cream. The hemisphere sits on top of the cone with the same radius. Find: (1) Total volume of ice cream (hemisphere + cone filled), and (2) Surface area of the waffle cone (lateral area only — no base since it's open).

Hemisphere volume = (2/3)πr³. Cone volume = (1/3)πr²h. Slant height ℓ = √(r² + h²). Lateral SA of cone = πrℓ.
Step 1

Calculate the slant height of the cone

Formula: \(\ell = \sqrt{r^2 + h^2}\)

ℓ = cm
Step 2

Calculate the volume of the cone

Formula: \(V_{cone} = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h\)

V(cone) = cm³
Step 3

Calculate the volume of the hemisphere

Formula: \(V_{hemisphere} = \frac{2}{3}\pi r^3\)

V(hemisphere) = cm³
Step 4

Total volume of ice cream

Formula: \(V_{total} = V_{cone} + V_{hemisphere}\)

V(total) = cm³
Step 5

Lateral surface area of the waffle cone

Formula: \(SA_{lateral} = \pi r \ell\)

SA(cone) = cm²

🎉 Ice Cream Challenge Complete!

Teaching Tip

Ask students: "Why don't we include the base circle in the cone's surface area?" (Because the ice cream sits on top — it's not a closed surface.) Also discuss: "If the ice cream melts, will it all fit inside the cone?" Compare hemisphere volume to cone volume!

Concrete & Paint Estimator

Calculate paint needed for walls and concrete for a foundation slab

🏠 The Scenario

You're building a rectangular room. Calculate: (1) Wall surface area for paint (subtract windows and door), (2) Volume of concrete for the foundation slab. Then estimate costs.

Wall area = perimeter × height. Subtract window areas (3×4=12 ft² each) and door areas (3×7=21 ft² each). Slab volume = L × W × thickness (convert inches to feet first!). Paint covers ~350 ft²/gallon. Concrete costs ~$125/cubic yard.
Step 1

Calculate total wall area (all 4 walls)

Formula: \(\text{Wall Area} = 2(L+W) \times H\)

Wall area = ft²
Step 2

Subtract windows and doors

Each window = 3×4 = 12 ft². Each door = 3×7 = 21 ft².

Paintable area = ft²
Step 3

Calculate gallons of paint needed

1 gallon covers approximately 350 ft². Round up!

Gallons = gallons
Step 4

Calculate slab volume in cubic feet

Convert slab thickness from inches to feet first! \(V = L \times W \times \text{thickness (ft)}\)

Slab volume = ft³
Step 5

Convert to cubic yards

There are 27 ft³ in 1 yd³. \(\text{yd}^3 = \frac{\text{ft}^3}{27}\)

Cubic yards = yd³
Step 6

Estimate total cost

Paint: $35/gallon. Concrete: $125/cubic yard. Round up quantities!

Total cost ≈ $

🎉 Estimate Complete!

Teaching Tip

The unit conversion from inches to feet for slab thickness is where most students make errors. Emphasize: "6 inches = 6/12 = 0.5 feet." Also discuss why we round UP for materials (you can't buy half a gallon of paint at most stores, and you don't want to run short on concrete!).

Convert temperatures from cities around the world between °C, °F, and K

🌍 The Scenario

You're checking weather reports from cities worldwide. Each city reports in Celsius. Convert each temperature to Fahrenheit and Kelvin to "unlock" the city. Formulas: \(°F = \frac{9}{5}C + 32\) and \(K = C + 273.15\)

0 / 8 cities unlocked

Teaching Tip

Ask students: "At what temperature are °F and °C equal?" (Answer: −40°). Also: "Can Kelvin ever be negative?" (No — absolute zero is 0 K). These are great conceptual checkpoints that go beyond rote formula application.
