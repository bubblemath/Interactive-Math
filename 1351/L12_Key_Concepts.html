Surface Area of Solid Figures

The total area that all surfaces of a solid occupy

The Big Idea

Surface area is the total area of all the faces (or surfaces) of a 3D solid. Think of it as the amount of wrapping paper needed to cover the object completely. We find it by "unfolding" the solid into a flat net, then calculating the area of each face and adding them together.

📖 Key Vocabulary

▼
TermDefinition
NetA flat, unfolded version of a 3D solid showing all faces laid out in 2D
Lateral Surface AreaThe sum of the areas of the lateral (side) faces only — does NOT include the bases
Total Surface AreaThe sum of the lateral surface area AND the area of all bases
Slant Height (ℓ)The distance along the slanted surface of a pyramid or cone (not the vertical height)

Surface Area Formulas

Cube
SA = 6s²
Rectangular Prism
SA = 2LW + 2LH + 2WH
Cylinder
SA = 2πr² + 2πrh
Pyramid
SA = B + ½Pℓ
Cone
SA = πr² + πrℓ
Sphere
SA = 4πr²

B = area of base, P = perimeter of base, ℓ = slant height, r = radius, h = height

🔲 Understanding Nets

▼

A net is what you get when you "unfold" a 3D solid flat. Each face becomes a 2D shape you can calculate the area of.

SolidNet ComponentsStrategy
Cube6 identical squaresFind area of one square, multiply by 6
Rectangular Prism3 pairs of rectanglesFind area of each pair (top/bottom, front/back, left/right)
Cylinder2 circles + 1 rectangleRectangle width = circumference of circle (2πr)
Pyramid1 base polygon + triangular facesEach triangle has base = polygon side, height = slant height
Cone1 circle + 1 sectorSector "unrolls" the lateral surface
SphereNo true net possibleUse formula SA = 4πr² directly

Teaching Tip

Have students physically cut out nets from paper and fold them into solids. This concrete experience builds the connection between 2D faces and 3D surfaces — essential for Van Hiele Level 0 → Level 1 progression.

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1: Surface Area of a Cube 🟢 Easy

▼

Problem

Find the total surface area of a cube with side length 5 cm.

Step 1
Identify the formula

A cube has 6 identical square faces:

SA = 6s²
Step 2
Substitute and calculate
SA = 6 × 5² SA = 6 × 25 SA = 150 cm²

✅ Final Answer

SA = 150 cm²

Your Turn

Find the surface area of a cube with side length 8 in. (Answer: 384 in²)

Example 2: Surface Area of a Rectangular Prism 🟢 Easy

▼

Problem

Find the total surface area of a rectangular prism with length 10 cm, width 6 cm, and height 4 cm.

Step 1
Identify the three pairs of faces
Top & Bottom: L × W = 10 × 6 = 60 cm² each Front & Back: L × H = 10 × 4 = 40 cm² each Left & Right: W × H = 6 × 4 = 24 cm² each
Step 2
Apply the formula
SA = 2LW + 2LH + 2WH SA = 2(60) + 2(40) + 2(24) SA = 120 + 80 + 48 SA = 248 cm²

✅ Final Answer

SA = 248 cm²

Your Turn

Find the SA of a box that is 12 in × 8 in × 5 in. (Answer: 392 in²)

Example 3: Cylindrical Jam Jar 🟡 Medium

▼

Problem (from your lesson notes)

A cylindrical jam jar has a radius of 2.5 inches and a height of 5 inches. Find the total surface area. Give the exact answer and round to two decimal places. (Use π key)

Step 1
Identify the parts of a cylinder's net

A cylinder unfolds into:

• 2 circles (top and bottom bases) • 1 rectangle (the lateral surface that "wraps around")
The rectangle's width equals the circumference of the circle: 2πr. This is the key insight for cylinder surface area!
Step 2
Write the formula
SA = 2πr² + 2πrh ↑ ↑ two bases lateral surface
Step 3
Substitute values
SA = 2π(2.5)² + 2π(2.5)(5) SA = 2π(6.25) + 2π(12.5) SA = 12.5π + 25π SA = 37.5π ← exact answer
Step 4
Calculate the decimal approximation
SA = 37.5π ≈ 37.5 × 3.14159... SA ≈ 117.81 in²

✅ Final Answer

SA = 37.5π ≈ 117.81 in²

Your Turn

A can has radius 3 cm and height 10 cm. Find the total SA. (Answer: 78π ≈ 245.04 cm²)

Example 4: Surface Area of a Cone 🟡 Medium

▼

Problem

Find the total surface area of a cone with radius 4 cm and slant height 9 cm. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Identify the parts
• Base: a circle with radius 4 cm • Lateral surface: a sector that "wraps" into the cone shape
Make sure you use the SLANT height (ℓ), not the vertical height (h), in the lateral area formula. These are different measurements!
Step 2
Apply the formula
SA = πr² + πrℓ SA = π(4)² + π(4)(9) SA = 16π + 36π SA = 52π
Step 3
Calculate
SA = 52 × 3.14 = 163.28 cm²

✅ Final Answer

SA = 52π ≈ 163.28 cm²

Your Turn

A cone has radius 6 in and slant height 10 in. Find the total SA. (Answer: 96π ≈ 301.44 in²)

Example 5: Silo (Cylinder + Hemisphere) 🔴 Hard

▼

Problem

A grain silo is shaped like a cylinder with a hemisphere on top. The cylinder has a radius of 10 ft and a height of 30 ft. Find the total exterior surface area. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Identify which surfaces are exposed
Exposed surfaces: • Cylinder lateral surface (the side wall) • Cylinder bottom base (the floor) • Hemisphere curved surface (the dome) NOT included: • Cylinder top base (covered by the hemisphere) • Hemisphere flat circle (attached to cylinder)
This is the key to composite surface area problems: identify which faces are INTERNAL (joined together) and exclude them!
Step 2
Calculate each exposed surface
Cylinder lateral: 2πrh = 2(3.14)(10)(30) = 1,884 ft² Cylinder bottom: πr² = 3.14(10²) = 314 ft² Hemisphere: 2πr² = 2(3.14)(10²) = 628 ft²
A full sphere SA = 4πr². A hemisphere is half, so its curved surface = 2πr².
Step 3
Add all exposed surfaces
Total SA = 1,884 + 314 + 628 = 2,826 ft²

✅ Final Answer

SA = 2,826 ft²

Your Turn

An ice cream cone is a cone (r = 3 cm, ℓ = 10 cm) topped with a hemisphere (r = 3 cm). Find the total exterior SA. (Answer: 150.72 cm²)

Example 6: Finding a Missing Dimension 🔴 Hard

▼

Problem

A sphere has a surface area of 804.56 cm². Find the radius. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Write the formula and substitute
SA = 4πr² 804.56 = 4(3.14)r² 804.56 = 12.56r²
Step 2
Solve for r²
r² = 804.56 ÷ 12.56 r² = 64
Step 3
Take the square root
r = √64 = 8 cm

✅ Final Answer

r = 8 cm

Your Turn

A cylinder has SA = 376.8 cm² and radius 6 cm. Find the height. (Answer: 4 cm)

Volume of Solid Figures

The amount of space a 3D solid occupies

The Big Idea

Volume measures the amount of 3D space inside a solid — think of it as how much water (or soil, or air) the shape can hold. For prisms and cylinders, volume = Base area × height. For pyramids and cones, volume is exactly ⅓ of the corresponding prism or cylinder. This ⅓ relationship is one of the most important ideas in this lesson!

Volume Formulas

Rectangular Prism
V = LWH
Any Prism
V = Bh
Cylinder
V = πr²h
Pyramid
V = ⅓Bh
Cone
V = ⅓πr²h
Sphere
V = ⁴⁄₃πr³

B = area of the base, h = perpendicular height (not slant height!)

🔍 The ⅓ Relationship: Why Pyramids & Cones

▼

If a pyramid and a prism have the same base and same height, the pyramid's volume is exactly ⅓ of the prism's. The same is true for a cone vs. a cylinder.

V_pyramid = ⅓ × V_prism (same base & height) V_cone = ⅓ × V_cylinder (same base & height) It takes exactly 3 cones of water to fill 1 cylinder!

Teaching Tip

Use a physical demonstration: fill a cone with water and pour it into a cylinder of the same base and height. It takes exactly 3 pours! This concrete experience is unforgettable for students and builds deep conceptual understanding.

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1: Volume of a Rectangular Prism 🟢 Easy

▼

Problem

Find the volume of a rectangular prism with length 8 cm, width 5 cm, and height 3 cm.

Step 1
Apply the formula
V = L × W × H V = 8 × 5 × 3 V = 120 cm³
Volume is always measured in CUBIC units (cm³, ft³, m³) because we're measuring three dimensions.

✅ Final Answer

V = 120 cm³

Your Turn

Find the volume of a box that is 12 in × 7 in × 4 in. (Answer: 336 in³)

Example 2: Volume of a Cylinder 🟢 Easy

▼

Problem

Find the volume of a cylinder with radius 6 cm and height 10 cm. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Apply the formula
V = πr²h V = 3.14 × 6² × 10 V = 3.14 × 36 × 10 V = 1,130.4 cm³

✅ Final Answer

V = 360π ≈ 1,130.4 cm³

Your Turn

A cylinder has radius 4 in and height 7 in. Find the volume. (Answer: 112π ≈ 351.68 in³)

Example 3: Volume of a Cone 🟡 Medium

▼

Problem

Find the volume of a cone with diameter 12 cm and height 9 cm. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Find the radius
r = d ÷ 2 = 12 ÷ 2 = 6 cm
You're given the DIAMETER, not the radius. Always check which one is provided!
Step 2
Apply the cone volume formula
V = ⅓πr²h V = ⅓ × 3.14 × 6² × 9 V = ⅓ × 3.14 × 36 × 9 V = ⅓ × 1,017.36 V = 339.12 cm³
Don't forget the ⅓! A cone is exactly ⅓ the volume of a cylinder with the same base and height.

✅ Final Answer

V = 108π ≈ 339.12 cm³

Your Turn

A cone has radius 5 in and height 12 in. Find the volume. (Answer: 100π ≈ 314 in³)

Example 4: Volume of a Sphere 🟡 Medium

▼

Problem

A basketball has a diameter of 9.4 inches. Find its volume. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Find the radius
r = 9.4 ÷ 2 = 4.7 in
Step 2
Apply the sphere volume formula
V = ⁴⁄₃ × π × r³ V = ⁴⁄₃ × 3.14 × (4.7)³
Step 3
Calculate r³ carefully
4.7³ = 4.7 × 4.7 × 4.7 = 22.09 × 4.7 = 103.823
When cubing a decimal, take it step by step: square it first, then multiply by the original number one more time.
Step 4
Finish the calculation
V = ⁴⁄₃ × 3.14 × 103.823 V = 4.1867 × 103.823 V ≈ 434.66 in³

✅ Final Answer

V ≈ 434.66 in³

Your Turn

A soccer ball has diameter 8.6 in. Find its volume. (Answer: ≈ 333.04 in³)

Example 5: Juana's Science Project (Triangular Prism) 🔴 Hard

▼

Problem (from your lesson notes)

Juana is building a container for her science project. The container is a triangular prism with a triangular base that has a base of 8 cm and a height of 6 cm. The prism is 20 cm long. She needs to paint the box (no top) and fill it with soil. Find both the surface area (without the top face) and the volume.

Step 1
Find the area of the triangular base
B = ½ × base × height B = ½ × 8 × 6 B = 24 cm²
Step 2
Find the volume (fill with soil)
V = B × h (length of prism) V = 24 × 20 V = 480 cm³
Step 3
Find the surface area for painting (no top)

We need to find the slant side of the triangle to calculate the lateral faces. Using the Pythagorean theorem (half-base = 4, height = 6):

slant = √(4² + 6²) = √(16 + 36) = √52 ≈ 7.21 cm
Step 4
Calculate each face for painting
Bottom rectangle: 8 × 20 = 160 cm² Two slant rectangles: 2 × (7.21 × 20) = 288.4 cm² Two triangular ends: 2 × 24 = 48 cm² Total paint area = 160 + 288.4 + 48 = 496.4 cm²
Since there's "no top," we don't include a top rectangular face. The "bottom" is the rectangle along the base of the triangle.

✅ Final Answer

Volume = 480 cm³ | Paint area ≈ 496.4 cm²

Example 6: Ice Cream Cone (Cone + Hemisphere) 🔴 Hard

▼

Problem

An ice cream cone has a cone height of 12 cm and a radius of 3 cm. A perfect hemisphere of ice cream sits on top. Find the total volume of ice cream + cone. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Volume of the cone
V_cone = ⅓πr²h V_cone = ⅓ × 3.14 × 3² × 12 V_cone = ⅓ × 3.14 × 9 × 12 V_cone = ⅓ × 339.12 V_cone = 113.04 cm³
Step 2
Volume of the hemisphere
V_hemisphere = ½ × ⁴⁄₃πr³ V_hemisphere = ½ × ⁴⁄₃ × 3.14 × 3³ V_hemisphere = ½ × ⁴⁄₃ × 3.14 × 27 V_hemisphere = ½ × 113.04 V_hemisphere = 56.52 cm³
Step 3
Add them together
V_total = 113.04 + 56.52 = 169.56 cm³

✅ Final Answer

V = 169.56 cm³

Your Turn

A silo is a cylinder (r = 5 m, h = 20 m) with a hemisphere on top. Find the total volume. (Answer: ≈ 1,832.67 m³)

Mass & Weight

Measuring how much matter an object contains

The Big Idea

Mass measures the amount of matter in an object (metric: grams, kilograms). Weight measures the gravitational force on an object (U.S.: ounces, pounds, tons). On Earth's surface, mass and weight are proportional, so we often use them interchangeably in everyday life. Converting between systems requires the approximate conversion factors from your lesson notes.

Conversion Factors

Metric
1 kg = 1,000 g
U.S.
1 lb = 16 oz
U.S.
1 ton = 2,000 lbs
Cross-System
1 kg ≈ 2.20 lbs
Cross-System
1 g ≈ 0.04 oz

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1: Kilograms to Grams 🟢 Easy

▼

Problem

Convert 3.75 kg to grams.

Step 1
Use dimensional analysis
3.75 kg × (1,000 g / 1 kg) = 3,750 g

✅ Final Answer

3.75 kg = 3,750 g

Your Turn

Convert 0.5 kg to grams. (Answer: 500 g)

Example 2: Pounds to Kilograms 🟡 Medium

▼

Problem

A package weighs 15 pounds. Convert to kilograms. (1 kg ≈ 2.20 lbs)

Step 1
Set up the unit fraction

We want to cancel "lbs" and keep "kg":

15 lbs × (1 kg / 2.20 lbs)
Step 2
Cancel and calculate
15 ÷ 2.20 = 6.82 kg
Cross-system conversions are always approximations. Your lesson notes emphasize this: the metric system was created scientifically, while the U.S. system grew from common usage, so there are no exact conversions between them.

✅ Final Answer

15 lbs ≈ 6.82 kg

Your Turn

Convert 180 lbs to kg. (Answer: ≈ 81.82 kg)

Example 3: Multi-Step Mass Conversion 🔴 Hard

▼

Problem

A recipe calls for 250 grams of flour. Your kitchen scale only measures in ounces. How many ounces do you need? (1 kg ≈ 2.20 lbs, 1 lb = 16 oz)

Step 1
Plan the conversion chain
grams → kilograms → pounds → ounces
Step 2
Build the dimensional analysis chain
250 g × (1 kg / 1,000 g) × (2.20 lbs / 1 kg) × (16 oz / 1 lb)
Step 3
Cancel units and calculate
250 × (1/1,000) × 2.20 × 16 = 0.25 × 2.20 × 16 = 0.55 × 16 = 8.8 oz

✅ Final Answer

250 g ≈ 8.8 oz

Your Turn

Convert 500 g to ounces using the same chain. (Answer: ≈ 17.6 oz)

Temperature Conversions

Fahrenheit, Celsius, and Kelvin

The Big Idea

Temperature measures the average kinetic energy of particles. Three scales are commonly used: Fahrenheit (U.S. daily life), Celsius (metric/scientific), and Kelvin (absolute scale used in physics). Unlike other unit conversions, temperature conversions involve both multiplication AND addition/subtraction — you can't use simple unit fractions.

Temperature Conversion Formulas

°F to °C
°C = ⁵⁄₉(°F − 32)
°C to °F
°F = ⁹⁄₅(°C) + 32
°C to K
K = °C + 273.15
K to °C
°C = K − 273.15

🌍 Key Reference Points

▼
Event°F°CK
Absolute Zero−459.67°−273.15°0
Water Freezes32°0°273.15
Room Temperature68°20°293.15
Body Temperature98.6°37°310.15
Water Boils212°100°373.15

Teaching Tip

Help students remember: 0°C and 100°C are the "clean" numbers (freezing and boiling of water). Fahrenheit uses 32° and 212° — less intuitive, but it's what we use daily in the U.S. Kelvin simply shifts Celsius up by 273.15 so there are no negative temperatures.

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1: Fahrenheit to Celsius 🟢 Easy

▼

Problem

Convert 77°F to Celsius.

Step 1
Write the formula
°C = ⁵⁄₉ × (°F − 32)
Step 2
Subtract 32 first (order of operations!)
°C = ⁵⁄₉ × (77 − 32) °C = ⁵⁄₉ × 45
Step 3
Multiply by ⁵⁄₉
°C = 5 × 45 ÷ 9 °C = 225 ÷ 9 °C = 25°
Tip: You can multiply by 5 first, then divide by 9 — or divide by 9 first if the number is divisible by 9 (like 45 ÷ 9 = 5, then 5 × 5 = 25).

✅ Final Answer

77°F = 25°C

Your Turn

Convert 212°F to Celsius. (Answer: 100°C — boiling point of water!)

Example 2: Celsius to Fahrenheit 🟢 Easy

▼

Problem

Convert 35°C to Fahrenheit.

Step 1
Write the formula
°F = ⁹⁄₅ × (°C) + 32
Step 2
Multiply first, then add 32
°F = ⁹⁄₅ × 35 + 32 °F = (9 × 35) ÷ 5 + 32 °F = 315 ÷ 5 + 32 °F = 63 + 32 °F = 95°
Order of operations matters! Multiply by ⁹⁄₅ FIRST, then add 32. A common mistake is adding 32 before multiplying.

✅ Final Answer

35°C = 95°F

Your Turn

Convert 0°C to Fahrenheit. (Answer: 32°F — freezing point!)

Example 3: Celsius to Kelvin 🟡 Medium

▼

Problem

The surface of the sun is approximately 5,500°C. Convert to Kelvin.

Step 1
Apply the formula
K = °C + 273.15 K = 5,500 + 273.15 K = 5,773.15
Kelvin is the simplest conversion — just add 273.15. There are no negative Kelvin temperatures because 0 K is absolute zero, the coldest possible temperature.

✅ Final Answer

5,500°C = 5,773.15 K

Your Turn

Liquid nitrogen boils at −196°C. Convert to Kelvin. (Answer: 77.15 K)

Example 4: Fahrenheit to Kelvin (Two-Step) 🔴 Hard

▼

Problem

An oven is set to 450°F. What is this temperature in Kelvin?

Step 1
Convert °F to °C first
°C = ⁵⁄₉ × (°F − 32) °C = ⁵⁄₉ × (450 − 32) °C = ⁵⁄₉ × 418 °C = 2,090 ÷ 9 °C ≈ 232.22°
Step 2
Convert °C to K
K = °C + 273.15 K = 232.22 + 273.15 K = 505.37
There's no direct °F to K formula in most textbooks. The standard approach is always: °F → °C → K (two steps).

✅ Final Answer

450°F ≈ 232.22°C ≈ 505.37 K

Your Turn

Body temperature is 98.6°F. Convert to Kelvin. (Answer: 37°C = 310.15 K)

Example 5: When Are °F and °C Equal? 🔴 Hard

▼

Problem

At what temperature do Fahrenheit and Celsius give the same number? (i.e., find x where x°F = x°C)

Step 1
Set up the equation

Let x = the temperature where °F = °C. Use the C-to-F formula with °C = x and °F = x:

x = ⁹⁄₅(x) + 32
Step 2
Solve for x
x = ⁹⁄₅x + 32 x − ⁹⁄₅x = 32 ⁵⁄₅x − ⁹⁄₅x = 32 −⁴⁄₅x = 32 x = 32 × (−⁵⁄₄) x = −40
Step 3
Verify
°C = ⁵⁄₉(−40 − 32) = ⁵⁄₉(−72) = −40 ✓
This is a famous result! −40° is the only temperature where Fahrenheit and Celsius agree. It's a great algebra problem that connects to real-world meaning.

✅ Final Answer

−40°F = −40°C
