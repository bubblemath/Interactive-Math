📐 Lesson 7: Triangles

Classification by Angles

Acute: All angles < 90°
Right: One angle = 90°
Obtuse: One angle > 90°

Classification by Sides

Scalene: No equal sides
Isosceles: At least 2 equal sides
Equilateral: All 3 sides equal

Angle Sum Property

\(m\angle A + m\angle B + m\angle C = 180°\)

Pythagorean Theorem

\(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\)
(Right triangles only!)

Triangle Inequality

Sum of any two sides > third side
\(a + b > c\), \(a + c > b\), \(b + c > a\)

Congruence Criteria

SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS
(NOT SSA or AAA!)

🔷 Lesson 8: Quadrilaterals & Circles

Quadrilateral Hierarchy

Parallelogram → Rectangle, Rhombus
Rectangle + Rhombus → Square

Parallelogram Properties

Opposite sides parallel & equal
Opposite angles equal
Diagonals bisect each other

Rectangle Properties

All angles = 90°
Diagonals are congruent

Rhombus Properties

All sides equal
Diagonals are perpendicular
Diagonals bisect angles

Circle Formulas

Circumference: \(C = 2\pi r = \pi d\)
Area: \(A = \pi r^2\)

Circle Parts

Radius, Diameter, Chord
Arc, Sector, Central Angle

🔄 Lesson 9: Constructions & Transformations

Euclidean Tools

Compass: Draws circles/arcs
Straightedge: Draws lines (no measuring!)

Key Constructions

Copy segment, Equilateral triangle
Perpendicular bisector, Angle bisector

Translation Rule

\((x, y) \to (x + a, y + b)\)
Vector: \(\langle a, b \rangle\)

90° CCW Rotation

\((x, y) \to (-y, x)\)

180° Rotation

\((x, y) \to (-x, -y)\)

270° CCW Rotation

\((x, y) \to (y, -x)\)

Isometry Properties

Preserves: Distance, Angles
Orientation: Preserved by translations/rotations
Reversed by reflections

Perpendicular Bisector

Set of all points equidistant
from two given points A and B

🎓 Educator's Pedagogy Lab

Van Hiele scenarios, common misconceptions, and "Explain to a 5th Grader" prompts for future teachers.

📊 Van Hiele Diagnostic Scenarios

Scenario 1: Quadrilateral Classification

A student says:

"A square isn't a rectangle because a square has four equal sides, but a rectangle doesn't."
Van Hiele Analysis

Level 1 (Analysis): The student recognizes properties but doesn't understand class inclusion. They see "rectangle" and "square" as separate categories rather than understanding that a square is a special type of rectangle.

Teaching Strategy: Use the quadrilateral hierarchy diagram. Ask: "Does a square have four right angles? Does it have opposite sides parallel? Then it meets ALL the requirements to be a rectangle—plus it has equal sides, making it a special rectangle!"

Scenario 2: Triangle Congruence

When asked if two triangles with three pairs of equal angles are congruent, a student says:

"Yes, they're congruent because all the angles match up. AAA proves congruence."
Van Hiele Analysis

Level 1 (Analysis): The student understands that congruence involves matching parts but confuses congruence with similarity. AAA proves similarity, not congruence!

Teaching Strategy: Draw two equilateral triangles of different sizes. "Both have three 60° angles. Are they the same size? No! AAA tells us the triangles have the same SHAPE (similar), but we need side information to know they're the same SIZE (congruent)."

Scenario 3: Rotation Understanding

A student applies the 90° CCW rotation rule and says:

"I used \((x, y) \to (y, -x)\) for 90° counterclockwise, but my answer is wrong."
Van Hiele Analysis

Level 2 (Informal Deduction): The student is attempting to apply a rule but has confused the 90° CCW rule with the 270° CCW (90° CW) rule.

Teaching Strategy: Create a mnemonic: "90° CCW: Negate the Y, then swap" → \((-y, x)\). Have students physically rotate a point on graph paper to verify the rule before memorizing it.

🚫 Common Misconceptions

❌ "The Pythagorean Theorem works for all triangles"

Student applies \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\) to an obtuse triangle and gets a wrong answer.
The Truth: The Pythagorean Theorem ONLY works for right triangles! For other triangles, you need the Law of Cosines: \(c^2 = a^2 + b^2 - 2ab\cos(C)\).
💡 Teaching Strategy

Always have students identify the right angle FIRST before applying the theorem. Ask: "Where is the 90° angle? Which side is the hypotenuse (opposite the right angle)?"

❌ "Circumference = πr"

Student forgets the 2 in the circumference formula.
The Truth: Circumference = \(2\pi r\) or \(\pi d\). The "2" is essential because circumference goes all the way around (diameter), not just halfway (radius).
💡 Teaching Strategy

Connect to the definition: "Circumference is the distance AROUND the circle. The diameter goes all the way across, so \(C = \pi d\). Since \(d = 2r\), we get \(C = 2\pi r\)."

❌ "Clockwise is positive for rotations"

Student assumes clockwise is positive because clocks go that way.
The Truth: In mathematics, counterclockwise is positive. This convention comes from the unit circle and is used consistently in trigonometry.
💡 Teaching Strategy

Mnemonic: "Math goes the opposite way from clocks!" Connect to the unit circle where angles increase counterclockwise from the positive x-axis.

❌ "I can measure with the straightedge"

Student uses ruler markings during a compass-and-straightedge construction.
The Truth: A straightedge is NOT a ruler! It has no markings. You can only use it to draw straight lines through existing points. To transfer distances, you must use the compass.
💡 Teaching Strategy

Cover ruler markings with tape, or use index cards as straightedges. Emphasize: "The compass is your distance-remembering tool. The straightedge just draws straight lines."

💬 "Explain to a 5th Grader" Prompts

📐 Why do the angles in a triangle always add up to 180°?

How would you explain this to a 5th grader without using formal proofs?

Sample Explanation: "Cut out a triangle from paper. Now tear off all three corners and put them together with their points touching. What do you see? They make a straight line! A straight line is 180°, so the three angles must add up to 180°. Try it with any triangle—it always works!"

🔷 Why is a square also a rectangle?

How would you explain class inclusion to a 5th grader?

Sample Explanation: "Think of it like dogs and animals. All dogs are animals, but not all animals are dogs. A rectangle is any shape with four right angles and opposite sides equal. A square has all of that PLUS all four sides are equal. So a square is a special, extra-perfect rectangle—like how a golden retriever is a special type of dog!"

🔄 What's the difference between a translation and a rotation?

How would you explain these transformations using everyday examples?

Sample Explanation: "A translation is like sliding a book across your desk—it moves but doesn't turn. Every part of the book moves the same direction and distance. A rotation is like spinning a fidget spinner—it turns around a center point. The center stays still while everything else goes around it. Sliding vs. spinning!"

📐 Why does the perpendicular bisector construction work?

How would you explain this compass construction to a 5th grader?

Sample Explanation: "Imagine you and your friend are standing at two spots. You both have the same length of rope. If you each stretch your rope out and walk in a circle, the places where your circles cross are exactly the same distance from both of you! Connect those crossing points, and you get a line that's perfectly in the middle—that's the perpendicular bisector!"
