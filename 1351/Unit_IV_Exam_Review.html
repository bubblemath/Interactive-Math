⚠️ Common Misconceptions Across All 3 Lessons

▸ L10 "Reflections change the size of a shape"

❌ Misconception: Students think reflecting a shape makes it bigger or smaller because it "looks different."
✅ Reality: Reflections are isometries — they preserve distance and angle measure. The image is congruent to the pre-image. Only the orientation changes (like a mirror).
🔧 Strategy: Have students measure side lengths and angles of both the pre-image and image. They'll discover everything matches. Use tracing paper — trace the original, flip it, and overlay.
🔍 Diagnostic: "Triangle ABC is reflected over the y-axis. Is triangle A'B'C' congruent to ABC? How do you know?"

▸ L10 "All quadrilaterals can tessellate"

❌ Misconception: Students assume any four-sided shape tiles the plane because squares do.
✅ Reality: Actually, ALL quadrilaterals (even non-convex ones) CAN tessellate! But only 3 regular polygons can: triangles, squares, and hexagons. The key test is whether the interior angles at a vertex sum to exactly 360°.
🔧 Strategy: Have students calculate interior angles for regular pentagons (108°), octagons (135°), etc. and test: does 360 ÷ angle = a whole number? Only 60°, 90°, and 120° pass.
🔍 Diagnostic: "Can a regular pentagon tessellate? Calculate 360° ÷ 108° and explain what the result tells you."

▸ L11 "Doubling the sides doubles the area"

❌ Misconception: "If I double every side of a rectangle, the area doubles too."
✅ Reality: Doubling all dimensions multiplies area by 2² = 4. A 3×5 rectangle has area 15. A 6×10 rectangle has area 60 — that's 4× more, not 2×. This connects directly to L10's dilation: area scales by k².
🔧 Strategy: Draw a 2×3 rectangle on grid paper (6 squares). Now draw a 4×6 rectangle (24 squares). Students count and see it's 4× more. Extend to k=3: area becomes 9× larger.
🔍 Diagnostic: "A square has side 4 and area 16. If you triple every side to 12, predict the new area before calculating."

▸ L11 "Using slant height instead of perpendicular height for area"

❌ Misconception: Students use the slant side of a triangle or parallelogram as the "height" in area formulas.
✅ Reality: The height in area formulas must be perpendicular to the base. The slant side is only the height if the shape is a right triangle or rectangle.
🔧 Strategy: Always draw the height as a dashed line with a right-angle square at the base. Color-code: base in blue, height in red (always with the ⊥ symbol). Ask: "Does this line make a 90° angle with the base?"
🔍 Diagnostic: "A parallelogram has sides 10 and 6, with a height of 5. Which numbers do you use for the area formula? Why not 10 × 6?"

▸ L12 "Forgetting the ⅓ for pyramids and cones"

❌ Misconception: Students write V = Bh for a pyramid or V = πr²h for a cone, omitting the ⅓ factor.
✅ Reality: Pyramids and cones taper to a point, holding exactly ⅓ the volume of their corresponding prism/cylinder. V(pyramid) = ⅓Bh, V(cone) = ⅓πr²h.
🔧 Strategy: The water-pouring experiment: fill a cone, pour into a cylinder (same base & height). It takes exactly 3 pours. Mnemonic: "Pointy shapes get the ⅓."
🔍 Diagnostic: "A cylinder has volume 300 cm³. A cone with the same base and height has what volume? If a student says 300, what did they forget?"

▸ L12 "Doubling dimensions doubles the volume"

❌ Misconception: "If I make the box twice as big in every direction, it holds twice as much."
✅ Reality: Doubling all dimensions multiplies volume by 2³ = 8. A 2×3×4 box (V=24) becomes a 4×6×8 box (V=192). That's 8× more! This extends L10's dilation concept: volume scales by k³.
🔧 Strategy: Build a 1×1×1 cube from unit cubes (1 cube). Build a 2×2×2 cube (8 cubes). Then 3×3×3 (27 cubes). The pattern 1, 8, 27 = 1³, 2³, 3³ is unmistakable.
🔍 Diagnostic: "A cube has side 3 and volume 27. If you double every side to 6, is the new volume 54 or 216? Explain."

▸ L12 "Using diameter instead of radius"

❌ Misconception: Students plug the diameter directly into formulas that require radius, getting answers 4× too large (for squared terms) or 8× too large (for cubed terms).
✅ Reality: Most formulas use radius (r), not diameter (d). Always check: r = d/2. If a sphere has diameter 10, then r = 5, and V = ⁴⁄₃π(5)³ = 523.6, NOT ⁴⁄₃π(10)³ = 4,188.8.
🔧 Strategy: Train students to write "r = ___" as Step 0 before any calculation. Create a classroom rule: "Step 0: Is this radius or diameter?" Color-code: radius in red, diameter in blue.
🔍 Diagnostic: "A basketball has diameter 9.4 inches. What number do you plug into the volume formula — 9.4 or 4.7?"

▸ L12 "Adding 32 when converting °F to °C"

❌ Misconception: Students write °C = ⁵⁄₉(°F + 32) instead of °C = ⁵⁄₉(°F − 32).
✅ Reality: Going FROM Fahrenheit, you SUBTRACT 32 first (undo the shift), then scale by ⁵⁄₉. The +32 is only used when going TO Fahrenheit.
🔧 Strategy: Always verify with known values: 32°F should give 0°C (freezing). 212°F should give 100°C (boiling). If your formula gives something else, it's wrong.
🔍 Diagnostic: "Convert 32°F to Celsius. If your answer isn't 0°C, find your error."

💬 "Explain to a 5th Grader" Prompts

🔄 Why does a reflected shape look 'backwards' but stay the same size?
Think about looking in a mirror. Your reflection looks exactly like you — same height, same everything — but your left hand looks like it's on the right side. That's what a reflection does to a shape: it flips it like a mirror image, but doesn't stretch or shrink anything. Every side stays the same length, every angle stays the same size. It's like tracing a shape, then flipping the paper over.
🔷 Why can't regular pentagons tile a floor with no gaps?
When you tile a floor, the shapes have to fit together perfectly at every corner — like puzzle pieces with no gaps and no overlaps. At each corner point, the angles have to add up to exactly 360° (a full turn). A regular pentagon's corner angle is 108°. Can you add 108° repeatedly to get exactly 360°? 108 + 108 + 108 = 324° (not enough). 108 × 4 = 432° (too much!). Since you can't get exactly 360°, pentagons always leave gaps or overlaps. Triangles (60° × 6 = 360°), squares (90° × 4 = 360°), and hexagons (120° × 3 = 360°) work perfectly!
📏 Why do we multiply by fractions that equal 1 to convert units?
Imagine you have 3 feet and want to know how many inches that is. You know 1 foot = 12 inches. So the fraction 12 inches / 1 foot equals 1 (because the top and bottom are the same amount, just in different words). Multiplying by 1 never changes a value! So 3 feet × (12 inches / 1 foot) = 36 inches. The "feet" cancel out like common factors in a fraction, and you're left with inches. It's like translating between languages — same amount, different words.
📦 Why is a pyramid's volume exactly ⅓ of a box with the same base and height?
Imagine filling a cone-shaped cup with water, then pouring it into a cylinder cup that has the same bottom and same height. You'd have to fill and pour exactly 3 times to fill the cylinder! That's because the pyramid/cone gets narrower as it goes up — it "tapers" to a point. A box stays the same width all the way up. The tapering means it holds exactly ⅓ as much. You can try this at home with a funnel and a glass!
🌡️ What is "absolute zero" and why can't anything be colder?
All the tiny atoms inside everything are constantly jiggling and bouncing around — that's what heat IS. When things get cold, the atoms slow down. At absolute zero (−273.15°C or 0 Kelvin), the atoms stop moving completely. You can't get colder because there's no more movement to take away! It's like trying to drive slower than 0 mph — you can't. That's why the Kelvin scale starts at 0: it's the true bottom of temperature.
