Transformations Overview

The four types with visual comparisons

▼
Transformation: A rule that assigns to each point in the plane a unique image point, creating a correspondence between a pre-image and its image.

The Big Four Transformations

Transformation Motion Preserves Distance? Preserves Orientation? Classification
Translation Slide ✅ Yes ✅ Yes Isometry (Rigid)
Rotation Turn ✅ Yes ✅ Yes Isometry (Rigid)
Reflection Flip ✅ Yes ❌ No (Reverses) Isometry (Rigid)
Dilation Scale ❌ No ✅ Yes Similarity (Non-Rigid)

Key Distinction

  • Isometries (Rigid Motions): Translations, Rotations, Reflections → Image is congruent to pre-image
  • Similarity Transformation: Dilations → Image is similar to pre-image (same shape, different size)
Level 1 students identify the motion type; Level 2 students understand which properties are invariant under each transformation.

Reflections

Line of reflection, construction method, coordinate rules

▼
Reflection: A transformation that "flips" a figure over a line of reflection. Each point and its image are equidistant from this line.

Construction Method (Compass & Straightedge)

  1. Given point P and line l, construct a line perpendicular to l through P
  2. Mark the intersection point M on line l
  3. Using compass, measure distance PM
  4. Mark P' on the opposite side of l such that PM = MP'

Coordinate Rules

Line of Reflection Rule Example
x-axis (x, y) → (x, −y) (3, 5) → (3, −5)
y-axis (x, y) → (−x, y) (3, 5) → (−3, 5)
Line y = x (x, y) → (y, x) (3, 5) → (5, 3)
Reflections reverse orientation — the cyclic order of vertices changes from clockwise to counterclockwise, or vice versa. This is what distinguishes reflections from translations and rotations.

Dilations

Center of dilation, scale factor, construction steps

▼
Dilation: A transformation that enlarges or reduces a figure by a scale factor k from a fixed center of dilation C.

Components

  • Center of Dilation (C): The fixed point from which all measurements are made
  • Scale Factor (k): The ratio of image length to pre-image length

Scale Factor Effects

Condition Result Term
|k| > 1 Figure gets larger Enlargement
0 < |k| < 1 Figure gets smaller Reduction
k = 1 Figure stays same size Identity
k < 0 Figure flips through center Negative Dilation

Construction Steps

  1. Draw rays from center C through each vertex of the pre-image
  2. Measure distance from C to each vertex
  3. Multiply each distance by k
  4. Mark new vertices along the rays at the calculated distances
  5. Connect the new vertices

Coordinate Rule (Center at Origin)

Dilation with center at origin and scale factor k:
(x, y) → (kx, ky)
Example: With k = 2, the point (3, 4) → (6, 8)

Tessellations

Definition, regular vs. semi-regular, the 360° rule

▼
Tessellation: A pattern of shapes that covers a plane with no gaps and no overlaps (also called a tiling).

The 360° Rule

At every vertex where shapes meet:
Sum of interior angles = 360°

Types of Tessellations

Type Description Examples
Regular Uses only ONE type of regular polygon Equilateral triangles, Squares, Regular hexagons
Semi-Regular (Archimedean) Uses TWO or MORE types of regular polygons with identical vertex arrangements 3.3.3.4.4 (triangles + squares)
Irregular Uses non-regular polygons Quadrilaterals, modified shapes

Which Regular Polygons Tessellate?

Polygon Interior Angle 360° ÷ angle Tessellates?
Triangle 60° 6 (whole number ✅) Yes
Square 90° 4 (whole number ✅) Yes
Pentagon 108° 3.33... ❌ No
Hexagon 120° 3 (whole number ✅) Yes
Heptagon ≈128.57° 2.8... ❌ No
Octagon 135° 2.67... ❌ No
Only triangles, squares, and hexagons form regular tessellations because their interior angles divide evenly into 360°.

Creating Tessellations (The Escher Method)

Translation method and rotation method

▼
Named after M.C. Escher, the Dutch artist famous for transforming simple geometric shapes into intricate tessellating creatures. His work "Study of Regular Division of the Plane with Reptiles" is a classic example.
📐 Method 1: Translation Method

Works with: Squares, Rectangles, Hexagons (shapes with parallel opposite sides)

  1. Start with a base shape (e.g., square)
  2. Cut a piece from one side
  3. Translate (slide) that piece to the opposite parallel side
  4. The modified shape will still tessellate!
🔄 Method 2: Rotation Method

Works with: Triangles, Squares

  1. Start with a base shape
  2. Find the midpoint of one side
  3. Cut a piece from one half of that side
  4. Rotate 180° around the midpoint to attach it to the other half
  5. Repeat for other sides as desired
This is a perfect Van Hiele Level 2 activity—students must apply their understanding of transformation properties to create functional geometric patterns.
🌍 Real-World Examples:
  • Honeycomb structures — hexagonal tessellations
  • Brick patterns — rectangular tessellations
  • Quilts and tile floors — various tessellations
  • Islamic geometric art — complex semi-regular tessellations
