📐 Quick Formula Reference

Mean (Average)

\(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum x}{n}\)
Sum of all values divided by the number of values. Sensitive to outliers.

Median

Middle value when data is ordered
For odd n: middle value. For even n: average of two middle values. Resistant to outliers.

Standard Deviation (Sample)

\(s = \sqrt{\frac{\sum(x - \bar{x})^2}{n-1}}\)
Measures spread around the mean. Larger s = more spread out data.

Range

\(\text{Range} = \text{Max} - \text{Min}\)
Simplest measure of spread. Very sensitive to outliers.

Z-Score

\(z = \frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\)
Number of standard deviations from the mean. |z| > 2 is unusual.

IQR (Interquartile Range)

\(\text{IQR} = Q_3 - Q_1\)
Spread of the middle 50% of data. Used to detect outliers.

🔀 Which Measure Should I Use?

Click on a measure to see when it's most appropriate:

📍 Mean

The arithmetic average

✅ Use when: Data is symmetric, no extreme outliers
❌ Avoid when: Data is skewed or has outliers (like income data)
📍 Median

The middle value

✅ Use when: Data is skewed, has outliers, or for ordinal data
❌ Avoid when: You need to use all data values in further calculations
📍 Mode

Most frequent value

✅ Use when: Data is categorical, or you want the "most popular" value
❌ Avoid when: All values are unique (no mode exists)
📏 Standard Deviation

Average distance from mean

✅ Use when: Data is approximately normal, comparing spread
❌ Avoid when: Data is heavily skewed (use IQR instead)
📏 IQR

Spread of middle 50%

✅ Use when: Data has outliers, detecting outliers, skewed data
❌ Avoid when: You need to account for all data values
📊 Z-Score

Standardized position

✅ Use when: Comparing values from different distributions
❌ Avoid when: Data is not approximately normal

💭 Reflection Prompts

Mean vs. Median Debate

A news report states: "The average home price in our city is $450,000." A critic responds: "That's misleading—the median home price is only $320,000." As a future teacher, how would you explain to students why these two numbers are so different and which one better represents "typical" home prices?

Standard Deviation in Context

Two classes take the same test. Class A has a mean of 75 with s = 5. Class B has a mean of 75 with s = 15. What does this tell you about the two classes? How would you explain the concept of "spread" to students who have never seen standard deviation before?

The "Unusual" Threshold

We often say a z-score beyond ±2 is "unusual." But who decided that? Is a z-score of 1.9 really that different from 2.1? How would you help students understand that statistical thresholds are conventions, not absolute truths?

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

❌ "The mean is always the best measure of center"
Students often default to calculating the mean without considering the data's shape. For skewed data or data with outliers, the median is often more representative.
Teaching Fix: Use income data as an example. If 9 people earn $50,000 and 1 person earns $1,000,000, the mean is $145,000 but the median is $50,000. Ask: "Which better represents the 'typical' salary?"
❌ "Standard deviation can be negative"
Students sometimes get negative values when calculating by hand and don't recognize the error. Since we square deviations, the result is always non-negative.
Teaching Fix: Emphasize that standard deviation measures distance, and distance is always ≥ 0. If you get a negative value, check your arithmetic!
❌ "A z-score of 0 means the student failed"
Students confuse z-scores with grades. A z-score of 0 simply means the value equals the mean—it's perfectly average, not a failure!
Teaching Fix: Explain that z-scores measure position relative to the group, not absolute performance. A z = 0 on a hard test might still be a good score!
❌ "Outliers should always be removed"
Students think outliers are "bad data" that should be deleted. But outliers can be legitimate data points that provide important information.
Teaching Fix: Distinguish between outliers due to errors (typos, measurement mistakes) and legitimate outliers (a CEO's salary in company data). Investigate before removing!
❌ "Q1 and Q3 are always data values"
Students assume quartiles must be actual values in the dataset. Depending on the method used, quartiles may be interpolated values.
Teaching Fix: Show examples where Q1 or Q3 falls between two data points. Explain that different software may use slightly different methods, which is okay!

🎓 Error Analysis Scenarios

Scenario 1: The Median Mistake

A student is asked to find the median of: 12, 8, 15, 3, 9. They answer "15" because it's in the middle of the list. What error did they make?
The student forgot to order the data first. The ordered data is: 3, 8, 9, 12, 15. The median is 9, not 15.

Scenario 2: The Range Confusion

A student calculates the range of {5, 8, 12, 15, 20} as "5 to 20." Is this correct? What feedback would you give?
The student described the interval, not the range. The range is a single number: 20 - 5 = 15. The range measures the spread, not the endpoints.

Scenario 3: The Z-Score Interpretation

A student calculates z = -1.5 for their test score and says, "I did terribly—my z-score is negative!" How would you respond?
A negative z-score means below average, not "terrible." With z = -1.5, the student is 1.5 standard deviations below the mean, which is within the typical range (|z| < 2). About 7% of students scored lower!

Scenario 4: The IQR Outlier

Given Q1 = 20, Q3 = 40, a student says "50 is an outlier because it's bigger than Q3." Is this reasoning correct?
Incorrect! Being above Q3 doesn't make a value an outlier. We need to calculate: IQR = 40 - 20 = 20. Upper fence = Q3 + 1.5(IQR) = 40 + 30 = 70. Since 50 < 70, it's NOT an outlier.

Scenario 5: The Grouped Mean

A student is finding the mean from a frequency table. They add up all the class midpoints and divide by the number of classes. What's wrong with this approach?
The student forgot to weight by frequency. The correct formula is: \(\bar{x} = \frac{\sum(m \cdot f)}{\sum f}\) where m is the midpoint and f is the frequency. Each class contributes proportionally to its frequency.

💡 Teaching Tips

1. Use Real Student Data

Collect anonymous data from your class (heights, shoe sizes, hours of sleep) and calculate statistics together. Students engage more when the data is about them!

2. The "Salary Game"

Have 10 students each represent a salary. Start with equal salaries, then make one student a "CEO" with 10x the salary. Watch how the mean jumps while the median stays stable. This viscerally demonstrates resistance to outliers.

3. Box Plot Body Language

Have students physically arrange themselves by height, then identify the "quartile people." The shortest person is Min, the tallest is Max, and students can find Q1, Median, and Q3 by counting positions.

4. The "Spread Your Arms" Activity

To teach standard deviation, have students spread their arms to show "how spread out" different datasets are. A dataset with s = 2 gets a small spread; s = 20 gets arms wide. This builds intuition before formulas.
