🔶 Lesson 6

Polyhedra & Euler's Formula

3D Shapes and the F + V - E = 2 Relationship

🧊

A polyhedron is a 3D solid bounded by flat polygonal faces. Euler's Formula reveals a surprising constant relationship between the number of faces, vertices, and edges in any simple polyhedron:

F + V - E = 2

📚 Polyhedron Vocabulary

🧊

Polyhedron

A 3D shape with flat faces and straight edges. The faces are polygonal regions.

⬜

Face (F)

The flat polygonal surface of a polyhedron. Like the "walls" of the shape.

📏

Edge (E)

The line segment where two faces meet. Like the "seams" of the shape.

📍

Vertex (V)

The point where three or more edges meet. Like the "corners" of the shape.

⭐ The Five Platonic Solids

Regular polyhedra where all faces are congruent regular polygons and the same number of faces meet at each vertex.

Tetrahedron

4 faces • 4 vertices • 6 edges

Cube (Hexahedron)

6 faces • 8 vertices • 12 edges

Octahedron

8 faces • 6 vertices • 12 edges

Dodecahedron

12 faces • 20 vertices • 30 edges

Icosahedron

20 faces • 12 vertices • 30 edges

🔢 Euler's Formula

F + V - E = 2

For any simple (no holes) convex polyhedron:

Faces + Vertices - Edges = 2

🎓 Educator's Lab

Teaching strategies for polyhedra and Euler's Formula.

🧠 Van Hiele Connection

Level 2 (Informal Deduction): Students discover that Euler's Formula applies to ALL simple polyhedra—not just specific shapes.

This is a powerful example of mathematical generalization: finding a property that holds universally.

Discovery Activity:

Have students count F, V, E for 5 different polyhedra BEFORE revealing Euler's Formula. Let them discover the pattern!

⚠️ Common Misconception

"Hidden" edges and vertices: When counting from a 2D drawing, students often miss edges and vertices that are "behind" the shape.

Fix: Use physical models (or the 3D viewer) so students can rotate and see ALL parts of the shape.

Tip: Teach students to use Euler's Formula as a CHECK—if F + V - E ≠ 2, they miscounted!

🛠️ Hands-On Activity

Toothpick & Marshmallow Polyhedra:

  • Toothpicks = Edges
  • Marshmallows = Vertices
  • The "holes" between toothpicks = Faces

Students build shapes and verify Euler's Formula with their own creations!

Challenge:

Can you build a shape where F + V - E ≠ 2? (Hint: Try a torus/donut shape!)

💡 Naming Tip

Prisms and Pyramids are named by their BASE:

  • "Triangular Prism" = triangle base
  • "Square Pyramid" = square base
  • "Pentagonal Prism" = pentagon base

Common confusion: A "Square Pyramid" has a square BASE, but its SIDES are triangles!
