🔄 Rotation Station

Turning Figures Around a Center Point | Isometry Investigation

🔄

A rotation is a "turn" that moves every point of a figure around a fixed center of rotation by a specific angle in a specific direction. Like translations, rotations are isometries—they preserve distance, angle measure, and shape. A rotation of 360° (or 0°) is the identity transformation!

📖 Key Concepts

Understanding rotations as isometries that turn figures around a center point.

🎯 Center of Rotation

The center of rotation is the fixed point around which all other points rotate. It can be inside, outside, or on the figure itself. The center point does not move!

Point \(O\) = Center

📐 Angle of Rotation

The angle of rotation tells us how far to turn the figure. Common angles are 90°, 180°, and 270°. A full rotation is 360°.

\(\theta\) = Angle of Rotation

↻ Direction

Counterclockwise (CCW) is the positive direction in mathematics. Clockwise (CW) is negative. 90° CW = 270° CCW!

CCW = Positive, CW = Negative

🔄 Isometry Properties

A rotation is an isometry (rigid motion) that preserves:

  • Distance (side lengths)
  • Angle measures
  • Orientation (no flipping!)

🔵 Pre-Image vs. Image

The pre-image is the original figure. The image is the rotated figure. We use prime notation: \(A \to A'\)

\(\triangle ABC \to \triangle A'B'C'\)

🎯 Identity Rotation

A rotation of 360° (or 0°) maps every point to itself. This is called the identity transformation.

\(360° = 0° =\) Identity

📋 Key Rotation Rules (About the Origin)

Rotation Direction Coordinate Rule What Happens
90° Counterclockwise \((x, y) \to (-y, x)\) Swap & negate first
180° Either direction \((x, y) \to (-x, -y)\) Negate both
270° CCW (or 90° CW) \((x, y) \to (y, -x)\) Swap & negate second

📝 Example from Your Notes

Rotate \(\triangle ABC\) with \(A(2, 3)\), \(B(5, 3)\), \(C(3, 6)\) by 90° counterclockwise about the origin.

Using the rule \((x, y) \to (-y, x)\):

  • \(A(2, 3) \to A'(-3, 2)\)
  • \(B(5, 3) \to B'(-3, 5)\)
  • \(C(3, 6) \to C'(-6, 3)\)

🎓 Educator's Lab

Teaching strategies, Van Hiele connections, and common misconceptions for rotations.

📊 Van Hiele Connections

Level 1 (Analysis): "The shape turned" — Students recognize that the figure rotated but may not quantify the angle or direction.

Level 2 (Informal Deduction): "It rotated 90° counterclockwise about the origin, so \((x,y) \to (-y,x)\)" — Students understand the precise algebraic rule.

💡 Progression Activity

Have students first describe rotations physically ("turn left"), then with angles ("90° left"), then with direction ("90° CCW"), and finally with coordinate rules.

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

"Clockwise is positive"

In mathematics, counterclockwise is the positive direction. This is opposite to clock hands! Reinforce with the unit circle.

"90° CW = 90° CCW"

Students may think direction doesn't matter. Show that 90° CW = 270° CCW—they produce the same image but are different rotations.

"The center moves too"

The center of rotation is FIXED. Only other points move around it. Use a thumbtack analogy!

🛠️ Teaching Strategies

  • Physical First: Use tracing paper and a pin at the center
  • Clock Analogy: "From 12 to 3 is 90° clockwise"
  • Isometry Dot Paper: Use the dot paper from your notes for hands-on practice
  • Connect to Unit Circle: Rotations follow the same direction conventions
  • Memorize the Rules: The three key rules (90°, 180°, 270°) are essential
💡 The "Thumbtack" Analogy

"Put a thumbtack through the center of rotation. Now spin the paper around the thumbtack—that's a rotation!"

💬 "Explain to a 5th Grader"

Rotation: "Imagine you're spinning a fidget spinner. The center stays still while everything else goes around it. That's a rotation!"

Center of Rotation: "It's like the hole in the middle of a merry-go-round. Everything spins around it, but it doesn't move."

90° vs 180°: "90° is like turning your head to look left. 180° is like turning all the way around to look behind you!"
