Triangle Classification Lab

Dual Classification by Angles AND Sides

🧠

Every triangle has TWO classifications—one by its angles (acute, right, obtuse) and one by its sides (scalene, isosceles, equilateral). This is Van Hiele Level 2 thinking: understanding that a shape can belong to multiple categories simultaneously!

📚 Triangle Classification

📐 Classification by Angles

Type Definition
Acute ALL three angles are less than 90°
Right Exactly ONE angle equals 90°
Obtuse Exactly ONE angle is greater than 90°
💡 Key Insight

A triangle can only have ONE right angle or ONE obtuse angle because the sum of all angles must equal 180°.

📏 Classification by Sides

Type Definition
Scalene NO sides are congruent (all different)
Isosceles AT LEAST two sides are congruent
Equilateral ALL three sides are congruent
💡 Key Insight

An equilateral triangle is also isosceles (it has at least two equal sides—it has three!). This is class inclusion!

🔗 The Dual Classification System

Every triangle receives two labels—one from each classification system. For example:

  • A triangle with a 90° angle and two equal sides is a Right Isosceles triangle
  • A triangle with all angles less than 90° and no equal sides is an Acute Scalene triangle
  • A triangle with all equal sides is an Acute Equilateral triangle (always acute!)

🎓 Educator's Lab

Teaching strategies and Van Hiele connections for triangle classification.

📊 Van Hiele Level 1 → Level 2

This applet bridges the critical transition:

  • Level 1: "This triangle has a right angle" (single property)
  • Level 2: "This is a Right Isosceles triangle" (dual classification)
🎯 Teaching Goal

Help students understand that a triangle belongs to TWO categories simultaneously—this is class inclusion thinking!

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

"Isosceles means EXACTLY two equal sides"

Actually, isosceles means AT LEAST two equal sides. An equilateral triangle IS isosceles!

"A tilted right triangle isn't a right triangle"

Orientation doesn't change classification. A right triangle is still right even if the right angle isn't at the bottom.

🗣️ "Explain to a 5th Grader"

Practice simplifying these concepts:

  • Acute: "All the corners are 'sharp' (less than a square corner)"
  • Right: "One corner is exactly like a square corner"
  • Obtuse: "One corner is 'wide open' (bigger than a square corner)"
  • Scalene: "All three sides are different lengths—like a weird, lopsided triangle"

🛠️ Manipulative Suggestions

  • Geoboards: Create triangles and measure angles with a protractor
  • AngLegs: Build triangles with specific side lengths
  • Patty Paper: Fold to compare angles and sides
  • Index Cards: Use the corner as a "right angle tester"
💡 Activity Idea

Have students create ALL 7 possible triangle types (the 3×3 grid minus the 2 impossible ones) using AngLegs, then sort them into a physical grid.
