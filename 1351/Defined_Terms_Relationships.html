📐 Lesson 5

Defined Terms & Relationships

Building Vocabulary from Undefined Terms

🏗️

Once we accept the undefined terms (point, line, plane), we can formally define other geometric objects by combining them in specific ways.

📚 Defined Terms in Geometry

These terms are formally defined using the undefined terms (point, line, plane) as building blocks.

—

Line Segment

Definition: A line segment consists of two points (called endpoints) and all points between them on a line.

\(\overline{AB}\)
Segment AB (finite length)

Key Property: Has a definite, measurable length.

→

Ray

Definition: A ray has one endpoint and extends infinitely in one direction through another point.

\(\overrightarrow{AB}\)
Ray AB (starts at A, through B)

Key Property: The first letter is ALWAYS the endpoint!

∠

Angle

Definition: An angle is formed by two rays that share a common endpoint called the vertex.

\(\angle ABC\) or \(\angle B\)
Vertex is the middle letter

Parts: Vertex (B) and two sides (rays BA and BC).

• • •

Collinear Points

Definition: Points are collinear if they all lie on the same straight line.

A, B, C are collinear
All on line \(\ell\)

Key Fact: Any 2 points are always collinear (they define a line).

▱

Coplanar Points

Definition: Points are coplanar if they all lie in the same plane.

A, B, C, D are coplanar
All in plane P

Key Fact: Any 3 points are always coplanar (they define a plane).

When two rays share a common endpoint (vertex), they form an angle.

📝 Key Facts to Remember

Any 2 Points

Are ALWAYS collinear (they define exactly one line)

Any 3 Points

Are ALWAYS coplanar (they define exactly one plane)

3+ Points Collinear?

Only if they ALL lie on the same straight line

4+ Points Coplanar?

Only if they ALL lie in the same flat plane

Parts of an Angle

Vertex (B): The common endpoint where the two rays meet.

Sides: The two rays that form the angle (\(\overrightarrow{BA}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BC}\)).

Interior: The region between the two sides.

⚖️ Line vs. Segment vs. Ray Comparator

Compare these three related but distinct geometric objects side by side.

Line

\(\overleftrightarrow{AB}\)
  • Extends infinitely in BOTH directions
  • Has no endpoints
  • Has infinite length
  • Arrows on both ends
Undefined term — we accept it without formal definition

Segment

\(\overline{AB}\)
  • Has two endpoints (A and B)
  • Has finite, measurable length
  • Includes all points between A and B
  • No arrows (stops at endpoints)
Defined term — built from points and line

Ray

\(\overrightarrow{AB}\)
  • Has one endpoint (A)
  • Extends infinitely in ONE direction
  • First letter = endpoint (order matters!)
  • One arrow (direction of extension)
⚠️ \(\overrightarrow{AB} \neq \overrightarrow{BA}\) — different rays!

⚠️ Common Confusion: Ray Direction

\(\overrightarrow{AB}\)

Starts at A, goes through B, continues forever

\(\overrightarrow{BA}\)

Starts at B, goes through A, continues forever

These are different rays pointing in opposite directions!

🎓 Educator's Lab

Teaching strategies and common misconceptions for future teachers.

💡 Teaching Tip: Ray vs. Segment Length

Students often think a ray is "shorter" than a line because it only has one arrow.

  • Emphasize: A ray is infinitely long in one direction
  • Only a segment has finite, measurable length
  • Ask: "Can you measure a ray with a ruler?" (No!)
🎯 Activity Idea

Have students try to "walk" a line, segment, and ray in the classroom. When do they stop? When do they keep going forever?

⚠️ Common Misconception: Ray Notation

Students frequently confuse \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BA}\).

❌ Student Error

"It doesn't matter which letter comes first—they're the same ray."

✅ Teaching Fix

Use the mnemonic: "The first letter is where you START." Draw both rays and show they point in opposite directions.

🔧 Hands-On: Collinear Points

Students struggle to visualize when 3+ points are collinear.

  • Use a string stretched taut as a line
  • Place objects along the string (collinear)
  • Move one object off the string (not collinear)
🎯 Activity Idea

"Stand in a line" game: Can 3 students stand so they're collinear? What about 4? How do you check?

🧠 3D Visualization: Coplanar

Coplanar points are harder because students must think in 3D.

  • Use a piece of cardboard as a plane
  • 3 points always define a plane (balance 3 pencils)
  • 4th point: Does it touch the cardboard?
🎯 Activity Idea

Use a corner of the room (3 walls meet). Points on one wall are coplanar. A point on a different wall is NOT coplanar with them.

🧠 Van Hiele Connection

Understanding defined terms marks progression through Van Hiele levels:

  • Level 0 (Visualization): "A segment is a short line." (Visual comparison)
  • Level 1 (Analysis): "A segment has two endpoints and finite length." (Properties)
  • Level 2 (Informal Deduction): "A segment is part of a line, restricted by two endpoints. An angle is formed when two rays share an endpoint." (Logical construction)

Helping students see how defined terms are built from undefined terms is a key step toward Level 2 thinking.

📝 "Explain to a 3rd Grader" Prompts

Line Segment:

"A line segment is like a piece of string with two ends. You can measure how long it is!"

Ray:

"A ray is like a flashlight beam—it starts at one spot and goes on forever in one direction!"

Angle:

"An angle is like opening a book—the spine is the corner, and the covers are the sides!"

Collinear:

"Collinear means standing in a perfectly straight line—like waiting for the bus!"
