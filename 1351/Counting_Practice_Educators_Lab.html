📐 Quick Formula Reference

Multiplication Principle
\(n_1 \times n_2 \times n_3 \times \cdots\)
Permutations (Order Matters)
\(P(n,r) = \frac{n!}{(n-r)!}\)
Combinations (Order Doesn't Matter)
\(C(n,r) = \frac{n!}{r!(n-r)!}\)
Expected Value
\(E(X) = \sum x \cdot P(x)\)

⚠️ Common Student Misconceptions

❌ "Order Always Matters" or "Order Never Matters"

Students often default to one approach without analyzing the specific problem. They may always use permutations because "math problems usually care about order" or always use combinations because "it's easier."

Teaching Strategy: Use the "job title test." Ask: "If we swapped two people's positions, would we have a DIFFERENT outcome?" President/VP swap = different (permutation). Committee member swap = same (combination).

❌ Forgetting to Subtract in (n-r)!

Students calculate P(10,3) as 10!/3! instead of 10!/(10-3)! = 10!/7!, confusing the permutation and combination formulas.

Teaching Strategy: Emphasize the "stopping point" concept. P(10,3) means "start at 10, count down 3 numbers: 10 × 9 × 8." The (n-r)! in the denominator cancels everything below that stopping point.

❌ Using Permutations When Repetition is Allowed

For problems like "How many 4-digit PINs are possible?", students try to use P(10,4) instead of 10⁴, not recognizing that digits can repeat.

Teaching Strategy: Ask "Can we use the same item again?" If yes, it's the multiplication principle (n^r). If no, then consider permutations or combinations. Create a decision tree poster for the classroom.

❌ Confusing "Select" with "Arrange"

Students see "select 3 from 10" and automatically think combination, even when the problem asks for an arrangement (like 1st, 2nd, 3rd place).

Teaching Strategy: Highlight action words: "arrange," "line up," "order," "rank" → Permutation. "Choose," "select," "pick," "committee," "group" → Combination. Have students underline these keywords.

❌ Expected Value = Most Likely Outcome

Students think E(X) = 2.5 for a die means you'll "probably roll a 2 or 3" rather than understanding it as a long-run average that may not even be a possible outcome.

Teaching Strategy: Use the "family size" example: Average family has 2.3 children, but no family actually has 2.3 children. Expected value is what you'd average over MANY trials, not what happens in one trial.

✅ Teaching Tips & Best Practices

🎯 Start with Tree Diagrams

Before introducing formulas, have students draw tree diagrams for small problems (3 shirts × 2 pants). This builds intuition for WHY we multiply. Then show how formulas are shortcuts for larger problems where drawing isn't practical.

🎯 Use the "Swap Test" for Permutation vs. Combination

After students identify their answer, ask: "If I swapped two of the selected items, would I count it as a different outcome?" If yes → permutation. If no → combination. This simple test catches most errors.

🎯 Connect to Probability

Show students that counting is the foundation of probability: P(E) = favorable outcomes / total outcomes. Both numerator and denominator often require counting techniques. This motivates WHY we learn these formulas.

🎯 Use Consistent Problem Categories

Group problems by context: 🏃 Races (permutation - places matter), 🎴 Card hands (combination - order doesn't matter), 🔐 Passwords (multiplication - repetition allowed), 👥 Committees (combination - just membership). Students learn to recognize patterns.

🎯 Factorial as "Countdown"

Teach n! as "start at n, multiply counting down to 1." This helps students see P(n,r) as "start at n, count down r times" and understand why we divide by (n-r)! to stop at the right place.

🎓 Scenario Cards for Error Analysis

Scenario 1: The Committee Calculation

A student calculates the number of ways to form a 3-person committee from 10 people as: 10 × 9 × 8 = 720. What's their error?

Click to reveal analysis
Error: The student used permutations (P(10,3) = 720) instead of combinations. A committee doesn't have ranked positions, so {Alice, Bob, Carol} is the same committee as {Carol, Alice, Bob}.

Correct Answer: C(10,3) = 720/3! = 720/6 = 120 committees

Teaching Approach: Ask: "If Alice, Bob, and Carol are on the committee, does it matter who was 'picked first'?" No—they're all just committee members. We divide by 3! to remove the duplicate arrangements.

Scenario 2: The License Plate Problem

A student is asked how many license plates can be made with 3 letters followed by 3 digits. They calculate: P(26,3) × P(10,3) = 15,600 × 720 = 11,232,000. What went wrong?

Click to reveal analysis
Error: The student used permutations, which assumes no repetition. License plates CAN repeat letters and digits (AAA-111 is valid).

Correct Answer: 26³ × 10³ = 17,576 × 1,000 = 17,576,000 plates

Teaching Approach: Ask: "Can a license plate have the same letter twice? Like AAB-123?" Yes! When repetition is allowed, use the multiplication principle with the FULL set of choices each time, not permutations.

Scenario 3: The Race Results

For a race with 30 runners, a student calculates the number of ways to award gold, silver, and bronze as C(30,3) = 4,060. What's the issue?

Click to reveal analysis
Error: The student used combinations, but medal positions ARE different. Gold-Silver-Bronze for {Alice, Bob, Carol} is different from {Carol, Bob, Alice}.

Correct Answer: P(30,3) = 30 × 29 × 28 = 24,360 ways

Teaching Approach: Ask: "Is getting gold the same as getting bronze?" No! The positions have different values, so order matters. Use the "swap test": swapping gold and bronze winners gives a different outcome.

Scenario 4: The Expected Value Misinterpretation

A game costs $5 to play. You win $20 with probability 0.2 and $0 otherwise. A student calculates E(X) = 0.2(20) + 0.8(0) = $4 and says "I expect to win $4 each game." What's wrong with this interpretation?

Click to reveal analysis
Error: The student forgot to account for the cost to play and misinterprets expected value as a per-game guarantee.

Correct Answer: E(X) = 0.2(20-5) + 0.8(0-5) = 0.2(15) + 0.8(-5) = 3 - 4 = -$1. On average, you LOSE $1 per game.

Teaching Approach: (1) Always subtract the cost from winnings. (2) Expected value is a LONG-RUN average, not what happens in any single game. After 100 games, you'd expect to be down about $100.

Scenario 5: The Yogurt Arrangement

A student has 3 cherry, 2 raspberry, and 2 blueberry yogurts (7 total) to arrange for the week. They calculate 7! = 5,040 arrangements. What did they miss?

Click to reveal analysis
Error: The student treated all yogurts as distinguishable, but yogurts of the same flavor are identical. Swapping two cherry yogurts doesn't create a new arrangement.

Correct Answer: 7!/(3!×2!×2!) = 5040/(6×2×2) = 5040/24 = 210 arrangements

Teaching Approach: This is a permutation with repetition. We divide by the factorial of each group of identical items to remove "duplicate" arrangements that look the same.
