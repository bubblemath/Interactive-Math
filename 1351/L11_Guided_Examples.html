Unit Conversion Examples

Converting between units using unit fractions

🔑 Key Concept

A unit fraction is a fraction equal to 1, where the numerator and denominator represent the same measure in different units. Multiplying by a unit fraction changes the unit without changing the value.

12 in / 1 ft = 1
4 qt / 1 gal = 1
1000 m / 1 km = 1

📝 Example 1: Feet to Inches Basic

▼

Problem

Convert 7.5 feet to inches.

Step 1
Identify the conversion relationship

We know that 1 foot = 12 inches. This is the relationship we'll use to build our unit fraction.

Step 2
Set up the unit fraction so unwanted units cancel

We want to eliminate feet and keep inches. Since "feet" is currently in the numerator, we need "feet" in the denominator of our unit fraction:

7.5 ft × (12 in / 1 ft)
The unit we want to get rid of goes in the denominator. The unit we want to keep goes in the numerator.
Step 3
Cancel units and multiply

The "ft" in the numerator cancels with the "ft" in the denominator:

7.5 ft × (12 in / 1 ft) = 7.5 × 12 in = 90 in

✅ Final Answer

7.5 feet = 90 inches

Your Turn

Convert 4.25 feet to inches using the same method. (Answer: 51 inches)

📝 Example 2: Gallons to Cups Intermediate

▼

Problem

Convert 3 gallons to cups.

Step 1
Identify the conversion chain

There's no single conversion from gallons directly to cups. We need a chain:

1 gallon = 4 quarts 1 quart = 2 pints 1 pint = 2 cups
Step 2
Build the chain of unit fractions

Line up each fraction so that intermediate units cancel step by step:

3 gal × (4 qt / 1 gal) × (2 pt / 1 qt) × (2 cups / 1 pt)
Each fraction equals 1. We're just "translating" from one unit to the next.
Step 3
Cancel units step by step
3 gal × (4 qt / 1 gal) × (2 pt / 1 qt) × (2 cups / 1 pt)

All intermediate units cancel, leaving only cups.

Step 4
Multiply the numbers
3 × 4 × 2 × 2 = 48 cups

✅ Final Answer

3 gallons = 48 cups

Your Turn

Convert 2.5 gallons to cups. (Answer: 40 cups)

📝 Example 3: Metric Conversion — Kilometers to Centimeters Intermediate

▼

Problem

Convert 2.5 kilometers to centimeters.

Step 1
Identify the metric relationships

In the metric system, everything is based on powers of 10:

1 km = 1,000 m 1 m = 100 cm
Remember the metric staircase: King Henry Died By Drinking Chocolate Milk (Kilo, Hecto, Deka, Base, Deci, Centi, Milli). Each step is ×10.
Step 2
Build the conversion chain
2.5 km × (1,000 m / 1 km) × (100 cm / 1 m)
Step 3
Cancel and multiply
2.5 km × (1,000 m / 1 km) × (100 cm / 1 m) = 2.5 × 1,000 × 100 cm = 250,000 cm

✅ Final Answer

2.5 km = 250,000 cm

Your Turn

Convert 0.75 km to millimeters. (Answer: 750,000 mm)

Dimensional Analysis Examples

Multi-step conversions including rates and squared/cubed units

🔑 Key Concept

Dimensional analysis chains multiple unit fractions together. Each fraction equals 1, so the value doesn't change — only the units do. For rates, you convert numerator and denominator units separately.

📝 Example 1: Miles per Hour to Feet per Second Advanced

▼

Problem

Convert 60 miles per hour to feet per second.

Step 1
Identify what needs to change

We need to change TWO units:

miles → feet (distance unit) hours → seconds (time unit)
Step 2
List the conversion relationships
1 mile = 5,280 feet 1 hour = 60 minutes 1 minute = 60 seconds
Step 3
Build the chain — convert miles to feet first

"Miles" is in the numerator, so put "miles" in the denominator to cancel:

60 mi/hr × (5,280 ft / 1 mi)
Step 4
Now convert hours to seconds

"Hours" is in the denominator, so put "hours" in the numerator to cancel. We go hours → minutes → seconds:

60 mi/hr × (5,280 ft / 1 mi) × (1 hr / 60 min) × (1 min / 60 sec)
When a unit is in the denominator of the original expression, you need it in the NUMERATOR of the unit fraction to cancel it.
Step 5
Cancel all units and compute
60 × 5,280 ft ─────────────── 1 × 60 × 60 sec = 316,800 ft / 3,600 sec = 88 ft/sec

✅ Final Answer

60 mph = 88 feet per second

Your Turn

Convert 45 miles per hour to feet per second. (Answer: 66 ft/sec)

📝 Example 2: Square Feet to Square Inches Advanced

▼

Problem

Convert 3 square feet to square inches.

Step 1
Understand what "square feet" means

"Square feet" means ft × ft. So we need to convert feet to inches TWICE — once for each dimension.

3 ft² = 3 × ft × ft
Common mistake: Students often multiply by 12 once and get 36. But ft² means ft × ft, so you must convert BOTH dimensions!
Step 2
Apply the conversion factor twice
3 ft² × (12 in / 1 ft) × (12 in / 1 ft)

Or equivalently:

3 ft² × (12 in / 1 ft)² = 3 × 144 in²
Step 3
Calculate
3 × 144 = 432 in²
The conversion factor for area is always the LINEAR factor SQUARED. For volume, it would be CUBED. This is why 1 ft² = 144 in² (not 12), and 1 ft³ = 1,728 in³ (not 12).

✅ Final Answer

3 ft² = 432 in²

Your Turn

Convert 5 square yards to square feet. (Answer: 45 ft² — because 1 yd = 3 ft, so 1 yd² = 9 ft²)

📝 Example 3: Medicine Dosage Conversion Intermediate

▼

Problem

A child weighs 55 pounds. The recommended dosage of a medication is 15 mg per kilogram of body weight. How many milligrams should the child receive? (1 kg ≈ 2.2 lbs)

Step 1
Convert pounds to kilograms
55 lbs × (1 kg / 2.2 lbs) = 25 kg
Step 2
Apply the dosage rate
25 kg × (15 mg / 1 kg) = 375 mg
Step 3
Or do it all in one chain
55 lbs × (1 kg / 2.2 lbs) × (15 mg / 1 kg) = 375 mg
This is a real-world application where unit errors can be dangerous. Dimensional analysis ensures the units work out correctly every time.

✅ Final Answer

The child should receive 375 mg

Your Turn

A child weighs 66 lbs. The dosage is 10 mg per kg. What is the dose? (Answer: 300 mg)

Perimeter & Circumference Examples

Finding the distance around shapes

🔑 Key Formulas

Square: P = 4s
Rectangle: P = 2L + 2W
Triangle: P = a + b + c
Circle: C = 2πr = πd

📝 Example 1: Finding Length from Perimeter Basic

▼

Problem

A rectangular field has a perimeter of 400 ft and a width of 73 ft. Find the length.

Step 1
Write the perimeter formula for a rectangle
P = 2L + 2W
Step 2
Substitute known values
400 = 2L + 2(73) 400 = 2L + 146
Step 3
Solve for L
400 - 146 = 2L 254 = 2L L = 127 ft
Step 4
Verify
P = 2(127) + 2(73) = 254 + 146 = 400 ✓
Always verify your answer by plugging it back into the original formula!

✅ Final Answer

The length of the field is 127 ft

Your Turn

A rectangle has P = 56 cm and length = 18 cm. Find the width. (Answer: 10 cm)

📝 Example 2: Circumference of a Circle Basic

▼

Problem

A circular garden has a diameter of 14 feet. Find the circumference. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Identify the radius

The diameter is 14 ft, so the radius is:

r = d / 2 = 14 / 2 = 7 ft
Always check: are you given the radius or the diameter? This is one of the most common errors in circle problems.
Step 2
Apply the circumference formula
C = 2πr = 2 × 3.14 × 7 = 43.96 ft

Or equivalently using diameter:

C = πd = 3.14 × 14 = 43.96 ft

✅ Final Answer

C = 43.96 feet

Your Turn

Find the circumference of a circle with radius 10 cm. (Answer: 62.8 cm)

📝 Example 3: Perimeter of a Composite Shape Intermediate

▼

Problem

A running track consists of a rectangle (100 m × 60 m) with a semicircle on each short end. Find the total distance around the track. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Identify the components of the perimeter

The track perimeter consists of:

• Two straight sides (the long sides of the rectangle): 100 m each • Two semicircles (on the short ends): each with diameter = 60 m
The short sides of the rectangle are NOT part of the perimeter — they're replaced by the semicircles.
Step 2
Calculate the straight portions
Straight sides = 2 × 100 = 200 m
Step 3
Calculate the curved portions

Two semicircles with diameter 60 m = one full circle with diameter 60 m:

Curved portion = πd = 3.14 × 60 = 188.4 m
Step 4
Add them together
Total perimeter = 200 + 188.4 = 388.4 m

✅ Final Answer

The track is 388.4 meters around

Your Turn

A track has straight sides of 80 m and semicircles with diameter 50 m. Find the total perimeter. (Answer: 317 m)

Area Formula Examples

Calculating the space inside shapes

🔑 Key Formulas

Rectangle: A = L × W
Triangle: A = ½bh
Parallelogram: A = bh
Trapezoid: A = ½(b₁+b₂)h
Circle: A = πr²
Reg. Polygon: A = ½ap

📝 Example 1: Area of a Triangle Basic

▼

Problem

Find the area of a triangle with a base of 12 cm and a height of 8 cm.

Step 1
Identify the formula
A = ½ × b × h
The height must be PERPENDICULAR to the base. It is NOT always a side of the triangle.
Step 2
Substitute and calculate
A = ½ × 12 × 8 A = ½ × 96 A = 48 cm²

✅ Final Answer

A = 48 cm²

Your Turn

Find the area of a triangle with base 15 in and height 10 in. (Answer: 75 in²)

📝 Example 2: Area of a Trapezoid Intermediate

▼

Problem

Find the area of a trapezoid with parallel sides (bases) of 5 cm and 7 cm, and a height of 17 cm.

Step 1
Identify the formula and the parts
A = ½ × (b₁ + b₂) × h

Where b₁ and b₂ are the two parallel sides (bases) and h is the perpendicular distance between them.

b₁ = 5 cm, b₂ = 7 cm, h = 17 cm
Step 2
Add the bases
b₁ + b₂ = 5 + 7 = 12 cm
Step 3
Apply the formula
A = ½ × 12 × 17 A = 6 × 17 A = 102 cm²
Think of the trapezoid formula as finding the "average base" (½(b₁+b₂)) and then multiplying by the height — just like a rectangle with the average width!

✅ Final Answer

A = 102 cm²

Your Turn

Find the area of a trapezoid with bases 8 ft and 14 ft, and height 6 ft. (Answer: 66 ft²)

📝 Example 3: Area of a Circle Basic

▼

Problem

Find the area of a circle with a diameter of 20 inches. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Find the radius
r = d / 2 = 20 / 2 = 10 in
The formula uses RADIUS, not diameter. If you're given the diameter, always divide by 2 first!
Step 2
Apply the area formula
A = πr² A = 3.14 × 10² A = 3.14 × 100 A = 314 in²
Step 3
Check: What if you accidentally used diameter?
WRONG: A = 3.14 × 20² = 3.14 × 400 = 1,256 in² ← 4× too large!
Using diameter instead of radius gives an answer that is exactly 4 times too large. This is a quick way to check if you made this common error.

✅ Final Answer

A = 314 in²

Your Turn

Find the area of a circle with radius 6 cm. (Answer: 113.04 cm²)

📝 Example 4: Area of a Parallelogram Basic

▼

Problem

A parallelogram has a base of 15 m and a height of 9 m. The slant side is 11 m. Find the area.

Step 1
Identify the correct measurements

For a parallelogram, we need the base and the perpendicular height (not the slant side).

base = 15 m, height = 9 m
The slant side (11 m) is NOT the height! The height is the perpendicular distance between the two parallel bases. Students often confuse these.
Step 2
Apply the formula
A = b × h = 15 × 9 = 135 m²
Why does this work? If you cut a triangle off one end of a parallelogram and move it to the other end, you get a rectangle with the same base and height. So A = bh, just like a rectangle!

✅ Final Answer

A = 135 m²

Your Turn

A parallelogram has base 20 cm, height 7 cm, and slant side 10 cm. Find the area. (Answer: 140 cm²)

Composite Figure Examples

Breaking complex shapes into simpler ones

🔑 Key Strategy

Composite figures are made of two or more basic shapes combined. The strategy is: (1) Identify the basic shapes, (2) Calculate each area separately, (3) Add areas for combined shapes or subtract for cutouts.

📝 Example 1: L-Shaped Room Intermediate

▼

Problem

An L-shaped room has outer dimensions of 20 ft × 15 ft, with a 6 ft × 8 ft rectangular piece removed from one corner. Find the total floor area.

Step 1
Strategy: Full rectangle minus the cutout

Think of the L-shape as a full rectangle with a rectangular piece removed:

L-shape area = Full rectangle − Removed rectangle
Step 2
Calculate the full rectangle
Full rectangle = 20 × 15 = 300 ft²
Step 3
Calculate the removed piece
Removed rectangle = 6 × 8 = 48 ft²
Step 4
Subtract
L-shape area = 300 − 48 = 252 ft²
Alternative method: You could also split the L into two rectangles and ADD their areas. Both methods give the same answer — use whichever feels more natural!

✅ Final Answer

A = 252 ft²

Your Turn

An L-shaped patio is 18 ft × 12 ft with a 4 ft × 5 ft corner removed. Find the area. (Answer: 196 ft²)

📝 Example 2: Rectangle with Circular Cutout Advanced

▼

Problem

A rectangular metal plate is 24 cm × 18 cm. A circular hole with diameter 10 cm is cut from the center. Find the remaining area. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Strategy: Rectangle minus circle
Remaining area = Rectangle area − Circle area
Step 2
Calculate the rectangle area
A_rect = 24 × 18 = 432 cm²
Step 3
Calculate the circle area

Diameter = 10 cm, so radius = 5 cm:

A_circle = πr² = 3.14 × 5² = 3.14 × 25 = 78.50 cm²
Step 4
Subtract
Remaining = 432 − 78.50 = 353.50 cm²

✅ Final Answer

A = 353.50 cm²

Your Turn

A 30 cm × 20 cm rectangle has a circle of radius 4 cm removed. Find the remaining area. (Answer: 549.76 cm²)

📝 Example 3: Rectangle with Semicircle on Top Advanced

▼

Problem

A window is shaped like a rectangle (4 ft wide × 6 ft tall) with a semicircle on top. Find the total area of the window. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Strategy: Rectangle plus semicircle
Total area = Rectangle area + Semicircle area
Step 2
Calculate the rectangle area
A_rect = 4 × 6 = 24 ft²
Step 3
Calculate the semicircle area

The semicircle sits on the 4 ft side, so its diameter = 4 ft and radius = 2 ft:

A_semi = ½ × πr² A_semi = ½ × 3.14 × 2² A_semi = ½ × 3.14 × 4 A_semi = 6.28 ft²
Step 4
Add them together
Total = 24 + 6.28 = 30.28 ft²

✅ Final Answer

A = 30.28 ft²

Your Turn

A window is 3 ft wide × 5 ft tall with a semicircle on top. Find the total area. (Answer: 18.53 ft²)

Sector & Arc Length Examples

Working with partial circles

🔑 Key Formulas

A sector is a "pie slice" of a circle. The fraction of the circle is determined by the central angle θ divided by 360°.

Arc Length = (θ/360) × 2πr
Sector Area = (θ/360) × πr²

📝 Example 1: Arc Length of a Pizza Slice Basic

▼

Problem

A pizza has a diameter of 16 inches and is cut into 8 equal slices. Find the crust length (arc length) of one slice. (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Find the central angle and radius
Central angle: θ = 360° ÷ 8 = 45° Radius: r = 16 ÷ 2 = 8 inches
Step 2
Find the fraction of the circle
Fraction = θ / 360° = 45° / 360° = 1/8
This makes sense! If you cut a pizza into 8 slices, each slice IS 1/8 of the circle.
Step 3
Apply the arc length formula
Arc length = (θ/360) × 2πr = (1/8) × 2 × 3.14 × 8 = (1/8) × 50.24 = 6.28 inches

✅ Final Answer

Arc length = 6.28 inches

Your Turn

A pizza with diameter 12 inches is cut into 6 slices. Find the crust length of one slice. (Answer: 6.28 in)

📝 Example 2: Sector Area — Windshield Wiper Intermediate

▼

Problem

A windshield wiper is 20 inches long and sweeps through an angle of 150°. What area does it clean? (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Identify the values
r = 20 inches (the wiper length IS the radius) θ = 150°
Step 2
Find the fraction of the circle
Fraction = 150° / 360° = 5/12
Step 3
Apply the sector area formula
Sector area = (θ/360) × πr² = (5/12) × 3.14 × 20² = (5/12) × 3.14 × 400 = (5/12) × 1,256 = 523.33 in²

✅ Final Answer

A ≈ 523.33 in²

Your Turn

A wiper is 18 inches long and sweeps 120°. Find the area cleaned. (Answer: 339.12 in²)

📝 Example 3: Perimeter of a Sector (Arc + Radii) Advanced

▼

Problem

A garden bed is shaped like a quarter circle (90° sector) with a radius of 10 feet. Find the total perimeter (the distance around the entire shape, including the two straight edges and the curved edge). (Use π ≈ 3.14)

Step 1
Identify all parts of the perimeter

A sector's perimeter has THREE parts:

Perimeter = Arc length + Radius + Radius Perimeter = Arc + 2r
Don't forget the two straight edges (the radii)! Students often calculate only the arc length and forget the straight sides.
Step 2
Calculate the arc length
Arc = (θ/360) × 2πr = (90/360) × 2 × 3.14 × 10 = (1/4) × 62.8 = 15.70 ft
Step 3
Add the two radii
Perimeter = Arc + 2r = 15.70 + 2(10) = 15.70 + 20 = 35.70 ft

✅ Final Answer

Perimeter = 35.70 ft

Your Turn

A 60° sector has radius 12 ft. Find the total perimeter. (Answer: 36.56 ft)

Regular Polygon Examples

Using the apothem to find area: A = ½ × apothem × perimeter

🔑 Key Formulas & Vocabulary

A regular polygon has all sides equal and all angles equal. The apothem is the perpendicular distance from the center to the midpoint of any side.

Perimeter: P = n × s
Area: A = ½ × a × P

where n = number of sides, s = side length, a = apothem, P = perimeter

📝 Example 1: Regular Hexagon Basic

▼

Problem

A regular hexagon has a side length of 8 cm and an apothem of 6.93 cm. Find the perimeter and area.

Step 1
Find the perimeter

A hexagon has 6 sides, all equal:

P = n × s = 6 × 8 = 48 cm
Step 2
Apply the area formula
A = ½ × a × P A = ½ × 6.93 × 48 A = ½ × 332.64 A = 166.32 cm²
Step 3
Why does this formula work?

Imagine dividing the hexagon into 6 triangles from the center. Each triangle has:

• Base = one side of the hexagon (8 cm) • Height = the apothem (6.93 cm) • Area of one triangle = ½ × 8 × 6.93 = 27.72 cm² • Total = 6 × 27.72 = 166.32 cm²
This is exactly the same as A = ½ × apothem × perimeter! The formula just combines all the triangles at once.

✅ Final Answer

P = 48 cm, A = 166.32 cm²

Your Turn

A regular hexagon has side length 10 m and apothem 8.66 m. Find the area. (Answer: 259.8 m²)

📝 Example 2: Regular Pentagon Intermediate

▼

Problem

The Pentagon building in Washington, D.C. is approximately a regular pentagon with a side length of 921 ft and an apothem of 633.8 ft. Find its floor area.

Step 1
Find the perimeter
P = 5 × 921 = 4,605 ft
Step 2
Apply the area formula
A = ½ × a × P A = ½ × 633.8 × 4,605 A = ½ × 2,918,649 A = 1,459,324.5 ft²
Step 3
Convert to a meaningful unit (optional)

Since 1 acre = 43,560 ft²:

1,459,324.5 ÷ 43,560 ≈ 33.5 acres
This is a great real-world connection! The actual Pentagon building is about 29 acres (the inner courtyard reduces the usable area).

✅ Final Answer

A = 1,459,324.5 ft² ≈ 33.5 acres

Your Turn

A regular pentagon has side length 12 m and apothem 8.26 m. Find the area. (Answer: 247.8 m²)

📝 Example 3: Connecting to Circle Area Advanced

▼

Problem

Show that the regular polygon area formula (A = ½ap) approaches the circle area formula (A = πr²) as the number of sides increases. Use a regular polygon inscribed in a circle of radius 10 cm.

Step 1
Understand the connection

As a regular polygon gets more and more sides, it looks more and more like a circle. Let's see what happens to A = ½ap:

As n → ∞: • The apothem (a) → the radius (r) • The perimeter (P) → the circumference (2πr)
Step 2
Substitute the limits into the formula
A = ½ × a × P ↓ ↓ A = ½ × r × 2πr A = ½ × 2πr² A = πr²
This is one of the most beautiful connections in geometry! The circle area formula is just the polygon formula taken to its limit.
Step 3
Verify with numbers (r = 10 cm)
Circle area = π × 10² = 314.16 cm² Regular polygon areas (inscribed in circle of r = 10): • Hexagon (6 sides): a ≈ 8.66, P = 60 → A = ½(8.66)(60) = 259.8 • Dodecagon (12 sides): a ≈ 9.66, P ≈ 62.0 → A = ½(9.66)(62) = 299.5 • 24-gon: a ≈ 9.91, P ≈ 62.6 → A = ½(9.91)(62.6) = 310.2 • 100-gon: a ≈ 9.995, P ≈ 62.8 → A = ½(9.995)(62.8) = 313.8 Getting closer and closer to 314.16!

✅ Final Answer

As n → ∞, A = ½ap → A = πr² ✓

Your Turn

Calculate the area of a regular 20-gon inscribed in a circle of radius 5 cm. Compare it to πr² = 78.5 cm². How close is it?
