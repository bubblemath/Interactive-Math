Only three regular polygons can tessellate the plane alone: equilateral triangles (60°), squares (90°), and regular hexagons (120°). This is because their interior angles are the only ones that divide evenly into 360° at a vertex!

📚 Key Concepts

Master the vocabulary and mathematics of tessellations!

🧩 Tessellation

A pattern of shapes that covers a plane with no gaps and no overlaps. Also called a "tiling."

The shapes must fit together perfectly at every vertex.

📐 Regular Tessellation

A tessellation made of only one type of regular polygon. There are exactly 3 regular tessellations.

Triangles, Squares, or Hexagons only!

🏛️ Semi-Regular Tessellation

A tessellation made of two or more types of regular polygons, where every vertex has the same configuration.

There are exactly 8 semi-regular tessellations.

🔢 Vertex Configuration

A notation listing the polygons meeting at each vertex, in order. For example, 3.3.3.3.6 means 4 triangles and 1 hexagon.

Numbers represent the number of sides of each polygon.

🔑 The 360° Rule

For a regular polygon to tessellate alone, its interior angle must divide evenly into 360°.

Interior Angle Formula: \(\frac{(n-2) \times 180°}{n}\) where n = number of sides

Only 60°, 90°, and 120° work because: 360 ÷ 60 = 6, 360 ÷ 90 = 4, 360 ÷ 120 = 3 (all whole numbers!)

🎓 Educator's Lab

Teaching strategies, common misconceptions, and Van Hiele connections for tessellations.

📊 Van Hiele Connections

Level 1 (Analysis): Students recognize tessellations and identify the shapes used.

Level 2 (Informal Deduction): Students understand WHY only certain shapes tessellate based on angle measures.

💡 Progression Activity

Have students PREDICT whether a shape will tessellate before testing. Ask: "What's the interior angle? Does it divide 360° evenly?"

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

"Any shape that fits together once will tessellate forever"

Not true! A shape must work at EVERY vertex, not just one. Pentagons can fit together locally but create gaps globally.

"Only regular polygons can tessellate"

Many irregular shapes tessellate! Any quadrilateral can tessellate, and so can many triangles and hexagons.

"Octagons tessellate because they're regular"

Regularity isn't enough! 135° doesn't divide 360° evenly, so octagons alone cannot tessellate.

🛠️ Teaching Strategies

  • Pattern Blocks: Physical manipulation before digital exploration
  • Tracing Paper: Students trace and rotate shapes to test tessellations
  • Angle Protractors: Measure interior angles and test the 360° rule
  • Escher Art: Show how mathematics creates beautiful art
  • Real-World Hunt: Find tessellations in floor tiles, honeycombs, soccer balls
💡 The "Gap Test"

Have students physically try to fit pentagons around a point. The visible gap makes the 360° rule concrete!

💬 "Explain to a 5th Grader"

Tessellation: "Imagine covering your floor with tiles so there are no gaps and no tiles overlap. That's a tessellation!"

Why Only 3?: "At every corner where tiles meet, the angles must add up to exactly 360° (a full turn). Only triangles, squares, and hexagons have angles that work!"

Vertex Configuration: "It's like a recipe that tells you which shapes meet at each corner. 4.4.4.4 means four squares meet at every corner."

🌍 Cultural Connections

🕌 Islamic Art

Islamic geometric patterns use tessellations extensively. The Alhambra palace in Spain contains all 17 possible wallpaper groups!

🎨 M.C. Escher

Dutch artist Escher created famous tessellations of birds, fish, and lizards by modifying basic shapes using translations and rotations.

🐝 Nature

Honeycomb is a natural hexagonal tessellation—bees discovered that hexagons use the least wax to create the most storage!
