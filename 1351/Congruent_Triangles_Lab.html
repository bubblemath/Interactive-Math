Congruent Triangles Lab

Understanding the Four Congruence Criteria

📐

Congruent triangles are EXACTLY the same size and shape. If two triangles are congruent, all their corresponding parts (sides and angles) are equal. We write \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF\) to show congruence!

📋 The Four Congruence Criteria

Click each card to flip and learn more about each criterion!

SSS
Side-Side-Side

All three pairs of corresponding sides are equal.

👆 Click to see more

SSS in Detail

If you know all three sides of a triangle, there is only one possible triangle you can make.

Example: If \(AB = DE\), \(BC = EF\), and \(CA = FD\), then \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF\).

Think of it: Like building with rigid sticks—once you have three specific lengths, only one triangle shape is possible!

SAS
Side-Angle-Side

Two sides and the included angle (the angle BETWEEN those sides) are equal.

👆 Click to see more

SAS in Detail

The angle must be between the two known sides. This is called the "included angle."

Example: If \(AB = DE\), \(\angle A = \angle D\), and \(AC = DF\), then \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF\).

Key Point: The angle acts like a "hinge" that determines how the two sides connect!

ASA
Angle-Side-Angle

Two angles and the included side (the side BETWEEN those angles) are equal.

👆 Click to see more

ASA in Detail

The side must be between the two known angles. This is called the "included side."

Example: If \(\angle A = \angle D\), \(AB = DE\), and \(\angle B = \angle E\), then \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF\).

Why it works: Two angles determine the third (since angles sum to 180°), and the side sets the scale!

AAS
Angle-Angle-Side

Two angles and a non-included side (NOT between the angles) are equal.

👆 Click to see more

AAS in Detail

The side is not between the two known angles—it's adjacent to one of them.

Example: If \(\angle A = \angle D\), \(\angle B = \angle E\), and \(BC = EF\), then \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF\).

Note: AAS is essentially equivalent to ASA because knowing two angles means you know the third!

📝 Quick Reference

SSS: 3 sides
SAS: 2 sides + included angle
ASA: 2 angles + included side
AAS: 2 angles + non-included side

⚠️ The Problem with SSA

When you have two sides and a non-included angle (the angle is NOT between the two sides), the "swinging" side can potentially hit the base at two different points!

This creates two different triangles with the same SSA information—so SSA cannot prove that two triangles are congruent.

Remember: For the angle to be useful, it must be the included angle (SAS) or you need two angles (ASA/AAS).

🤔 What About AAA?

AAA (Angle-Angle-Angle) also does NOT prove congruence! If two triangles have the same angles, they are similar (same shape) but not necessarily congruent (same size). One could be a scaled-up version of the other!

We'll explore similarity in the next applet!

📝 Correspondence Rule

When we write \(\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF\), the order tells us:
• A ↔ D (first letters correspond)
• B ↔ E (second letters correspond)
• C ↔ F (third letters correspond)

So: AB ↔ DE, BC ↔ EF, CA ↔ FD, ∠A ↔ ∠D, ∠B ↔ ∠E, ∠C ↔ ∠F

🎓 Educator's Lab

Teaching strategies and Van Hiele connections for triangle congruence.

📊 Van Hiele Connection

Triangle congruence bridges Van Hiele levels:

  • Level 1: Recognizing congruent triangles visually ("they look the same")
  • Level 2: Using properties to PROVE congruence (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS)
  • Level 3: Understanding WHY these criteria work (deductive reasoning)
🎯 Teaching Goal

Move students from "they look congruent" to "I can PROVE they're congruent using specific criteria."

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

"SSA proves congruence"

The ambiguous case shows why SSA fails. Use the "swinging door" demonstration!

"AAA proves congruence"

AAA proves SIMILARITY, not congruence. Draw two triangles with the same angles but different sizes.

"The included part doesn't matter"

Emphasize: SAS needs the angle BETWEEN the sides; ASA needs the side BETWEEN the angles.

🛠️ Manipulative Ideas

  • Patty Paper: Trace one triangle and overlay it on another to verify congruence physically.
  • Straws/Sticks: Build triangles with specific side lengths to demonstrate SSS.
  • Protractors + Rulers: Have students construct triangles given SAS or ASA information.
  • GeoGebra: Dynamic geometry software to explore the ambiguous case interactively.
💡 Activity Idea

Give pairs of students the same SSS measurements and have them build triangles independently—they should get congruent results!

🗣️ "Explain to a 5th Grader"

Practice simplifying the concepts:

  • Congruent: "Exactly the same size and shape—like identical twins!"
  • SSS: "If all three sides match, the triangles are twins."
  • SAS: "Two sides and the corner between them—like a hinge that can only open one way."
  • Why not SSA: "The third side can 'swing' to two different spots, making two different triangles."
