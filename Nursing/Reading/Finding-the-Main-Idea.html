📖 TEAS Reading: Finding the Main Idea

Topic, main idea, and central theme — what a passage is really saying

📖 What Is the Main Idea?

The main idea is the single most important point an author makes — the one sentence that sums up what the whole passage is saying. It’s different from the topic (what the passage is about) and from the supporting details (the facts and examples that back it up). Click the paragraph below to see how those pieces fit together.

📋 The Core Ideas

🔍 Topic vs. Main Idea vs. Details

PieceWhat it isExample
TopicWhat the passage is about (a word or phrase)“handwashing”
Main ideaThe author’s main point about the topic (a full sentence)“Handwashing prevents the spread of infection.”
DetailsFacts, examples, and reasons that support the main idea“Soap removes germs”; “sanitizer kills microbes”

🎯 Central Theme & Central Idea

  • The central theme is the deeper message or lesson of a piece — common in stories (for example, “perseverance pays off”).
  • In informational text, the same idea is often called the central idea: the overall point across several paragraphs.
  • A theme is usually implied — you infer it from what happens, rather than reading it in one sentence.

🧭 How to Find the Main Idea

  • Ask “Who or what is this about?” → that’s the topic.
  • Then ask “What’s the main point about it?” → that’s the main idea.
  • Check the first and last sentences — the main idea often lives in the topic sentence.
  • Sum it up in one sentence. A good main idea covers the whole passage — not too broad, not too narrow.

🌟 Why Wrong Answers Are Wrong

  • Too broad: bigger than the passage (covers more than the author actually discusses).
  • Too narrow: only one detail, not the whole point.
  • Off-topic: not actually stated or discussed in the passage.
  • True but minor: a real supporting detail — but not the main point.

✏️ Example 1: The main idea is stated

Good sleep is vital to health. During deep sleep, the body repairs tissue, strengthens the immune system, and stores memories. People who sleep too little get sick more often.
  1. Topic? (Who/what is this about?) → sleep and health.
  2. Main point about it? → the first sentence says it plainly: good sleep is vital to health.
  3. The other sentences (repairs tissue, stores memories) are supporting details — evidence for that point.

Main idea: “Good sleep is essential to staying healthy.” It covers the whole passage.

💭 Example 2: The main idea is implied

She checked the chart twice, confirmed the dose with a second nurse, and scanned the medication barcode. Only then did she give the medicine.
  1. No single sentence states the point — so you infer it from the details.
  2. Every action is a safety step taken before giving medicine.
  3. Put together, they point to one idea.

Implied main idea: “The nurse takes careful steps to give medication safely.”

⚖️ Example 3: Main idea vs. a detail

Exercise offers many benefits: a stronger heart, a better mood, a healthier weight, and improved sleep.
  1. “Exercise improves sleep” is true — but it’s just one benefit (too narrow).
  2. “Exercise has many health benefits” covers all four — that’s the main idea.
  3. A main idea covers the whole passage; a detail covers only part of it.

Main idea: “Exercise provides many health benefits.” The individual benefits are supporting details.
