📑 What Does It Mean to Compare Texts?

On the TEAS, you'll often read two passages about the same topic and answer questions about how they relate. To compare is to find similarities; to contrast is to find differences. The trick is that two texts can share a topic but still differ in purpose, main idea, tone, or details. Read both carefully, then ask: where do they agree, and where do they part ways?

🔗 Ways Two Texts Can Relate

RelationshipWhat it looks like
AgreeBoth make the same point or reach the same conclusion
Disagree / conflictThey reach opposite conclusions about the same topic
Same topic, different purposeOne informs, the other persuades (or entertains)
One adds detailBoth agree, but one gives more facts or examples
Different toneSame facts, but one is calm and one is urgent

🔎 Signal Words: Compare vs. Contrast

Compare (similarities)Contrast (differences)
both, alike, similarlyhowever, unlike, whereas
also, likewise, in the same wayon the other hand, in contrast, but
agree, share, in commondiffer, instead, while

🚩 Watch Out For…

  • Same topic ≠ same opinion. Two texts about vaccines can still disagree.
  • True of only one passage. A tempting answer may describe just Passage A or just B — not both.
  • Reversed details. An answer might swap which passage said what.
  • Too extreme. "They completely disagree" is often wrong when they mostly overlap.

🌟 How to Compare Two Passages

  1. Read both and name the shared topic.
  2. For each, ask: what's the main idea, the purpose, and the tone?
  3. Line them up: where do they agree? Where do they differ?
  4. Check each answer choice against both texts — is it true of A, B, both, or neither?
  5. Watch signal words like "however" and "both" that reveal the relationship.

🤝 Example 1: Find What They Share

Passage AGetting enough sleep helps patients heal. Rest gives the body time to repair tissue and fight infection.
Passage BSleep is medicine. Well-rested patients recover faster and report less pain during their stay.
  1. Name the shared topic: both are about sleep and healing.
  2. Line up the main ideas: A says rest helps the body repair; B says rest speeds recovery.
  3. The similarity: both agree that sleep helps patients heal and recover.

Takeaway: to compare, find the point both authors make — not a detail from just one.

↔️ Example 2: Find the Difference

Passage ALimiting kids' screen time is essential — too many hours are linked to poorer sleep and less active play.
Passage BScreens aren't the enemy. Educational apps and video calls with family can be valuable; what matters is how they're used.
  1. Same topic: children and screen time.
  2. But the main ideas differ: A argues for limiting screens; B argues that how screens are used matters more than how long.
  3. That's a contrast — the authors disagree about the best approach.

Takeaway: two texts can share a topic and still reach different conclusions. Name the disagreement precisely.

🎭 Example 3: Same Topic, Different Purpose

Passage AThe flu vaccine is updated each year to match expected strains. It is given by injection and takes about two weeks to build protection.
Passage BDon't wait — get your flu shot today! A few seconds now can save you a miserable week in bed.
  1. Both are about the flu vaccine (same topic).
  2. A's purpose is to inform — it explains facts calmly.
  3. B's purpose is to persuade — its urgent tone pushes you to act.

Takeaway: ask why each author wrote. Same facts can serve very different purposes and tones.
