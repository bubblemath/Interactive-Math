📊 Reading Charts & Graphics

On the TEAS you won't just read paragraphs — you'll read graphics: tables, bar graphs, line graphs, circle (pie) graphs, and pictographs. The trick is the same every time: find the one piece of information the question asks for and ignore the rest. Below, click each graphic type to see what it's best at and how to read it.

🧭 Every Graphic Has These Parts

  • Title — tells you what the whole graphic is about. Read it first.
  • Axes / headings — the labels along the bottom and side (or the row and column names).
  • Units — mg, °C, patients, %, hours. The number is meaningless without them.
  • Scale — how much each gridline is worth. Bars/points land between lines sometimes.
  • Key / legend — explains colors or symbols (essential for circle graphs and pictographs).

🌟 How to Read Any Graphic

  • Read the title and the units first — they tell you what you're looking at.
  • Underline exactly what the question asks — a value? the biggest? a difference? a trend?
  • Go straight to that one spot. Trace a row to a column, or a bar up to the scale.
  • Watch the scale. Gridlines may count by 2s, 5s, 10s — not always 1s.
  • Ignore the extra data. Most of the graphic is not the answer.

📊 Example 1: Reading a Bar Graph

Question: On which day were the most patients seen, and how many?

  1. Read the title and units: patients seen each day.
  2. The question wants the tallest bar — scan across and find it: Thursday.
  3. Trace the top of that bar left to the scale. It lines up with 22.

Answer: Thursday, with 22 patients. (The value label on top confirms it — but you should be able to read it from the scale too.)

📈 Example 2: Reading a Line Graph (a Trend)

Question: Between Day 2 and Day 4, what happened to the patient's temperature?

  1. Find Day 2 on the bottom axis, then read up to the point: 38.9 °C.
  2. Find Day 4 the same way: 37.2 °C.
  3. The line goes down — the fever is falling. The drop is 38.9 − 37.2 = 1.7 °C.

Answer: It fell by 1.7 °C. A line sloping down means the value is decreasing over time.

🥧 Example 3: Reading a Circle Graph (Part of a Whole)

Question: 200 units of blood were collected. How many were type O?

  1. The circle graph shows type O = 45% of the whole.
  2. "Of the whole" means multiply: 45% of 200 = 0.45 × 200.
  3. 0.45 × 200 = 90 units.

Answer: 90 units. A circle graph shows percentages — to get a count, multiply the percent by the total.
