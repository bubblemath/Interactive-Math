⚖️ What Is an Argument?

In reading, an argument isn't a fight — it's a claim backed by support. The author states a position and tries to convince you with reasons and evidence. Your job as a reader is to find the claim, look at what backs it up, and decide: is the support strong enough to believe it? Strong arguments rest on relevant, reliable, sufficient evidence — not just opinion, a single story, or "everyone knows."

🧪 Is the Evidence Strong? Ask R‑R‑S

TestAsk yourself…
RelevantDoes this actually relate to the claim — or is it off-topic?
ReliableIs the source trustworthy and unbiased? Who is saying it, and why?
SufficientIs there enough support — or just one story or a single number?

🚩 Weak-Support Red Flags

  • Opinion dressed as proof: "It's obviously the best." (says nothing — no evidence)
  • One anecdote: "It worked for my aunt." (a single story isn't data)
  • Everyone knows / everyone's doing it: popularity isn't proof (bandwagon)
  • Hasty generalization: one case → a sweeping rule for everyone
  • Emotional appeal only: tugging feelings instead of giving reasons
  • Biased source: the seller of a product "proving" it works

🌟 How to Evaluate an Argument

  1. Find the claim. What does the author want you to believe or do?
  2. Find the support. What reasons and evidence are given?
  3. Test it with R‑R‑S: is the evidence relevant, reliable, and sufficient?
  4. Watch for red flags: opinion, one anecdote, "everyone knows," emotion, bias.
  5. Decide: is the claim well-supported, or is it running ahead of its evidence?

📌 Example 1: Claim vs. Evidence

Our unit should switch to the new IV pumps. Since we started testing them, medication errors have dropped by nearly half, and nurses report they're easier to program.
  1. Find the claim — the point the author wants: "Our unit should switch to the new IV pumps."
  2. Find the evidence — what backs it up: errors dropped by half, and nurses find them easier.
  3. Notice the claim is an opinion/recommendation; the evidence is the support that makes it convincing.

Takeaway: the claim tells you what to believe; the evidence tells you why.

🔗 Example 2: Is the Evidence Relevant?

Claim: "This hand sanitizer kills germs effectively." Support offered: "It comes in a nice-smelling lavender scent and a sleek bottle."
  1. Ask: does the support actually relate to the claim about killing germs?
  2. Scent and bottle design say nothing about germ-killing — they're off-topic.
  3. Relevant support would be a lab result, like "kills 99.9% of common bacteria."

Takeaway: evidence has to connect to the exact claim. Pleasant but off-topic details aren't support.

🚩 Example 3: Spot the Weak Support

Claim: "This herbal tea cures the common cold." Support: "My neighbor drank it once and felt better the next day."
  1. The support is a single anecdote — one person, one time.
  2. People often recover from colds on their own, so this doesn't show the tea did anything (not sufficient).
  3. Strong support would be a controlled study comparing tea-drinkers to non-drinkers.

Takeaway: one story isn't data. Ask whether there's enough reliable evidence to trust the claim.
