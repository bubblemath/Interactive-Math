📖 TEAS Reading: Main Idea vs. Detail

Telling the umbrella point from the details that hold it up

📖 Supporting Details

The main idea is the big, general point of a passage. Supporting details are the specific facts, examples, and reasons that hold it up — like the legs under a table. A detail is important, but on its own it never covers the whole passage. Click the diagram below to see how the pieces stack together.

📋 The Core Ideas

⚖️ Main Idea vs. Detail

How big?General — covers the whole passageSpecific — covers just one point
Its jobMakes the author’s main pointBacks up the main idea
How many?Usually oneUsually several

🔎 Types of Supporting Details

  • Facts & statistics: measurable support — “infection rates fell by 30%.”
  • Examples: a specific instance — “one patient walked the day after surgery.”
  • Reasons: the “why” — “because clean hands stop germs from spreading.”
  • Descriptions: details that paint the picture — “the ward was calm and quiet.”

🚫 Relevant vs. Irrelevant Details

  • A relevant detail clearly supports the main idea.
  • An irrelevant detail is true or interesting but doesn’t back up the main point — watch for these on the TEAS.
  • Quick test: ask, “Does this sentence help prove the main idea?” If not, it doesn’t belong.

🌟 The Umbrella Test

  • The main idea is the umbrella; every detail fits under it.
  • If a sentence covers everything else → it’s the main idea.
  • If a sentence gives one specific fact, example, or reason → it’s a detail.
  • Details are specific; main ideas are general.

✏️ Example 1: Which sentence is the main idea?

A daily walk is good for your whole body. It strengthens the heart and lungs. It helps control blood sugar. It can even lift your mood.
  1. Try the umbrella test: which sentence covers all the others?
  2. “It strengthens the heart,” “controls blood sugar,” and “lifts your mood” are each one specific benefit — details.
  3. “A daily walk is good for your whole body” is general enough to cover them all.

Main idea: “A daily walk is good for your whole body.” The rest are supporting details.

🔎 Example 2: What type of detail is it?

Handwashing prevents infection. In one study, it cut hospital infections by 30%. For instance, one unit went a full month with zero cases.
  1. “Cut infections by 30%” gives a number → a fact/statistic.
  2. “For instance, one unit went a month with zero cases” gives a specific instance → an example.
  3. Both are supporting details — just different types.

Takeaway: details come in flavors — facts, statistics, examples, and reasons — but all sit under the main idea.

🚫 Example 3: Spot the detail that doesn’t belong

Our unit cut infections this year. We added hand-sanitizer stations. We trained staff every month. Also, the cafeteria changed its menu.
  1. The main idea is that the unit cut infections.
  2. Sanitizer stations and monthly training help explain the drop — relevant details.
  3. A menu change doesn’t affect infection rates — it’s an irrelevant detail.

Answer: “The cafeteria changed its menu” doesn’t support the main idea, so it doesn’t belong.
