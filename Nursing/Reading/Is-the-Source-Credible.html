🔎 What Makes a Source Credible?

A credible source is one you can reasonably trust. Not everything in print or online is reliable — so before you believe a claim, ask a few questions about where it came from. Look at who wrote it, whether it's backed by evidence, how recent it is, why it was written, and where it was published. One weak cue doesn't always sink a source, but several red flags together should make you cautious.

🔑 The Five Cues — What to Ask

CueAsk yourself…
AuthorWho wrote it? Are they qualified or an expert?
EvidenceAre claims backed by facts, data, or citations you can check?
CurrencyIs it recent enough for the topic? (Health changes fast.)
PurposeWhy was it written — to inform, or to sell or persuade?
Source typeWhere is it published? A journal or .gov, or a random blog?

🚦 Green Flags vs. Red Flags

Green flags (more credible)Red flags (be cautious)
Named, qualified authorNo author, or an unqualified one
Cites checkable evidenceBold claims with no support
Recent dateNo date, or clearly outdated
Aims to informMainly wants to sell or push a side
Journal, .gov, .edu, major health orgAnonymous blog, forum, ad, or meme

🧠 Don't Be Fooled By…

  • Popularity. Thousands of shares don't make a claim true.
  • A professional look. A slick website can still be an ad.
  • Confident wording. "Doctors hate this trick!" is hype, not evidence.
  • The topic sounding right. Being about health doesn't make a source reliable.
  • One good cue. An expert author can't rescue a biased, outdated source.

🌟 How to Judge a Source

  1. Author: who wrote it, and are they qualified?
  2. Evidence: can you check the facts behind the claims?
  3. Currency: is it recent enough for this topic?
  4. Purpose: is it trying to inform you or sell you?
  5. Source type: is the publisher reputable?
  6. Weigh the cues together — then decide how much to trust it.

👍 Example 1: A Credible Source

Source“Early Mobility After Surgery,” a 2023 article in a peer-reviewed nursing journal, written by a registered nurse with a PhD, citing 14 studies.
  1. Author: an RN with a PhD — a qualified expert. 👍
  2. Evidence: it cites 14 studies you can check. 👍
  3. Currency: published in 2023 — recent. 👍
  4. Purpose & source type: a peer-reviewed journal exists to inform, not sell. 👍

Verdict: green flags across the board — this is a credible source.

👎 Example 2: A Questionable Source

SourceA blog post titled “This Miracle Tea Cures Everything!” — no author, no date, no citations, and a large “Buy Now” button linking to the seller's store.
  1. Author: none listed — you can't judge expertise. 👎
  2. Evidence: a bold claim with nothing to back it. 👎
  3. Currency: no date at all. 👎
  4. Purpose: the “Buy Now” button shows it's built to sell — a strong reason for bias. 👎

Verdict: multiple red flags — not a credible source.

⚖️ Example 3: One Good Cue Isn't Enough

SourceA 1998 pamphlet from a soda company, written by a food scientist it employs, arguing that sugary drinks are a healthy source of energy.
  1. Author: a food scientist does have real training — that cue looks like a 👍.
  2. Currency: but it's from 1998, and nutrition guidance has changed a lot. 👎
  3. Purpose: it's published by the soda company to defend its own product — a clear conflict of interest. 👎

Verdict: one green flag can't rescue an outdated source with an obvious bias. Weigh the cues together — this one isn't credible.
