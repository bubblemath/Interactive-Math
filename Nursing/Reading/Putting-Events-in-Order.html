📖 Sequence of Events

Sequence is the order in which things happen. To follow a story or a process, you need to know what came first, what came next, and what came last. Writers show order with dates and times — and with signal words like first, next, then, before, after, and finally. Click along the timeline below to meet them.

Endfinally, at last, in the end

↔️ Before & After

  • Before tells you an event happened earlier than another one.
  • After tells you an event happened later, following another.
  • Careful: “She locked the door after turning off the lights” means the lights went off first.

📅 Order from Dates, Times & Ages

  • Dates & years: the earlier date comes first (2018 before 2021).
  • Clock times: 8:00 a.m. comes before 12:00 p.m.
  • Ages & stages: a baby crawls (7 months) before walking (12 months).
  • Watch for flashbacks — “a week earlier…” describes something that happened before the current moment.

🌟 How to Find the Order

  • Underline the signal words — first, next, then, before, after, finally.
  • Note any dates, times, or ages — they pin events in time.
  • Ask “what had to happen first?” — some events must come before others.
  • Don’t assume the text is in order — a story may jump back in time.

🔀 Put the Events in Order

Drag the events into the order they happened — or use the ▲ ▼ buttons to move each one. Then press Check order. Use time clues and signal words to figure out what came first.

    ✏️ Example 1: Order with signal words

    First, the nurse washed her hands. Next, she put on gloves. Then she checked the IV. Finally, she recorded her notes.
    1. Underline the signals: First → Next → Then → Finally.
    2. They give the order directly: wash hands, gloves, check IV, record notes.
    3. “Finally” marks the last step — recording notes.

    Order: washed hands → put on gloves → checked IV → recorded notes.

    📅 Example 2: Order from a time clue

    She locked the door after turning off all the lights.
    1. The signal word is after.
    2. “Locked the door after turning off the lights” means the lights went off first.
    3. So the order is: turn off lights → lock the door.

    Watch out: the first event mentioned isn’t always the first event that happened.

    ⏪ Example 3: A flashback

    The wound was clean and nearly healed, though a week earlier it had been red and swollen.
    1. Right now: the wound is clean and nearly healed.
    2. The phrase “a week earlier” points back in time.
    3. So red and swollen came first; healing came later.

    Order: red and swollen (earlier) → clean and healed (now).
