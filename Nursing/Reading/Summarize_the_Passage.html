📖 TEAS Reading: Summarize the Passage

Boiling a passage down to its main points — briefly and accurately

📖 What Is a Summary?

A summary is a short restatement of a passage’s most important points, written in your own words. A good summary keeps the main idea and the key supporting points — and leaves out the minor details, examples, and any opinions. Click the diagram below to see what stays in and what gets cut.

📋 The Core Ideas

✅ What Makes a Good Summary

  • Complete: it includes the main idea and the key supporting points.
  • Brief: it’s much shorter than the original — no minor details or examples.
  • In your own words: it restates the ideas, rather than copying sentences.
  • Objective & accurate: it stays true to the passage and adds no opinions or outside information.

🔀 Summary vs. Main Idea vs. Paraphrase

TermWhat it is
Main ideaThe single central point of the passage
SummaryThe main idea plus the key points, briefly, in your own words
ParaphraseRestating one part in your own words, at about the same length

🧭 How to Summarize

  • 1. Find the main idea — the author’s central point.
  • 2. Pick the key supporting points — the few that really matter.
  • 3. Drop the extras — minor details, examples, and repetition.
  • 4. Put it in your own words, and keep it short.

🌟 Watch for These Bad Summaries

  • Too long: it repeats almost the whole passage instead of condensing it.
  • Incomplete: it grabs one small point and misses the main idea.
  • Adds information: it slips in an opinion or a fact that isn’t in the passage.
  • Distorts: it changes or overstates what the passage actually said.

🔎 Pick the Best Summary

Read the passage, then choose the best summary. Pick a weaker one and you’ll see exactly what’s wrong — too long, incomplete, or adding/distorting information.

✏️ Example 1: Build a summary step by step

Regular exercise does a lot for the body. It strengthens the heart, helps control weight, and lifts mood. It can even improve sleep. Best of all, even short daily activity adds up over time.
  1. Main idea: regular exercise has many benefits.
  2. Key points: heart, weight, mood, sleep — and small amounts add up.
  3. Drop & condense: group the four benefits as “several ways,” keep the “adds up” point.
  4. In your own words: shorten it.

Summary: “Regular exercise benefits the body and mind in several ways, and even a little each day adds up.”

📏 Example 2: Too long vs. just right

Telehealth lets patients meet providers by video. It saves travel time and helps rural patients reach specialists. Some exams still need to be done in person, but for check-ins it’s far more convenient.
  1. Too long: “Telehealth lets patients meet providers by video, saving travel and helping rural patients reach specialists, and though some exams need to be in person, check-ins are more convenient.” → that just repeats the passage.
  2. Just right: keep the point and the key limit, cut the rest.

Summary: “Telehealth makes many visits more convenient, especially for rural patients, though some care still needs to be in person.”

🚫 Example 3: Don’t add or distort

A hospital cut its infection rate through a focus on hand hygiene: monthly staff training, sanitizer stations in every hallway, and tracking. Infections dropped by nearly a third.
  1. ❌ “Every hospital should require monthly training.” → adds a recommendation the passage never makes.
  2. ❌ “Hand hygiene eliminates all infections.” → distorts it (a third isn’t all).
  3. ✅ Stick to what the passage says.

Summary: “By focusing on hand hygiene, a hospital cut its infection rate by nearly a third.”
