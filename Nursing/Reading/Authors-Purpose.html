📖 Author’s Purpose

Every text is written for a reason. The author’s purpose is why the author wrote it. Most purposes fit into three slices — easy to remember as PIE: Persuade, Inform, Entertain. Click a slice below to learn what each one looks like.

📋 The Core Ideas

🔎 How to Spot the Purpose

PurposeClues to look for
PersuadeOpinions; words like should, best, must; a call to action
InformFacts, steps, and definitions; no opinions
EntertainCharacters, a plot, humor, vivid description, feelings

👁️ Point of View

Point of viewPronounsFeel
First personI, me, we, usThe narrator is in the story
Second personyou, yourSpeaks directly to the reader
Third personhe, she, they, itAn outside voice tells it

🎯 Purpose & Point of View Together

  • Instructions often use second person (“you”) to inform: “You should check the dose.”
  • Personal stories often use first person (“I”) to entertain.
  • News reports often use third person to inform.
  • Ask both: Why was this written, and whose voice is telling it?

🌟 The Quick Test

  • Full of facts and steps? → Inform.
  • Trying to change your mind or get you to act? → Persuade.
  • Telling a story or making you laugh or feel? → Entertain.
  • For point of view, just follow the pronouns.

📘 Example 1: Informing

The kidneys filter waste from the blood and help balance the body’s fluids. Each kidney holds about a million tiny filters called nephrons.
  1. Ask: facts, opinions, or story? These are facts.
  2. No opinions, no “should,” no characters or plot.
  3. The author is teaching how the kidneys work.

Purpose: to inform. Fact-based explanation is the giveaway.

📣 Example 2: Persuading

You really should get a flu shot this year. It’s quick and free at most clinics, and it protects both you and your patients. Don’t wait — schedule yours today.
  1. Spot the clues: “should,” “don’t wait,” and a call to action (“schedule yours today”).
  2. The author wants you to do something.

Purpose: to persuade. It’s pushing you toward an action.

🎭 Example 3: Entertaining (and point of view)

Rain drummed on the clinic windows as I hummed an old tune, twirling my pen and daydreaming about the beach I’d visit once my shift finally ended.
  1. There’s a scene, mood, and description — no facts or argument.
  2. Purpose: to entertain.
  3. The pronouns “I” and “my” tell you the point of view is first person.

Two questions, two answers: purpose = entertain; point of view = first person.
