📖 Text Structure

Text structure is the way an author organizes information. Recognizing the structure helps you follow the ideas and find what you need. There are five common structures — each with its own signal words. Click each one below to learn how to spot it.

📋 The Core Ideas

🔑 Structures & Their Signal Words

StructureWhat it doesSignal words
SequencePuts events or steps in orderfirst, next, then, finally
Cause & EffectShows why something happens and its resultsbecause, so, as a result
Compare & ContrastShows how things are alike and differentboth, unlike, however
Problem & SolutionNames a problem and how to fix itproblem, solution, solve
DescriptionGives features or details of a topicfor example, such as, features

📊 Structures & Graphic Organizers

  • Sequence → a timeline or flowchart of steps.
  • Cause & Effect → arrows from causes to effects.
  • Compare & Contrast → a Venn diagram (two overlapping circles).
  • Problem & Solution → a “problem” box leading to a “solution” box.
  • Description → a web with the topic in the center and details around it.

🌟 How to Find the Structure

  • Hunt for signal words — they’re the fastest clue.
  • Ask what the author is doing: ordering? explaining why? comparing? solving? describing?
  • Picture the organizer that would fit — timeline, Venn, arrows.
  • Some texts mix structures — pick the main one.

➜ Example 1: Cause & Effect

Because the patient hadn’t eaten all day, her blood sugar dropped. As a result, she felt dizzy and had to sit down.
  1. Spot the signals: “because” and “as a result.”
  2. They connect a cause (no food) to its effects (low blood sugar, dizziness).

Structure: cause & effect — it explains why something happened and what followed.

◐ Example 2: Compare & Contrast

Both tablets and capsules deliver medicine, but they differ in form. Unlike a tablet, a capsule has a gelatin shell that dissolves quickly.
  1. Signals: “both,” “but,” “unlike.”
  2. The passage shows a similarity (both deliver medicine) and a difference (the shell).

Structure: compare & contrast — picture a Venn diagram.

⚠ Example 3: Problem & Solution

Long ER wait times frustrated patients. To solve this, the hospital added a fast-track lane for minor injuries, which cut waits in half.
  1. First a problem is named: long wait times.
  2. Then the signal “to solve this” introduces the solution: a fast-track lane.

Structure: problem & solution. Careful — it also has a cause/effect flavor, but the main move is problem → fix.
