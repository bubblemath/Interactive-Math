📖 TEAS Reading: Making Inferences

Reading between the lines — drawing conclusions the text supports

📖 What Is an Inference?

An inference is a logical conclusion you reach by combining clues in the text with what you already know. The author doesn’t say it directly — you “read between the lines.” The trick is to draw only conclusions the evidence actually supports, without jumping too far. 📋 The Core Ideas

🔀 Stated Fact vs. Inference vs. Guess

TypeWhat it is
Stated factWritten directly in the text — no thinking required
InferenceFigured out from clues in the text plus what you know
Wild guessNot backed by the text — goes too far

🕵️ How to Make a Valid Inference

  • 1. Find the clues — the actions, words, and details the author gives you.
  • 2. Add what you know — your everyday knowledge of how things work.
  • 3. Draw a conclusion the clues actually support.
  • 4. Check it — could you point to evidence in the text? If not, it goes too far.

🚫 Inferences That Go Too Far

  • Not supported: there’s no evidence for it in the text.
  • Contradicts: it goes against what the passage says.
  • Overgeneralization: it turns one moment into “always” or “everyone.”
  • A good inference is cautious — it stays close to the evidence.

🌟 The Evidence Test

  • Ask: “What in the text makes me think so?” A valid inference always has an answer.
  • Clues + what you know = inference.
  • If you can’t point to a clue, you’re guessing, not inferring.
  • Stay close to the evidence — don’t leap to the most dramatic option.

✏️ Example 1: Clues → conclusion

The waiting room was packed — every seat full, a few people leaning against the walls. One woman kept glancing at the clock and sighing.
  1. Clues: every seat full, people against the walls; a woman glancing at the clock and sighing.
  2. What you know: a full room means busy; clock-watching and sighing usually mean impatience.
  3. Conclusion: the clinic is busy and the woman is getting impatient.

Careful: you can’t conclude she’s a nurse or that the clinic is “always” crowded — there’s no evidence for that.

🩺 Example 2: Infer the situation

On her first shift, Maya double-checked every dose, asked a senior nurse to confirm her steps, and re-read the chart twice before acting.
  1. Clues: “first shift,” double-checking, asking for confirmation, re-reading.
  2. What you know: new people are extra careful to avoid mistakes.
  3. Conclusion: Maya is being careful because she’s new.

Too far: “Maya is an experienced nurse” contradicts “first shift”; “Maya dislikes her job” has no support.

⚖️ Example 3: Don’t jump too far

Ben looked at his exam results, quietly slid the paper into his bag without a word, and stared out the window.
  1. Supported: Ben seems disappointed — the silence and staring are clues.
  2. Not supported: “Ben failed” — we’re never told his actual score.
  3. A good inference reads the mood without inventing facts.

Best conclusion: “Ben is probably disappointed.” Notice we stay close to the clues.
