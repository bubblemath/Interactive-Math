Telling what can be proven from what someone believes

📖 Fact vs. Opinion

A fact can be proven true or false — you can check it with evidence. An opinion is a belief, feeling, or judgment that can’t be proven; a reasonable person could disagree. The quickest test is one question: can this be proven? Click through the flowchart below.

🚩 Opinion Signal Words

  • Feeling & belief: I think, I believe, I feel, in my view.
  • Judgment: best, worst, beautiful, terrible, amazing, boring.
  • Should words: should, ought to, must (as advice).
  • Spot one of these and you’re probably looking at an opinion.

💡 A Tricky Point

  • A fact doesn’t have to be true — it just has to be checkable.
  • “The body has 300 bones” is a fact-type statement (you can check it) — it just happens to be false.
  • An opinion, by contrast, can’t be proven right or wrong at all.

🌟 The One-Question Test

  • Ask: “Could I prove this with evidence?”
  • Yes → it’s a fact (measurements, dates, records, observation).
  • No → it’s an opinion (a feeling, belief, or judgment).
  • Don’t ask “Do I agree?” — agreement has nothing to do with it.

📏 Example 1: Testing a statement

“Water boils at 100°C at sea level.”
  1. Ask the one question: can this be proven?
  2. Yes — you can measure it with a thermometer.
  3. It has a specific number you could check.

Answer: fact — it can be verified with evidence.

💭 Example 2: Spotting an opinion

“Nursing is the most rewarding career of all.”
  1. Look for signal words: “most rewarding” is a judgment.
  2. Could someone reasonably disagree? Yes.
  3. There’s no way to prove it with evidence.

Answer: opinion — it’s a personal judgment.

🔀 Example 3: Both in one sentence

“The new wing has 20 beds, and honestly, it’s the nicest unit in the whole hospital.”
  1. “The new wing has 20 beds” → can be counted → fact.
  2. “it’s the nicest unit” → a judgment (“nicest,” “honestly”) → opinion.

Takeaway: one sentence can hold both — separate the checkable part from the judgment.
