🔀 What Is a Flowchart?

A flowchart (or decision tree) is a picture of a process. You start at the top, follow the arrows, and do each step in order. When you reach a decision — a diamond with a yes/no question — you answer it and follow the matching arrow. Nurses read flowcharts all the time: triage steps, medication protocols, and "what to do if…" algorithms. Reading one correctly means following exactly one path from start to end.

🔑 What Each Shape Means

ShapeLooks likeWhat it means
TerminalRounded ovalThe start or end of the process
ProcessRectangleAn action — one step to do
DecisionDiamondA yes/no question that splits the path
ArrowLine with a pointThe direction to move next
Branch label"Yes" / "No" on an arrowWhich arrow to follow, based on your answer

➡️ How the Flow Works

  • Start at the terminal (oval) at the top and read downward.
  • Follow the arrows — they show the one direction you may move.
  • At a diamond, answer the question. Then take only the arrow whose label matches your answer (Yes or No).
  • You never do both branches. A decision sends you down one path, not both.
  • Keep going until you reach an end — the final oval or last action box.
  • Some flowcharts loop back: an arrow can send you to repeat an earlier step until a condition changes.

🌟 How to Trace a Flowchart

  1. Find "Start." Put your finger on the first oval.
  2. Read each box and move along the arrow out of it.
  3. At a diamond, ask the question about your patient — then pick the matching Yes or No arrow.
  4. Don't skip ahead. Only follow arrows that actually connect to where you are.
  5. Stop when you hit an end box. That's your answer.

➡️ Example 1: Follow a Straight Path

  1. Start at the oval: "Wash hands."
  2. Follow the arrow down to "Put on gloves," then to "Check ID band."
  3. The last arrow reaches the end oval, "Begin care."

Takeaway: with no diamonds, you just follow the arrows in order — so the third step here is "Check ID band."

💠 Example 2: Answer a Decision

Your patient's temperature is 101.8 °F.

  1. Read the diamond: "Temp above 100.4 °F?"
  2. 101.8 is above 100.4, so the answer is Yes.
  3. Follow the Yes arrow — not the No arrow — to "Notify the provider."

Takeaway: the diamond's question decides which single arrow you take. Match your answer to the label.

🔁 Example 3: Read a Loop

  1. After giving carbs and rechecking, you reach "Still below 70?"
  2. If Yes, the arrow loops back to give another dose — you repeat the step.
  3. You only leave the loop when the answer becomes No, which sends you to "Give a snack."

Takeaway: a loop repeats a step until a condition changes. Watch the arrow that points backward.
