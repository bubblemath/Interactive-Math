Independent, dependent, and controlled variables — and the control group

🎚️ The Parts of an Experiment

Every good experiment changes one thing, measures what happens, and keeps everything else the same — all while comparing against a group that gets no treatment. Those parts have names: the independent, dependent, and controlled variables, plus the control group. Click the diagram below to meet each one.

⚖️ Control Group vs. Experimental Group

  • The experimental group gets the treatment (the real change being tested).
  • The control group gets no treatment — it’s the baseline you compare against, so you can tell whether the treatment actually did anything.
  • A placebo (like a sugar pill) lets the control group be treated identically except for the one real difference.

🕵️ How to Spot Them in a Scenario

Read the experiment and ask four quick questions:

  • What did they change on purpose? → the independent variable.
  • What did they measure to get a result? → the dependent variable.
  • What did they keep the same for everyone? → the controlled variables (constants).
  • Which group got nothing (or a placebo)? → the control group.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Independent = what you change (cause); dependent = what you measure (effect).
  • The dependent variable “depends on” the independent variable.
  • Controlled variables (constants) are kept the same to keep the test fair.
  • Control group = no treatment; experimental group = gets the treatment.
  • Change only one variable at a time, or you can’t tell what caused the result.

🕵️ Variable Detective

Read each experiment, then pick out its parts one at a time. Get one right and it locks into the worksheet; pick a wrong one and you’ll learn what that piece actually is.

✏️ Example 1: A drug trial

📝 Researchers test whether a new medicine lowers cholesterol. One group takes the medicine; another takes a placebo. Both groups eat the same diet. After 8 weeks, cholesterol is measured.

  1. Changed on purpose → the medicine vs. placebo. That’s the independent variable.
  2. Measured → cholesterol level. That’s the dependent variable.
  3. Kept the same → the diet. That’s a controlled variable (constant).
  4. Got no real treatment → the placebo group. That’s the control group.

One-line summary: changing the medicine (IV) to see its effect on cholesterol (DV), with diet held constant and the placebo group as the control.

🌡️ Example 2: Temperature and dissolving

📝 A student tests how water temperature affects how fast sugar dissolves. She uses cold, warm, and hot water — same amount of water and sugar each time — and times how long the sugar takes to disappear.

  1. Independent variable: the water temperature (the thing she changes).
  2. Dependent variable: the time it takes the sugar to dissolve (what she measures).
  3. Controlled variables: the amount of water and amount of sugar (kept the same).

Watch out: it’s easy to flip the IV and DV. The temperature is set by her (independent); the dissolving time is the response (dependent).

⚖️ Example 3: Why the control group matters

📝 A clinic gives every patient a new vitamin and notices most feel more energetic. Can they conclude the vitamin worked?

  1. There’s no control group — no group that skipped the vitamin to compare against.
  2. The patients might feel better for other reasons (the season, expecting to feel better, etc.).
  3. Without a comparison group, you can’t tell whether the vitamin caused the change.

Answer: no — a fair test needs a control group that doesn’t get the treatment, so the two can be compared.
