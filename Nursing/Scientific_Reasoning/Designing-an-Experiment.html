📋 The Core Ideas

💡 The Hypothesis

  • A hypothesis is a testable prediction — an educated guess based on observation and research, not a random guess.
  • It’s often written in “If… then…” form: If [I change this], then [this will happen].
  • It must be falsifiable — it has to be possible to prove it wrong with an experiment. “Plants are pretty” can’t be tested; “If plants get more light, then they grow taller” can.

⚖️ Control Group & a Fair Test

  • The control group does not get the treatment — it’s the baseline you compare against. The experimental group does get it.
  • A fair test changes only one variable at a time and keeps everything else constant. Otherwise you can’t tell what caused the result.
  • A placebo (like a sugar pill) lets the control group be treated identically except for the real drug.

📊 Data & Conclusions

  • Quantitative data are numbers and measurements (temperature, height); qualitative data are descriptions (color, texture).
  • A conclusion states whether the data support or reject the hypothesis — and honest scientists report results either way.
  • Bigger, repeated samples make results more reliable.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Order: observe → research → hypothesis → experiment → analyze → conclude (then repeat).
  • Hypothesis = testable, falsifiable prediction (often “If… then…”).
  • Independent = what you change; dependent = what you measure; controlled = what you keep the same.
  • Control group = the no-treatment baseline for comparison.
  • Fair test: change only one variable at a time.

✏️ Example 1: Find the variables

📝 A nurse tests whether a new hand sanitizer lowers infections. Half the hospital units switch to the new sanitizer; half keep the old one. After three months she compares infection rates.

  1. Independent variable (what’s changed): the type of sanitizer (new vs. old).
  2. Dependent variable (what’s measured): the infection rate.
  3. Control group: the units that kept the old sanitizer — the baseline for comparison.
  4. Constants: same time period, same hand-washing rules, similar units — kept the same so only the sanitizer differs.

Hypothesis: “If units switch to the new sanitizer, then their infection rate will go down.”

⚖️ Example 2: Spot the control group

📝 To test if a fertilizer speeds plant growth, Group A gets fertilizer and Group B gets none. Both get the same water, soil, and sunlight.

  1. The independent variable is the fertilizer (given or not).
  2. The dependent variable is the plant growth you measure.
  3. Group B (no fertilizer) is the control group — the comparison.
  4. Water, soil, and sunlight are controlled variables, kept identical so the test is fair.

Why it matters: without Group B, you’d have nothing to compare against — you couldn’t prove the fertilizer made the difference.

💡 Example 3: Which is a good hypothesis?

📝 Which statement is a proper scientific hypothesis? (a) “Music is nice.” (b) “Why do people sleep?” (c) “If students listen to music while studying, then their test scores will change.”

  1. (a) is an opinion — it can’t be tested or proven wrong.
  2. (b) is a question, not a prediction.
  3. (c) is testable and in “If… then…” form — you can design an experiment and measure the result.

Answer: (c). A good hypothesis predicts a measurable outcome and could be proven wrong.
