What Is a Linear Inequality?

An inequality looks just like an equation, but instead of an equals sign it uses an inequality symbol. The big difference: an equation has a single answer, but an inequality has a whole range of answers. For example, x > 3 means every number greater than 3 works — 3.1, 4, 100, and so on.

SymbolMeansExample
<less thanx < 5 (x is below 5)
>greater thanx > 5 (x is above 5)
≤less than or equal tox ≤ 5 (5 is included)
≥greater than or equal tox ≥ 5 (5 is included)
✓

Solving: Almost Like an Equation

You solve an inequality the same way you solve an equation — use inverse operations to get the variable by itself. Add, subtract, multiply, and divide on both sides to isolate x.

📋 Steps to Solve

  1. Simplify each side if needed (combine like terms).
  2. Undo addition or subtraction by doing the opposite to both sides.
  3. Undo multiplication or division to get x alone.
  4. Watch the sign: if you multiply or divide by a negative number, flip the inequality (next section!).
⇄

The One Rule That's Different: Flip It!

⚠️ The Flip Rule

When you multiply or divide both sides by a NEGATIVE number, you must flip the inequality sign. The < becomes >, and ≤ becomes ≥ (and vice-versa).

−2x < 6 → divide by −2 → x > −3

✅ When you DO flip

Only when you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number (like −2 or −5).

🚫 When you DON'T flip

Adding or subtracting any number never flips the sign. Multiplying or dividing by a positive number never flips it either.

💡

Quick check

Not sure if you flipped correctly? Pick a number from your answer range and plug it into the original inequality. If it makes a true statement, you're right.

📈

Graphing on a Number Line

⚪ Open circle

Use an open (hollow) circle for < and >. The endpoint is not included.

⚫ Closed circle

Use a closed (filled) circle for ≤ and ≥. The endpoint is included.

➡️ Shade the direction

Shade toward the numbers that work: to the right for "greater than," to the left for "less than."

Example: x > −3 — open circle at −3, shaded to the right.

Example 1: One step, no flip

One-Step

Solve and graph:

x + 4 < 9

📝 Step-by-Step Solution

1
Undo +4 Subtract 4 from both sides. (Adding/subtracting never flips the sign.)
x + 4 − 4 < 9 − 4
2
Simplify
x < 5
✅ Answer: x < 5

Example 2: Divide by a positive — no flip

One-Step

Solve and graph:

3x ≥ 12

📝 Step-by-Step Solution

1
Undo ×3 Divide both sides by 3. Since 3 is positive, the sign stays the same.
3x ÷ 3 ≥ 12 ÷ 3
2
Simplify
x ≥ 4
✅ Answer: x ≥ 4

Example 3: Divide by a negative — FLIP!

Flip Rule

Solve and graph:

−2x < 6

📝 Step-by-Step Solution

1
Divide by −2 → FLIP Divide both sides by −2. Because −2 is negative, flip the sign: < becomes >.
−2x ÷ (−2) > 6 ÷ (−2)
2
Simplify
x > −3
✅ Answer: x > −3

Example 4: Two steps, then a flip

Flip Rule

Solve:

5 − 2x ≤ 11

📝 Step-by-Step Solution

1
Undo +5 Subtract 5 from both sides (no flip — it's subtraction).
−2x ≤ 6
2
Divide by −2 → FLIP Divide both sides by −2. It's negative, so flip: ≤ becomes ≥.
x ≥ −3
✅ Answer: x ≥ −3

Example 5: Variables on both sides

Both Sides

Solve:

4x + 3 > x + 12

📝 Step-by-Step Solution

1
Gather x Subtract x from both sides.
3x + 3 > 12
2
Undo +3 Subtract 3 from both sides.
3x > 9
3
Divide by 3 3 is positive — no flip.
x > 3
✅ Answer: x > 3

Example 6: Subtracting a variable term

Flip Rule

Solve:

8 − 3x > 20

📝 Step-by-Step Solution

1
Undo +8 Subtract 8 from both sides.
−3x > 12
2
Divide by −3 → FLIP Negative divisor, so flip: > becomes <.
x < −4
✅ Answer: x < −4
