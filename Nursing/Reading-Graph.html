📖 How to Read and Understand Graphs

Graphs are visual representations of data that help us quickly understand patterns, trends, and relationships. On the TEAS exam, you'll encounter various graph types that test your ability to extract and interpret information accurately. These visuals appear in clinical logs, patient summaries, medication records, dosage logs, lab summaries, and trend analyses.

🎯 Your 3-Step Data Interpretation Playbook

  1. Find Context: Read the title, scan headers/axes, and note units (e.g., mg, %, feet)
  2. Target Data: For tables, find the cell using row and column. For charts/graphs, find the specific bar, slice, or point
  3. Read the Value: Read the number from the cell, axis, or label. Double-check the scale and units before answering

📊 Graph Types You'll Encounter

📊 1. Bar Graphs

Purpose: Compare quantities across different categories

  • Identify which category has the highest or lowest value by comparing bar heights
  • Compare values between categories by reading the scale on the y-axis
  • Look for patterns: Are values increasing? Decreasing? Clustered?
  • Example: "Category B is the highest with 8. Category E is the lowest with 2"

📈 2. Line Graphs

Purpose: Show changes over time or continuous data

  • Identify trends: Is the line going up (increasing), down (decreasing), or flat (stable)?
  • Find specific values at given points by tracing from the x-axis up to the line, then across to the y-axis
  • Compare multiple data series when there are multiple lines
  • Look for the steepest sections (rapid change) vs. flat sections (little change)

🥧 3. Pie Charts (Circle Graphs)

Purpose: Show parts of a whole (percentages)

  • All sections must add up to 100% (the whole)
  • Larger sections represent larger proportions
  • Calculate actual values: multiply the percentage by the total
  • Compare sections visually or by their percentage labels

📋 4. Data Tables

Purpose: Organize precise numerical data in rows and columns

  • Read column headers and row labels carefully
  • Find the intersection of the correct row and column for specific values
  • Calculate totals by adding values in a row or column
  • Calculate averages by dividing the sum by the number of values

⚬ 5. Scatter Plots

Purpose: Show relationships between two variables

  • Positive correlation: Points trend upward (as x increases, y increases)
  • Negative correlation: Points trend downward (as x increases, y decreases)
  • No correlation: Points are scattered randomly with no clear pattern
  • Use the trend to predict values not shown on the graph

🌟 TEAS Exam Success Tips

  1. Read the title first – It tells you what the graph is about
  2. Check the axes labels – Know what's being measured and the units
  3. Note the scale – Does it start at zero? What are the intervals?
  4. Look for keywords in questions – "highest," "lowest," "trend," "difference," "total"
  5. Eliminate wrong answers – Cross out options that don't match the data
  6. Double-check your reading – Make sure you're looking at the right bar, point, or section
  7. Watch for tricky scales – Some graphs don't start at zero, which can be misleading
  8. Practice estimation – You often don't need exact values, just close approximations

✏️ Worked Examples

📊 Example 1: Bar Graph Interpretation

Question: Based on the bar graph showing monthly sales, which month had the highest sales, and what was the difference between the highest and lowest months?

📝 Step-by-Step Solution:

  • Step 1: Read the title – "Monthly Sales (in thousands)"
  • Step 2: Identify the highest bar – April at $9,000
  • Step 3: Identify the lowest bar – February at $3,000
  • Step 4: Calculate the difference – $9,000 - $3,000 = $6,000

Answer: April had the highest sales ($9,000), and the difference between highest and lowest is $6,000.

📈 Example 2: Line Graph Analysis

Question: Analyze the temperature trend over the week. During which period did the temperature increase most rapidly?

📝 Step-by-Step Solution:

  • Step 1: Identify the overall trend – Temperature generally increases then decreases
  • Step 2: Look for the steepest upward slope – Between Tuesday and Wednesday
  • Step 3: Calculate the change – From 68°F to 75°F = 7°F increase in one day
  • Step 4: Compare to other periods – This is the largest single-day increase

Answer: The temperature increased most rapidly between Tuesday and Wednesday, rising 7°F in one day.

🥧 Example 3: Pie Chart Interpretation

Question: If a hospital has 200 patients total, how many patients are in the Emergency department? Which department has the fewest patients?

📝 Step-by-Step Solution:

  • Step 1: Find Emergency percentage – 25%
  • Step 2: Calculate: 25% × 200 = 0.25 × 200 = 50 patients
  • Step 3: Identify smallest section – Pediatrics at 10%
  • Step 4: Verify: 35% + 25% + 20% + 10% + 10% = 100% ✓

Answer: Emergency has 50 patients. Pediatrics has the fewest at 10% (20 patients).

📋 Example 4: Table Data Interpretation

Student Quiz 1 Quiz 2 Quiz 3 Quiz 4
Alex 85 90 88 92
Jordan 78 82 80 85
Taylor 92 88 95 90

Question: What is Taylor's average quiz score? Who showed the most improvement from Quiz 1 to Quiz 4?

📝 Step-by-Step Solution:

  • Step 1: Find Taylor's scores – 92, 88, 95, 90
  • Step 2: Calculate average – (92 + 88 + 95 + 90) ÷ 4 = 365 ÷ 4 = 91.25
  • Step 3: Calculate each student's improvement (Quiz 4 - Quiz 1):
  • Alex: 92 - 85 = 7 points
  • Jordan: 85 - 78 = 7 points
  • Taylor: 90 - 92 = -2 points (decreased)

Answer: Taylor's average is 91.25. Alex and Jordan both improved by 7 points (tied for most improvement).

⚬ Example 5: Scatter Plot Analysis

Question: What type of correlation exists between study hours and test scores? If a student studies for 7 hours, what score might they expect?

📝 Step-by-Step Solution:

  • Step 1: Observe the pattern – Points trend upward from left to right
  • Step 2: Identify correlation type – Positive correlation (as study hours increase, scores increase)
  • Step 3: To predict for 7 hours, follow the trend line
  • Step 4: At 6 hours ≈ 85, at 8 hours ≈ 92, so 7 hours ≈ 88-89

Answer: There is a positive correlation. A student studying 7 hours might expect a score around 88-89.
