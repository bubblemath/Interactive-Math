🍎 TEAS Science: The Digestive Tract

How food travels, gets broken down, and is absorbed

🍽️ From Bite to... Bathroom

The digestive system turns the food you eat into nutrients your cells can use, and gets rid of what's left. Food travels through one long tube — the digestive (GI) tract — while several accessory organs (like the liver and pancreas) pitch in without ever touching the food directly. 🛣️ The Tract: the Path Food Takes

  1. Mouth — chewing (mechanical) plus salivary amylase starts breaking down carbohydrates (chemical).
  2. Esophagus — pushes food to the stomach using wave-like contractions (peristalsis).
  3. Stomach — churns food and adds acid and pepsin to start protein digestion; food becomes a soupy mix called chyme.
  4. Small intestine — the main site of digestion AND absorption; villi absorb nutrients into the blood.
  5. Large intestine (colon) — absorbs water and forms solid waste (feces).
  6. Rectum & anus — store and then eliminate waste.

🤝 Accessory Organs (helpers, not part of the path)

  • Salivary glands — make saliva (with amylase) for the mouth.
  • Liver — makes bile, which breaks fats into small droplets.
  • Gallbladder — stores bile and releases it into the small intestine.
  • Pancreas — sends digestive enzymes into the small intestine.

Key idea: food never travels through these organs. They deliver helpful substances into the tract — that's why they're called "accessory."

⚙️ Two Kinds of Digestion

TypeWhat it meansExamples
MechanicalPhysically breaking food into smaller piecesChewing in the mouth; churning in the stomach
ChemicalEnzymes break large molecules into small onesAmylase (carbs), pepsin (protein), lipase (fat)

Where it starts: carbohydrate digestion begins in the mouth; protein digestion begins in the stomach; most digestion and absorption is completed in the small intestine.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Order of the tract: mouth → esophagus → stomach → small intestine → large intestine → rectum/anus.
  • The small intestine is the main site of nutrient absorption (using villi).
  • The large intestine absorbs water and forms feces.
  • Liver makes bile, gallbladder stores it, pancreas makes enzymes — all accessory organs.
  • Carb digestion starts in the mouth; protein digestion starts in the stomach.
  • Peristalsis is the muscle wave that moves food along.

🥪 Example 1: The Journey of a Sandwich

📝 What happens at each stop

Mouth (chew + amylase)→ Esophagus (peristalsis)→ Stomach (acid + pepsin → chyme)→ Small intestine (absorb nutrients)→ Large intestine (absorb water)→ Rectum / anus (eliminate)

The big idea: the tract is one continuous tube. Food is broken down mechanically and chemically as it moves; nutrients are absorbed in the small intestine, water is reclaimed in the large intestine, and the leftovers are eliminated.

🧪 Example 2: Where Does Each Nutrient Start Breaking Down?

📝 Step-by-Step Reasoning

  • Carbohydrates start in the mouth — saliva contains amylase, which begins splitting starches.
  • Proteins start in the stomach — acid and the enzyme pepsin go to work there.
  • Fats are mostly handled in the small intestine — bile (from the liver) breaks them into droplets and pancreatic lipase digests them.
  • Everything is finished and absorbed in the small intestine.

Memory aid: "Carbs in the mouth, protein in the stomach, finish in the small intestine."

🤝 Example 3: Tract vs. Accessory Organs

📝 Why the liver and pancreas aren't "stops"

  • Step 1: Trace where the food physically goes: mouth → esophagus → stomach → small intestine → large intestine → out. The liver, gallbladder, and pancreas are not on that path.
  • Step 2: Instead, those organs send substances in. The liver makes bile, the gallbladder stores and releases it, and the pancreas adds enzymes — all into the small intestine.
  • Step 3: Because food never passes through them, they're called accessory organs.

Watch out: a common test trap is listing the liver or pancreas as part of the food's path. They help digest, but they aren't stops on the tube.
