🧬 Glands & Hormones

TEAS Anatomy & Physiology — Endocrine System

The endocrine system is a network of glands that release hormones — chemical messengers — directly into your bloodstream. These hormones travel to target cells and regulate processes like metabolism, growth, reproduction, and stress responses.

Quick Memory Tips 💡

  • Insulin = "Into cells" — it lets glucose into cells, lowering blood sugar.
  • Glucagon = "Glucose-a-gon (go!)" — it tells the liver to release glucose.
  • Calcitonin = "Calcium-tone-it-down" — lowers blood calcium.
  • PTH = "Pushes calcium To High" — raises blood calcium.
  • Cortisol = "Court-is-stressful" — the stress hormone.

Let's walk through the thinking process you'll use in the matching game below. For each scenario, we identify the Gland → Hormone → Effect.

Example 1 — "A patient's blood sugar is dangerously high."

Gland: Pancreas (specifically the beta cells of the Islets of Langerhans)
Hormone: Insulin
Effect: Insulin allows glucose to enter cells, lowering blood sugar (hypoglycemic effect).

🩺 Clinical connection: In Type 1 diabetes, the pancreas produces little or no insulin, so patients need exogenous insulin to manage blood glucose.

Example 2 — "A nurse notices a patient is anxious, with rapid heart rate and elevated BP during an emergency."

Gland: Adrenal Medulla
Hormone: Epinephrine (Adrenaline)
Effect: Triggers the fight-or-flight response — increases heart rate, blood pressure, and blood glucose for quick energy.

🩺 Clinical connection: EpiPens deliver synthetic epinephrine to counteract severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis).

Example 3 — "A patient reports fatigue, weight gain, and feeling cold all the time."

Gland: Thyroid
Hormone: T3 / T4 (Thyroxine)
Effect: Thyroid hormones regulate metabolism and body temperature. Low levels (hypothyroidism) cause fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance.

🩺 Clinical connection: Hypothyroidism is commonly treated with synthetic levothyroxine (Synthroid).

Example 4 — "A postpartum mother is having difficulty with milk production."

Gland: Anterior Pituitary
Hormone: Prolactin
Effect: Stimulates and maintains milk production (lactation) after childbirth.

🩺 Clinical connection: Oxytocin (posterior pituitary) works alongside prolactin — it triggers the milk "let-down" reflex.
