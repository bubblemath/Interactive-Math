The endocrine system — the glands, the hormones they release, and what those hormones do

⚗️ The Body's Chemical Messengers

The endocrine system is a network of glands that release hormones — chemical messengers — straight into the bloodstream. The blood carries each hormone to its target, where it changes how the body works: your blood sugar, your metabolism, your growth, your stress response, even your sleep. It works more slowly than the nervous system, but its effects last much longer. Explore the figure below — click any gland to learn it, or switch to Label Challenge to test yourself.

🩸 Two Big Balancing Acts

Blood sugar and blood calcium are each kept steady by a pair of opposite hormones — an antagonistic pair, one to push the level up and one to bring it down.

  • Blood sugar: the pancreas releases insulin to lower it (after a meal) and glucagon to raise it (between meals).
  • Blood calcium: the parathyroid releases PTH to raise it, and the thyroid releases calcitonin to lower it. (Calcitonin "tones down" calcium.)

🔁 How Hormones Are Controlled: Negative Feedback

Most hormones are kept in check by negative feedback — like a thermostat. When a level gets high enough, that very level signals the gland to stop releasing the hormone.

  • Example: the pituitary releases TSH, which tells the thyroid to make thyroxine.
  • When thyroxine rises high enough, it signals the pituitary to slow down on TSH — so thyroxine doesn't climb forever.
  • The result is a steady, balanced level. This stability is called homeostasis.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Endocrine = glands + hormones + bloodstream. Slower than the nervous system, but longer-lasting.
  • The pituitary is the "master gland"; the hypothalamus controls it.
  • Insulin lowers blood sugar, glucagon raises it — both from the pancreas.
  • Adrenaline (from the adrenal glands) drives the fight-or-flight response.
  • Thyroxine (thyroid) sets your metabolic rate.
  • PTH raises calcium; calcitonin lowers it. Most hormones use negative feedback.

🩸 Example 1: How the Body Controls Blood Sugar

📝 Insulin and glucagon — an antagonistic pair

  • Step 1: Both hormones come from the same gland — the pancreas.
  • Step 2: After you eat, blood sugar rises. The pancreas releases insulin, which moves sugar out of the blood and into cells — lowering blood sugar.
  • Step 3: Hours later, between meals, blood sugar falls. The pancreas releases glucagon, which tells the liver to release stored sugar — raising blood sugar.
  • Step 4: The two hormones do opposite jobs, so together they hold blood sugar steady (homeostasis).

Answer: insulin lowers blood sugar and glucagon raises it. If you mix them up, remember: insulin lets sugar into cells.

🔗 Example 2: Tracing a Hormone From Gland to Effect

📝 What happens when blood calcium drops too low?

  • Trigger: blood calcium falls below normal.
  • Gland: the parathyroid glands sense the low calcium.
  • Hormone: they release parathyroid hormone (PTH) into the blood.
  • Effect: PTH pulls calcium from bone and helps the body hold onto calcium, so blood calcium rises back to normal.

Answer: the full chain is parathyroid → PTH → raises blood calcium. The thyroid's calcitonin does the reverse when calcium is too high. Every hormone question can be read as this same chain: which gland, which hormone, what effect?

🔁 Example 3: The "Master Gland" and Negative Feedback

📝 Why is the pituitary called the master gland?

  • Step 1: The hypothalamus (in the brain) signals the pituitary.
  • Step 2: The pituitary releases TSH, which travels to the thyroid and tells it to make thyroxine.
  • Step 3: Thyroxine raises metabolism. As thyroxine climbs, it signals the pituitary to ease off TSH — this is negative feedback.
  • Step 4: Because the pituitary's hormones control several other glands like this, it earns the name "master gland."

Answer: the pituitary is the master gland because it directs other glands — but it is itself controlled by the hypothalamus and by negative feedback. Nothing in the system runs unchecked.
