The skeletal system — bones, their names, and what they do

🦴 The Framework of the Body

The skeletal system is the body's frame. An adult skeleton has 206 bones that support you, protect your organs, let you move, store minerals like calcium, and even make your blood cells. Explore the skeleton below — click any bone to learn it, or switch to Label Challenge to test yourself.

🛠️ Five Jobs of the Skeleton

  • Support — gives the body its shape and holds it up.
  • Protection — the skull guards the brain, ribs guard the heart and lungs, vertebrae guard the spinal cord.
  • Movement — bones act as levers that muscles pull on at the joints.
  • Mineral storage — bones store calcium and phosphorus.
  • Blood cell production — red bone marrow makes red and white blood cells.

🧭 Two Divisions of the Skeleton

DivisionWhat's in it
Axial (center)Skull, vertebral column (spine), and rib cage (ribs + sternum)
Appendicular (limbs)Arms, legs, and the girdles that attach them (shoulder & pelvic girdles)

Memory aid: the axial skeleton is the central axis; the appendicular skeleton is your appendages (limbs).

🔗 Joints & Connective Tissue

  • Joints are where two bones meet. Hinge joints (elbow, knee) bend one way; ball-and-socket joints (hip, shoulder) move in many directions.
  • Ligaments connect bone to bone.
  • Tendons connect muscle to bone.
  • Cartilage cushions the ends of bones so they don't grind together.

Don't mix these up: ligaments tie bones together; tendons tie muscle down to bone.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • The adult skeleton has 206 bones.
  • The femur (thigh bone) is the longest and strongest bone.
  • Axial = skull, spine, rib cage. Appendicular = limbs and girdles.
  • Bones store calcium; red bone marrow makes blood cells.
  • Ligaments = bone to bone; tendons = muscle to bone.

🧭 Example 1: Axial or Appendicular?

📝 How to decide quickly

Ask: "Is this bone part of the central axis, or part of a limb?"

Axial (central axis)
  • Skull
  • Vertebrae (spine)
  • Ribs
  • Sternum
Appendicular (limbs + girdles)
  • Humerus, radius, ulna (arm)
  • Femur, tibia, fibula (leg)
  • Clavicle, scapula (shoulder girdle)
  • Pelvis (pelvic girdle)

Quick check: the femur is in your leg → appendicular. The sternum is in your central chest → axial.

🗣️ Example 2: Common Name → Medical Name

📝 The ones the TEAS loves to test

  • Collarbone = clavicle
  • Shoulder blade = scapula
  • Breastbone = sternum
  • Thigh bone = femur
  • Kneecap = patella
  • Shin bone = tibia (the fibula is the thinner bone beside it)

Tip: the two forearm bones are the radius (thumb side) and ulna (pinky side). The single upper-arm bone is the humerus.

🔗 Example 3: Ligament vs. Tendon

📝 A two-word rule

  • Ligament connects bone to bone — it holds the skeleton together at the joints.
  • Tendon connects muscle to bone — it lets a muscle pull on a bone to move it.

Memory aid: think "tendons tie muscle down to bone." Whatever's left (bone to bone) is the ligament.
