🫘 TEAS Science: Kidneys & the Urinary System

How your body filters the blood, makes urine, and keeps your fluids in balance

🫘 Your Body's Filtration Plant

The urinary system cleans your blood. Your two kidneys filter out waste and extra water, turning them into urine. The urine then travels down the ureters, is stored in the bladder, and leaves through the urethra. Along the way the kidneys also balance your water, salts, and pH and help control blood pressure. OrganWhat it does

Ureters (2)Muscular tubes that carry urine down from each kidney to the bladder
BladderStretchy sac that stores urine until you release it
UrethraTube that carries urine from the bladder out of the body

Don't mix them up: ureters (with an "s" sound for "two") run from the kidneys to the bladder; the single urethra is the final exit tube.

💧 Inside the Kidney: The Nephron

Each kidney holds about a million tiny filtering units called nephrons — the functional unit of the kidney. Each nephron makes urine in three steps:

  1. Filtration — at the glomerulus (a ball of capillaries), blood pressure pushes water and small molecules out of the blood into the nephron. Blood cells and proteins are too big and stay behind.
  2. Reabsorption — the body takes back what it needs (almost all the glucose, most of the water, needed salts) into the blood.
  3. Secretion — the blood adds a few extra wastes (like excess H+ and K+) into the tubule.

Whatever is left is urine. Walk through all three steps on the Worked Examples tab.

⚖️ More Than Waste Removal

The kidneys do far more than make urine. They also help:

  • Balance water and electrolytes (sodium, potassium, and more) to keep your fluids steady.
  • Control blood pressure (by adjusting water and releasing the enzyme renin).
  • Maintain acid–base (pH) balance by secreting hydrogen ions.
  • Remove urea, the main nitrogen waste from breaking down protein.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Path of urine: kidney → ureter → bladder → urethra → out.
  • Nephron = the filtering unit; glomerulus = where filtration happens.
  • Three steps: filtration → reabsorption → secretion.
  • Healthy urine has no glucose and no protein/blood (those stay in the blood).
  • Kidneys regulate water, electrolytes, pH, and blood pressure and remove urea.
  • Dehydrated? Urine becomes more concentrated (darker, smaller amount).

🚰 Example 1: The Path of Urine

📝 Follow one drop from kidney to outside the body

  • Step 1: Urine forms inside the nephrons of the kidney and drains into the renal pelvis.
  • Step 2: From there it travels down a ureter (one tube per kidney).
  • Step 3: The ureter empties into the bladder, which stores the urine.
  • Step 4: When you go, the urine leaves through the urethra.

Answer: the order is kidney → ureter → bladder → urethra → out. Memory hook: things go down the ureTer To the bladder; the ureTHRA is the final exit.

🍬 Example 2: Why Is There No Glucose in Healthy Urine?

📝 Filtered, then taken back

  • Step 1: Glucose is a small molecule, so during filtration it passes out of the blood into the nephron along with water and salts.
  • Step 2: But the body wants to keep glucose. During reabsorption, the tubule pulls all of it back into the blood.
  • Step 3: So in a healthy person, by the time the filtrate becomes urine, there is no glucose left.

Answer: healthy urine has no glucose because it is fully reabsorbed. If blood sugar is very high (as in diabetes), there is more glucose than the tubule can reabsorb, so some spills into the urine — which is why "sugar in the urine" is a classic diabetes sign.

🩸 Example 3: The Path of Blood Through the Kidney

📝 Blood in, blood out

  • Step 1: Blood enters through the renal artery.
  • Step 2: It reaches the glomerulus, where filtration removes water and small wastes.
  • Step 3: The blood continues to capillaries wrapped around the tubule, where reabsorption and secretion fine-tune it.
  • Step 4: The cleaned blood leaves through the renal vein.

Answer: blood travels renal artery → glomerulus → capillaries → renal vein. Notice the two separate journeys: blood enters and exits by vessels, while the waste it dropped off becomes urine and exits by the ureter. Don't confuse the two paths!
