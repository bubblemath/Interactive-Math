🖐️ TEAS Science: Skin & Integument

The integumentary system — the layers of your skin and the structures packed inside them

🖐️ Your Body's Largest Organ

The skin is the main part of the integumentary system (which also includes hair, nails, and glands). It's actually the body's largest organ, and it does far more than cover you up: it keeps pathogens and water out, helps control your temperature, lets you feel touch and pain, and even helps make vitamin D. The skin is built in three layers — the epidermis on top, the dermis in the middle, and the hypodermis beneath. 📋 The Core Ideas

🧱 The Three Layers (Surface → Deep)

Learn them in order from the outside in. Each layer has a clear job and clear contents.

LayerWhat it isKey contents
Epidermis (outer)Thin, tough, waterproof — and has no blood vessels of its ownKeratin, dead surface cells, melanocytes (make melanin)
Dermis (middle)Thick, living layer where most of the "action" isBlood vessels, nerves & receptors, hair follicles, sweat & oil glands, collagen & elastin
Hypodermis (deepest, subcutaneous)Mostly fat; sits below the skin properAdipose (fat) tissue — insulates, cushions, anchors skin to muscle & bone

Memory aid: Epidermis is External; the Dermis is the Deeper working layer; Hypo- means "under," so the hypodermis is underneath it all.

🧰 Structures in the Skin

  • Hair follicle & shaft — hair grows from a follicle rooted in the dermis; the shaft is dead keratin.
  • Sebaceous (oil) gland — attached to a follicle; releases sebum to lubricate and waterproof skin and hair.
  • Sweat gland — a coiled gland in the dermis; releases sweat that cools you as it evaporates.
  • Arrector pili muscle — a tiny muscle that pulls a hair upright, creating "goosebumps."
  • Sensory receptors — nerve endings in the dermis that detect touch, pressure, temperature, and pain.
  • Blood vessels — run through the dermis (never the epidermis); help control temperature.

🎯 What the Skin Does (Functions)

  • Protection: a barrier against pathogens, injury, UV rays, and water loss.
  • Temperature regulation: sweating cools you; blood vessels widen to release heat or narrow to keep it.
  • Sensation: receptors let you feel touch, pressure, temperature, and pain.
  • Vitamin D: with sunlight, the skin helps make vitamin D for healthy bones.
  • Excretion: a small amount of waste leaves the body in sweat.

🔑 Key Terms

  • Keratin — the tough, waterproof protein of the epidermis, hair, and nails.
  • Melanin — the pigment that gives skin color and absorbs UV; made by melanocytes in the epidermis.
  • Collagen & elastin — proteins in the dermis that give skin strength and stretch.
  • Sebum — the oily substance from sebaceous glands.
  • Adipose tissue — the fat that fills the hypodermis.
  • Integumentary system — skin plus hair, nails, and glands.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Order from the surface in: Epidermis → Dermis → Hypodermis.
  • The epidermis has NO blood vessels of its own; the dermis does.
  • The dermis holds the "action": follicles, glands, nerves, and vessels.
  • The hypodermis is mostly fat — it insulates, cushions, and anchors.
  • Skin is the body's largest organ; its main job is protection.
  • Keratin waterproofs, melanin gives color & blocks UV, and sweat + blood-vessel changes control temperature.

🧱 Example 1: The Three Layers, Top to Bottom

📝 Walk down through the skin

  • Epidermis (outer): the thin, tough top layer. Its surface is dead, keratin-filled cells that waterproof and protect. It has no blood vessels of its own. New cells (and melanin) are made at its base.
  • Dermis (middle): the thick, living layer. This is where you find blood vessels, nerves and receptors, hair follicles, sweat glands, and oil glands, all held in collagen and elastin.
  • Hypodermis (deepest): mostly fat (adipose tissue). It insulates against heat loss, cushions against bumps, and anchors the skin to muscle and bone.

Answer: from the surface inward, the order is epidermis → dermis → hypodermis. If a question mentions blood vessels or glands, think dermis; if it mentions fat or insulation, think hypodermis.

🌡️ Example 2: How Your Skin Cools You Down

📝 Two cooling tools working together

  • Step 1: You overheat (exercise or hot weather). Your body needs to release heat.
  • Step 2 — sweat: Sweat glands in the dermis release sweat onto the surface. As that sweat evaporates, it carries heat away and cools you.
  • Step 3 — blood vessels: Blood vessels in the dermis widen (dilate), bringing more warm blood near the surface so heat can escape. (When you're cold, they narrow to keep heat in.)

Answer: the skin regulates temperature with sweating plus blood-vessel dilation. Both happen in the dermis — another reason the dermis is the busy middle layer.

🛡️ Example 3: Why the Epidermis Is Such a Good Barrier

📝 What makes the outer layer so protective?

  • Keratin: the surface cells are packed with keratin, a tough protein that makes the skin waterproof — keeping water in and pathogens out.
  • Dead, layered cells: the outer epidermal cells are dead and tightly stacked, so there's no easy way in for germs.
  • Melanin: melanocytes at the base make melanin, which absorbs harmful UV rays and helps protect deeper cells.
  • Constant renewal: the epidermis sheds and replaces its cells continually, so damaged or infected surface cells are pushed off.

Answer: the epidermis is a great barrier because of keratin, its dead stacked cells, and melanin — all working without any blood vessels of its own.
