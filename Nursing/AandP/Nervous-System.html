⚡ Your Body's Communication Network

The nervous system is your body's high-speed messaging system. Its basic cell is the neuron, which carries electrical signals. Those signals come in through the dendrites and go out through the axon. 🔌 Parts of a Neuron & the Signal Path

  • Dendrites — receive incoming signals and carry them toward the cell body.
  • Cell body (soma) — holds the nucleus and keeps the neuron alive.
  • Axon — carries the impulse away from the cell body.
  • Myelin sheath — fatty insulation that speeds the impulse up.
  • Axon terminals — pass the signal to the next cell using chemical messengers (neurotransmitters).
  • Synapse — the tiny gap the signal crosses to reach the next cell.

One-line memory aid: signals come IN through dendrites and go OUT through the axon.

🔀 Three Types of Neurons

  • Sensory (afferent) neurons — carry signals toward the CNS from your senses and body. Afferent = Arrive at the CNS.
  • Motor (efferent) neurons — carry signals away from the CNS to muscles and glands. Efferent = Exit the CNS.
  • Interneurons — connect neurons within the CNS and tie sensory input to motor output.

🗂️ Two Main Divisions

DivisionWhat's in itJob
Central (CNS)Brain + spinal cordThe control and processing center
Peripheral (PNS)All nerves outside the brain and spinal cordCarries messages between the CNS and the rest of the body

The PNS splits into the somatic system (voluntary — moving your muscles) and the autonomic system (involuntary — heart, digestion). The autonomic system has two opposite modes: sympathetic ("fight or flight") and parasympathetic ("rest and digest").

🧩 Key Brain Parts

  • Cerebrum — the largest part; thinking, memory, senses, and voluntary movement.
  • Cerebellum — balance, coordination, and smooth movement.
  • Brainstem (medulla) — automatic survival functions: heart rate, breathing, blood pressure.
  • Spinal cord — the express pathway between brain and body; also runs fast reflexes.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • The neuron is the basic cell of the nervous system.
  • Dendrites receive, the axon sends, and myelin speeds the signal up.
  • CNS = brain + spinal cord; PNS = all the nerves outside them.
  • Sensory/afferent → toward the CNS; motor/efferent → away from the CNS.
  • Sympathetic = fight or flight; parasympathetic = rest and digest.
  • The medulla controls heart rate and breathing; the cerebellum handles balance.

⚡ Example 1: Trace a Signal Through a Neuron

📝 From receiving end to the next cell

Dendrites (receive)→ Cell body→ Axon→ Axon terminals→ Synapse→ Next neuron

The big idea: the signal is electrical as it moves through the neuron, but to cross the synapse it becomes chemical — the axon terminals release neurotransmitters that drift across the gap to the next cell.

🔥 Example 2: You Touch Something Hot

📝 Sensory in, motor out

  • Step 1: Heat receptors in your fingertip fire. Sensory (afferent) neurons rush the "Hot!" message toward the CNS.
  • Step 2: The spinal cord and brain process it.
  • Step 3: Motor (efferent) neurons carry the "Move!" command away from the CNS to your arm muscles.
  • Step 4: You pull your hand back.

Memory aid: Afferent = Arrive at the CNS (sensory in); Efferent = Exit the CNS (motor out).

🦵 Example 3: The Reflex Arc

📝 Why your hand pulls back before it even hurts

  • Step 1: A sensory neuron detects the danger and signals the spinal cord.
  • Step 2: An interneuron in the spinal cord passes the message straight to a motor neuron — without waiting for the brain.
  • Step 3: The motor neuron fires the muscle, and you pull away almost instantly.

The big idea: a reflex takes a shortcut through the spinal cord, which is why it's so fast. The brain finds out a moment later (that's when you feel the pain).
