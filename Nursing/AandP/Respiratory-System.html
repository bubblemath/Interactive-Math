🫁 TEAS Science: The Respiratory System

How air travels, how we breathe, and how gases are exchanged

🌬️ Breathing In, Breathing Out

The respiratory system has one main job: bring oxygen (O₂) into the body and remove carbon dioxide (CO₂). Air travels down a branching set of tubes to tiny air sacs called alveoli, where the real magic — gas exchange — happens. 🛣️ The Path of Air (top to bottom)

  1. Nasal cavity / nose — warms, moistens, and filters the air.
  2. Pharynx (throat) — shared passage for air and food.
  3. Larynx (voice box) — holds the vocal cords; the epiglottis flap covers it during swallowing so food doesn't enter the airway.
  4. Trachea (windpipe) — kept open by C-shaped cartilage rings.
  5. Bronchi — the trachea splits into a right and left bronchus, one per lung.
  6. Bronchioles — smaller and smaller branches.
  7. Alveoli — tiny air sacs where gas exchange happens.

🔄 Gas Exchange — the Whole Point

  • Gas exchange happens in the alveoli, which are wrapped in tiny capillaries.
  • Oxygen moves from the alveoli into the blood (to be carried to the body).
  • Carbon dioxide moves from the blood into the alveoli (to be breathed out).
  • Both gases move by diffusion — from where there is MORE to where there is LESS. No energy required.
  • The alveoli and capillary walls are each just one cell thick, so gases pass easily.

Ties to the heart: blood arrives at the lungs low on oxygen (via the pulmonary artery) and leaves rich in oxygen (via the pulmonary veins).

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Alveoli are the site of gas exchange.
  • The diaphragm is the primary muscle of breathing.
  • The right lung has 3 lobes; the left lung has 2 lobes (smaller, to make room for the heart).
  • The epiglottis keeps food out of the trachea.
  • Gases move by diffusion: high concentration → low concentration.
  • The respiratory system also helps regulate blood pH by controlling CO₂.

🎞️ Gas-Exchange Animation

Watch what happens at a single alveolus. O₂ moves down into the blood; CO₂ moves up out of the blood to be exhaled. Each gas drifts from where it's crowded to where there's room — that's diffusion.

O₂ (oxygen) — into blood CO₂ (carbon dioxide) — out of blood
Why does this happen? The freshly breathed-in air in the alveolus is packed with oxygen, so oxygen diffuses into the oxygen-poor blood. Meanwhile the blood returning from the body is packed with carbon dioxide, so CO₂ diffuses the other way — into the alveolus — and you breathe it out. Direction is always from MORE to LESS.

🗺️ Example 1: Trace a Breath of Air

📝 From the nose to the air sacs

Nose / nasal cavity→ Pharynx→ Larynx→ Trachea→ Bronchi→ Bronchioles→ Alveoli

The big idea: the airway keeps branching into smaller and smaller tubes — like an upside-down tree — until air reaches the alveoli, the only place where oxygen can actually cross into the blood.

🫧 Example 2: Follow One Oxygen Molecule

📝 Step-by-Step Reasoning

  • Step 1: You inhale, and oxygen-rich air reaches an alveolus.
  • Step 2: The alveolus has lots of O₂; the blood in the surrounding capillary has little. So O₂ diffuses from the alveolus into the blood.
  • Step 3: The O₂ hops onto a red blood cell and rides to the heart's left side.
  • Step 4: The left ventricle pumps it out through the aorta to the body, where cells use it.

Answer: Oxygen travels alveolus → blood → heart → body. Carbon dioxide makes the reverse trip: body → blood → alveolus → exhaled.

⬇️ Example 3: What Happens When You Inhale?

📝 Step-by-Step Reasoning

  • Step 1: The diaphragm contracts and flattens, pulling downward.
  • Step 2: This makes the chest cavity bigger (more volume).
  • Step 3: More volume means lower pressure inside the lungs.
  • Step 4: Air always moves from higher to lower pressure, so air rushes in.

Answer: Inhaling is driven by the diaphragm contracting, which lowers the pressure inside the chest so air flows in. To exhale, the diaphragm relaxes, volume shrinks, pressure rises, and air flows back out.
