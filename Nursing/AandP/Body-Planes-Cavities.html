🧭 TEAS Science: Body Planes & Cavities

Anatomical terminology — the planes that slice the body, the directions that describe it, and the cavities that hold its organs

🧭 Speaking the Language of Anatomy

Before you can learn where anything is in the body, you need a shared map. Anatomy gives us three tools for that: planes (imaginary flat cuts through the body), directional terms (precise words like superior and medial), and body cavities (the hollow spaces that hold our organs). All of these assume the body is in anatomical position — standing upright, facing forward, arms at the sides, palms forward. 📋 The Core Ideas

🔪 The Three Planes

A plane is an imaginary flat cut through the body. Each one divides the body a different way.

PlaneAlso calledDivides into
SagittalMedian (if at the midline)Left and right (midsagittal = equal halves)
FrontalCoronalFront (anterior) and back (posterior)
TransverseHorizontal / cross-sectionTop (superior) and bottom (inferior)

Memory aid: a sagittal arrow flies straight ahead splitting left/right; frontal faces you (front/back); transverse cuts across like a belt (top/bottom).

🧭 Directional Terms (they come in pairs)

Each term has an opposite. They always assume the body is in anatomical position.

TermMeansOpposite
SuperiorToward the head / upperInferior (toward the feet)
Anterior (ventral)Toward the frontPosterior / dorsal (toward the back)
MedialToward the midlineLateral (away from the midline)
ProximalNearer the trunk / point of attachmentDistal (farther from the trunk)
SuperficialToward the surfaceDeep (farther from the surface)

📦 Body Cavities

The body's organs sit in two main cavities, each with smaller parts.

  • Dorsal cavity (toward the back): holds the nervous system's control center.
    • Cranial cavity — the brain.
    • Spinal (vertebral) cavity — the spinal cord.
  • Ventral cavity (toward the front): holds most internal organs. The diaphragm splits it in two:
    • Thoracic cavity (chest, above the diaphragm) — heart and lungs.
    • Abdominopelvic cavity (below the diaphragm) — stomach, intestines, liver, kidneys (abdominal) and bladder & reproductive organs (pelvic).

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Sagittal = left/right, frontal/coronal = front/back, transverse = top/bottom.
  • Superior/inferior, anterior/posterior, medial/lateral, proximal/distal, superficial/deep — always learn them as opposite pairs.
  • Ventral = anterior (front); dorsal = posterior (back).
  • Dorsal cavity = cranial + spinal (brain & spinal cord). Ventral cavity = thoracic + abdominopelvic.
  • The diaphragm separates the thoracic cavity from the abdominopelvic cavity.
  • All terms assume anatomical position: standing, facing forward, palms forward.

🔪 Example 1: Telling the Three Planes Apart

📝 Match each plane to what it divides

  • Sagittal plane: a vertical cut that divides the body into left and right. If it runs exactly down the midline into equal halves, it's the midsagittal (median) plane; off-center, it's parasagittal.
  • Frontal (coronal) plane: a vertical cut that divides the body into front (anterior) and back (posterior).
  • Transverse (horizontal) plane: a horizontal cut that divides the body into top (superior) and bottom (inferior) — like a CT "slice."

Quick check: if a question says a cut separates top from bottom, it's transverse. Left from right → sagittal. Front from back → frontal/coronal.

🧭 Example 2: Reading a Sentence of Directional Terms

📝 "The knee is superior to the ankle but inferior to the hip."

  • Superior means toward the head, so the knee is above the ankle. ✔️
  • Inferior means toward the feet, so the knee is below the hip. ✔️
  • Both can be true at once — directional terms are always relative (this part compared with that part).

On limbs, switch to proximal/distal: the knee is proximal to (nearer the trunk than) the ankle, and distal to (farther from the trunk than) the hip. Remember: proximal is protein-shake-close to the body's core.

📦 Example 3: Sorting Organs Into Cavities

📝 Where does each organ live?

  • Brain → cranial cavity (part of the dorsal cavity).
  • Spinal cord → spinal/vertebral cavity (also dorsal).
  • Heart & lungs → thoracic cavity (the chest part of the ventral cavity, above the diaphragm).
  • Stomach, intestines, liver, kidneys → abdominal portion of the abdominopelvic cavity (below the diaphragm).
  • Bladder & reproductive organs → pelvic portion of the abdominopelvic cavity.

The key divider: the diaphragm separates the thoracic cavity (heart/lungs) from the abdominopelvic cavity (digestive and pelvic organs).
