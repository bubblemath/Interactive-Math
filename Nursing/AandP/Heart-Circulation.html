🫀 Your Heart: a Two-Sided Pump

The heart is a muscular pump with four chambers. The right side handles oxygen-poor (deoxygenated) blood and sends it to the lungs. The left side handles oxygen-rich (oxygenated) blood and sends it to the rest of the body. Explore the diagram below — click any part to see what it does.

🏠 The Four Chambers

  • Right atrium: receives oxygen-poor blood returning from the body.
  • Right ventricle: pumps that blood to the lungs.
  • Left atrium: receives oxygen-rich blood returning from the lungs.
  • Left ventricle: pumps oxygen-rich blood to the whole body. It has the thickest wall because it works the hardest.

Memory aid: Atria are the receiving rooms (blood comes in); ventricles are the exit pumps (blood goes out).

🚪 The Four Valves (one-way doors)

  • Tricuspid valve: between the right atrium and right ventricle.
  • Pulmonary valve: between the right ventricle and the pulmonary artery.
  • Mitral (bicuspid) valve: between the left atrium and left ventricle.
  • Aortic valve: between the left ventricle and the aorta.

Memory aid: TRIcuspid is on the Right (try... right). The mitral valve is on the left and has two flaps (bicuspid). Valves keep blood moving in one direction and prevent backflow.

🩸 The Great Vessels & Two Exceptions

  • Vena cava (superior & inferior): large veins returning oxygen-poor blood from the body to the right atrium.
  • Aorta: the largest artery; carries oxygen-rich blood from the left ventricle to the body.
  • Pulmonary artery: carries oxygen-poor blood to the lungs — the only artery with deoxygenated blood.
  • Pulmonary veins: carry oxygen-rich blood from the lungs to the heart — the only veins with oxygenated blood.

Remember the rule that always holds: arteries carry blood AWAY from the heart; veins carry blood TOWARD the heart. That's true even for the two "exception" vessels — it's the oxygen level that flips, not the direction.

PulmonaryHeart → lungs → heart (picks up oxygen)Right side

Vessel types: arteries (thick, muscular, high pressure, carry away), veins (thinner, have valves, carry back), and capillaries (tiny, one-cell-thick — where oxygen and carbon dioxide are actually exchanged).

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • The heart has 4 chambers; the left ventricle has the thickest wall.
  • The pulmonary artery is the only artery carrying deoxygenated blood; pulmonary veins are the only veins carrying oxygenated blood.
  • The heart's natural pacemaker is the SA (sinoatrial) node, located in the right atrium.
  • A normal resting adult heart rate is about 60–100 beats per minute.
  • Capillaries are where gas exchange happens.

🗺️ Example 1: The Complete Path of Blood

📝 Follow one drop of blood, start to finish

Body→ Vena cava→ Right atrium→ Tricuspid valve→ Right ventricle→ Pulmonary valve→ Pulmonary artery→ Lungs→ Pulmonary veins→ Left atrium→ Mitral valve→ Left ventricle→ Aortic valve→ Aorta→ Body

The big idea: blood makes a figure-eight through two pumps. The right side is the short trip to the lungs to pick up oxygen (and drop off carbon dioxide); the left side is the long trip out to the body to deliver that oxygen.

🫁 Example 2: Why Does the Pulmonary Artery Carry "Blue" Blood?

📝 Step-by-Step Reasoning

  • Step 1 — Where is the blood coming from? The right ventricle, which holds oxygen-poor blood returned from the body.
  • Step 2 — Where is it going? To the lungs, to pick up oxygen.
  • Step 3 — Artery or vein? It carries blood away from the heart, so by definition it's an artery.
  • Step 4 — The takeaway: "Artery" describes direction (away from the heart), not oxygen level. Since this blood hasn't reached the lungs yet, it's still deoxygenated.

Answer: The pulmonary artery is an artery because it leaves the heart, but it carries deoxygenated blood because it's on its way to the lungs. The pulmonary veins are the mirror image — veins (toward the heart) carrying oxygen-rich blood back from the lungs.

💪 Example 3: Why Is the Left Ventricle So Muscular?

📝 Step-by-Step Reasoning

  • Step 1: The right ventricle only has to push blood a short distance — to the lungs, which sit right next to the heart.
  • Step 2: The left ventricle has to push blood to the entire body — head to toe — against much higher pressure.
  • Step 3: More work needs more muscle, so the left ventricle's wall is the thickest of the four chambers.

Answer: The left ventricle has the thickest wall because it powers systemic circulation — pumping oxygen-rich blood to the whole body.
