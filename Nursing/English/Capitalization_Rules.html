Capitalization Rules

When does a word earn a capital letter? It comes down to a few clear rules. Learn them once, and you'll fix sentences on autopilot.

Key Concepts

Capitalization is really two questions: Is this the start of a sentence? and Is this a specific name (a proper noun)? If yes to either, capitalize. Tap any card to open it.

💡 Big idea: A proper noun names one specific person, place, or thing (Maria, Houston, Tylenol). A common noun names a general kind of thing (nurse, city, medicine) and stays lowercase.

Step-by-step: three rules in action

1. Proper noun vs. common noun

Sentence: "my friend maria drove to houston."
"my" begins the sentence → capital My.
"maria" names a specific person → capital Maria. "houston" is a specific city → capital Houston.
"friend" and "drove" are common words → stay lowercase.

✓ "My friend Maria drove to Houston."

2. Calendar names — but not seasons

Sentence: "we return on monday after the winter break."
"we" begins the sentence, and "monday" is a day of the week → both capital.
"winter" is a season — seasons are not capitalized.

✓ "We return on Monday after the winter break."

3. Titles: before a name vs. on their own

Sentence: "nurse chen asked the doctor to review it."
"nurse chen" — a title right before a name → capital Nurse Chen.
"the doctor" has no name after it → it's a general title → lowercase.

✓ "Nurse Chen asked the doctor to review it."
