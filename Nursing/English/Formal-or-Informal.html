Formal or Informal?

Language has a "dial" for formality. You turn it up for a chart or a professional email, and down for a text to a friend. The skill is matching the setting to your audience and purpose.

Key Concepts

Formal and informal aren't "good" and "bad" — they're settings you choose to fit your audience. Tap any card to open it.

💡 Big idea: Formal and informal language aren't "right" and "wrong" — they're tools. The skill is register-matching: choosing the level of formality that fits your audience and purpose. In healthcare writing, that usually means clear, professional, formal language.

Step-by-step

1. The same idea in two registers

Formal

"The patient reports mild nausea and declined breakfast."

Informal

"Pt was kinda queasy and didn't wanna eat 🙁"

The formal version uses precise wording, no contractions, and an objective tone.
The informal version uses slang (kinda, wanna), a contraction, and an emoji.
For a medical chart, the formal version is the right match.

2. Match the audience

Audience: a text to a coworker who's your friend, asking to swap shifts.
"Hey! Any chance you could swap Friday? 🙏" fits — friendly and casual is expected here.
"I am writing to formally request an exchange of shifts" would feel stiff and odd for a friend.

✓ Informal isn't "wrong" — it's the right match for a casual audience.

3. Fix the register

You email your nurse manager: "Hey, gonna be like 20 min late, sry!"
Slang (gonna), text-speak (sry), and a casual tone don't fit a professional audience.
Turn the dial up: "I wanted to let you know I will be about 20 minutes late. I apologize for the inconvenience."

✓ Formal doesn't mean fancy — it means clear and professional.
