Clear & Concise

Good writing says exactly what it means with no wasted words. The skill is trimming the redundant and the empty — the words that repeat an idea or add nothing — so your meaning comes through clearly.

Key Concepts

Most wordiness comes from a few repeatable patterns. Learn to spot them, and trimming becomes automatic. Tap any card to open it.

💡 Big idea: Concise writing says exactly what it means with no wasted words. To trim, ask of each word: “If I delete this, does the meaning change?” If not, cut it.

Step-by-step

1. A redundant pair

Wordy: "The nurse reviewed the patient's past history."
A history is always a record of the past.
So "past" and "history" say the same idea twice.
Cut "past" — nothing is lost.

✓ Concise: "The nurse reviewed the patient's history."

2. An empty intensifier

Wordy: "Her symptoms are very unique."
Unique means one of a kind — it's an absolute.
Something can't be "very" one of a kind; there are no degrees of unique.
Cut "very."

✓ Concise: "Her symptoms are unique."

3. A wordy phrase

Wordy: "The nurse stayed late in order to finish charting."
"in order to" is a three-word way to say one word: "to."
Cut "in order" and keep "to."

✓ Concise: "The nurse stayed late to finish charting."
