Multiple-Meaning Words

Words like culture, positive, and discharge each carry more than one meaning. The word itself never tells you which one — the sentence around it does. Read the context, then pick the meaning that fits.

Key Concepts

A multiple-meaning word is a familiar word that has two or more established meanings. Which one is "live" depends entirely on the sentence. Tap any card to open it.

💡 Big idea: The same word can name a lab sample or a set of customs (culture), a hopeful mood or a test result (positive), a fluid or the act of sending someone home (discharge). You don't choose the meaning — the context does. Your job is to read the sentence and match the word to the meaning that fits.

Step-by-step

1. Everyday vs. clinical — “acute”

The ER mostly treats acute injuries that appear suddenly.
  • adj. severe and coming on suddenly
  • adj. sharp or keen (a sense or a mind)
“Acute” has two common senses: sudden and severe, or keen and sharp-minded.
The clue is “appear suddenly” — that fits the first sense, not a keen mind.
So here acute means severe and coming on suddenly.

2. Noun or verb? — “discharge”

The nurse charted a thick discharge draining from the wound.
  • noun fluid that flows out of the body
  • verb to release a patient from care
Is “discharge” naming a thing or describing an action?
“a thick discharge … draining from the wound” — the word “a” before it and “draining” show it's a thing (a noun).
So discharge here is the fluid sense, not “to release.”

3. One word, two jobs in one sentence — “record”

Please record the result in her medical record.
The first “record” follows “Please” and takes an object — it's an action, a verb: to write down.
The second “record,” after “her medical,” names a thing — a noun: the file of information.
Same spelling, two meanings — the position in the sentence tells you which is which.
Read to the end and ask “thing or action?” — the context settles it.
