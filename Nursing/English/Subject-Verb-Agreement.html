Subject–Verb Agreement

A verb has to match its subject: one subject takes a singular verb, more than one takes a plural verb. The trick is finding the real subject — then the right verb usually falls into place.

Key Concepts

Step-by-step

1. Ignore the phrase in the middle

Sentence: "The box of gloves ___ on the counter."
Find the subject — it's box. "of gloves" is just a describing phrase, so cross it out.
One box → singular → is.

✓ "The box of gloves is on the counter."

2. Indefinite pronoun as the subject

Sentence: "Each of the nurses ___ a locker."
The subject is Each, not "nurses." "Each" is always singular — think "each one."
Singular → has.

✓ "Each of the nurses has a locker."

3. Subjects joined by "or / nor"

Sentence: "Neither the doctor nor the nurses ___ on call."
Two subjects are joined by "nor." With or/nor, match the verb to the subject nearer the verb — here, "nurses."
"nurses" is plural → are. (Flip them — "nor the doctor" — and it becomes "is.")

✓ "Neither the doctor nor the nurses are on call."
