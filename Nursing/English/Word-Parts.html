Word Parts (Medical Focus)

Long medical words look scary until you break them apart. Almost every one is built from a few pieces — a prefix, a root, and a suffix. Learn the pieces and you can decode words you've never seen, and build new ones on purpose.

Key Concepts

Medical words are built like LEGO. Learn the three kinds of pieces and the linking vowel, then meet the most common ones. Tap any card to open it.

💡 Big idea: Most medical terms follow the pattern prefix + root + suffix. Translate each piece, then read them as a plain-English phrase — usually starting from the ending. Example: hypo·glyc·emia = low (hypo) + sugar (glyc) + blood condition (emia) → low blood sugar.

Step-by-step decoding

1. Add up the pieces

gastr·itis
Split the word: gastr + itis.
gastr/o is the root for stomach; -itis is the suffix for inflammation.
Read them together.
gastritis = inflammation of the stomach

2. Start from the suffix

nephr·ectomy
The ending usually names the procedure, so define it first: -ectomy means surgical removal.
Then the root: nephr/o means kidney.
Suffix first, then root — "removal of … kidney."
nephrectomy = surgical removal of a kidney

3. Watch the linking vowel

cardi·o·megaly
cardi/o = heart, -megaly = enlargement.
The o in the middle is a combining vowel. It's kept because -megaly starts with a consonant — but it carries no meaning.
Ignore the o and translate the real parts.
cardiomegaly = an enlarged heart
