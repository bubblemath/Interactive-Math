Fragments & Run-ons

A complete sentence has just the right amount: a subject, a verb, and a finished thought. Too little and it's a fragment; too much crammed together and it's a run-on. Learn to spot each one — and fix it.

Key Concepts

It all comes down to the complete sentence. Once you can spot one, fragments and run-ons are easy to catch. Tap any card to open it.

💡 Big idea: Every sentence needs three things — a subject, a verb, and a complete thought. Too little and you have a fragment; two complete thoughts crammed together and you have a run-on.

Step-by-step

1. Fixing a fragment

✗ Fragment: "Because the patient was dehydrated."
It starts with "Because," a dependent word — it gives a reason but never says what happened.
On its own, it leaves you hanging. It needs a complete sentence to lean on.
Attach it to a main clause that delivers the point.

✓ "Because the patient was dehydrated, the nurse started an IV."

2. A comma splice — four ways to fix it

✗ Run-on: "The monitor beeped, the nurse looked up."
Two complete sentences are joined by only a comma. Any one of these four fixes works:
Period: "The monitor beeped. The nurse looked up."
Semicolon: "The monitor beeped; the nurse looked up."
Comma + FANBOYS: "The monitor beeped, so the nurse looked up."
Subordinate one clause: "When the monitor beeped, the nurse looked up."

✓ Pick whichever fits your meaning best — all four are correct.

3. Fixing a fused run-on

✗ Run-on: "The IV was empty the pump alarmed."
Two complete sentences are run together with no punctuation at all.
The quickest fix is a period; a semicolon works if the ideas are closely linked.

✓ "The IV was empty. The pump alarmed."  (or "…empty; the pump alarmed.")
