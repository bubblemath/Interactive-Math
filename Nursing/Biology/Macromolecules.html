🧬 The Big Four

Living things are built from four families of large molecules called macromolecules: carbohydrates, lipids, proteins, and nucleic acids. Three of them are polymers — long chains built by linking small repeating units called monomers (lipids are the exception). All four are organic, meaning they’re built around carbon. 🔗 Monomers & Polymers

  • A monomer is a single building block; a polymer is many monomers linked into a chain.
  • Dehydration synthesis links monomers together by removing a water molecule.
  • Hydrolysis breaks a polymer apart by adding water — this is what digestion does to food.

🥗 The Three Macronutrients

On a nutrition label, the three macronutrients are carbohydrates, fats (lipids), and proteins — the macromolecules your body uses for fuel and building material. (Nucleic acids aren’t counted as a macronutrient, but they’re in every cell you eat.)

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Monomer shortcuts: carbs → monosaccharides; proteins → amino acids; nucleic acids → nucleotides; lipids → glycerol + fatty acids.
  • Proteins do the work: enzymes, antibodies, muscle — all protein.
  • Nucleic acids = DNA & RNA → store genetic info.
  • Lipids = long-term energy, insulation, and the cell membrane (phospholipids).
  • All four are organic → they all contain carbon.
  • Building uses dehydration synthesis; breaking down uses hydrolysis.

🔎 Example 1: Identify a Macromolecule from Clues

📝 Clue: "Its monomer is an amino acid, and it acts as an enzyme."

Step 1 \u2014 Check the monomer. The building block is the amino acid. Amino acids link together to form one thing: a protein.

Step 2 \u2014 Check the job. It acts as an enzyme. Enzymes are proteins that speed up reactions. ✔ Same answer.

Answer: Protein.

Fast monomer guide: amino acid → protein · monosaccharide → carbohydrate · nucleotide → nucleic acid · glycerol + fatty acids → lipid. The monomer almost always gives it away.

🔗 Example 2: Monomers → Polymers (and Back)

📝 How building blocks join and split

  • Building up (dehydration synthesis): two monomers join, and a water molecule is removed. Many monomers linked this way form a polymer (e.g., many glucose units → starch).
  • Breaking down (hydrolysis): a polymer is split by adding water, releasing the monomers.
  • Healthcare tie-in: digestion is hydrolysis. Your body adds water and enzymes to break starch into glucose, and proteins into amino acids, small enough to absorb.

Memory hook: dehydration = lose water to build; hydrolysis = add water to break.

🍎 Example 3: Macromolecules on a Nutrition Label

📝 Connecting the label to the biology

  • "Total Carbohydrate" (incl. sugars & fiber) → carbohydrates → quick energy.
  • "Total Fat" → lipids → stored energy, insulation, and cell membranes.
  • "Protein" → proteins → build and repair tissue, enzymes, antibodies.

These three are the macronutrients. The fourth macromolecule, nucleic acids (DNA/RNA), isn’t listed as a macronutrient — but it’s present in every cell of the food, carrying genetic information.
