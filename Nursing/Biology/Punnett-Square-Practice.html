🧬 TEAS Biology: Punnett Square Practice

Predict how traits pass from parents to offspring

🧬 Genetics Basics

You inherit two copies of most genes \u2014 one from each parent. A Punnett square is a simple grid that predicts the chances of an offspring inheriting a particular combination. Before we build one, here are the words you’ll need. Don’t worry about memorizing them all at once \u2014 the calculator and practice problems will make them click.

🔤 Gene & Allele

A gene is a set of instructions for a trait (like eye color). An allele is one version of that gene. For eye color, B (brown) and b (blue) are two alleles of the same gene.

💪 Dominant & Recessive

  • A dominant allele shows up whenever it is present. We write it as a capital letter (B).
  • A recessive allele only shows up when two copies are present. We write it lowercase (b).
  • So BB and Bb both show the dominant trait (brown eyes); only bb shows the recessive trait (blue eyes).

🆚 Genotype vs. Phenotype

TermMeansExample
GenotypeThe alleles an organism hasBB, Bb, or bb
PhenotypeThe trait you can seeBrown eyes or blue eyes

👥 Homozygous vs. Heterozygous

  • Homozygous = two of the same allele (BB or bb). "Homo" = same.
  • Heterozygous = two different alleles (Bb). "Hetero" = different. A heterozygous individual is a carrier of the recessive allele.

🔲 What a Punnett Square Shows

The two alleles from one parent go across the top; the two from the other parent go down the side. Each box combines one allele from each parent, showing a possible offspring genotype. Here is a classic Bb × Bb cross:

Bb
BBBBb
bBbbb

Result: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb  →  3 brown : 1 blue (75% brown, 25% blue).

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Capital = dominant, lowercase = recessive. One dominant allele is enough to show the dominant trait.
  • A recessive trait needs two recessive alleles (bb).
  • Bb × Bb → 3:1 phenotype ratio (75% dominant, 25% recessive).
  • Bb × bb → 1:1 phenotype ratio (50/50).
  • A heterozygous individual (Bb) is a carrier.
  • A Punnett square shows probability for each child \u2014 not a guarantee.

✏️ Example 1: Heterozygous × Heterozygous (Bb × Bb)

📝 Two brown-eyed carriers have a child

  1. Set up: put B and b across the top (Parent 1) and B and b down the side (Parent 2).
  2. Fill the boxes: BB, Bb, Bb, bb.
  3. Genotype ratio: 1 BB : 2 Bb : 1 bb.
  4. Phenotype ratio: 3 show the dominant trait (BB, Bb, Bb) : 1 shows the recessive (bb) → 3 brown : 1 blue.

Answer: each child has a 75% chance of brown eyes and a 25% chance of blue eyes.

🩺 Example 2: Two Carriers of a Recessive Condition (Nn × Nn)

📝 A genetic-counseling classic

Suppose a recessive condition: N = unaffected, n = affected. Both parents are unaffected carriers (Nn).

  1. Boxes: NN, Nn, Nn, nn.
  2. Genotype ratio: 1 NN : 2 Nn : 1 nn.
  3. Read it: 25% unaffected non-carrier (NN), 50% unaffected carriers (Nn), 25% affected (nn).

Answer: each child has a 25% chance of being affected \u2014 which is why two healthy parents can still have an affected child. This is exactly the kind of reasoning nurses use in genetic counseling.

🔬 Example 3: Homozygous Dominant × Homozygous Recessive (BB × bb)

📝 When a cross hides the recessive allele

  1. Boxes: every box is Bb.
  2. Genotype: 100% Bb (all heterozygous).
  3. Phenotype: 100% show the dominant trait (brown eyes)\u2026
  4. \u2026but every child is now a carrier of the recessive allele, even though none show it.

Takeaway: a recessive allele can be carried silently and reappear in a later generation. Use the calculator above to confirm each of these crosses!
