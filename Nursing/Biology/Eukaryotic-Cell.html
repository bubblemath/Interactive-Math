Cell structure & organelles — the parts of the cell and the job each one does

🔬 Meet the Cell

Every living thing is built from cells. A eukaryotic cell (like the animal cell shown here, and the cells in your body) is defined by having a true, membrane-bound nucleus plus many specialized compartments called organelles — each with its own job. A handy way to think about it: the cell is a tiny factory, and the organelles are its departments. Also good to know: centrioles help organize cell division in animal cells, and vacuoles store materials (small in animal cells, one large one in plant cells).

🆚 Eukaryotic vs. Prokaryotic

  • Eukaryotic cells (animals, plants, fungi) have a membrane-bound nucleus and membrane-bound organelles. They are larger and more complex.
  • Prokaryotic cells (bacteria) have no nucleus and no membrane-bound organelles — their DNA floats freely. They are smaller and simpler.

🌱 Animal vs. Plant Cells

Both are eukaryotic, but plant cells have three extras that animal cells lack:

  • A rigid cell wall outside the membrane (support and shape).
  • Chloroplasts for photosynthesis (capturing light energy).
  • A large central vacuole for storage and water balance.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Eukaryotic = has a nucleus. Prokaryotic (bacteria) = no nucleus.
  • Nucleus = DNA & control. Mitochondria = ATP/energy. Ribosomes = proteins.
  • Protein path: ribosome → rough ER → Golgi → out.
  • The cell membrane is selectively permeable — it chooses what crosses.
  • Plant-only: cell wall, chloroplasts, large central vacuole.
  • The cell is the basic unit of life (cell theory).

🏭 Example 1: The Cell as a Tiny Factory

📝 Match each organelle to its factory role

  • Nucleus = the manager's office — holds the blueprints (DNA) and runs the show.
  • Ribosomes = assembly workers — build the products (proteins).
  • Rough ER = the assembly line — proteins are processed as they move along.
  • Golgi apparatus = the shipping department — packages and sends products out.
  • Mitochondria = the power plant — supply energy (ATP) to run everything.
  • Lysosomes = the cleanup crew — break down and recycle waste.
  • Cell membrane = the security gate — controls what comes in and goes out.

When a question describes a job, match it to the department: "makes energy" → mitochondria; "ships proteins" → Golgi; "controls entry" → membrane.

🧬 Example 2: Following a Protein's Journey

📝 From instructions to finished product

  1. Nucleus: the DNA holds the instructions for the protein.
  2. Ribosome: reads the instructions and builds the protein from amino acids.
  3. Rough ER: the new protein is folded and processed as it moves through.
  4. Golgi apparatus: the protein is modified, packaged, and labeled for delivery.
  5. Out of the cell (or to its destination): the packaged protein ships out.

Remember the order: nucleus → ribosome → rough ER → Golgi → out. This pathway is a TEAS favorite.

🔍 Example 3: Eukaryotic vs. Prokaryotic (and Plant vs. Animal)

📝 Spot the differences

  • Eukaryotic vs. prokaryotic: the key word is nucleus. Eukaryotic cells have a membrane-bound nucleus and organelles; prokaryotic cells (bacteria) do not.
  • Plant vs. animal (both eukaryotic): plant cells add a cell wall, chloroplasts, and a large central vacuole. Animal cells have none of those three.
  • Shared by both: nucleus, cell membrane, cytoplasm, mitochondria, ribosomes, ER, and Golgi.

If a question asks what a plant cell has that an animal cell doesn't, think: wall, chloroplast, big vacuole.
