⚡ TEAS Biology: Cellular Respiration

How cells turn glucose + oxygen into usable energy (ATP)

⚡ The Big Idea

Cellular respiration is how your cells release the energy stored in food. They take in glucose (a sugar) and oxygen, and release usable energy as ATP, plus the waste products carbon dioxide and water. The whole thing can be summed up as:

Glucose+O₂ → ATP+CO₂+H₂O

📋 The Core Ideas

➡️ Inputs & Outputs

MoleculesWhy
Inputs (reactants)Glucose + Oxygen (O₂)The fuel and the gas needed to release its energy
Outputs (products)ATP + Carbon dioxide (CO₂) + Water (H₂O)Usable energy, plus waste you breathe and release

Memory hook: you breathe IN what respiration needs (oxygen) and breathe OUT what it makes (carbon dioxide).

🔢 The Three Stages

StageWhereKey point
1. GlycolysisCytoplasmSplits glucose; makes a little ATP; no oxygen needed
2. Krebs cycleMitochondrionReleases CO₂; loads electron carriers
3. Electron transport chainMitochondrionMakes the most ATP; oxygen accepts electrons → water

🌬️ Aerobic vs. Anaerobic

  • Aerobic respiration uses oxygen and makes a large amount of ATP (this is the full three-stage process).
  • Anaerobic respiration / fermentation happens without oxygen and makes only a small amount of ATP (think tired, burning muscles during hard exercise).

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Equation: glucose + O₂ → ATP + CO₂ + H₂O.
  • Inputs: glucose & oxygen. Outputs: ATP, CO₂, water.
  • ATP = the cell’s usable energy.
  • Glycolysis → cytoplasm. Krebs cycle & ETC → mitochondrion.
  • The electron transport chain makes the most ATP; oxygen accepts the electrons at the end.
  • Mitochondria are the powerhouse of the cell because most ATP is made there.

🍞➡️⚡ Example 1: Trace the Energy from Food to ATP

📝 From a bite of food to usable energy

  1. Glucose enters. Food is broken down to glucose, the fuel sugar.
  2. Glycolysis (cytoplasm): glucose is split in two, making a small amount of ATP.
  3. Krebs cycle (mitochondrion): the pieces are broken down further, releasing CO₂ and loading electron carriers.
  4. Electron transport chain (mitochondrion): those electrons power the production of most of the ATP. Oxygen accepts the electrons at the end, forming water.
  5. Result: lots of ATP for the cell to use, plus CO₂ and water as waste.

Equation: glucose + O₂ → ATP + CO₂ + H₂O.

🔄 Example 2: Respiration vs. Photosynthesis

📝 They’re roughly opposites

  • Photosynthesis (in plants) uses CO₂ + water + light to build glucose + O₂. It stores energy.
  • Cellular respiration (in all cells) uses glucose + O₂ to make ATP + CO₂ + water. It releases energy.

Notice the inputs of one are the outputs of the other. A common TEAS trap is mixing up which process makes glucose (photosynthesis) and which one uses it (respiration).

🏃 Example 3: Why Muscles Burn During Hard Exercise

📝 When oxygen runs short

  • With enough oxygen, muscles use aerobic respiration (all three stages) and make plenty of ATP.
  • During intense exercise, oxygen can’t arrive fast enough, so muscles switch to anaerobic respiration (fermentation).
  • Fermentation makes only a little ATP and produces lactic acid, which contributes to that burning, fatigued feeling.

Takeaway: oxygen lets cells make far more ATP. That’s why aerobic respiration is the body’s main energy pathway \u2014 and why we breathe.
