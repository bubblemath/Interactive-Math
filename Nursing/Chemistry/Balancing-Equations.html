⚖️ TEAS Chemistry: Balancing Equations

Make both sides match — because atoms are never lost or made

⚖️ Reactions & the Big Rule

A chemical equation is a recipe: reactants (what you start with) on the left, an arrow, then products (what you make) on the right. The one rule that drives everything is the law of conservation of mass — atoms are never created or destroyed, just rearranged. So a correct equation must have the same number of each kind of atom on both sides. We make that happen by adjusting coefficients. First, let’s name the parts.

📋 The Core Ideas

🔢 Coefficients vs. Subscripts

  • A coefficient is the big number in front of a formula. It multiplies the whole formula. You change these to balance.
  • A subscript is the small number inside a formula (the 2 in H₂O). It tells how many atoms of that element are in the substance. Never change these — that would turn it into a different substance.
  • To count atoms of an element: coefficient × subscript. Example: 2 H₂O has 2 × 2 = 4 hydrogen atoms and 2 × 1 = 2 oxygen atoms.

⚖️ What “Balanced” Means

An equation is balanced when every element has the same total number of atoms on the left and the right. Look at water:

EquationH (left → right)O (left → right)Balanced?
H₂ + O₂ → H₂O2 → 2 ✓2 → 1 ✗No
2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O4 → 4 ✓2 → 2 ✓Yes ✅

We didn’t touch a single subscript — we just put coefficients in front until both sides matched.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Reactants are on the left, products on the right; the arrow means “reacts to form.”
  • Conservation of mass: atoms are only rearranged, so both sides need equal atoms of each element.
  • Change coefficients (big, in front) to balance — never subscripts (small, inside).
  • Atom count = coefficient × subscript.
  • Balanced answers use the smallest whole numbers that work.

✏️ Example 1: Water — H₂ + O₂ → H₂O

📝 Balance the formation of water

  1. Count as written (all 1s): left has 2 H and 2 O; right has 2 H and 1 O. Oxygen doesn’t match.
  2. Put a 2 in front of H₂O → right now has 4 H and 2 O. Oxygen matches, but hydrogen is off (2 left, 4 right).
  3. Put a 2 in front of H₂ → left now has 4 H. Both sides: 4 H, 2 O. ✓

Answer: 2 H₂ + O₂ → 2 H₂O. Notice we only added coefficients — never changed a subscript.

🌬️ Example 2: Ammonia — N₂ + H₂ → NH₃

📝 Balance the making of ammonia

  1. Nitrogen: left has 2 N (from N₂), right has 1. Put a 2 in front of NH₃ → right has 2 N and 6 H.
  2. Hydrogen: right now has 6 H; left has 2. Put a 3 in front of H₂ → left has 6 H.
  3. Check: N is 2 = 2 ✓, H is 6 = 6 ✓.

Answer: N₂ + 3 H₂ → 2 NH₃. Balance one element at a time and re-check the others.

🔥 Example 3: Methane burning — CH₄ + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

📝 Balance the combustion of methane

  1. Carbon: 1 = 1 already ✓.
  2. Hydrogen: left has 4 (CH₄); right has 2 (H₂O). Put a 2 in front of H₂O → right has 4 H.
  3. Oxygen: right now has 2 (CO₂) + 2 (2 H₂O) = 4. Put a 2 in front of O₂ → left has 4 O. ✓

Answer: CH₄ + 2 O₂ → CO₂ + 2 H₂O. Save oxygen for last — it often appears in several products.
