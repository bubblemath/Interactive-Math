🔗 TEAS Chemistry: Ionic vs. Covalent Bonds

How atoms join — by giving electrons away, or by sharing them

🔗 Why Atoms Bond

Atoms “want” a full outer shell of electrons — that’s their most stable, comfortable state (think of how the noble gases already have one and barely react). To get there, atoms team up by forming bonds. There are two main ways to do it: transfer electrons (an ionic bond) or share them (a covalent bond). Flip between the two below to see the difference.

🔗 Sharing vs. Transfer

Switch between the two bond types to see what happens to the electrons.

📋 The Two Bond Types

↗️ Ionic Bonds — Electrons Are Transferred

  • One atom gives electrons; the other takes them.
  • Forms between a metal and a nonmetal. The metal loses electrons (becomes a positive ion); the nonmetal gains them (becomes a negative ion).
  • The bond is the attraction between the opposite charges. Example: NaCl (table salt).
  • Ionic compounds form solid crystals, have high melting points, and conduct electricity when dissolved in water (think electrolytes!).

🤝 Covalent Bonds — Electrons Are Shared

  • Atoms share one or more pairs of electrons so each one “counts” a full shell.
  • Forms between two nonmetals. No ions and no full charges are created.
  • A group of atoms held together this way is a molecule. Examples: H₂O (water), CO₂, O₂.
  • Covalent (molecular) compounds usually have lower melting points than ionic ones.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Ionic = transfer electrons; covalent = share electrons.
  • Metal + nonmetal → ionic. Nonmetal + nonmetal → covalent.
  • Ionic bonds make ions (+/−) held by opposite-charge attraction; covalent bonds make molecules.
  • Atoms bond to reach a full outer shell (the octet rule — usually 8 electrons).
  • Ionic compounds dissolve into ions (electrolytes) and conduct electricity in water.

✏️ Example 1: Table salt (NaCl)

📝 What kind of bond holds NaCl together?

  1. Identify the elements: sodium (Na) is a metal; chlorine (Cl) is a nonmetal.
  2. Metal + nonmetal → ionic bond. Na gives its 1 outer electron to Cl.
  3. Now Na is Na⁺ and Cl is Cl⁻. The opposite charges attract — that attraction is the bond.

Answer: ionic. (And NaCl dissolved in water gives the Na⁺ and Cl⁻ ions that make saline an electrolyte solution.)

🤝 Example 2: Water (H₂O) and carbon dioxide (CO₂)

📝 What kind of bonds are in H₂O and CO₂?

  1. Check the elements: H, O, and C are all nonmetals.
  2. Nonmetal + nonmetal → covalent bond. The atoms share electron pairs instead of transferring them.
  3. The result is a molecule (H₂O, CO₂) — no ions and no full charges.

Answer: both are covalent. Most molecules in the body (water, glucose, proteins) are held together by covalent bonds.

🔮 Example 3: Predict from the periodic table (Mg + O)

📝 Will magnesium and oxygen form an ionic or covalent bond?

  1. Magnesium (Mg) is a metal (group 2); oxygen (O) is a nonmetal.
  2. Metal + nonmetal → ionic. Mg gives away 2 electrons (→ Mg²⁺); O takes 2 (→ O²⁻).
  3. The +2 and −2 charges attract to form MgO.

Answer: ionic. You can often predict the bond just from where the elements sit on the periodic table.
