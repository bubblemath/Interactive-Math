⚛️ What Is an Atom?

Everything around you — and inside you — is made of atoms. An atom is the smallest piece of an element that still counts as that element. Atoms are built from just three particles: protons, neutrons, and electrons. Learn how those three fit together and a big chunk of TEAS chemistry falls into place. Let’s start by exploring the parts.

📋 The Three Particles

🧩 Protons, Neutrons & Electrons

Electron (e⁻)Negative (−1)≈ 0 (tiny)In shells around the nucleus

A handy memory hook: proton is positive. Neutrons are neutral. Electrons are the tiny negative ones zipping around the outside.

🔢 Atomic Number & Mass Number

  • Atomic number = number of protons. This is the atom’s ID badge — it decides which element it is. All carbon atoms have 6 protons; all oxygen atoms have 8.
  • Mass number = protons + neutrons. (Electrons are too light to count.) So carbon with 6 protons and 6 neutrons has a mass number of 12.
  • To find neutrons, subtract: neutrons = mass number − atomic number.

⚡ Charge & Ions

  • A neutral atom has equal protons and electrons, so the charges cancel out.
  • Charge = protons − electrons. If they’re not equal, the atom is an ion.
  • Lose electrons → more protons than electrons → positive ion (cation), like Na⁺.
  • Gain electrons → more electrons than protons → negative ion (anion), like Cl⁻.
  • Ions matter in healthcare: sodium (Na⁺), potassium (K⁺), calcium (Ca²⁺), and chloride (Cl⁻) are electrolytes your body needs.

🧬 Isotopes

Isotopes are atoms of the same element (same number of protons) with different numbers of neutrons. Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 are both carbon — 6 protons each — but Carbon-14 has 2 extra neutrons, so it has a bigger mass number. Changing neutrons changes the mass, not the element.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Protons decide the element. Atomic number = number of protons.
  • Mass number = protons + neutrons. Neutrons = mass number − atomic number.
  • Charge = protons − electrons. Equal = neutral; unequal = ion.
  • Cation = positive (lost electrons); anion = negative (gained electrons).
  • Isotopes = same protons, different neutrons.
  • Electron shells fill from the inside out: first shell holds 2, the next holds 8.

✏️ Example 1: Which element is it?

📝 An atom has 6 protons. What element is it?

  1. The atomic number = number of protons, so this atom’s atomic number is 6.
  2. The atomic number is the element’s ID. Element number 6 is carbon (C).
  3. Notice we didn’t need the neutrons or electrons — protons alone decide the element.

Answer: it’s carbon. Any atom with 6 protons is carbon, no matter how many neutrons or electrons it has.

🔢 Example 2: Find the mass number (and neutrons)

📝 A carbon atom has 6 protons and 6 neutrons. What is its mass number? What about Carbon-14?

  1. Mass number = protons + neutrons = 6 + 6 = 12. This atom is Carbon-12.
  2. Carbon-14 has a mass number of 14. To find its neutrons: neutrons = mass number − atomic number = 14 − 6 = 8 neutrons.
  3. Carbon-12 and Carbon-14 are isotopes: same protons (6), different neutrons (6 vs. 8).

Answer: mass number 12; Carbon-14 has 8 neutrons. Same element, different mass.

⚡ Example 3: Charge of an ion

📝 A sodium atom has 11 protons. It loses 1 electron, leaving 10 electrons. What is its charge?

  1. Charge = protons − electrons = 11 − 10 = +1.
  2. More protons than electrons → a positive ion (cation). We write it Na⁺.
  3. The proton count didn’t change, so it’s still sodium — just a charged version of it.

Answer: +1 (Na⁺). Sodium ions are a key electrolyte in the body — exactly the kind of detail the TEAS likes to test.
