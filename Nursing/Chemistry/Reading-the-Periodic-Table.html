📋 The Core Ideas

↔️ Periods & Groups

  • Periods are the 7 horizontal rows. The period number = how many electron shells the atom has.
  • Groups (also called families) are the 18 vertical columns. Elements in the same group have similar properties because they have the same number of outer (valence) electrons.
  • Atomic number increases by 1 as you move left to right, top to bottom.

👪 Key Families to Know

FamilyGroupWhat’s special
Alkali metals1Soft, very reactive metals (Li, Na, K)
Alkaline earth metals2Reactive metals (Mg, Ca)
Transition metals3–12The classic metals (iron, copper, gold)
Halogens17Very reactive nonmetals (F, Cl, Br, I)
Noble gases18Unreactive — full outer shell (He, Ne, Ar)

🪙 Metals, Nonmetals & Metalloids

  • Metals (left and middle) — shiny, conduct electricity, bend without breaking. Most of the table is metal.
  • Nonmetals (upper right, plus hydrogen) — dull, poor conductors, often gases.
  • Metalloids — sit along the “staircase” between them (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te) and share properties of both.

🌟 High-Yield TEAS Facts

  • Periods = rows (period number = number of electron shells).
  • Groups = columns (same group → similar properties & same valence electrons).
  • Group 1 = alkali metals · Group 2 = alkaline earth · Group 17 = halogens · Group 18 = noble gases.
  • Atomic number = number of protons, and it climbs by 1 across the table.
  • Metals on the left, nonmetals on the upper right, metalloids on the staircase between.

✏️ Example 1: Find an element’s address

📝 Where is chlorine (Cl) on the table, and what family is it in?

  1. Find chlorine. Its row is the 3rd one down → period 3.
  2. Its column is the 17th → group 17.
  3. Group 17 is the halogens — very reactive nonmetals.

Answer: chlorine is in period 3, group 17 — a halogen. Period = row, group = column. That’s the whole trick.

⚡ Example 2: Use the group to predict behavior

📝 Sodium (Na) is in group 1. What does its group tell you?

  1. Group 1 (except hydrogen) is the alkali metals.
  2. Group 1 elements have 1 valence electron, which they lose easily — so they’re very reactive and form +1 ions (Na⁺).
  3. Same group = similar behavior, so lithium and potassium act a lot like sodium.

Answer: being in group 1 tells you sodium is a soft, very reactive metal that forms Na⁺. Na⁺ is also a key electrolyte in the body.

🛡️ Example 3: Why noble gases are different

📝 Neon (Ne) is in group 18. Why is it so unreactive?

  1. Group 18 is the noble gases.
  2. They have a full outer electron shell (8 valence electrons, except helium’s 2).
  3. A full shell is stable, so noble gases rarely gain, lose, or share electrons — they hardly react at all.

Answer: neon’s full outer shell makes it stable and unreactive — the opposite of the eager group 1 metals.
