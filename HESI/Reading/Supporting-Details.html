🪑 The "Table" Analogy

Think of a paragraph like a table. The Main Idea is the tabletop—the central claim. Supporting Details are the legs that hold it up.

Fact
Statistic

Without strong supporting details, the main idea "falls down."

💡 Success Strategies for Supporting Detail Questions

  • Identify the main idea FIRST — You can't find supporting details without knowing what they support
  • Look for signal words — "For example," "specifically," "because," "such as" introduce details
  • Distinguish Major from Minor — Major details directly prove the main idea; minor details add extra information
  • Watch for "Does NOT support" — HESI often asks which detail does NOT belong
  • Fact vs. Opinion — Supporting details should be verifiable facts, not opinions

⚠️ Common Pitfalls

  • True ≠ Supporting — A statement can be TRUE but not support THIS specific main idea
  • Don't confuse detail types — Examples, statistics, and reasons serve different purposes
  • Inferred details — Some questions ask about details that are IMPLIED, not stated directly

📚 The Detail Reference Vault

🎯 Major vs. Minor Details

Major Details directly support and prove the main idea. Without them, the main idea would be unsupported. Minor Details add extra information, clarify major details, or provide "flavor"—but aren't essential to proving the main point.

Main Idea: Hand hygiene prevents infections.
Major Detail: Studies show handwashing reduces infection rates by 50%.
Minor Detail: The CDC recommends washing for at least 20 seconds.

🚦 Signal Words for Details

These words and phrases often introduce supporting details:

for example specifically such as for instance in addition furthermore because since according to studies show

🔍 Explicit vs. Inferred Details

Explicit details are stated directly in the passage—you can point to the exact words. Inferred details are implied by the evidence but not stated directly. You must "read between the lines."

Passage: "The patient's blood pressure was 180/110. He mentioned he'd run out of medication last week."

Explicit Detail: BP was 180/110
Inferred Detail: The patient's hypertension is uncontrolled (implied by the high BP and missed medication)

⚖️ Fact vs. Opinion

Facts can be verified or proven. Opinions express beliefs, judgments, or feelings. Strong supporting details are usually facts, not opinions.

Fact: "The medication reduced pain scores by 40%."
Opinion: "This medication is the best option for pain management."

Clue words for opinions: best, worst, should, believe, think, feel, probably

🏥 Nursing Context: Evidence-Based Practice

💡 Why Supporting Details Matter in Nursing

In nursing, "supporting details" are the symptoms, vital signs, lab results, and patient statements that support a primary diagnosis or nursing assessment. Just as a paragraph's main idea needs evidence, a nursing diagnosis needs clinical data to support it. Your ability to identify relevant supporting details directly impacts patient care quality.

📋 Clinical Applications

📊 Assessment Data

Subjective data (what the patient says) and objective data (what you observe/measure) are the supporting details for your nursing assessment. "Patient reports pain of 8/10" supports the assessment of acute pain.

📝 Care Plan Rationales

Every nursing intervention needs supporting evidence. "Elevate head of bed 30°" is supported by the detail "to reduce aspiration risk in patients with dysphagia."

🔍 Shift Report Analysis

During handoff, you must quickly identify which details are critical (major) vs. background information (minor) to prioritize your care.

📋 Policy Interpretation

Hospital policies state requirements (main ideas) and provide reasons/evidence (supporting details). Understanding both helps you follow protocols correctly.

🔗 Inferred Details in Clinical Practice

Nurses constantly make inferences from supporting details. If a patient has elevated temperature, increased WBC count, and reports feeling "achy," you infer infection even if no one has stated "the patient has an infection." This skill—drawing conclusions from evidence—is exactly what HESI tests with inferred detail questions.

🎯 The "SBAR" Connection

The SBAR communication framework mirrors main idea + supporting details:

  • Situation: The main idea (what's happening)
  • Background: Supporting details (relevant history)
  • Assessment: Your inference from the details
  • Recommendation: Action based on the evidence
