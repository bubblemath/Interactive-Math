INFERENCE

An inference is a logical conclusion based on evidence—NOT a guess!

💡 Success Strategies for Inference Questions

  • Look for clues, not statements — Inferences are based on what's IMPLIED, not what's directly stated
  • Use the "evidence test" — Can you point to specific text that supports your conclusion?
  • Watch for signal phrases — "It can be inferred," "The author suggests," "Most likely means"
  • Eliminate extremes — Words like "always," "never," "all" are usually wrong
  • Stay close to the text — Don't bring in outside knowledge that isn't supported

⚠️ The "Too Far" Trap

  • Over-inferring — Drawing conclusions that go BEYOND what the text supports
  • Using outside knowledge — Bringing in facts not mentioned or implied in the passage
  • Confusing inference with opinion — Your personal beliefs don't count as evidence
  • Ignoring contradicting evidence — All parts of the passage must support your inference

📚 The Inference Reference Vault

🔍 Inference vs. Observation

Observation (Explicit): What is directly stated in the text. You can point to the exact words.

Inference (Implicit): What is logically concluded based on evidence. It's NOT stated but IS supported.

Example:
Text: "Maria grabbed her umbrella as she headed out the door."
Observation: Maria took an umbrella.
Inference: Maria expects rain or bad weather.

🎯 Types of Inferences on HESI

  • Logical Conclusions: What can be reasonably concluded from the facts?
  • Author's Purpose: Why did the author write this? (inform, persuade, entertain)
  • Tone/Attitude: How does the author feel about the topic?
  • Predictions: What will likely happen next based on the evidence?
  • Character/Subject Inferences: What can we conclude about a person or thing?

🚦 Signal Phrases for Inference Questions

When you see these phrases, you're being asked to infer:

🏥 Nursing Context: Clinical Judgment

💡 Why Inference = Clinical Judgment

In nursing, inference IS clinical judgment. Every day, nurses "read between the lines" of patient presentations, vital signs, and behaviors to draw conclusions that aren't explicitly stated. A patient who is grimacing, guarding their abdomen, and refusing to eat is communicating pain—even if they say "I'm fine."

The same skills you use to infer meaning from HESI passages are the skills you'll use to infer patient needs from clinical data.

📋 Clinical Inference Applications

🩺 Assessment Inferences

Patient is diaphoretic, clutching chest, BP elevated → Infer possible cardiac event (not stated, but implied by symptoms)

📊 Lab Value Interpretation

WBC elevated, fever, productive cough → Infer respiratory infection (the diagnosis isn't stated, but the evidence points to it)

👤 Patient Communication

Patient avoids eye contact, gives one-word answers, sighs frequently → Infer emotional distress or depression

📝 Documentation Clues

"Patient found on floor, confused, bed rails down" → Infer fall occurred (the word "fall" may not appear)

🔗 The HESI-to-Bedside Connection

HESI Passage: "The patient's call light had been on for 45 minutes. When the nurse entered, the sheets were wet and the patient refused to make eye contact."

Inference: The patient likely had an incontinence episode and feels embarrassed.

Clinical Application: This same inference skill helps you recognize patient shame, prioritize emotional support, and document sensitively.

🎯 Practice: Clinical Scenario

Scenario: Mrs. Thompson, 78, was admitted for hip replacement. Her daughter visits daily but always leaves before the physician rounds. When asked about her mother's pain management, the daughter says, "Whatever the doctor thinks is best."

What can be inferred?

  • The daughter may feel intimidated by medical authority
  • There may be a communication barrier with the healthcare team
  • The daughter might benefit from being present during rounds

Notice: None of these are stated—they're inferred from the behavioral clues.
