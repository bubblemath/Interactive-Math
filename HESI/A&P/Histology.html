🧬 The Four Tissue Types

Epithelial Tissue

Covering & Lining

Covers body surfaces, lines cavities and organs, and forms glands. Cells are tightly packed with minimal extracellular matrix.

Simple Squamous Simple Cuboidal Simple Columnar Stratified Squamous Pseudostratified Transitional

Connective Tissue

Support & Connection

Supports, protects, and binds other tissues. Characterized by abundant extracellular matrix with scattered cells.

Loose (Areolar) Adipose Dense Regular Dense Irregular Cartilage Bone Blood

Muscle Tissue

Movement & Contraction

Specialized for contraction and movement. Contains contractile proteins (actin and myosin) that enable shortening.

Skeletal Cardiac Smooth

Nervous Tissue

Communication & Control

Transmits electrical signals for communication and control. Composed of neurons and supporting neuroglia cells.

Neurons Neuroglia (CNS) Neuroglia (PNS)

📍 Tissue Location Map

Tissue Locations by Type

Epithelial Tissue

Skin: Stratified squamous (protection)

Lungs: Simple squamous (gas exchange)

Intestines: Simple columnar (absorption)

Kidney tubules: Simple cuboidal (secretion)

Bladder: Transitional (stretching)

Trachea: Pseudostratified ciliated (mucus movement)

Connective Tissue

Under skin: Loose/Areolar (cushioning)

Around organs: Adipose (insulation, storage)

Tendons: Dense regular (strength)

Dermis: Dense irregular (resistance)

Joints: Cartilage (cushioning)

Skeleton: Bone (support)

Blood vessels: Blood (transport)

Muscle Tissue

Attached to bones: Skeletal (voluntary movement)

Heart wall: Cardiac (pumping blood)

Organ walls: Smooth (involuntary movement)

Blood vessels: Smooth (regulate diameter)

Digestive tract: Smooth (peristalsis)

Nervous Tissue

Brain: Neurons + neuroglia (processing)

Spinal cord: Neurons + neuroglia (relay)

Peripheral nerves: Neurons (signal transmission)

Ganglia: Neuron cell bodies (relay stations)

⚡ Structure-Function Connections

🔷 Epithelial Tissue Functions

🛡️

Protection

Stratified squamous protects against abrasion (skin, mouth, esophagus)

🔄

Absorption

Simple columnar with microvilli absorbs nutrients (small intestine)

💧

Secretion

Cuboidal and columnar cells secrete mucus, enzymes, hormones

🌬️

Filtration

Simple squamous allows rapid diffusion (lungs, blood vessels)

↔️

Stretch

Transitional epithelium stretches (bladder, ureters)

🔷 Connective Tissue Functions

🏗️

Support

Bone and cartilage provide structural framework

🔗

Binding

Dense connective tissue connects muscles to bones (tendons) and bones to bones (ligaments)

🛡️

Protection

Bone protects organs; adipose cushions

🚚

Transport

Blood transports oxygen, nutrients, wastes, hormones

🔋

Storage

Adipose stores energy; bone stores minerals

🔷 Muscle Tissue Functions

🏃

Voluntary Movement

Skeletal muscle moves the skeleton (walking, lifting)

❤️

Pump Blood

Cardiac muscle contracts rhythmically to circulate blood

🔄

Move Substances

Smooth muscle moves food (peristalsis), regulates blood flow

🌡️

Heat Production

Muscle contraction generates body heat

🔷 Nervous Tissue Functions

⚡

Signal Transmission

Neurons conduct electrical impulses rapidly

🧠

Integration

Process and interpret sensory information

🎯

Control

Coordinate body activities and responses

🛠️

Support (Neuroglia)

Protect, nourish, and insulate neurons

🔍 Tissue Detective

Use this decision tree to identify unknown tissues systematically!

Step 1: Is there a visible extracellular matrix (space between cells)?
YES - Lots of space/material between cells → Likely Connective Tissue
NO - Cells are tightly packed together → Continue to Step 2
Step 2: Are the cells elongated/fiber-like?
YES - Long, fiber-shaped cells → Likely Muscle Tissue (go to Step 2a)
NO - Cells are not elongated fibers → Continue to Step 3
Step 2a: What do you see in the muscle cells?
Striations + Multiple nuclei at periphery → Skeletal Muscle
Striations + Single central nucleus + Intercalated discs → Cardiac Muscle
No striations + Spindle-shaped + Single central nucleus → Smooth Muscle
Step 3: Do the cells have long projections (axons/dendrites)?
YES - Star-shaped cells with long projections → Nervous Tissue
NO - No long projections → Likely Epithelial Tissue (go to Step 4)
Step 4: How many layers of cells?
Single layer - All cells touch basement membrane → Simple Epithelium (go to Step 5)
Multiple layers - Stacked cells → Stratified Epithelium (go to Step 6)
Appears layered but all cells touch basement → Pseudostratified Epithelium
Step 5: What shape are the cells?
Flat/scale-like (fried egg appearance) → Simple Squamous
Cube-shaped (as tall as wide) → Simple Cuboidal
Column-shaped (taller than wide) → Simple Columnar
Step 6: What shape are the surface cells?
Flat surface cells → Stratified Squamous
Dome-shaped/rounded surface cells (can stretch) → Transitional
Connective Tissue: What's in the matrix?
Loose fibers, lots of space → Loose (Areolar) Connective
Large clear cells (fat storage) → Adipose Tissue
Parallel collagen fibers → Dense Regular Connective
Cells in lacunae, firm matrix → Cartilage
Concentric rings (osteons), hard matrix → Bone
Liquid matrix with floating cells → Blood

💡 Memory Tricks

  • 🔹 "CENT" = Four tissue types: Connective, Epithelial, Nervous, Muscle (Tissue)
  • 🔹 Epithelial naming: # of layers + cell shape (Simple Squamous = 1 layer + flat)
  • 🔹 Skeletal muscle: "Striped and Strapped" to bones, Voluntary
  • 🔹 Cardiac muscle: "Cardiac has Intercalated discs" (like ID cards for the heart)
  • 🔹 Smooth muscle: "Smooth = Slow and involuntary" (no stripes, no control)
  • 🔹 Connective tissue: "Connect the dots" - lots of matrix BETWEEN cells

⚠️ Common HESI Traps

  • ❌ Pseudostratified vs. Stratified: Pseudo LOOKS layered but ALL cells touch the basement membrane
  • ❌ Cardiac vs. Skeletal: Both are striated! Look for intercalated discs (cardiac) vs. multiple peripheral nuclei (skeletal)
  • ❌ Simple Squamous vs. Stratified Squamous: Count the layers! Simple = 1, Stratified = many
  • ❌ Cartilage vs. Bone: Both have cells in lacunae, but bone has concentric rings (osteons)
  • ❌ Blood is Connective Tissue: Yes! The plasma is the liquid matrix
