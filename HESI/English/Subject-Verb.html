📝 HESI Subject-Verb Agreement

Master the rules that make subjects and verbs work together

📋 HESI Grammar Section Overview

Subject-verb agreement is one of the most frequently tested grammar concepts on the HESI A2 exam. The rule is simple: subjects and verbs must agree in number. A singular subject needs a singular verb; a plural subject needs a plural verb. The challenge comes when sentences include "interrupters" that try to confuse you!

💡 Why Subject-Verb Agreement Matters in Nursing

Clear documentation requires grammatically correct sentences. Agreement errors can create ambiguity in patient records, potentially leading to miscommunication between healthcare providers. For example: "The patient's symptoms is improving" vs. "The patient's symptoms are improving"—the first is incorrect and unprofessional.

🎯 The Core Rule

Singular

The nurse monitors vitals.
Singular subject (no S) + Singular verb (with S)

Plural

The nurses monitor vitals.
Plural subject (with S) + Plural verb (no S)
⚠️ The "S" Rule Memory Trick

In present tense, singular subjects and plural verbs are opposites when it comes to "S":

  • Singular subject (nurse) → verb adds S (walks, monitors, checks)
  • Plural subject (nurses) → verb has no S (walk, monitor, check)

📚 Subject-Verb Agreement Reference Vault

📌 Basic Agreement

Singular subjects take singular verbs. Plural subjects take plural verbs.
✓ The patient is resting.
✓ The patients are resting.

👥 Collective Nouns

Words like staff, team, family, group, committee are usually singular when acting as one unit.
✓ The medical team is ready.
✓ The staff meets every Monday.
✗ The team are ready.

🔗 Compound Subjects with AND

Two subjects joined by AND = plural (usually).
✓ The doctor and nurse are consulting.
✗ The doctor and nurse is consulting.

⚖️ OR / NOR (Proximity Rule)

With OR, NOR, EITHER...OR, NEITHER...NOR, the verb agrees with the closest subject.
✓ Neither the doctor nor the nurses were available.
✓ Neither the nurses nor the doctor was available.

🚧 Intervening Phrases

Phrases between subject and verb don't change agreement. Ignore them!
✓ The nurse, along with the aides, is checking vitals.
✗ The nurse, along with the aides, are checking vitals.

🔄 Inverted Sentences

In sentences starting with There is/are or Here is/are, the subject comes AFTER the verb.
✓ There are three patients waiting.
✓ Here is the medication.

📋 Indefinite Pronouns Cheat Sheet

🔵 Always SINGULAR

  • • everyone, everybody, everything
  • • someone, somebody, something
  • • anyone, anybody, anything
  • • no one, nobody, nothing
  • • each, either, neither
  • • one, another, much

🟢 Always PLURAL

  • • both
  • • few
  • • many
  • • several
  • • others

🟡 DEPENDS on Context

  • • all
  • • any
  • • most
  • • none
  • • some

Look at what follows "of"

⚠️ Medical Terminology Traps
  • Data - Technically plural (datum is singular), but often used as singular
  • Criteria - Plural (criterion is singular): "The criteria are met"
  • Measles, mumps, diabetes, news - Singular despite the "s": "Measles is contagious"
  • Mathematics, physics, statistics - Singular when referring to the subject: "Statistics is required"

🎯 Common Interrupter Phrases to IGNORE

These phrases do NOT affect subject-verb agreement:

  • along with
  • as well as
  • in addition to
  • together with
  • accompanied by
  • including
  • such as
  • rather than

🏥 Nursing Documentation Spotlight

Why Agreement Matters in Healthcare

Subject-verb agreement errors in clinical documentation can undermine your professional credibility and potentially create confusion about patient status. Clear, grammatically correct charting is a hallmark of professional nursing practice.

📋 Charting Corrections

❌ Incorrect

"The patient's family members is waiting in the lobby."

Error: "Members" is plural, so the verb should be "are."

✓ Correct

"The patient's family members are waiting in the lobby."

Rule: Plural subject (members) requires plural verb (are).

❌ Incorrect

"Each of the medications were administered on time."

Error: "Each" is always singular, regardless of what follows "of."

✓ Correct

"Each of the medications was administered on time."

Rule: "Each" is singular → singular verb "was."

❌ Incorrect

"The nursing staff are attending the training session."

Error: "Staff" is a collective noun acting as one unit.

✓ Correct

"The nursing staff is attending the training session."

Rule: Collective nouns (staff, team, group) are usually singular.

❌ Incorrect

"There is multiple factors contributing to the patient's condition."

Error: The subject "factors" is plural and comes after the verb.

✓ Correct

"There are multiple factors contributing to the patient's condition."

Rule: In "There is/are" sentences, look AFTER the verb for the subject.

🎯 Quick Self-Check Strategy

The Cross-Out Method: When you see a complex sentence, mentally cross out prepositional phrases and interrupters. What remains is your subject-verb core.

Original: "The nurse, along with the medical students, monitors the patient."

Core: "The nurse monitors the patient." ✓

⚠️ Common Documentation Traps
  • "A number of" = plural ("A number of patients are waiting")
  • "The number of" = singular ("The number of patients is increasing")
  • "None" can be singular or plural depending on context
  • Amounts/measurements are usually singular ("Ten milligrams is the correct dose")
