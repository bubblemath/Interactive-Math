💪 Pronoun Power: Why This Matters

Pronoun clarity isn't just about grammar tests—it's about patient safety:

  • Shift Handoffs: "She needs her medication at 0800" — Which patient? Which medication?
  • Documentation: Vague pronouns in charts can lead to medication errors and miscommunication
  • Legal Protection: Clear documentation protects you and your patients
  • Team Communication: Ambiguous references waste time and create confusion

Bottom line: Every pronoun error on HESI is practice for preventing real-world medical errors.

🎯 The "Who vs. Whom" Shortcut

This is a high-frequency HESI topic! Use this simple replacement test:

He = WHO  |  Him = WHOM

Test it: "___ called the doctor?"

→ Try: "He called the doctor" ✓ (makes sense)

→ Answer: WHO called the doctor?

Test it: "To ___ should I give the report?"

→ Try: "Give the report to him" ✓ (makes sense)

→ Answer: To WHOM should I give the report?

🔗 Relative Pronouns Guide

WHO / WHOM / WHOSE — Use for people
"The nurse who administered the medication documented it."
THAT — Use for essential/restrictive information (no commas)
"The medication that was ordered has arrived." (specifies which medication)
WHICH — Use for non-essential/extra information (with commas)
"The medication, which was ordered yesterday, has arrived." (adds extra info)
Quick Test: Can you remove the clause without losing essential meaning?
→ Yes = use which (with commas)
→ No = use that (no commas)

🏥 Clinical Clarity Spotlight

See how pronoun errors in healthcare settings can create dangerous ambiguity—and how to prevent them.

📋 Charting Corrections

❌ Poor Documentation

"The nurse told the patient that she needed to take her medication before she left."
Problem: Who needs to take medication? The nurse or the patient? Who is leaving? This ambiguity could lead to a missed dose or medication error.

✅ Clear Documentation

"The nurse instructed Mrs. Johnson to take her prescribed medication before discharge."
Why it works: Specific names replace ambiguous pronouns. The action and recipient are crystal clear.

❌ Poor Shift Report

"Dr. Smith spoke with the patient's family member. He said he was concerned about his condition."
Problem: Who is concerned—the doctor or the family member? Whose condition? This could affect care decisions.

✅ Clear Shift Report

"Dr. Smith spoke with the patient's son. The son expressed concern about his father's declining appetite."
Why it works: Each person is clearly identified. The concern and its subject are unambiguous.

❌ Dangerous Ambiguity

"Give it to them in Room 302 before they leave."
Problem: What is "it"? Who are "they"? This vague instruction could result in wrong medication, wrong patient, or wrong timing.

✅ Safe Communication

"Administer the 10mg Lisinopril to Mr. Chen in Room 302 before his 1400 discharge."
Why it works: Specific medication, dose, patient name, room, and timing. No room for error.

🌈 Gender-Inclusive Language in Clinical Settings

Modern healthcare embraces inclusive language. Here's how to use singular "they/them" professionally:

Traditional: "Each patient should bring his or her insurance card."
Inclusive: "Each patient should bring their insurance card."
✓ Accepted in modern professional and academic writing
When gender is unknown: "The patient in Room 204 requested their discharge papers."
✓ Appropriate when you haven't confirmed the patient's pronouns
Respecting patient identity: "Alex uses they/them pronouns. They reported their pain level as 4/10."
✓ Document and use patients' stated pronouns

💡 HESI Note: While HESI traditionally tests "his or her" as correct, many nursing programs now accept singular "they." Know your program's preference, but understand both are grammatically defensible.
