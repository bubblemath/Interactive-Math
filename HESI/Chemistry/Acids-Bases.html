📋 HESI Chemistry: Acids and Bases Overview

Why Acids and Bases Matter

Understanding acids and bases is critical for nursing practice. This knowledge helps you comprehend:

  • Blood pH Balance: Recognizing acidosis and alkalosis
  • Medication Actions: How antacids neutralize stomach acid
  • IV Fluid Management: Understanding solution pH compatibility
  • Lab Values: Interpreting arterial blood gas (ABG) results

Clinical Connection: Blood pH

Normal Blood pH Range: 7.35 - 7.45

6.8 (Death) ← Acidosis Normal Alkalosis → 7.8 (Death)
  • Acidosis (pH < 7.35): Too much acid or too little base in blood
  • Alkalosis (pH > 7.45): Too much base or too little acid in blood
  • Causes: Respiratory (CO₂ levels) or Metabolic (HCO₃⁻ levels)

📖 Acid-Base Reference Vault

🔬 Definitions: Two Theories

Arrhenius Theory

Acid: Produces H⁺ ions in water

Base: Produces OH⁻ ions in water

Limited to aqueous (water) solutions

Brønsted-Lowry Theory

Acid: Proton (H⁺) DONOR

Base: Proton (H⁺) ACCEPTOR

More broadly applicable

🎯 Memory Trick: B-A-D

B-A-D

Bases Accept, Acids Donate (protons/H⁺)

📊 The pH Scale

0 7 14
← ACIDIC (High H⁺) NEUTRAL BASIC/ALKALINE (Low H⁺) →

Common Pitfall: The Inverse Relationship

Lower pH = MORE acidic = HIGHER [H⁺] concentration

Higher pH = MORE basic = LOWER [H⁺] concentration

Remember: pH and [H⁺] move in OPPOSITE directions!

💪 Strong vs. Weak Acids & Bases

🔴 Strong Acids (100% dissociate)

  • HCl - Hydrochloric acid
  • HNO₃ - Nitric acid
  • H₂SO₄ - Sulfuric acid
  • HBr - Hydrobromic acid
  • HI - Hydroiodic acid
  • HClO₄ - Perchloric acid

🔵 Strong Bases (100% dissociate)

  • NaOH - Sodium hydroxide
  • KOH - Potassium hydroxide
  • Ca(OH)₂ - Calcium hydroxide
  • Ba(OH)₂ - Barium hydroxide
  • LiOH - Lithium hydroxide

Weak Acids & Bases

Only partially dissociate in water:

  • Weak Acids: CH₃COOH (acetic/vinegar), H₂CO₃ (carbonic), H₃PO₄ (phosphoric)
  • Weak Bases: NH₃ (ammonia), NaHCO₃ (baking soda)

Why It Matters: pH Math on HESI

The HESI tests your understanding of the logarithmic relationship:

  • Each pH unit = 10× change in [H⁺] concentration
  • pH 3 is 10 times more acidic than pH 4
  • pH 3 is 100 times more acidic than pH 5
  • If [H⁺] = 10⁻⁴ M, then pH = 4

🔢 pH Calculation Practice

If [H⁺] = 10⁻ˣ, then pH = x

Example 1:

[H⁺] = 10⁻³ M

pH = 3 (Acidic)

Example 2:

[H⁺] = 10⁻⁹ M

pH = 9 (Basic)

Example 3:

[H⁺] = 10⁻⁷ M

pH = 7 (Neutral)

Common Pitfall: Powers of 10

A solution with pH 2 is NOT twice as acidic as pH 4!

It's actually 100 times (10²) more acidic because each pH unit = 10× difference.

⚖️ Neutralization Reactions

What is Neutralization?

When an acid and base react, they neutralize each other to form a salt and water.

Acid + Base → Salt + Water

📝 Example Reactions

HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Hydrochloric acid + Sodium hydroxide → Table salt + Water

H₂SO₄ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₄ + 2H₂O

Sulfuric acid + Sodium hydroxide → Sodium sulfate + Water

Clinical Connection: Antacids

Antacids work through neutralization!

Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O

Milk of Magnesia (base) neutralizes excess stomach acid (HCl), relieving heartburn and indigestion.

🛡️ Buffer Systems

What is a Buffer?

A buffer is a solution that resists changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. Buffers contain:

  • A weak acid and its conjugate base, OR
  • A weak base and its conjugate acid

🩸 The Bicarbonate Buffer System

Clinical Connection: Blood pH Regulation

Your blood uses the bicarbonate buffer system to maintain pH between 7.35-7.45:

H₂CO₃ ⇌ H⁺ + HCO₃⁻
  • If blood becomes too acidic: HCO₃⁻ (bicarbonate) absorbs excess H⁺
  • If blood becomes too basic: H₂CO₃ (carbonic acid) releases H⁺
  • Lungs help: Exhaling CO₂ removes acid from the body
  • Kidneys help: Excrete or retain H⁺ and HCO₃⁻

Why Buffers Matter in Nursing

  • IV Fluids: Many are buffered to prevent pH shock
  • Blood Transfusions: Blood products contain buffers
  • ABG Interpretation: Understanding HCO₃⁻ levels helps diagnose acid-base disorders
  • Medication Stability: Some drugs require buffered solutions

Common Pitfall: Buffer Capacity

Buffers can only resist pH changes up to a point. If too much acid or base is added, the buffer becomes overwhelmed and pH will change dramatically.

This is why severe acidosis or alkalosis can be life-threatening—the body's buffers are overwhelmed!
