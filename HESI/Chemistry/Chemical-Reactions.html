📚 Reaction Reference Vault

The Big 5 Reaction Types

🔨 Synthesis (Combination)

A + B → AB

Pattern: Two or more substances combine to form ONE product

2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O

Think: "Building something new"

💥 Decomposition

AB → A + B

Pattern: ONE compound breaks down into two or more simpler substances

2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂

Think: "Breaking apart"

🔄 Single Replacement

A + BC → AC + B

Pattern: One element replaces another in a compound

Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂

Think: "Trading places (one swap)"

🔀 Double Replacement

AB + CD → AD + CB

Pattern: Two compounds exchange partners

NaCl + AgNO₃ → NaNO₃ + AgCl↓

Think: "Partner swap (two swaps)"

🔥 Combustion

Fuel + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O

Pattern: Substance reacts with oxygen, releases energy

CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O

Think: "Burning with oxygen"

⚖️ Balancing Equations: The Rules

Law of Conservation of Mass

Matter cannot be created or destroyed—atoms on the reactant side must equal atoms on the product side.

Common Pitfall: Coefficients vs. Subscripts

Coefficients (big numbers in front): ✅ CAN change to balance

Subscripts (small numbers below): ❌ NEVER change—they define the compound!

Example: 2H₂O means 2 molecules of water. Changing the subscript would create a different substance!

🔍 Signs of a Chemical Reaction

🎨 Color Change

A new substance with different properties forms

💨 Gas Production

Bubbles or fizzing indicate gas release

⬇️ Precipitate Formation

Solid forms from mixing two solutions

🌡️ Temperature Change

Heat released (exothermic) or absorbed (endothermic)

Clinical Connection: Antacid Reactions

When a patient takes an antacid (like Mg(OH)₂), it undergoes a double replacement reaction with stomach acid (HCl):

Mg(OH)₂ + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + 2H₂O

This neutralization reaction reduces stomach acidity and relieves heartburn!

💡 Balancing Tips

  1. Count atoms on each side first
  2. Balance metals first, then nonmetals
  3. Balance hydrogen and oxygen last
  4. Use the smallest whole number coefficients
  5. Double-check your work!

Remember: Only Change Coefficients!

The coefficient goes in front of the entire formula and multiplies ALL atoms in that molecule.

Example: 2H₂O = 4 hydrogen atoms + 2 oxygen atoms

🔍 Pattern Recognition Tips

  • Synthesis: Multiple reactants → ONE product
  • Decomposition: ONE reactant → Multiple products
  • Single Replacement: Element + Compound → Different element + Different compound
  • Double Replacement: Two compounds swap ions (often forms precipitate)
  • Combustion: Contains O₂ as reactant, produces CO₂ and H₂O

⚡ Oxidation-Reduction (Redox)

What is Redox?

Redox reactions involve the transfer of electrons between substances. One substance loses electrons (oxidation) while another gains electrons (reduction).

OIL RIG

Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons)

Reduction Is Gain (of electrons)

OXIDATION

Loses electrons

Charge becomes MORE positive

Example: Na → Na⁺ + e⁻

REDUCTION

Gains electrons

Charge becomes MORE negative

Example: Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻

Key Redox Concepts

🔋 Oxidizing Agent

The substance that causes oxidation in another substance

It gains electrons (is reduced itself)

⚡ Reducing Agent

The substance that causes reduction in another substance

It loses electrons (is oxidized itself)

Example Redox Reaction

2Na + Cl₂ → 2NaCl

Sodium (Na)

Na → Na⁺ + e⁻

OXIDIZED (loses electron)

Reducing agent

Chlorine (Cl)

Cl + e⁻ → Cl⁻

REDUCED (gains electron)

Oxidizing agent

Clinical Connection: Cellular Respiration

Cellular respiration is a redox reaction!

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + Energy
  • Glucose (C₆H₁₂O₆) is OXIDIZED (loses electrons)
  • Oxygen (O₂) is REDUCED (gains electrons)
  • This releases the energy your cells need to function!

Common Pitfall: Confusing Oxidation and Reduction

Remember: LEO the lion says GER

Lose Electrons = Oxidation

Gain Electrons = Reduction

💧 Simplified Solubility Rules

Why Solubility Matters

Solubility rules help you predict whether a precipitate (solid) will form when two solutions are mixed. This is crucial for:

  • Predicting products in double replacement reactions
  • Understanding IV medication compatibility
  • Recognizing when chemical reactions occur

The "Always Soluble" Rule: NAG SAG

NAG SAG

These ions form compounds that are ALWAYS SOLUBLE in water

✅ NAG - Always Soluble Cations

Na⁺ (Sodium) NH₄⁺ (Ammonium) K⁺ (Potassium)

Group 1 metals + Ammonium = Always dissolve!

✅ SAG - Always Soluble Anions

NO₃⁻ (Nitrate) CH₃COO⁻ (Acetate) ClO₃⁻ (Chlorate)

Nitrates, Acetates, and most Chlorates = Always dissolve!

Common Precipitates: The "Troublemakers"

❌ Usually INSOLUBLE (Form Precipitates)

These ions often form solids when combined:

Ag⁺ (Silver) Pb²⁺ (Lead) Hg₂²⁺ (Mercury I)

Common precipitates:

  • AgCl - Silver chloride (white precipitate)
  • PbI₂ - Lead iodide (yellow precipitate)
  • BaSO₄ - Barium sulfate (white precipitate)

Quick Reference Table

Ion Type Solubility Exceptions
Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺ Always Soluble None!
NO₃⁻ (Nitrates) Always Soluble None!
Cl⁻, Br⁻, I⁻ (Halides) Usually Soluble Ag⁺, Pb²⁺, Hg₂²⁺
SO₄²⁻ (Sulfates) Usually Soluble Ba²⁺, Pb²⁺, Ca²⁺
OH⁻ (Hydroxides) Usually Insoluble Na⁺, K⁺, Ba²⁺
CO₃²⁻, PO₄³⁻ Usually Insoluble Na⁺, K⁺, NH₄⁺

Clinical Connection: IV Compatibility

Nurses must check IV medication compatibility because mixing incompatible drugs can form precipitates that:

  • Block IV lines
  • Cause emboli if injected
  • Reduce medication effectiveness

Example: Calcium gluconate + Sodium bicarbonate → Calcium carbonate precipitate (CaCO₃)↓
