📋 HESI Chemistry Exam Overview

Why the Periodic Table Matters

The Periodic Table is the "map" of chemistry—it organizes all elements and reveals patterns in their properties. For nursing, understanding the periodic table helps you comprehend:

  • Electrolyte imbalances (Na⁺, K⁺, Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺)
  • Medication interactions involving metals
  • Why certain elements are toxic vs. essential
  • Chemical bonding in biological molecules

Common Pitfall: Atomic Number vs. Atomic Mass

Atomic Number = Number of protons (whole number, defines the element)

Atomic Mass = Weighted average of isotopes (decimal number)

Remember: The smaller whole number is ALWAYS the atomic number!

📊 Groups (Columns)

Vertical columns numbered 1-18

  • Elements in same group have similar properties
  • Same number of valence electrons
  • Group number often indicates valence electrons (Groups 1-2, 13-18)

📏 Periods (Rows)

Horizontal rows numbered 1-7

  • Period number = number of electron shells
  • Properties change gradually across a period
  • Elements get smaller left to right

🔬 The Staircase Rule

A "staircase" line separates metals from nonmetals:

  • Left of staircase: Metals
  • Right of staircase: Nonmetals
  • Along the staircase: Metalloids (B, Si, Ge, As, Sb, Te)

Clinical Connection: The "Big Four" Electrolytes

These elements from Groups 1 and 2 are critical in nursing:

  • Sodium (Na⁺): Fluid balance, nerve impulses
  • Potassium (K⁺): Heart rhythm, muscle contraction
  • Calcium (Ca²⁺): Bone health, blood clotting, muscle function
  • Magnesium (Mg²⁺): Enzyme function, nerve transmission

Notice: Na and K are in Group 1 (form +1 ions), Ca and Mg are in Group 2 (form +2 ions)!

Common Pitfall: Trend Direction Confusion

Memory Trick: Electronegativity and Ionization Energy both INCREASE toward Fluorine (top-right corner, excluding noble gases).

Atomic Radius is the OPPOSITE—it INCREASES toward Francium (bottom-left corner).

Clinical Connection: Why Trends Matter in Nursing

  • Electronegativity: Explains why oxygen is so good at "grabbing" electrons in cellular respiration
  • Ionization Energy: Why alkali metals (Na, K) easily lose electrons to form the ions your body needs
  • Atomic Radius: Affects how ions move through cell membranes and channels

🔥 Reactivity Patterns

Understanding WHY elements react helps you predict their behavior:

⚡ Alkali Metals (Group 1)

Reactivity: VERY HIGH (increases down the group)

Why? Only 1 valence electron—easy to lose!

  • React violently with water
  • Must be stored in oil
  • Form +1 ions (Na⁺, K⁺)
  • Soft enough to cut with a knife
HESI Focus: Na and K are the most commonly tested

🧂 Halogens (Group 17)

Reactivity: VERY HIGH (decreases down the group)

Why? Need only 1 electron to complete octet!

  • React with metals to form salts
  • Exist as diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂)
  • Form -1 ions (Cl⁻, Br⁻)
  • Fluorine is most reactive element
HESI Focus: Chlorine (Cl) in body fluids

💤 Noble Gases (Group 18)

Reactivity: NONE (inert/unreactive)

Why? Full valence shell—already stable!

  • Do not form compounds naturally
  • Exist as single atoms (monatomic)
  • Used in lighting (neon signs)
  • Helium used in medical imaging
HESI Focus: Why they don't react

🦴 Alkaline Earth Metals (Group 2)

Reactivity: HIGH (but less than alkali metals)

Why? 2 valence electrons to lose

  • React with water (less violently)
  • Form +2 ions (Ca²⁺, Mg²⁺)
  • Essential for bone structure
  • Important in muscle function
HESI Focus: Ca and Mg in body functions

⚖️ Metals vs. Nonmetals

🔩 Metals

  • Luster: Shiny appearance
  • Conductivity: Good conductors of heat/electricity
  • Malleability: Can be hammered into sheets
  • Ductility: Can be drawn into wires
  • State: Solid at room temp (except Hg)
  • Electrons: LOSE electrons → form cations (+)

💨 Nonmetals

  • Luster: Dull appearance
  • Conductivity: Poor conductors (insulators)
  • Malleability: Brittle when solid
  • Ductility: Cannot be drawn into wires
  • State: Various states at room temp
  • Electrons: GAIN electrons → form anions (-)

Common Pitfall: Hydrogen's Location

Hydrogen is placed in Group 1 but is NOT a metal!

It's a nonmetal that can lose OR gain 1 electron depending on the reaction.

Clinical Connection: Electrolyte Replacement

When patients have electrolyte imbalances, understanding periodic groups helps:

  • Hypokalemia (low K⁺): K is in Group 1, forms +1 ion, critical for heart rhythm
  • Hypocalcemia (low Ca²⁺): Ca is in Group 2, forms +2 ion, needed for muscle contraction
  • Hypernatremia (high Na⁺): Na is in Group 1, affects fluid balance
