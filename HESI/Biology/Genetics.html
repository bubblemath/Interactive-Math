💡 Test-Taking Tips

  • Read the ENTIRE question first
  • Underline key terms (dominant, carrier)
  • Draw Punnett squares on scratch paper
  • Check if asking for genotype OR phenotype
  • Remember: carriers are heterozygous
  • X-linked = usually affects males more

⚠️ Common Pitfalls to Avoid

  • ❌ Confusing genotype (genetic makeup: AA, Aa, aa) with phenotype (observable trait: tall, short)
  • ❌ Forgetting that carriers are heterozygous (Aa) and don't show the recessive trait
  • ❌ Mixing up codominance (both alleles fully expressed) with incomplete dominance (blended phenotype)
  • ❌ Forgetting males have only one X chromosome for sex-linked traits

📖 Genetics Reference Vault

Allele
A variant form of a gene. Each person inherits two alleles for each gene, one from each parent.
Genotype
The genetic makeup of an organism (e.g., AA, Aa, aa). Represents the actual alleles present.
Phenotype
The observable physical characteristics of an organism (e.g., brown eyes, tall height).
Homozygous
Having two identical alleles for a trait (AA = homozygous dominant, aa = homozygous recessive).
Heterozygous
Having two different alleles for a trait (Aa). Also called a "carrier" for recessive disorders.
Dominant Allele
An allele that is expressed when present (represented by capital letter). Only one copy needed to show trait.
Recessive Allele
An allele only expressed when homozygous (aa). Represented by lowercase letter. "Hidden" in carriers.
Carrier
A heterozygous individual (Aa) who carries one copy of a recessive allele but doesn't show the trait.
Punnett Square
A diagram used to predict the genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a genetic cross.
Pedigree
A family tree diagram showing the inheritance of a trait across multiple generations.

⚠️ Common Mistake

Don't confuse DNA and RNA base pairing! Remember: DNA = A-T, RNA = A-U. The HESI often tests this distinction.

🔵 Mendelian (Complete) Dominance

One allele completely masks the other. Heterozygotes show the dominant phenotype.

Example: Brown eyes (B) dominant over blue (b)
BB = Brown, Bb = Brown, bb = Blue

🟡 Incomplete Dominance

Neither allele is completely dominant. Heterozygotes show a blended intermediate phenotype.

Example: Snapdragon flowers
RR = Red, Rr = Pink, rr = White

🔴 Codominance

Both alleles are fully expressed simultaneously. No blending occurs.

Example: ABO Blood Types
IAIB = Type AB (both A and B antigens)

🟣 Sex-Linked (X-Linked)

Genes located on the X chromosome. Males (XY) are more often affected since they have only one X.

Example: Color blindness, Hemophilia
XcY = Affected male, XCXc = Carrier female

🔍 How to Read a Pedigree

Pedigrees show inheritance patterns across generations. Squares = males, Circles = females. Filled shapes = affected individuals. Half-filled = carriers (for recessive traits).

💡 Pattern Recognition Tips

  • • Autosomal Dominant: Affected individuals in every generation; affected parent → affected child
  • • Autosomal Recessive: Can skip generations; two unaffected parents can have affected child
  • • X-Linked Recessive: More males affected; affected males have carrier mothers

🏥 Genetic Disorders Reference

📋 Commonly Tested Genetic Disorders

The HESI frequently tests your knowledge of these genetic disorders, their inheritance patterns, and clinical significance for nursing practice.

Sickle Cell Anemia

Autosomal Recessive
Key Characteristics
  • Abnormal hemoglobin (HbS)
  • Red blood cells become sickle-shaped
  • Causes blockages in blood vessels
Symptoms
  • Pain crises
  • Anemia and fatigue
  • Increased infection risk
🏥 Why It Matters

Carriers (HbAS) have sickle cell trait and are resistant to malaria. Common in African, Mediterranean, and Middle Eastern populations.

Cystic Fibrosis

Autosomal Recessive
Key Characteristics
  • Defective CFTR protein
  • Thick, sticky mucus production
  • Affects lungs and digestive system
Symptoms
  • Chronic lung infections
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Poor growth and weight gain
🏥 Why It Matters

Most common fatal genetic disease in Caucasians. 1 in 25 people of European descent are carriers.

Huntington's Disease

Autosomal Dominant
Key Characteristics
  • CAG trinucleotide repeat expansion
  • Progressive neurodegeneration
  • Onset typically 30-50 years old
Symptoms
  • Involuntary movements (chorea)
  • Cognitive decline
  • Psychiatric symptoms
🏥 Why It Matters

Only ONE copy of the mutant allele needed. 50% chance of passing to offspring. Genetic testing available but raises ethical considerations.

Tay-Sachs Disease

Autosomal Recessive
Key Characteristics
  • Deficiency of hexosaminidase A enzyme
  • Lipid accumulation in brain
  • Progressive neurological deterioration
Symptoms
  • Developmental regression
  • Seizures
  • Cherry-red spot on retina
🏥 Why It Matters

Higher frequency in Ashkenazi Jewish population. Carrier screening recommended. Usually fatal by age 4-5.

Hemophilia A

X-Linked Recessive
Key Characteristics
  • Deficiency of clotting factor VIII
  • Impaired blood clotting
  • Primarily affects males
Symptoms
  • Prolonged bleeding
  • Easy bruising
  • Joint bleeding and damage
🏥 Why It Matters

Females are usually carriers (XHXh). Affected males inherit from carrier mothers. Treatment: factor VIII replacement.

Color Blindness

X-Linked Recessive
Key Characteristics
  • Defective cone photoreceptors
  • Red-green most common type
  • Affects ~8% of males
Symptoms
  • Difficulty distinguishing colors
  • Red-green confusion most common
  • Usually not progressive
🏥 Why It Matters

Classic example of X-linked inheritance on HESI. Remember: affected father + carrier mother = 50% affected sons, 50% carrier daughters.
