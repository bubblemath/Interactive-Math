Reading statistical claims · finding the broken link

Most misleading statistics are true numbers answering a different question.

The figure in the headline is usually real. What breaks is the chain connecting it to the conclusion: who was actually measured, what was actually recorded, what was done with the numbers, and what got claimed at the end. So "is this number right?" is the wrong opening question. The right one is which link gave way — and sometimes, importantly, none of them did.

How it works

Walk the chain, one link at a time

Twelve rungs, each targeting one place a claim can break — then two rungs where the flaw could be anywhere. Every claim is fictional. Crucially, about one claim in four is perfectly sound, and "the claim is well supported" is always on the menu: the goal is to read carefully, not to manufacture objections.

Why it matters

The last skill you'll still be using in ten years

Claims arrive without workings

Nobody hands you the raw data. You get a sentence and a number, and you have to judge what the sentence can support — which is exactly what these rungs rehearse.

Skepticism needs aim

"I don't trust statistics" is not a skill. Naming the specific broken link is, because it tells you what evidence would actually settle the question.

Being fair is part of it

Some claims are well supported. Recognizing those matters as much as spotting the bad ones — otherwise doubt becomes just another reflex.

A statistic is the end of a long chain, and every link is a place where care was either taken or skipped. Who was measured, what was measured, what was done, what was claimed. Walk those four questions in order and you'll find almost every problem worth finding — and, just as often, you'll find a claim that stands up perfectly well and deserves to be believed.
