Discrete distributions · multiply across, add down

The probabilities are the weights.

A distribution is a table, and every question this lesson asks is answered the same way: multiply across each column, then add down. That is the mean. That is the variance. That is the expected payback on a raffle ticket. Nothing else is happening — but none of it is legal until ΣP(x) = 1.

The one question

Before any formula: does the column add to 1? If a table is not a legal distribution, no amount of correct arithmetic afterwards can rescue it.

The formula, and its two relatives
E(X) = Σ[ x · P(x) ]multiply across  ×  add down

The variance is the same move on a new column: σ² = Σ[(x − μ)² · P(x)] — replace each x by its squared distance from the mean, weight it by P(x), add. And the expected net gain on a game is the same move again, once you bake the cost into the x-values. One habit, three formulas. The only thing that ever changes is what you put in the first row.

✋

The cost is paid whether you win or lose. A $2 ticket that pays $50 is a net gain of $48, not $50 — and "you win nothing" is still an outcome, worth −$2, that must appear in the table. Students lose more marks to a missing losing row than to any arithmetic in this lesson.

Why expected value matters

The number that prices everything

It is how risk is sold

Insurance premiums, lottery tickets, casino floors, warranty plans. Every one of them is priced so that E(X) lands slightly in the house's favour — and stays there, forever.

Fair has a definition

E(X) = 0. Not "everyone has a chance", not "someone wins eventually" — the expected net gain is exactly zero. Anything else has an edge, and the edge has an owner.

It is not a prediction

E(X) is a long-run average. It need not be a value X can even take: a household cannot contain 2.3 children, yet 2.3 is a perfectly good expected value.

A probability distribution is the first object in this course that is genuinely useful rather than merely descriptive. It lets you answer a question no amount of data summarising can touch: given the odds, what is this worth? That is the question behind every insurance policy ever written, every bet ever taken, and every decision made under uncertainty — which is to say, every decision.
