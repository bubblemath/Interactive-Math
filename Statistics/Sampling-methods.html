📊 Sampling & Experimental Design

Interactive Learning Experience

📚 Key Terms & Definitions

Population The entire group of individuals that we want information about.
Census A complete count of the population.
Sample A part of the population that we actually examine to gather information.
Sampling Design The method used to choose the sample from the population.
Sampling Frame A list of every individual in the population.
Bias A systematic error in measuring the estimate that favors certain outcomes.

🎲 Types of Sampling Designs

Simple Random Sample (SRS) Every individual has an equal chance of being selected; every set of n individuals has an equal chance of being selected.
Stratified Random Sample Population is divided into homogeneous groups (strata), then SRS is pulled from each stratum.
Systematic Random Sample Select sample by following a systematic approach; randomly select where to begin.
Cluster Sample Based on location; randomly pick a location and sample all individuals there.

⚠️ Sources of Bias

Voluntary Response Bias People choose to respond; usually only those with strong opinions participate.
Convenience Sampling Asking people who are easy to ask; produces biased results.
Undercoverage Some groups of the population are left out of the sampling process.
Nonresponse Bias Occurs when individuals chosen for the sample can't be contacted or refuse to cooperate.
Response Bias Behavior of respondent or interviewer causes bias; wrong answers given.
Question Wording Bias Wording can influence answers; connotation of words or technical language affects responses.

🔬 Experimental Design Terms

Observational Study Measures characteristics without attempting to influence variables.
Cross-sectional Study Collecting data about individuals at a current point in time.
Retrospective Study Looks back to compare how frequently exposure to a risk factor is present.
Prospective Study Looks forward to see if a certain outcome will happen.
Designed Experiment Actively imposes treatment to observe the response.
Experimental Unit The single individual to which treatments are assigned.
Factor The explanatory variable in an experiment.
Level A specific value for the factor.
Response Variable What you measure in the experiment.
Treatment A specific experimental condition applied to the units.
Control Group A group used to compare the factor against; can be a placebo or current standard.
Placebo A "dummy" treatment that has no physical effect.
Blinding Method used so units do not know which treatment they are getting.
Double-Blind Neither the units nor the evaluator know which treatment a subject received.
Confounding Variable Its effect on the response cannot be separated from the effects of the explanatory variable.

🏰 A FAIRY TALE 🏰

While strolling through a glen, a giddy English girl tripped on a rather large, almost gigantic frog. The girl staggered but regained her footing and was about to go on when the frog began to speak and gesticulate to gain the girl's attention.

"I have not always been a frog," he croaked. The frog's green coloring seemed to glow brightly as he continued. "I was once a gracious knight. A gentleman called Gallant George Grenville, but was changed into this ghastly frog you now see by an ungodly, magical genie. The spell can only be broken if I gain a girl's good graces and spend a night in her garden."

The agog girl was skeptical, of course. She gazed at the frog's pleading eyes and soon her doubts gave way to her giddy nature. Giggling, she decided to grant the frog's wish and took him home straightway, putting him by her garden gate.

That night the girl slept grandly and sure enough, when she awoke the following morning, there alongside her garden gate was the gracious knight, George Grenville.

Well, strangely enough, for a long, long time the girl's mother did not believe that story.

🤔 Reflection Questions

  1. Did your count match the actual count? Why or why not?
  2. Should everyone in class have the same count? In theory yes, but in practice, human error makes perfect census counts difficult!
  3. What does this tell you about a Census? Even when we try to count EVERYTHING, errors occur. Censuses are time-consuming, expensive, and still prone to mistakes.
  4. When might sampling be better than a census? When the population is large, when counting is destructive, when time/money is limited, or when perfect accuracy isn't required.
