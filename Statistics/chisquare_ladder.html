Chi-square tests · Lesson 24

How far do the counts stray from what you expected?

Every chi-square test is the same idea: under H₀, work out the counts you'd expect in each category, then measure how far the observed counts drift from them. That total gap, χ² = Σ(O−E)²/E, is one right-skewed number. Three tests share this one machine — goodness of fit, independence, and homogeneity — and knowing which question you're asking is most of the skill.

Expected counts rule

Every expected count must be at least 5. Chi-square is upper-tailed: a large χ² (counts far from expected) is the evidence against H₀.

The three tests
Goodness of fit
One variable, one sample, vs. a claimed distribution. E = n × claimed proportion. df = k − 1.
Independence
One sample, cross-classified by two variables. E = row×col/total. df = (r−1)(c−1).
Homogeneity
Several independent samples, one variable. Same math as independence — only the hypotheses change.
📐

Independence and homogeneity use the exact same table math. Same expected counts, same df, same χ². The only thing that tells them apart is the design: independence takes one sample and splits it by two variables; homogeneity takes several separate samples and compares one variable across them. Read how the data was collected, and the hypotheses follow.

Why it matters

The test for "does the pattern hold?"

Counts, not averages

When the data is categories — colors, preferences, outcomes — there's no mean to compare. Chi-square is how you ask whether the pattern of counts matches a claim or links two traits.

Expected is the whole idea

The test lives or dies on the expected counts: what H₀ predicts each cell should hold. Get those right and the gap tells the story; get them wrong and the test is meaningless.

Design names the test

Independence or homogeneity isn't a math question — it's about how the data was sampled. The same table can belong to either, depending on where the numbers came from.

Lesson 24 is one machine and one habit: build the expected counts under H₀, measure the gap, and read a right-skewed number. Decide which of the three questions you're asking, state the hypotheses in context, confirm every expected count is at least 5, and let the calculator turn observed-minus-expected into a p-value — then say what that means about the categories in front of you.
