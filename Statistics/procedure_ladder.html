Every test is easy once you know which one it is.

You can already run all nine procedures. The trouble on a cumulative exam is that the problem never names the test — it just describes a study and waits. So this ladder drills the reading, not the arithmetic: what kind of data, how many samples, how were they collected? Answer those three questions and the procedure names itself.

How it works

Read the study, walk the tree, name the test

Eleven rungs walk the tree one fork at a time, then mix every procedure together. There is no computing here at all — no p-values, no test statistics. The entire skill is identification: which procedure, which hypotheses, which conditions.

01

Three questions

Categorical or quantitative? One sample, two, or several? Independent or paired? Every procedure in the course sits at the end of one path through those forks.

Watch the near-misses

Some pairs are genuinely easy to confuse — two-proportion z against chi-square homogeneity, paired against two-sample. One rung does nothing but those.

🧭

The data type is the first and biggest fork. Ask what a single observation looks like. If it's a measured number — a time, a price, a score — you're on the means side. If it's a category the individual falls into — a color, a preference, yes or no — you're on the categorical side, and the question becomes whether you're comparing one percentage or a whole distribution of categories.

Why it matters

The skill the exam actually tests

Real problems don't come labeled

A chapter's homework tells you which test to run because of where it sits in the book. A cumulative exam — and every study you'll ever read — does not.

Setup errors outrank math errors

Running the wrong procedure flawlessly still gives the wrong answer. Naming the test correctly is worth more than every calculation that follows it.

It ties the course together

Seeing all nine side by side shows what they share: a null that says "nothing is going on," a statistic measuring surprise, and a p-value you compare to α.

This is the capstone habit: before computing anything, describe the study out loud. What is one observation — a number or a category? How many samples were taken, and were they linked? Is a benchmark being claimed, or two groups compared? Answer those and the procedure, its hypotheses, and its conditions all follow. The nine tests aren't nine unrelated recipes; they're one idea pointed at nine different designs.
