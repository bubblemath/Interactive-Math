Sample proportions · does it earn a normal curve?

A proportion has to earn its curve.

A sample mean got a normal curve almost for free. A sample proportion does not: it only turns normal when you expect at least 10 successes and 10 failures — np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10. Pass that check and everything is the sampling-distribution machinery you already know, running on a new spread: σp̂ = √[p(1−p)/n].

The move, and the toll you pay first
σp̂ = √[ p(1−p) / n ]   →   z = (p̂ − p) / σp̂check np ≥ 10 and n(1−p) ≥ 10  ·  then standardize

The center is the population proportion: μp̂ = p. The spread is √[p(1−p)/n] — largest when p is near 0.5, and always shrinking as n grows. Once the conditions pass, every question is the mean ladder again: P(p̂ > k) = 1 − Φ(z), a middle is a subtraction, and the inverse hands you the area and asks for the cutoff, p̂ = p + z·σp̂.

✋

Both conditions, on p — not just one. A rare event with a big sample can pass np ≥ 10 and fail n(1−p) ≥ 10, or the reverse. Check both. Note that 1 − p is the failure proportion (the proportion you'd call p̂ for "not a success"). Everything is done in decimals: a scenario that says 12% means p = 0.12, and every answer is a decimal rounded to 4 places, with z rounded to 2 dp before the table — exactly as in the other CLT ladders.

Why sample proportions matter

The math behind every poll

It is what "margin of error" means

Every poll you have ever read reports a proportion and a margin. That margin is built from √[p(1−p)/n] — the same standard error you are learning to compute right now.

Rare events break the curve

The success/failure check is not busywork. Sample a 1-in-1000 defect with n = 200 and you expect 0.2 defects — no bell curve can describe that, and the normal approximation quietly lies. The condition is what stops it.

Spread peaks at a coin flip

p(1−p) is largest at p = 0.5. A 50/50 split is the hardest thing to pin down, and a landslide is the easiest — which is exactly why close elections need bigger samples.

This is the last of the sampling-distribution lessons, and it completes the pattern. Means, then proportions: two different statistics, but the same deep fact underneath — take a sample, and the thing you compute from it has a predictable normal distribution centered on the truth, with a spread that shrinks as the sample grows. That single idea, that a sample statistic is itself a random variable with a known curve, is the entire foundation of statistical inference. Every confidence interval and every hypothesis test in the rest of the course is built directly on top of the two standard errors you now know: σ/√n for a mean, and √[p(1−p)/n] for a proportion.
