A p-value answers one question — and it isn't the one you want.

It answers: if nothing were really going on, how surprising would data like this be? That's it. It does not tell you the probability that nothing is going on, it does not tell you the result was "3% luck," and it says nothing at all about whether the effect is big enough to care about. Nearly every misreading of a p-value comes from quietly flipping that conditional around — the same reversal you met in conditional probability, now with real consequences.

Mind the direction

p = P(data this extreme given H₀ true). It is not P(H₀ true given the data). Reversing a conditional changes everything.

03

Small p ≠ big effect

A p-value reflects evidence that an effect exists. How large it is, and whether that matters, is a separate question entirely.

What it does and doesn't say
A small p-value does say
  • Data like this would be unusual if H₀ were true.
  • There is evidence against H₀.
  • Something other than chance may be at work.
A small p-value does not say
  • The probability that H₀ is true is small.
  • The effect is large or practically important.
  • The result will replicate next time.
  • The study was well designed or free of bias.
🔎

The cliff that isn't there. One study reports p = 0.049 and another p = 0.051. One gets called "significant" and the other doesn't — but the evidence in them is very nearly identical. The 0.05 line is a useful convention, not a boundary in nature, and treating it as a wall is how a smooth scale of evidence turns into a misleading yes-or-no verdict.

Why it matters

You will read these claims for the rest of your life

Headlines quote p-values

"Statistically significant" appears in news about medicine, education, and policy every week — usually without an effect size, an interval, or a count of how many things were tested.

The errors are systematic

The common misreadings aren't random slips. They all flip the conditional in the same direction, and they all make results sound more certain than they are.

Good questions are short

How big was the effect? How precise is the estimate? How many analyses were run? Three questions will tell you more than any p-value on its own.

The honest summary is modest: a p-value measures how surprising your data would be in a world where nothing is going on. That is genuinely useful and genuinely limited. It cannot tell you how likely that world is, how big the effect is, whether the study was any good, or whether anyone tried twenty analyses before settling on this one. Ask for the interval, ask for the effect size, ask what else was tested — and treat 0.05 as a signpost rather than a verdict.
