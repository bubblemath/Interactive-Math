Union fluency · count the overlap, subtract it

There is only one union rule.

Textbooks give you two — a general one and a special one for mutually exclusive events — and then you have to remember which is which. But mutually exclusive is not a different formula. It is the case where the overlap happens to be zero. Learn one rule, always subtract the overlap, and the only skill left is counting the overlap correctly.

The one question

Before every union: can both events happen on a single trial? If yes, count the overlap and subtract it. If no, the overlap is 0 — and subtracting it changes nothing.

The only formula you need
P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

When A and B are mutually exclusive, P(A ∩ B) = 0, and the rule quietly becomes P(A) + P(B). You do not switch formulas — you just find that there was nothing to subtract. Spade or King: 13 + 4 − 1 = 16, because the king of spades was counted twice. Ace or King: 4 + 4 − 0 = 8, because no card is both.

✋

Odds are not probability. If P(E) = 1/6, the odds in favor are 1 : 5 — not 1 : 6. Probability compares favorable to total; odds compare favorable to unfavorable. That single off-by-one is the most common error in this lesson, and it is worth burning into memory now.

Why this matters

Overlap is everywhere

Double counting is the enemy

Add two groups that share members and you have counted the shared ones twice. P(A ∩ B) is not a technicality — it is the correction that makes the arithmetic honest.

The complement saves work

"At least one" is nearly always easier as 1 − P(none). When counting the winners is painful, count the losers and subtract.

Odds are a different language

Bookmakers, doctors, and risk reports all speak in odds. Translating fluently in both directions is what lets you check whether a claim is as good as it sounds.

Most probability errors are not errors of reasoning — they are errors of bookkeeping. Somebody added two overlapping groups and forgot that a few members belonged to both. Somebody heard "three to one" and thought it meant a three-in-one chance. The rules in this lesson exist to keep the books straight: subtract what you counted twice, flip to the complement when it is easier, and know exactly what you are comparing to what. Get the bookkeeping right and the reasoning takes care of itself.
