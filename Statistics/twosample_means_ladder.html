Two-sample inference with means · Lesson 23

One question decides everything: are they paired?

Two groups, two means to compare — but first, one fork in the road. If each value in group 1 has a natural partner in group 2 (same person before and after, same item at two stores, same day morning and afternoon), the data is matched pairs: collapse it to one column of differences and test d̄. If the groups are separate and unrelated, it's independent: compare the two means directly. Answer that first question and the whole test picks itself.

The two procedures
Independent — 2-sample t
Two separate groups. Random variable: x̄₁ − x̄₂. Hypotheses: H₀: μ₁ = μ₂.
Matched pairs — paired t
One difference per pair. Random variable: d̄. Hypotheses: H₀: μ_d = 0.
🔗

Matched pairs isn't about the two groups — it's about the one difference. Once the data is paired, you throw away the two separate columns and work with a single column of differences, running what is really a one-sample t on d̄. Which means the sign of the alternative depends entirely on how you defined the difference — get "Before − After" backwards and every conclusion flips.

Why it matters

Pairing is a design choice with real power

Pairing removes noise

Measuring the same subjects twice cancels out person-to-person variation, so a real effect is much easier to detect. A study designed as paired can find differences an independent design would miss.

The wrong test wastes the data

Treat paired data as independent and you throw away the very structure that made it powerful — and can reach the wrong conclusion. Naming the design correctly is the whole game.

Direction is easy to flip

"Before − After" versus "After − Before" reverses the sign of every difference. The alternative hypothesis has to match the definition, or the test answers the opposite question.

Lesson 23 is one decision followed by familiar machinery: paired or independent — then hypotheses, assumptions, calculate, conclude. If the values are linked, reduce them to differences and test d̄ against 0; if they're separate, compare μ₁ and μ₂ directly. Spot the structure, define the difference carefully, let the calculator return t and a p-value, and read that number back into the claim.
