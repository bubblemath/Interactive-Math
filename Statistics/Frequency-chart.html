📚 Key Concepts Summary

Converting Between Fractions, Decimals & Percents

🔄 Quick Conversion Rules

Fraction → Decimal: Divide the numerator by the denominator

Decimal → Percent: Multiply by 100 (move decimal 2 places right)

Percent → Decimal: Divide by 100 (move decimal 2 places left)

Percent → Fraction: Put over 100 and simplify

Decimal → Fraction: Use place value, then simplify

Frequency Distribution Terms

Sample Size (n)

The total number of observations in your sample. We use "n" to describe sample size.

n = total count of data values

Frequency

The number of times a particular value is observed in the data set.

Count of each value

Relative Frequency

The proportion or percentage of times a particular value is observed.

Relative Freq = Frequency ÷ n

Cumulative Frequency

The running total of frequencies. Add each frequency to all previous frequencies.

Running total of frequencies

Cumulative Relative Frequency

The accumulation of previous relative frequencies. Shows proportion at or below each value.

Running total of relative frequencies

💡 Key Relationships

• All Relative Frequencies should sum to 1.00 (or 100%)

• The last Cumulative Frequency should equal n (sample size)

• The last Cumulative Relative Frequency should equal 1.00

✏️ Worked Examples

Example 1: Fraction to Decimal to Percent

Convert 34 to decimal and percent

1
Fraction to Decimal: Divide numerator by denominator
34 = 3 ÷ 4 = 0.75
2
Decimal to Percent: Multiply by 100
0.75 × 100 = 75%
✓
Final Answer: 34 = 0.75 = 75%

Convert 0.42 to fraction and percent

1
Decimal to Percent: Multiply by 100
0.42 × 100 = 42%
2
Decimal to Fraction: Use place value (42 hundredths)
0.42 = 42100
3
Simplify: Find GCF of 42 and 100 (GCF = 2)
42 ÷ 2100 ÷ 2 = 2150
✓
Final Answer: 0.42 = 42% = 2150

Convert 30% to decimal and fraction

1
Percent to Decimal: Divide by 100
30% ÷ 100 = 0.30
2
Percent to Fraction: Put over 100
30% = 30100
3
Simplify: Find GCF of 30 and 100 (GCF = 10)
30 ÷ 10100 ÷ 10 = 310
✓
Final Answer: 30% = 0.30 = 310

Example 2: Building a Frequency Table

Data: Hours spent on homework (n = 15)

2, 2, 2, 3, 3, 4, 4, 4, 5, 5, 5, 5, 5, 6, 6

1
Count frequencies: How many times does each value appear?
2
Calculate Relative Frequency: Frequency ÷ n (where n = 15)
3
Calculate Cumulative Frequency: Running total of frequencies
4
Calculate Cumulative Relative Frequency: Running total of relative frequencies
Hours (x) Frequency (f) Relative Frequency Cumulative Frequency Cumulative Relative Freq
2 3 3/15 = 0.200 3 0.200
3 2 2/15 = 0.133 3 + 2 = 5 0.200 + 0.133 = 0.333
4 3 3/15 = 0.200 5 + 3 = 8 0.333 + 0.200 = 0.533
5 5 5/15 = 0.333 8 + 5 = 13 0.533 + 0.333 = 0.867
6 2 2/15 = 0.133 13 + 2 = 15 0.867 + 0.133 = 1.000
Total n = 15 1.000 ✓ 15 ✓ 1.000 ✓

📖 How to Read This Table

• 53.3% of students studied 4 hours or less (Cum. Rel. Freq at x=4)

• 8 students studied 4 hours or less (Cum. Freq at x=4)

• The most common study time was 5 hours (highest frequency = 5)
