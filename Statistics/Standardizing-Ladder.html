The normal model · how many σ from the mean?

There is only one normal curve.

Heights, exam scores, battery lifetimes — they arrive in centimetres, points and hours, and none of that survives contact with z = (x − μ)/σ. Standardizing throws the units away and leaves a single question: how many standard deviations from the mean is this? Every normal problem you will ever meet is that question, followed by one lookup.

03

The one question

The table only ever tells you the area to the left. So a right tail is 1 − that, and a middle is a subtraction. Everything else is bookkeeping.

The move, and the one lookup that follows it
z = (x − μ) / σ   →   P(X ≤ x) = Φ(z)standardize  ×  look left

Φ(z) is the area under the standard normal curve to the left of z — and it is the only thing the table knows. P(X ≥ x) = 1 − Φ(z). P(a ≤ X ≤ b) = Φ(z₂) − Φ(z₁), the bigger area minus the smaller. And the inverse question runs the arrow backwards: it hands you the area, you find z in the body of the table, and unstandardize with x = μ + zσ.

✋

Round z to two decimal places before you look it up. The table has two-decimal rows, so z = 1.3333… becomes z = 1.33, and Φ(1.33) = 0.9082. This ladder follows that convention everywhere. If you use unrounded z on a calculator you may see a slightly different last digit — that is the rounding, not an error, and it is why z-scores are always reported to 2 dp.

Why the normal matters

The curve everything drifts toward

z is a universal currency

Is 62 on a test worse than 88 on a different one? Raw numbers cannot say. z can: it puts both on the same scale, and the bigger |z| is the stranger result.

The empirical rule is the sanity check

68 – 95 – 99.7. You should know before you touch a table that 2σ out is about 95%, so a tail beyond it is about 2.5%. If your lookup disagrees wildly, your lookup is wrong.

Backwards is where the money is

"Set the warranty so only 1% fail before it." Nobody asks for a probability — they ask for a cutoff. That is the inverse problem, and it is the one businesses actually pay for.

The uniform distribution taught you that probability is area, and you could measure it yourself because the edge was a straight line. Here the edge is a curve, and there is no elementary formula for the area beneath it — so the world agreed on one curve, computed its areas once, and printed them in a table. That is all standardizing is: a change of units that lets you use somebody else's arithmetic. It is the single most reused idea in statistics, and everything from here on out depends on it.
