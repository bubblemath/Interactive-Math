Counting fluency · does order matter?

A combination is a permutation with the duplicates divided out.

You are not learning two formulas. Count the arrangements — that's P(n, r). Then notice that every group of r items got counted r! times over, once for each way of shuffling it. Divide by r! and the duplicates vanish: that's C(n, r). The r! in the denominator isn't decoration. It's the correction.

The one question

Before reaching for a formula, ask: would swapping two of the chosen items give a different result? Gold and silver — yes. Two people on a committee — no. That's the whole decision.

Everything on one line
C(n, r) = P(n, r) ÷ r!

Choosing 3 people from 12 for President, VP and Secretary gives P(12,3) = 1,320. Choosing 3 people from 12 for a committee gives C(12,3) = 220 — and 1,320 ÷ 3! = 1,320 ÷ 6 = 220. Same 220 committees; each one was counted six times when we bothered about who held which title. Order matters → keep the duplicates. Order doesn't → divide them out.

✋

You have been counting like this all along. Two dice have 6 × 6 = 36 outcomes. Three coins have 2 × 2 × 2 = 8. Those were the multiplication principle, quietly doing its job back in the probability lessons. Every "count the sample space" question you have ever answered was really a counting problem — this lesson just gives it a name and sharper tools.

Why counting matters

Probability is counting, wearing a disguise

The denominator was always this

Every probability you have computed was n(E) ÷ n(S). These are the tools that let you count n(S) when it is far too big to list — like the 2,598,960 possible poker hands.

Big numbers, fast

A six-character plate has 17,576,000 possibilities. Nobody enumerated them. Somebody multiplied — and that is the entire point of a counting principle.

Dividing out duplicates

The same move appears twice: divide by r! for combinations, divide by n₁!n₂!… for repeated letters. Both are corrections for having counted identical things as if they were different.

Counting sounds like the easiest thing in mathematics, and it is quietly one of the hardest. The difficulty is never the arithmetic — it is knowing what you are counting, and whether you have counted anything twice. A committee is not an ordered list. Two identical letters are not two different letters. Get those distinctions right and the formulas fall out on their own; get them wrong and no amount of careful multiplication will save you. Ask the question first: does order matter? Then compute.
