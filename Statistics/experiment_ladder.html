Watching is not the same as doing.

One question separates every study you will ever read: did the researcher impose a treatment, or only observe? Impose, and you have an experiment — the only design that can prove cause. Observe, and all you can prove is that two things travel together. This ladder drills that call, then takes an experiment apart piece by piece.

The anatomy drilled in rungs 4–6
Part
What it is
Units
Who or what receives a treatment — 15 plots, 200 patients
Factor
The variable the researcher changes on purpose — fertilizer type
Level
One specific value the factor takes — Brand B
Treatment
One combination of levels actually applied — Brand B in a gas oven
Response
What gets measured at the end — crop yield in bushels

Factor and level are the pair students swap. Fertilizer type is the factor — the knob. Brand B is a level — one setting of that knob. And with two factors, treatments multiply: 3 pan materials × 2 oven types is not 5, it is 6.

✋

Only an experiment can prove cause. Observational studies can find that coffee drinkers get more lung cancer — and be perfectly correct, and completely wrong about why, because coffee drinkers smoked more. That hidden third variable is a confounder, and randomly assigning treatments is the only known way to disarm it. That is the entire reason experiments exist.

Why design matters

The design sets the ceiling on what you may claim

Association is not causation

An observational study can only ever say these travel together. If a headline claims a cause and the study only watched, the headline has overreached — and now you can say exactly why.

Randomization is the whole trick

Randomly assigning treatments doesn't remove confounders — it spreads them evenly across the groups, so they can't masquerade as the effect. That's the one idea an experiment is built around.

Blinding protects the answer

People who know they got the real drug feel better anyway; evaluators who know what to expect see what they expect. Double-blind exists because both of those are real, measurable effects.

Every study is a promise about what it can support. A cross-sectional survey promises a snapshot. A prospective study promises to watch what happens next. Only an experiment — treatments imposed, units randomly assigned, a control group standing by, evaluators kept in the dark — promises to tell you what caused what. Learn to identify which promise a study is making, and you will stop being fooled by conclusions that its design never entitled it to draw.
