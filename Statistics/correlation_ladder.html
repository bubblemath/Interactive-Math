Scatterplots & correlation · Lesson 18

r measures one straight line — and nothing else.

A correlation coefficient is a single number between −1 and 1 that answers exactly one question: how tightly do the points hug a straight line? Every classic mistake comes from asking it to do more — to prove a cause, to notice a curve, to shrug off an outlier, to care whether you measured in inches or centimeters. This ladder trains the reading, then the number, then the long list of things the number quietly refuses to tell you.

The strength scale

By magnitude: none |r|<0.1, weak 0.1–0.5, moderate 0.5–0.8, strong ≥0.8. Sign gives direction. Estimates count if you're within 0.1.

What r refuses to tell you
It doesn't prove cause
A tight correlation can be a lurking variable or pure coincidence. Association is not causation.
It only sees straight lines
A perfect U-shape can give r ≈ 0. A small r means "not linear," not "no relationship."
It bends to outliers
r is not resistant. A single stray point can inflate or collapse it.
It ignores units & labels
Switch inches to cm, or swap which variable is x — r does not move at all.
✋

A small r is not the same as "no relationship." r only measures the linear part. Points can march along a clean parabola — an obviously strong pattern — and still return r ≈ 0, because none of that relationship is a straight line. Always look at the plot before you trust the number.

Why it matters

The most over-trusted number in statistics

It compresses — and hides

Boiling a whole scatterplot down to one number is powerful and dangerous. Two completely different clouds can share the same r. The plot holds the story; r is only its headline.

Causation is the costly error

"They're correlated, so one causes the other" is where correlation does real damage — in headlines, policy, and spending. The fix is cheap: ask what third thing might be driving both.

Reading beats computing

You'll rarely compute r by hand — a calculator or the correlation app does that. The skill worth owning is judgment: what the cloud shows, and what the number leaves out.

Every rung here is the same discipline in miniature: look at the cloud first · read direction, form, and strength · then reach for r — and remember its four blind spots. A correlation coefficient is a sharp, honest tool for exactly one job. Knowing that job, and its edges, is most of what this lesson is for.
