Graph fluency · pick it, then read it

A graph is a claim about the data.

Choosing a graph is not decoration — it is a decision about what kind of data you have and how much of it you are willing to throw away. A dot plot keeps every value. A histogram keeps only the shape. A box plot keeps only five numbers. This ladder drills the choice, the reading, and the arithmetic underneath all of it.

How it works

Start anywhere

Eleven rungs run from a single yes/no call up to verifying that a plot actually matches its data. Nothing is locked — climb in order, or jump to the skill you need.

02

Earn each badge

Get four in a row on a rung to earn its badge. Answer with the mouse or hit 1–5 on the keyboard — including on the arithmetic rungs, where speed is half the skill.

03

Every plot is freshly drawn

The dot plots, histograms, and stem-and-leaf displays are generated from random data every time. There is no answer key to memorize — only the reading.

What it is for — and what it keeps
Bar graph
Categories, compared side by side. Bars have gaps.
Pie chart
Categories as parts of one whole — the slices must total 100%.
Stem & leaf
Numbers — keeps every original value, sorted by leading digit.
Dot plot
Numbers — keeps every original value, stacked on a number line.
Histogram
Numbers — keeps only the shape. Values vanish into bins; bars touch.
Box plot
Numbers — keeps only five: min, Q1, median, Q3, max.

The gaps are not a style choice. Nothing exists between "Dog" and "Cat," so the bars stand apart. Everything exists between 70 and 80, so the bars must touch. That one sentence is the whole bar-vs-histogram distinction — and it is rung 3.

✋

The tail names the skew. A pile of data on the left with a long thin tail stretching right is skewed right — even though most of the data sits on the left. Students call this "skewed left" because that is where the data is. Look at where the distribution reaches, not where it sits.

Why the graph matters

Every graph throws something away

Detail is the currency

A dot plot shows all 20 values. A histogram of the same data shows five bars — the values are gone. That trade buys you clarity at scale, and it is a choice you should make on purpose.

The wrong graph tells a lie

A pie chart of categories that don't sum to a whole is meaningless. A bar graph with gaps drawn over numeric bins claims those numbers have holes in them. The graph asserts something about the data.

Shape drives everything next

Skewed data means the mean gets dragged toward the tail and the median becomes the honest measure of center. That decision, made in the next chapter, starts with a graph you read correctly here.

There is no such thing as a neutral graph. Choosing one is choosing what to show and what to discard — every value, or just the shape, or just five numbers. And once it is drawn, reading it is its own discipline: finding the bin a value fell into, recovering an original number from a stem and a leaf, seeing which direction a distribution reaches. Learn to pick the right display and to read it honestly, and the rest of the course — center, spread, inference — has solid ground to stand on.
