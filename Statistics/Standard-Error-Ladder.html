The central limit theorem · one individual, or an average?

An average is steadier than an individual.

Ask about one soldier and the spread is σ. Ask about the average of a sample of them, and that average barely moves — its spread shrinks to σ/√n, the standard error. That is the one new idea in this whole lesson. Everything else is the standardizing you already know, run on a distribution that has quietly grown narrower.

03

The one question

Before any arithmetic: is this about one individual, or about a mean of n? Individual → spread is σ. Mean → spread is σ/√n. Choose the wrong one and every number after it is wrong.

The move, and the one quantity that is new
σx̄ = σ / √n   →   z = (x̄ − μ) / σx̄shrink the spread  ×  standardize as usual

The center does not move: μx̄ = μ. Only the spread changes, and it always shrinks — dividing by √n, never by n. Once you have the standard error, every question is the standardizing ladder again: P(x̄ ≥ k) = 1 − Φ(z), a middle is a subtraction, and the inverse hands you the area and asks for the cutoff, x̄ = μ + z·σx̄.

✋

Divide by √n, not n. With σ = 12 and n = 9, the standard error is 12/√9 = 12/3 = 4, not 12/9. And this whole machinery is only licensed when x̄ is normal: that holds for any n if the population is already normal, but for a skewed population you need a large sample — n ≥ 30 — before the central limit theorem makes x̄ normal for you. z-scores are still rounded to 2 dp before lookup, exactly as in the Standardizing Ladder.

Why the standard error matters

Why we trust averages

It is why polling works

One person's opinion tells you almost nothing. The average of a thousand tells you a great deal — because σ/√n has quietly shrunk the uncertainty by a factor of about 32.

The shape fixes itself

The population can be lopsided, spiky, bimodal — anything. Take large enough samples and the distribution of x̄ comes out normal regardless. That is the central limit theorem, and it is close to magic.

Diminishing returns are built in

Because it is √n, not n, halving your error costs four times the sample. Every study that has ever argued about sample size is arguing about this square root.

The Standardizing Ladder gave you one normal curve and a way to read it. This lesson hands you a second one hiding inside the first: take the average of a sample, and its behavior is also normal — same center, but narrower, and normal even when the thing you sampled was not. Almost all of statistical inference is built on that single sentence. Every confidence interval and every hypothesis test you will ever meet is, underneath, a question about how far a sample mean has wandered from μ — measured, as always, in standard errors.
