Geometric distribution · waiting for the first success

Binomial counts successes. Geometric counts trials until the first one.

A binomial setting fixes the number of trials and asks how many succeed. A geometric setting flips that: you keep going until you succeed, so the number of trials is what's random. To need exactly k trials, the first k − 1 must all fail and the kth must succeed — which is why the formula is (1 − p)k−1 · p, with no combinations anywhere. There's only one arrangement that works: the success has to come last.

03

X starts at 1

X counts trials including the successful one, so the smallest possible value is 1 — you can't succeed in zero trials.

The two settings, side by side
Binomial
A fixed number of trials n. X counts how many succeed, so X = 0, 1, …, n. E(X) = np
Geometric
Trials continue until the first success. X counts trials used, so X = 1, 2, 3, … with no upper limit. E(X) = 1/p
The commands
geometpdf(p, k)P(X = k) — exactly k trials needed
geometcdf(p, k)P(X ≤ k) — success within the first k trials
1 − geometcdf(p, k)P(X > k) — still no success after k trials
1 − geometcdf(p, k − 1)P(X ≥ k) — takes at least k trials
🎯

Why E(X) = 1/p makes sense. If a success happens about one time in six, you should expect to wait about six trials for one — so a probability of 1/6 gives an expected wait of 6. The rarer the event, the longer the wait, and the expected wait is just the reciprocal. No summation required.

Why it matters

Waiting-time questions are everywhere

The natural question

How many calls until a sale? How many parts until a defect? How many attempts until it works? These are waiting-time questions, and binomial can't answer them.

Rare events wait a long time

E(X) = 1/p turns a small probability into a concrete expectation. A 2% response rate means about 50 attempts per response — a fact worth knowing before you start.

The past doesn't count

Independent trials have no memory. Ten failures in a row don't make the next attempt any likelier to succeed — the gambler's fallacy, formally disproved.

Everything here follows from one picture: fail, fail, …, fail, succeed. Multiply k − 1 failures by one success and you have the formula; add up the first k terms and you have "within k trials"; subtract from 1 and you have "still waiting." Recognize the setting, pick the boundary carefully, and let geometpdf and geometcdf handle the rest.
