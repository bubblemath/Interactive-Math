Problem 1: Soldiers' Head Circumference

The army reports that the distribution of head circumference among soldiers is approximately normal with:

μ = 22.8 inches
σ = 1.1 inches
Part A: What is the probability that a randomly selected soldier's head will have a circumference that is greater than 23.5 inches?
Step 1: Identify this as a population problem (single soldier, not a sample mean)
Step 2: Use the calculator with these values:
• Population Mean (μ): 22.8
• Population Std Dev (σ): 1.1
• Sample Size (n): 1 (single observation)
• Select: "Greater than"
• Value: 23.5
Step 3: Read the probability from the calculator output
Part B: What is the probability that a random sample of 5 soldiers will have an average head circumference that is greater than 23.5 inches?
Step 1: Identify this as a sampling distribution problem (average of 5 soldiers)
Step 2: Use the calculator with these values:
• Population Mean (μ): 22.8
• Population Std Dev (σ): 1.1
• Sample Size (n): 5
• Select: "Greater than"
• Value: 23.5
Step 3: Note that the standard error is σ/√n = 1.1/√5 = 0.492
Step 4: Read the probability from the calculator output
Problem 2: Pinedale Children's Fishing Pond - Trout Length

A team of biologists has determined that the length of trout in the pond follows a normal distribution with:

μ = 10.2 inches
σ = 1.4 inches
Part A: What is the probability that a single trout taken at random from the pond is between 8 and 12 inches long?
Step 1: Identify this as a population problem (single trout)
Step 2: Use the calculator with these values:
• Population Mean (μ): 10.2
• Population Std Dev (σ): 1.4
• Sample Size (n): 1
• Select: "Between"
• Lower Value: 8
• Upper Value: 12
Part B: What is the probability that the mean length of 5 trout taken at random is between 8 and 12 inches long?
Step 1: Identify this as a sampling distribution problem (average of 5 trout)
Step 2: Use the calculator with these values:
• Population Mean (μ): 10.2
• Population Std Dev (σ): 1.4
• Sample Size (n): 5
• Select: "Between"
• Lower Value: 8
• Upper Value: 12
Part C: What sample mean would be at the 95th percentile? (Assume n = 5)
Step 1: This is an inverse problem - we know the probability and need to find the value
Step 2: Use the calculator with these values:
• Population Mean (μ): 10.2
• Population Std Dev (σ): 1.4
• Sample Size (n): 5
• Select: "Inverse (find x̄)"
• Percentile: 0.95 (or 95%)
Problem 3: Soft-Drink Bottler - Can Volume

A soft-drink bottler claims that, on average, cans contain 12 oz of soda. The actual volume is normally distributed with:

μ = 12 oz (claimed)
σ = 0.16 oz

16 cans are randomly selected and a mean of 12.1 oz is calculated.

Question: What is the probability that the average of 16 cans will exceed 12.1 oz?
Step 1: Identify this as a sampling distribution problem (average of 16 cans)
Step 2: Use the calculator with these values:
• Population Mean (μ): 12
• Population Std Dev (σ): 0.16
• Sample Size (n): 16
• Select: "Greater than"
• Value: 12.1
Step 3: Note the standard error: σ/√n = 0.16/√16 = 0.04
Problem 4: Hot Dog Fat Content

A hot dog manufacturer asserts that one of its brands has an average fat content of 18 grams per hot dog with:

μ = 18 grams (claimed)
σ = 1 gram

An independent testing organization analyzes a random sample of 36 hot dogs and finds a sample mean of 18.4 grams.

Question: What is the probability that the sample mean of 36 hot dogs is greater than 18.4 grams?
Step 1: Identify this as a sampling distribution problem (average of 36 hot dogs)
Step 2: Use the calculator with these values:
• Population Mean (μ): 18
• Population Std Dev (σ): 1
• Sample Size (n): 36
• Select: "Greater than"
• Value: 18.4
Step 3: Note the standard error: σ/√n = 1/√36 = 0.1667
