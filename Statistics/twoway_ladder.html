Two-way tables · marginal, joint & conditional distributions

A count means nothing until you ask "out of what?"

One number sitting in a cell can answer three different questions. Divide it by the grand total and you get its share of everyone. Divide by its row total and you get a percent within that group. Divide by its column total and you get a percent within the other group — a completely different number from the same cell. The wording of the question picks the denominator, and picking the wrong one is how most mistakes here happen.

How it works

Pick the denominator, then divide

Twelve rungs run from reading a table to spotting Simpson's paradox. Everything is computed by hand — the numbers are small, and the point is knowing what to divide by, not the arithmetic. Percents are given to one decimal place.

Read the question first

"Of the students who passed…" means a column total. "Among group-study students…" means a row total. "Of everyone surveyed…" means the grand total.

03

Association is comparison

Two variables are associated when the conditional distributions differ across groups. If every group looks the same, there's no association.

The three distributions
Marginal
One variable on its own, ignoring the other. row total ÷ grand total. It lives in the margins of the table — hence the name.
Joint
Both categories at once, as a share of everyone. cell ÷ grand total. "What percent of all people are both A and B?"
Conditional
Restricted to one group. cell ÷ that group's total. Each group's conditional distribution adds to 100%.
🔍

Two questions that sound the same and aren't. "What percent of smokers develop the disease?" divides by the number of smokers. "What percent of disease cases are smokers?" divides by the number of cases. Same cell on top, completely different denominators underneath — and often wildly different answers. Whenever you read a percent in the news, the first thing to ask is which one it is.

Why it matters

Most statistics you meet are conditional percents

The denominator is the claim

Headlines quote percentages without saying what they're out of. Knowing which denominator was used is often the difference between an alarming statistic and a boring one.

It sets up the chi-square test

Comparing conditional distributions by eye is exactly what the test for independence does formally. This is the picture behind that arithmetic.

Grouped data can lie

Simpson's paradox — where a pattern in every subgroup reverses when you combine them — is only visible if you know how to look at the tables separately.

The habit this builds is small and permanent: before computing any percent, name its denominator out loud. Grand total for joint and marginal, row or column total for conditional. Then to judge association, line up the conditional distributions side by side and ask whether the groups look alike — because that comparison, not any single number, is what tells you whether the two variables travel together.
