Center fluency · size or order?

The mean feels size. The median only feels order.

That one sentence explains everything in this lesson. The mean is a balance point — it feels how far away every value is, so a single extreme number leans on it hard. The median is a rank — it only feels which value sits in the middle, and dragging the largest value further out doesn't change that. This ladder drills the arithmetic, then drills the judgment.

This is why "average salary" and "median salary" are different numbers, and why the news reports one when it wants you alarmed and the other when it wants you calm. Change the sum, and the mean moves. Change the order, and the median moves. Nothing else touches them.

✋

Sort first. Every single time. The median is the middle of the ordered data — not the middle of the list as it was handed to you. Grabbing the middle number off an unsorted list is the most common error in this entire lesson, and it is completely avoidable.

Why center matters

Choosing a center is choosing a story

The mean can lie honestly

Put a billionaire in a room of ten teachers and the mean net worth says everyone is rich. Not a single number was faked. The measure was simply the wrong one for the data.

Grouped data is a weighted mean

Once values are lost inside classes, you must use midpoints — and weight them by frequency. Averaging the midpoints alone silently pretends every class was equally full.

The ogive is an old friend

It is nothing but the cumulative relative frequency column from the last lesson, drawn as a curve. Same numbers, new picture — and now you can read a percentile straight off it.

There is no single "center" of a data set — there are three, and they answer different questions. What is typical? What is in the middle? What happens most? When the data are tame, the three land close together and the choice hardly matters. When the data are skewed, or carry an outlier, or are made of categories rather than numbers, the choice becomes the whole analysis. Learn what each measure feels, and you will never again report an average that quietly tells the wrong story.
