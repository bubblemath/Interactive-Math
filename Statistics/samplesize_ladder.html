Margin of error · planning a study · solving for n

Margin of error is a promise. Sample size is the price.

Every confidence interval is estimate ± margin of error, and that margin has just three ingredients: how confident you want to be, how variable the population is, and how many observations you collect. Only the last one is yours to choose. And because n sits under a square root, the price climbs steeply — halving the margin of error costs four times the sample. That single fact is why national polls stall at about ±3% and why "just survey more people" is an expensive sentence.

The two formulas
Proportions
ME = z*·√(p̂(1−p̂)/n)
Solved for n: n = p*(1−p*)·(z*/ME)²
With no pilot estimate, use the conservative p* = 0.5.
Means
ME = z*·σ/√n
Solved for n: n = (z*·σ/ME)²
Needs an estimate of σ, usually from a pilot study or past data.
The cost rule
ME is proportional to 1/√n. To cut the margin to half, multiply n by 4; to cut it to a third, multiply n by 9.
Round up, always
Whatever decimal the formula gives, take the next whole number. n = 1067.1 → 1068. This is the one place in statistics where you never round to nearest.
💰

Why polls stop at ±3%. With p* = 0.5 at 95% confidence, a ±3% margin needs about 1068 people. Want ±1.5%? That's about 4269 — four times the cost for one extra decimal of confidence in the headline. Beyond a point the curve flattens so much that more data stops buying meaningful precision, and the money is better spent reducing bias instead.

Why it matters

This is the question you answer first

Studies are budgets

Every survey has a cost per response. Sample-size calculation is how a precision goal becomes a number of dollars — and how you find out whether the study is possible at all.

Diminishing returns are real

Going from 100 to 400 responses halves your margin. Going from 10,000 to 40,000 halves it again, at a hundred times the cost. Knowing where the curve flattens is a practical skill.

Precision isn't accuracy

A huge sample gives a tiny margin of error around possibly the wrong number. Margin of error measures sampling variability only — it says nothing about bias.

The whole topic is one formula read in two directions. Forwards, it tells you the precision a planned study will deliver; backwards, it tells you the sample a required precision demands. Set the margin, choose the confidence, estimate the variability, solve, round up. And when the answer comes back impossible, that's information too — better to learn it before the surveys go out than after.
