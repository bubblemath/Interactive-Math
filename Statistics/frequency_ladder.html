Table fluency · divide across, add down

Four columns. Two operations.

A frequency table looks like four separate procedures to memorize. It isn't. Moving across a row means dividing by n. Moving down a column means keeping a running total. Every mistake in this unit is dividing when you should have added, or adding when you should have divided — and this ladder drills that one question until it's automatic.

Your table comes with three built-in alarms. The relative frequencies must sum to 1.000. The last cumulative frequency must equal n. The last cumulative relative frequency must equal 1.000. If any of those fails, you have an error — and you know it before the teacher does. Rung 11 turns those three alarms into a skill.

Why the table matters

It is the bridge to everything after it

Relative frequency is probability

The proportion of the data at a value is the empirical probability of that value. When probability arrives next chapter, it will arrive as a column you already know how to build.

Cumulative answers "or less"

Percentiles, quartiles, and the median are all questions of the form what fraction is at or below? That is the cumulative relative column, and nothing else.

You can check yourself

Almost no arithmetic in this course comes with a built-in proof of correctness. This does. Three invariants, and a table that fails any of them is a table you can fix before you hand it in.

Fractions, decimals, and percents are the same number wearing three coats — and a relative frequency is simply that number, produced by a division you already know how to do. Once the four columns stop being four procedures and become one grid with two moves, the table stops being something to survive and becomes something to use: a place where "how many," "what share," and "how many at or below" all live side by side, each one checkable against the others.
