Every design is an answer to one question.

The four sampling designs are not four things to memorize. They are four answers to "what, exactly, was chosen at random?" — a person, a starting point, or a group. And when nothing was chosen at random, you are not looking at a design at all. You are looking at bias. This ladder drills both calls until they take two seconds.

Stratified and cluster both start by splitting the population into groups, which is exactly why they get confused. The whole difference is the last line: some from all, or all from some. Rung 3 does nothing but drill that one sentence.

✋

A big sample does not fix a broken one. In 1936 the Literary Digest surveyed ten million people and got the election wrong; Gallup asked fifty thousand and got it right. Size never rescues a sample that was chosen badly — it only makes you more confident about the wrong answer. How beats how many, every time.

Why the design matters

The sample decides everything after it

Bias is not random error

Random error shrinks as the sample grows. Bias does not — it is a systematic tilt, and it survives any sample size. That is the single most important sentence in this unit.

Randomness is the whole warranty

Every inference you will ever make assumes the sample was chosen by chance. Break that assumption and the margin of error, the confidence interval, and the p-value all quietly become fiction.

You'll use this outside class

Online polls, product reviews, call-in votes, and "9 out of 10 dentists" all fail the same tests you're drilling here. Learning to spot the flaw is a life skill wearing a math costume.

A sampling design is a promise: that the people you asked stand in fairly for the people you didn't. Every bias on this ladder is a different way of breaking that promise — by asking the wrong people, by letting people volunteer themselves, by never reaching some of them at all, or by asking a question so loaded that the answer was decided before anyone spoke. Learn to hear the promise break, and you will never again be impressed by a survey just because it was large.
