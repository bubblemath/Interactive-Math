Type I & II errors · z-test vs t-test · Lesson 21

Two ways to be right. Two ways to be wrong.

Reality is in one of two states — H₀ is true, or it's false — and you make one of two calls: reject, or fail to reject. Cross them and you get four outcomes. Two are correct; the other two are the mistakes every test risks: a Type I error (convicting the innocent) and a Type II error (letting the guilty walk). This ladder is about naming those errors, weighing their consequences, and picking the right test — z or t — for the job.

03

z or t?

One question decides the test: is the population σ known? Known → z-test. Unknown → t-test (df = n − 1). Same hypotheses, same conclusion.

The two errors, side by side
Type I error — α
Reject H₀ when it's actually true. A false positive: you claim an effect that isn't there. Its probability is α, the significance level.
Type II error — β
Fail to reject H₀ when it's actually false. A false negative: you miss a real effect. Its probability is β.
⚖️

The consequence is not the definition. "Reject a true H₀" defines a Type I error; the consequence is what that mistake costs in the real world — an innocent convicted, a safe plant penalized, a good shipment tossed. And which error is worse isn't fixed: it depends entirely on the situation and whose side you're on.

Why it matters

Every decision risks a specific mistake

The stakes aren't symmetric

Approving a useless drug and shelving a lifesaving one are both errors — but not equal ones. Choosing α is really choosing which mistake you can least afford to make.

Whose side changes the answer

A supplier fears a good shipment rejected; the buyer fears a bad one accepted. Same test, opposite worries. "Which error is worse" is a question about values, not just statistics.

Same logic, right ruler

z and t answer the identical question and reach the identical kind of conclusion. Reaching for the wrong one — using z when σ is unknown — is a setup error, not a math error.

Strip it down and Lesson 21 is two habits: read every result inside the 2×2 grid, and match the test to what you actually know. Name the error, trace its real consequence, judge which one the situation can least tolerate, and set α accordingly — then pick z or t by a single question about σ. The arithmetic is the app's job; the judgment is yours.
