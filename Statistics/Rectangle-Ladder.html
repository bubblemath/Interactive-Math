Probability is just width.

A uniform distribution is a rectangle. It sits over the interval [a, b], its height is 1/(b − a), and its total area is exactly 1. Every question in this lesson asks for the area of a piece of that rectangle — and the area of a rectangle is width × height. So every answer is a width, divided by the total width. There is one idea here, and it is geometric.

The one question

Before any formula: what is the width of the strip you want, and what is the width of the whole box? Divide the first by the second. That is the probability.

The formula, and why there is only one
P(c ≤ X ≤ d) = (d − c) / (b − a)the strip you want  ÷  the whole box

Everything else is this, in disguise. P(X ≤ k) is the strip from a to k. P(X ≥ k) is the strip from k to b. μ = (a + b)/2 is the middle of the box. The inverse question hands you the area and asks for the edge — the same equation, solved for a different letter. And a conditional probability simply throws away the part of the box you now know you are not in, and rescales what is left.

✋

A point has no width, so P(X = k) = 0. Always. That is not a rounding artefact — it is what continuous means, and it is why < and ≤ give the same answer here, unlike in the discrete lesson. It also means the rectangle can never help you outside [a, b]: there is no box out there to measure, so a strip that runs off the end must be clipped first.

Why the uniform matters

The simplest continuous thing there is

It is the "no information" case

You know the bus comes every 12 minutes and nothing else. Every arrival moment is as good as any other — U(0, 12). It is what ignorance looks like when written down.

It teaches area before the normal does

In the normal distribution, probability is still area — but the boundary is a curve, so you need a table. Here the boundary is a straight line and you can measure it yourself. Same idea, no machinery.

It is where random numbers come from

Every random number generator on earth produces U(0, 1) first. Everything else — normals, binomials, simulations — is built by bending that rectangle into another shape.

The uniform distribution is the bridge. Up to now a probability has been a number you looked up in a table, one cell at a time. From here on it is an area under a curve, and there are infinitely many values, each with probability zero, yet somehow the whole thing still adds to one. That is a genuinely strange idea — and the rectangle is the one place you can see it happen without any calculus at all.
