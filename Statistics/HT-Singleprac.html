Overview: The Logic of Hypothesis Testing

In hypothesis testing, we start by assuming a null hypothesis (H₀)—the "status quo" or "no effect" model. We then collect data and ask: "How surprising is this result if H₀ were true?"

  • We retain the null hypothesis until the facts make it unlikely beyond a "reasonable doubt." Then—and only then—we reject the hypothesis of innocence.
  • p-value is the probability of a result as far out as the result we got, assuming the null hypothesis is true.
  • Decision rules: If p-value < α, reject H₀. If p-value ≥ α, fail to reject H₀.
  • Hypotheses are always about population parameters (like μ), not sample statistics (like x̄).

Do Say / Don't Say

✓ Do say: "Fail to reject H₀" (not "accept H₀")

✓ Do say: "There is enough evidence to support the claim that…" (when rejecting H₀)

✓ Do say: "There is not enough evidence to support the claim that…" (when failing to reject H₀)

✗ Don't say: "Accept H₀" or "We proved the claim"

✗ Don't say: Hypotheses in terms of x̄ (always use μ)

Guided Workflow: The Four Parts of a Hypothesis Test

  1. Hypotheses. State H₀ and Hₐ in terms of the population parameter μ.
    • Example: H₀: μ = μ₀; Hₐ: μ > μ₀ (or μ < μ₀, or μ ≠ μ₀)
  2. Model. Identify the test (one-sample z-test with known σ). Check assumptions:
    • Independence (random sample or random assignment)
    • Approximate normality (population is normal, or n ≥ 30, or sample looks roughly symmetric)
  3. Mechanics. Calculate the test statistic and p-value:
    • z = (x̄ − μ₀) / (σ / √n)
    • Find the p-value based on the direction of Hₐ (one-tailed or two-tailed)
  4. Conclusion. Compare p-value to α:
    • If p-value < α → Reject H₀: "There is enough evidence to support the claim that…"
    • If p-value ≥ α → Fail to reject H₀: "There is not enough evidence to support the claim that…"
