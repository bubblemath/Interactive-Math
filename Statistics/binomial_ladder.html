Binomial fluency · count the ways, price one way

Every winning sequence costs exactly the same.

That is the whole formula. S-S-F and F-S-S are different sequences, but both are worth p·p·q — the same product, reshuffled. So you never price them one at a time: you count how many there are, price a single one, and multiply. C(n,x) does the counting. pˣq^(n−x) does the pricing. Nothing else is happening.

The one question

Before any formula: does p stay the same on every trial? Sample with replacement and it does. Sample without, and it doesn't — and nothing on this page applies.

The formula, in two halves
P(X = x) = C(n, x) · pˣ · q^(n−x)count the ways  ×  price one way

The left half is Lesson 10 — a combination, counting how many arrangements of x successes fit into n trials. The right half is Lesson 9 — independent events multiplied together, giving the probability of one particular arrangement. The only new idea in this lesson is that you are allowed to multiply them — and you are allowed because every one of those arrangements has the same probability.

✋

"Success" is just the thing you're counting. It does not have to be good. If 29% of stores charge you incorrectly and you want the probability that some store overcharges you, then success = being overcharged and p = 0.29. Students lose more marks to naming p backwards than to any arithmetic in this lesson.

Why the binomial matters

The distribution that runs the world

It is everywhere

Defect rates, poll responses, drug trials, click-through rates, quality control. Anything repeated a fixed number of times with a yes/no answer is binomial — which is most of applied statistics.

The complement does the heavy lifting

P(at least one) means adding up every case from 1 to n. Or you compute 1 − P(none) — a single term. Same answer, a tenth of the work.

μ and σ turn it into judgment

Expecting 5 and seeing 9 — is that strange? Only μ ± 2σ can tell you. The rule you learned back in Variation is what makes this distribution useful.

The binomial distribution is where the whole course finally converges. It needs the combinations from counting, the independent multiplication from conditional probability, the complement rule from unions, and the two-standard-deviation rule from variation — all in a single formula, all at once. That is not a coincidence and it is not a coincidence that it comes last. Everything you have been learning was building toward the ability to answer one very practical question: I expected this many. I saw that many. Should I be surprised?
