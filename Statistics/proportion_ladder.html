Same test. Now you're counting.

The four-part machinery hasn't changed — hypotheses, assumptions, calculate, conclude. What's new is narrow: the parameter is a proportion p, built from counts as p̂ = x/n; the assumptions become success/failure conditions; and when you compare two groups, the null says their proportions are equal. Read the story, spot one sample or two, and the rest is machinery you already run.

03

One sample or two?

A single claimed value p₀ → one-sample (H₀: p = p₀). Two groups compared → two-sample (H₀: p₁ = p₂). Spotting which comes first.

The conditions to check
One-sample
An SRS, and the normal approximation holds: np₀ ≥ 10 and n(1−p₀) ≥ 10 (using the claimed p₀).
Two-sample
Two independent SRSs, populations at least 10n, and at least 5 successes and 5 failures in each group.
📊

Proportions come from counting. When a problem reports "x out of n" — 90 of 400, 730 of 1000 — the parameter is a proportion, and p̂ = x/n. If it instead reports an average of measured values, that's a mean. Naming the parameter correctly is the first fork in the road, and it decides everything after it.

Why it matters

Most real claims are proportions

Percentages run the news

Approval ratings, recognition rates, defect rates, side-effect rates — the claims people argue over are almost always proportions. This is the test that decides whether a percentage really moved.

Comparing two groups

Does the treatment beat the control? Do younger buyers differ from older ones? The two-sample test is how a difference in percentages becomes evidence instead of noise.

The setup is the skill

The applet returns a p-value in a click. Knowing whether it's one sample or two, what the hypotheses are, and whether the conditions even allow the test — that's the part worth owning.
