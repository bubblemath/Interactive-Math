Probability fluency · count, count, divide

Every mistake here is a counting mistake.

There is only one formula in this lesson — P(E) = n(E) ÷ n(S) — and almost nobody gets it wrong. What people get wrong is the counting. Two dice have 36 outcomes, not 11. "At least one head" is 7 outcomes, not 1. A red face card is 6 cards, not 38. Fix the counting and the probability takes care of itself.

Step 5 is free and almost nobody uses it. A probability cannot be negative and it cannot exceed 1. If your answer is 7/6, you did not make a probability error — you made a counting error, and you now know it before anyone grades you.

✋

The sums are not the outcomes. Rolling two dice gives sums from 2 to 12 — but those eleven sums are not equally likely, so you cannot divide by 11. The equally likely thing is the ordered pair: (2,3) and (3,2) are different outcomes. That is why there are 36, why there are six ways to make a 7, and why there is only one way to make a 2.

Why probability matters

The mathematics of not knowing

Theoretical is a promise

P(heads) = 1/2 is a claim about a fair coin, argued from symmetry. It owes nothing to data. It is what should happen.

Empirical is a report

508 heads in 1000 flips is a claim about what did happen. It is measured, not deduced — and it will come out differently next time.

They converge — slowly

Ten flips can easily give seven heads and mean nothing at all. A thousand flips will sit close to 500. That is the law of large numbers, and it is why one bad week is not evidence.

Probability is the discipline of being precise about uncertainty. It refuses to say what will happen and insists instead on saying how often — and it holds you to a standard that intuition never meets. Intuition says a coin that landed heads five times is "due" for tails. Intuition says a sum of 7 and a sum of 2 are just two of eleven possibilities. Both are wrong, and both are wrong for the same reason: intuition never counted the sample space. Count it, and the uncertainty becomes something you can actually reason with.
