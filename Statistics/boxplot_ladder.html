Five-number summary · boxplots · outliers

Five numbers, and the picture draws itself.

Minimum, Q1, median, Q3, maximum — that's the entire summary. The box spans the middle half of the data, the line inside marks the median, and the whiskers reach out to the rest. Anything more than 1.5 IQRs beyond the box stops being part of the whisker and gets plotted as its own dot: an outlier. Learn to build those five numbers and you can read any distribution's center, spread, and skew at a glance.

How it works

Build the five numbers, then read the picture

Eleven rungs run from ordering a data list to comparing two distributions side by side. Unlike the inference ladders, you compute everything here yourself — the numbers are small and the arithmetic is honest.

01

Order first, always

Every quartile depends on sorted data. Sort the list, find the median, then find the medians of the two halves — leaving the median out of both.

02

Earn each badge

Four in a row earns a rung's badge. Click a choice or press 1–4; type numbers and press Enter to move between boxes.

03

Fences find outliers

Compute Q1 − 1.5·IQR and Q3 + 1.5·IQR. Any value outside those fences is an outlier and gets its own dot.

The two rules that matter
Quartiles
Q1 is the median of the values below the median; Q3 is the median of the values above it. When n is odd, the median itself belongs to neither half. IQR = Q3 − Q1.
The 1.5 × IQR rule
Fences at Q1 − 1.5·IQR and Q3 + 1.5·IQR. Values outside are outliers, drawn as dots; whiskers stop at the most extreme value that is not an outlier.
📏

A boxplot hides the shape but shows the skeleton. You can't see gaps, clusters, or how many values sit in one spot — a boxplot of a bell curve and of a two-humped distribution can look identical. What it does show beautifully is center, spread, skew, and outliers, which is exactly what you need when comparing several groups at once.

Why it matters

The fastest honest picture of a distribution

Comparison is the point

One boxplot is useful; five stacked side by side are powerful. Comparing salaries across departments or scores across sections is where boxplots beat every other graph.

Outliers are information

The 1.5 IQR rule flags unusual values automatically. Sometimes it's a typo; sometimes it's the most interesting data point you have. Either way you want to know.

Resistance decides the summary

The median and IQR barely move when one value goes extreme. That's why skewed data with outliers gets reported with median and IQR, not mean and standard deviation.

This whole topic is one habit and one rule: sort, split, and check the fences. Sort the data and take the median; split at the median and take the median of each half for Q1 and Q3; subtract for the IQR; step 1.5 IQRs past each side of the box to see whether anything falls outside. What's left is a picture you can read in a second — and compare against another one just as fast.
