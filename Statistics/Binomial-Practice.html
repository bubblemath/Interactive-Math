Key Terms & Formulas

Binomial Experiment Success/Failure Independent Trials At Least

🎯 Binomial Probability Formula

\[ P(X = x) = C(n,x) \cdot p^x \cdot (1-p)^{n-x} \]

The probability of getting exactly x successes in n independent trials.

Variables:
• \(n\) = number of trials
• \(x\) = number of successes desired
• \(p\) = probability of success on one trial
• \(1-p\) = probability of failure (often called \(q\))

✅ Three Conditions for Binomial

1. Fixed Number of Trials
The experiment is repeated a specific number of times (n)
2. Two Outcomes Only
Each trial results in either Success or Failure
3. Independent Trials
Probability of success (p) stays constant for each trial

🔢 Combination Component

\[ C(n,x) = \frac{n!}{x!(n-x)!} \]

This counts the number of ways to arrange x successes among n trials.

Example: For 5 trials with 3 successes:
\(C(5,3) = \frac{5!}{3! \cdot 2!} = \frac{120}{6 \cdot 2} = 10\) ways

📊 "At Least" Problems

\[ P(\text{at least } k) = 1 - P(X < k) \]

Use the complement rule for "at least one" problems:

At least one success:
\(P(X \geq 1) = 1 - P(X = 0)\)

This is often easier than adding up all the individual probabilities!

📈 Mean & Standard Deviation

\[ \mu = np \quad \quad \sigma = \sqrt{np(1-p)} \]

For a binomial distribution:

• Mean (μ) = expected number of successes

• Standard Deviation (σ) = spread of the distribution

Example: n=10, p=0.3
μ = 10(0.3) = 3 successes expected
σ = √(10 × 0.3 × 0.7) ≈ 1.45

❓ Is It Binomial?

Ask: Fixed n? Two outcomes? Independent?

YES - Binomial:

• Coin flips, dice (success = specific outcome)

• Sampling WITH replacement

NO - Not Binomial:

• Sampling WITHOUT replacement (probability changes)

• More than two outcomes

📝 Specific Examples from Lesson

🪙 Coin Flip Example

Scenario: Flip a fair coin 5 times. What is the probability of getting heads all 5 times?

Setup: n = 5, p = 0.5, x = 5

👁️ Blue Eyes Example

Scenario: The chance of having blue eyes is 15%. A researcher selects 5 individuals. Find the probability that exactly 4 have blue eyes.

Setup: n = 5, p = 0.15, x = 4

🎓 College Attendance Example

Scenario: 59% of college students attend more than one institution. In a sample of 6 students, find P(exactly 3 attend multiple institutions).

Setup: n = 6, p = 0.59, x = 3

🛒 FTC Checkout Scanner Example

Scenario: According to the FTC, 29% of stores with checkout scanners do not accurately charge customers. If you shop at 3 stores, what is P(incorrectly charged in at least one)?

Setup: n = 3, p = 0.29, find P(X ≥ 1)
