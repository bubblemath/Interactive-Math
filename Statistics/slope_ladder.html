Inference for the regression slope · reading output

The line you fit is one sample's line. Would another sample have sloped the same way?

Your least-squares line has a slope b — but that's a statistic, computed from the one sample you happened to collect. The question that matters is about β, the slope of the true relationship in the whole population. If β = 0 the line is flat and x tells you nothing about y. So every test here asks one thing: is the slope you observed far enough from zero to be believed?

How it works

Read the printout, test the slope

Twelve rungs run from "what is β?" to a full interpretation. Software produces the standard error, so your job is to read the output correctly and compute the two things it doesn't hand you: the test statistic and the degrees of freedom.

01

You compute two things

t = b / SE_b and df = n − 2. Everything else — the coefficient, its standard error, the p-value — you read off the table.

03

Use the slope row

Regression output has a row for the intercept and a row for the predictor. The test is about the predictor's row — reading the wrong one is the most common mistake.

The conditions — LINER
L

I
Independent
Observations are independent of one another.
N
Normal

E
Equal variance
The spread of residuals stays about the same across all x — no fanning.

Random
The data come from a random sample or randomized experiment.
📉

Why n − 2? Every degree of freedom is a piece of information you spend estimating something. A one-sample t-test spends one, on the mean. A regression line has two estimated pieces — a slope and an intercept — so you spend two, leaving df = n − 2. Using n − 1 here is the single most common slip on this test.

Why it matters

A slope without a p-value is just a drawing

Any scatter gives a slope

Fit a line to pure noise and you'll still get a non-zero slope. The test is what separates a real relationship from a line that happened to tilt.

Output is the real skill

Nobody computes the standard error of a slope by hand. Reading a printout — the right row, the right column — is what actually gets used in every field that uses regression.

The interval says more

The p-value says whether the slope is real; the confidence interval says how big it plausibly is. "Between 0.4 and 2.9 points per hour" tells you far more than "p < 0.05."

This lesson finishes what the regression chapter started: the line describes your sample, the test decides whether it describes the population. Check LINER, read the slope row, compute t = b/SE_b on n − 2 degrees of freedom, compare the p-value to α, and say what it means about x and y. And when you want the size of the effect rather than just its existence, build the interval — if it misses zero, so does the relationship.
