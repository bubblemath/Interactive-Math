🎯 Model Selection

From Your Course Materials

Division can be modeled in multiple ways. As a future teacher, you need to select the appropriate model based on the problem context and student needs.

  • Repeated Subtraction: "How many groups of 3 are in 18?"
  • Set (Partition) Model: "Share 18 cookies among 3 friends"
  • Missing Factor: "3 × ? = 18"
Which Model Would You Use?

Scenario 1

"Ms. Sallay's class consists of 18 students and she wants to split the students into cooperative learning groups of 3 students. How many groups are needed?"
Model Reference Guide
Model Question Type Example
Repeated Subtraction "How many groups of ___ are in ___?" 18 students into groups of 3
Set (Partition) "Share ___ equally among ___" 18 cookies among 3 friends
Missing Factor "___ times what equals ___?" 3 × ? = 18

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Repeated Subtraction is best when the group SIZE is known
  • Partition is best when the NUMBER of groups is known
  • Missing Factor connects division to multiplication facts
  • Students should experience ALL models to build flexible thinking

🔍 Error Analysis

Diagnosing Student Errors

Understanding WHY students make errors is essential for effective teaching. Each error type requires a different intervention strategy.

Common Division Errors
Error Type 1: Place Value Confusion
84 ÷ 4 = 201

Student wrote: "8 ÷ 4 = 2, 4 ÷ 4 = 1, so 201"

✓ Diagnosis

Error: Student divided each digit separately without understanding place value.

Intervention: Use base-10 blocks to show that 84 = 8 tens + 4 ones. When we divide 8 tens by 4, we get 2 tens (20), not just 2.

Error Type 2: Forgetting the Zero
306 ÷ 3 = 12

Student skipped the middle step when quotient digit is 0

✓ Diagnosis

Error: Student forgot to write 0 in the quotient when 3 doesn't go into 0.

Intervention: Emphasize that every place value needs a digit. Use expanded form: 306 = 300 + 0 + 6. 300÷3=100, 0÷3=0, 6÷3=2. Answer: 102.

Error Type 3: Remainder Larger Than Divisor
47 ÷ 5 = 7 R 12

Student stopped dividing too early

✓ Diagnosis

Error: Remainder (12) is larger than divisor (5), which is impossible.

Intervention: Teach the rule: "Remainder must ALWAYS be less than the divisor." If remainder ≥ divisor, you can divide again! 47 ÷ 5 = 9 R 2.

Error Type 4: Division by Zero Confusion
8 ÷ 0 = 0

Student confused 0 ÷ 8 = 0 with 8 ÷ 0

✓ Diagnosis

Error: Student confused the two zero rules.

Intervention: Clarify: 0 ÷ 8 = 0 (zero divided by anything is zero), but 8 ÷ 0 is UNDEFINED (you cannot divide by zero). Use the missing factor model: 0 × ? = 8 has no solution!

Error Type 5: Basic Fact Error
56 ÷ 8 = 6

Student recalled incorrect multiplication fact

✓ Diagnosis

Error: Student confused 8 × 6 = 48 with 8 × 7 = 56.

Intervention: Build fact fluency with strategies. Use the mnemonic: "5, 6, 7, 8 → 56 = 7 × 8". Practice fact families: 7 × 8 = 56, 8 × 7 = 56, 56 ÷ 7 = 8, 56 ÷ 8 = 7.

🤔 For Your Teaching Practice

"When a student makes an error, resist the urge to simply correct it. Instead, ask: 'Can you explain your thinking?' Understanding the student's reasoning helps you provide targeted intervention rather than generic re-teaching."

🔄 Connecting to Multiplication

From Your Course Materials

The missing factor model leads to the definition of division. Division is the inverse operation of multiplication, just as subtraction is the inverse of addition.

Definition: a ÷ b = c if and only if a = b · c

Fact Family Generator
7 × 8 = 56
8 × 7 = 56
56 ÷ 7 = 8
56 ÷ 8 = 7
Think Multiplication to Divide

Transform division into multiplication:

Division Problem Think Multiplication Answer
56 ÷ 8 = ? 8 × ? = 56 7
72 ÷ 9 = ? 9 × ? = 72 8
42 ÷ 6 = ? 6 × ? = 42 7
63 ÷ 7 = ? 7 × ? = 63 9
Inverse Operations Comparison
Addition ↔ Subtraction Multiplication ↔ Division
3 + 5 = 8 3 × 5 = 15
8 - 5 = 3 15 ÷ 5 = 3
8 - 3 = 5 15 ÷ 3 = 5

Just as subtraction "undoes" addition, division "undoes" multiplication.

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Teach fact families together—don't separate multiplication and division
  • Use triangle fact cards with the product at the top
  • Emphasize: "If you know one fact, you know three more!"
  • The missing factor model is the bridge between operations

📝 Word Problem Types

Two Types of Division Problems

From your course materials, division problems fall into two categories:

  • Measurement (Quotitive): "How many groups?" — Group SIZE is known
  • Partitive (Sharing): "How many in each group?" — NUMBER of groups is known
Identify the Problem Type

Problem

"Ms. Sallay's class consists of 18 students and she wants to split the students into cooperative learning groups of 3 students. How many groups are needed?"
Problem Type Reference
Type What's Known What's Unknown Example
Measurement Group SIZE NUMBER of groups "18 students into groups of 3"
Partitive NUMBER of groups Group SIZE "18 cookies shared among 3 friends"
Key Phrases to Look For
Measurement Phrases
  • "How many groups of ___?"
  • "How many ___ fit in ___?"
  • "How many times does ___ go into ___?"
  • "Groups of ___"
Partitive Phrases
  • "Share equally among ___"
  • "Divide into ___ equal parts"
  • "How many for each ___?"
  • "Split between ___"

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Students often struggle more with partitive problems
  • Use manipulatives: dealing cards (partitive) vs. making groups (measurement)
  • Both types use the same division equation but model differently
  • Have students write their own problems of each type

➗ Remainders in Context

What Do We Do With the Remainder?

In real-world problems, the context determines how to handle remainders. Students must learn to interpret remainders appropriately.

Four Ways to Handle Remainders
Strategy When to Use Example
Round UP Need complete groups, can't leave anyone out 25 students need vans that hold 6. Need 5 vans (not 4 R 1)
Round DOWN Can only use complete groups $25 for toys that cost $6 each. Can buy 4 toys.
Report Remainder Need to know what's left over 25 cookies among 6 friends: 4 each, 1 left over
Express as Fraction/Decimal Exact answer needed 25 ÷ 6 = 4⅙ or 4.167
Practice: What Would You Do?
"A school is taking 25 students on a field trip. Each van holds 6 students. How many vans are needed?"

25 ÷ 6 = 4 R 1

What is the correct answer?

🤔 For Your Teaching Practice

"Remainder problems are where math meets the real world. Always ask students: 'Does your answer make sense in this situation?' A student who answers '4.17 vans' has done the math correctly but hasn't interpreted the context."
