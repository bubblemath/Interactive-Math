🎯 From Your Course Materials

Prime factorization is the process of breaking down a composite number into a product of prime numbers. This is fundamental to understanding GCF and LCM.

📝 Factorization

Each expression of a number as a product of factors.

12 = 1 × 12
12 = 2 × 6
12 = 3 × 4
12 = 2 × 2 × 3

🔢 Prime Factorization

A factorization containing only prime numbers.

12 = 2 × 2 × 3 = 2² × 3
24 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 = 2³ × 3
7875 = 3² × 5³ × 7

⭐ Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Each composite number can be written as a product of primes in one and only one way except for the order of the prime factors.

No matter how you factor 12,
you always get 2² × 3
Two Methods from Your Course Materials
Method Description Best For
Tree Method Branch down from the number, splitting into factor pairs until all leaves are prime Visual learners, showing multiple paths
Stacked (Ladder) Method Divide by primes from the side, working down until you reach 1 Systematic approach, organized work

🤔 For Your Teaching Practice

"The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic guarantees that every composite number has a UNIQUE prime factorization. This is why we excluded 1 from being prime—if 1 were prime, factorizations would not be unique."

🌳 Factor Tree Method

How It Works

Start with your number at the top. Find any two factors (other than 1 and itself). Branch down to those factors. Continue until all branches end in prime numbers (circled in green). List primes from smallest to largest.

Example: Two Paths to Factor 48

Different starting points lead to the same prime factorization!

Path 1: 48 = 4 × 12

= 2⁴ × 3

Path 2: 48 = 6 × 8

= 2⁴ × 3

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Circle prime numbers to show that branch is complete
  • Let students discover that different paths give the same answer
  • List final primes from smallest to largest
  • Use exponential notation for repeated factors (Grade 7+)

📐 Stacked (Ladder) Method

How It Works

Write the number. Divide by the smallest prime that divides evenly. Write the quotient below. Repeat until you reach 1. The prime factors are on the left side.

  • This method naturally produces factors in order
  • More systematic and less prone to errors than trees
