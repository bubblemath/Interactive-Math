⚖️ The Fundamental Relationship

For Any Two Natural Numbers a and b:

GCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b

This means we can find LCM if we know GCF (and vice versa)!

🔍 From Your Course Materials

This relationship is powerful because:

  • If GCF is easy to find (using Euclidean Algorithm), we can quickly calculate LCM
  • LCM(a, b) = (a × b) ÷ GCF(a, b)
  • The Venn diagram method shows WHY this relationship works

⭕ Venn Diagram Visualization

Why the Relationship Works

The Venn diagram shows how prime factors distribute between two numbers. Each prime factor appears exactly once in the entire diagram:

  • Left only: Factors unique to the first number
  • Intersection: Factors common to both (these form the GCF)
  • Right only: Factors unique to the second number
  • Entire diagram: All factors together form the LCM

Why GCF × LCM = a × b

Example: 12 and 18

12 = (Left only) × (Intersection) = 2 × 2 × 3 = 2 × 6

18 = (Intersection) × (Right only) = 2 × 3 × 3 = 6 × 3

12 × 18 = (Left) × (Intersection) × (Intersection) × (Right)

GCF × LCM = (Intersection) × (Left × Intersection × Right)

The intersection appears in both products, so GCF × LCM = a × b!

🤝 Relatively Prime Numbers

Definition

Two numbers a and b are relatively prime (or coprime) if and only if:

GCF(a, b) = 1

This means they share NO common prime factors!

🔍 Key Insight

When GCF(a, b) = 1, the fundamental relationship simplifies:

  • GCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b
  • 1 × LCM(a, b) = a × b
  • LCM(a, b) = a × b

So for relatively prime numbers, the LCM is simply their product!

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Relatively prime doesn't mean both numbers are prime!
  • 8 and 15 are relatively prime, but neither is a prime number
  • This concept is important for simplifying fractions: a/b is in lowest terms when GCF(a,b) = 1
  • Consecutive integers proof: If d divides both n and n+1, then d divides (n+1) - n = 1, so d = 1

❓ GCF or LCM: Which Do I Use?

The Key Question

Students often confuse when to use GCF vs LCM. Here's a simple guide:

  • GCF: When you're DIVIDING or SPLITTING things into equal groups
  • LCM: When you're finding when things COINCIDE or REPEAT together

🔴 Use GCF When...

You need to DIVIDE or SPLIT things equally

largest greatest divide equally split into groups simplify fractions cut into pieces

🟣 Use LCM When...

You need to find when things HAPPEN TOGETHER

least smallest together again same time common denominator schedule
Real-World Examples
Problem Type Use Example
Splitting items into equal groups GCF 24 red and 36 blue tiles into identical groups
Simplifying fractions GCF Reduce 24/36 to lowest terms
Cutting boards to same length GCF Cut 24ft and 36ft boards into equal pieces
When events coincide LCM Buses arrive every 12 and 15 minutes
Finding common denominators LCM Add 1/8 + 1/12
Buying equal quantities LCM Hot dogs (10/pack) and buns (8/pack)
🎯 Quick Decision Quiz

Problem: You have 18 red flowers and 24 yellow flowers. What is the greatest number of identical bouquets you can make using all the flowers?

Use GCF - We're dividing/splitting into equal groups
Use LCM - We're finding when things coincide
