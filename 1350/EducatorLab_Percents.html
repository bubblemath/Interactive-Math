✅ True/False Analyzer

📝 From Your Course Materials

These scenarios come directly from your course materials. Can you identify which statements are mathematically valid?

📋 Scenario 1: Leo's Raises

"Leo got a 10% raise at the end of his first year on the job and a 10% raise after another year. His total raise was 20% of his original salary."
❌ FALSE!

The Math:
• Original salary: $100 (for easy calculation)
• After Year 1: $100 × 1.10 = $110
• After Year 2: $110 × 1.10 = $121

Total raise: $121 - $100 = $21 = 21% (not 20%!)

Why? The second 10% raise is calculated on the NEW, HIGHER base ($110), not the original ($100). This is the Percent Addition Trap!

📋 Scenario 2: Juanita's Credit Cards

"Juanita paid 45% of their first credit card bill of $620 and 48% of the second credit card bill of $380. They paid 45% + 48% = 93% of the total bill of $1000."
❌ FALSE!

The Math:
• 45% of $620 = $620 × 0.45 = $279
• 48% of $380 = $380 × 0.48 = $182.40
• Total paid: $279 + $182.40 = $461.40

Actual percent: $461.40 ÷ $1000 = 46.14% (not 93%!)

Why? You cannot add percentages when they refer to DIFFERENT bases! 45% of $620 and 48% of $380 are percentages of different amounts.

📋 Scenario 3: Emanual's Budget

"Emanual spent 25% of his salary on food and 40% on housing. Emanual spent 25% + 40% = 65% of his salary on food and housing."
✅ TRUE!

The Math:
• Let salary = $100
• Food: 25% of $100 = $25
• Housing: 40% of $100 = $40
• Total: $25 + $40 = $65 = 65% of $100 ✓

Why does this work? Both percentages refer to the SAME base (Emanual's salary). When the base is the same, you CAN add percentages!

🔑 The Golden Rule

You can only add percentages when they refer to the SAME base!

🔍 Error Diagnosis

📝 Identify What Went Wrong

As future educators, you'll need to quickly diagnose student errors. Click "Diagnose" to see the explanation!

🚨 Error Type 1: The Percent Addition Trap

Student's Work:
"A price increased by 20% and then decreased by 20%.
20% - 20% = 0%
So the price is back to the original!"
❌ Diagnosis: Different Bases!

The Reality:
• Original price: $100
• After 20% increase: $100 × 1.20 = $120
• After 20% decrease: $120 × 0.80 = $96

Result: The final price is $96, which is 4% LESS than the original!

Teaching Tip: The 20% decrease is calculated on the LARGER amount ($120), so it removes more dollars than the 20% increase added.

🚨 Error Type 2: Decimal Conversion Error

Student's Work:
"Find 6% of $50
6% = 0.6
0.6 × $50 = $30"
❌ Diagnosis: Forgot to divide by 100!

The Correct Conversion:
• 6% = 6 ÷ 100 = 0.06 (not 0.6!)
• 0.06 × $50 = $3

The student wrote 0.6 instead of 0.06

Teaching Tip: Remind students that "percent" means "per hundred." To convert, move the decimal point TWO places left, not one!

🚨 Error Type 3: Finding the Original (Reverse Percent)

Student's Work:
"A shirt is on sale for $60 after a 25% discount.
Original price = $60 + 25% of $60
= $60 + $15 = $75"
❌ Diagnosis: Wrong base for the percentage!

The Correct Method:
• $60 is 75% of the original (100% - 25% = 75%)
• Original = $60 ÷ 0.75 = $80

Check: $80 × 0.25 = $20 discount → $80 - $20 = $60 ✓

Teaching Tip: The 25% discount was taken from the ORIGINAL price, not from $60. The student added 25% of the wrong base.

🚨 Error Type 4: Percent of a Percent

Student's Work:
"What is 50% of 50%?
50% + 50% = 100%"
❌ Diagnosis: Confused "of" with addition!

The Correct Method:
• "Of" means multiply!
• 50% of 50% = 0.50 × 0.50 = 0.25 = 25%

Teaching Tip: The word "of" in math almost always means multiplication. 50% OF 50% is half of a half, which is a quarter (25%).

🔗 Connecting to Prior Knowledge

📝 Building on Previous Lessons

Percents connect directly to concepts from Lessons 9, 10, and 11!

📊 Fractions (Lesson 9)

Connection: "Percent" means "per 100"

n% = n/100

Example: 75% = 75/100 = 3/4

Teaching Tip: Use the Fundamental Law of Fractions to simplify percent-to-fraction conversions!

🔢 Decimals (Lesson 10)

Connection: Moving the decimal 2 places = dividing by 100

75% = 0.75

Example: 6% = 0.06 (not 0.6!)

Teaching Tip: This is where the conversion error happens—students forget to move TWO places!

⚖️ Ratios (Lesson 11)

Connection: A percent is a part-to-whole ratio where the whole is always 100

75% = 75:100

Example: "75 out of 100" = 75%

Teaching Tip: Use the percent proportion: is/of = %/100

The Percent Proportion

From Lesson 11: Proportions

is/of = %/100

"is" = Part | "of" = Whole | "%" = Percent

Problem Type Setup Example
Find the Part x/of = %/100 What is 20% of 50? → x/50 = 20/100 → x = 10
Find the Percent is/of = x/100 10 is what % of 50? → 10/50 = x/100 → x = 20%
Find the Whole is/x = %/100 10 is 20% of what? → 10/x = 20/100 → x = 50
