📖 What Are Terminating Decimals?

📝 Definition

A terminating decimal is a decimal that has a finite number of digits. In other words, it ends!

From Your Course Materials

"A decimal that has a finite number of digits."

Examples of terminating decimals:

  • 0.5 (one digit after decimal)
  • 0.75 (two digits after decimal)
  • 0.125 (three digits after decimal)
  • 31.75 (terminates at hundredths place)
  • 4.152 (terminates at thousandths place)
Terminating vs. Non-Terminating
Type Definition Examples
Terminating Finite number of digits 0.5, 0.75, 0.125, 0.875
Repeating Digits or pattern repeats forever 0.333..., 0.1666..., 0.142857...
Irrational Non-terminating, non-repeating π = 3.14159..., √2 = 1.41421...

🎯 The Big Question

How can we tell if a fraction will produce a terminating decimal without actually dividing?

🔑 The Rule for Terminating Decimals

🔑 The Key Rule

A fraction (in lowest terms) will produce a terminating decimal if and only if the denominator has only 2s and 5s as prime factors.

📝 In Other Words...

When you factor the denominator completely:

  • If you see only 2s and 5s → TERMINATES ✓
  • If you see any other prime (3, 7, 11, etc.) → REPEATS ✗
Examples: Checking the Denominator
Fraction Denominator Prime Factorization Terminates?
1/4 4 2² ✓ Yes (0.25)
3/5 5 5 ✓ Yes (0.6)
7/8 8 2³ ✓ Yes (0.875)
3/20 20 2² × 5 ✓ Yes (0.15)
1/3 3 3 ✗ No (0.333...)
5/6 6 2 × 3 ✗ No (0.8333...)
1/7 7 7 ✗ No (0.142857...)
⚠️ Important: Lowest Terms First!

Always simplify the fraction to lowest terms before checking!

Example: Does 6/15 terminate?
Step 1: Simplify first: 6/15 = 2/5 (divide by GCD = 3)
Step 2: Check denominator: 5 = 5 (only 5s!)
Result: ✓ Yes, it terminates! 6/15 = 2/5 = 0.4

❓ Why Only 2s and 5s?

🔑 The Key Insight

Because 10 = 2 × 5

Our decimal system is base 10, and 10 only has 2 and 5 as prime factors!

📝 The Connection to Powers of 10

A terminating decimal can always be written as a fraction with a power of 10 as the denominator:

  • 0.5 = 5/10 = 5/10¹
  • 0.75 = 75/100 = 75/10²
  • 0.125 = 125/1000 = 125/10³

Since 10 = 2 × 5, any power of 10 only has 2s and 5s as factors!

Powers of 10 and Their Factors
Power of 10 Value Prime Factorization
10¹ 10 2 × 5
10² 100 2² × 5²
10³ 1000 2³ × 5³
10⁴ 10000 2⁴ × 5⁴
Why It Works: Converting to Power of 10

Example: Why does 3/8 terminate?

Step 1: Factor the denominator: 8 = 2³
Step 2: To make a power of 10, we need equal 2s and 5s
Step 3: Multiply by 5³/5³ to balance:
3/8 × 125/125 = 375/1000
Step 4: Check: 1000 = 2³ × 5³ = 10³ ✓
Result: 3/8 = 375/1000 = 0.375
Why 1/3 Doesn't Terminate

Example: Why does 1/3 repeat?

Step 1: Factor the denominator: 3 = 3
Step 2: Can we make this a power of 10?
Problem: No matter what we multiply by, we can't eliminate the 3!
3 × anything = still has 3 as a factor
We can never get a denominator that's purely 2s and 5s.
Result: 1/3 = 0.333... (repeats forever)

📚 Teaching Tip

Help students understand this by asking:

  • "What are the only prime factors of 10?" (2 and 5)
  • "What are the only prime factors of 100?" (2 and 5)
  • "What are the only prime factors of ANY power of 10?" (2 and 5)

This connects to the Fundamental Law of Fractions: we can only convert to a power of 10 if the denominator "fits" into a power of 10.

🔄 Converting Terminating Decimals to Fractions

📝 The Method: Use Place Value

To convert a terminating decimal to a fraction:

  • Step 1: Write the decimal digits as the numerator
  • Step 2: Write the place value of the last digit as the denominator
  • Step 3: Simplify using GCD
