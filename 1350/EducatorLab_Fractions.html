🔍 Error Analysis

📝 Common Fraction Errors

Understanding WHY students make mistakes is crucial for effective teaching. Each error reveals a misconception that needs to be addressed.

Common Errors Reference
Error Type Example Misconception
Adding Denominators 1/2 + 1/3 = 2/5 ❌ Treats fractions like whole numbers
Larger Denom = Larger Fraction 1/8 > 1/4 ❌ Ignores that pieces get smaller
Cross-Multiply for Addition Uses multiplication method for adding Confuses comparison with operation
Not Simplifying Leaves 4/8 instead of 1/2 Doesn't recognize equivalence
Forgetting to Invert 2/3 ÷ 4/5 = 8/15 ❌ Multiplies without reciprocal

📚 Intervention Strategies

  • Adding Denominators: Use visual models to show why 1/2 + 1/3 ≠ 2/5
  • Comparing Fractions: Have students draw or fold paper to see piece sizes
  • Division Errors: Connect to "how many groups" meaning
  • Not Simplifying: Practice finding GCF and equivalent fractions

🔗 Connecting to Prior Knowledge

📝 Building on Previous Lessons

Fractions connect deeply to concepts students have already learned. Making these connections explicit helps students see mathematics as a coherent whole.

🔢 GCF Connection

Simplifying Fractions

To reduce 12/18 to lowest terms, find GCF(12, 18) = 6

12/18 = 2/3

✖️ LCM Connection

Common Denominators

To add 1/4 + 1/6, find LCM(4, 6) = 12

3/12 + 2/12 = 5/12

➗ Division Connection

Fraction as Division

3/4 means 3 ÷ 4

"3 pizzas shared by 4 people"

Division Models Applied to Fractions
Division Model Whole Number Example Fraction Example
Repeated Subtraction 18 ÷ 3: How many 3s in 18? 3/4 ÷ 1/4: How many 1/4s in 3/4? (Answer: 3)
Partition (Sharing) 18 cookies among 3 friends 1/2 pizza among 3 people = 1/6 each
Missing Factor 18 ÷ 3 = ? means 3 × ? = 18 3/4 ÷ 1/2 = ? means 1/2 × ? = 3/4

From your course materials: "The missing factor model leads to the definition of division."

Fundamental Law of Fractions

a/b = (a × n)/(b × n) for any integer n ≠ 0

This law is the foundation for:

  • Finding equivalent fractions
  • Simplifying fractions (divide by GCF)
  • Finding common denominators (multiply to get LCM)
  • Comparing fractions
