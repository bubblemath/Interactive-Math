✨ From Your Course Materials

The Least Common Multiple (LCM) of two non-zero whole numbers a and b is the least non-zero whole number that is simultaneously a multiple of a and a multiple of b.

🌭 Classic Word Problem

From your materials: Hot dogs are usually sold 10 to a package, while hot dog buns are usually sold 8 to a package. What is the least number of packages of each you must buy so that there is an equal number of hot dogs and buns?

Solution: Find LCM(10, 8) = 40. You need 4 packages of hot dogs and 5 packages of buns!

📝 Definition

LCM(a, b) is the smallest positive number that is a multiple of both a and b.

LCM(4, 6) = 12
LCM(8, 12) = 24
LCM(3, 4) = 12

⚡ Special Cases

Important cases to remember:

LCM(a, 1) = a
LCM(a, a) = a
If GCF(a,b) = 1, then
LCM(a,b) = a × b

🔗 GCF Connection

The fundamental relationship:

GCF(a,b) × LCM(a,b) = a × b

So: LCM(a,b) = (a × b) ÷ GCF(a,b)
Methods to Find LCM
Method Description Best For
Listing Multiples List multiples of each number until you find a common one Small numbers, building understanding
Prime Factorization Find prime factorizations, take each prime with largest exponent Medium numbers, connecting to GCF
Venn Diagram Product of ALL factors in the entire diagram Visual learners, seeing GCF/LCM together
GCF-LCM Product LCM(a,b) = (a × b) ÷ GCF(a,b) When GCF is already known
Division by Primes Divide both numbers by common primes, multiply all divisors and remainders Multiple numbers, systematic approach

🤔 For Your Teaching Practice

"When using prime factorization for GCF, we take the smallest exponent. But when we use prime factorization for LCM, we are taking the largest exponent."

📋 Intersection-of-Sets Method

How It Works

List multiples of each number. Find the multiples that appear in both lists. The smallest of these common multiples is the LCM.

Find LCM by Listing Multiples

Enter two numbers and click "Find LCM"

Example from Your Course Materials: LCM(8, 12)

Multiples of 8

8 16 24 32 40 48 ...

Multiples of 12

12 24 36 48 60 72 ...

Common multiples: {24, 48, 72, ...}

LCM(8, 12) = 24

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Start with the larger number's multiples - you'll find the LCM faster
  • Circle or highlight common multiples in both lists
  • This method builds understanding but is slow for large numbers
  • Use colored rods to show multiples visually

🔢 Prime Factorization Method

How It Works

Find the prime factorization of each number. The LCM is the product of all prime factors, each raised to the largest power it appears in either factorization.

Find LCM by Prime Factorization

Enter two numbers and click "Find LCM"

Example from Your Course Materials: GCF vs LCM

Find GCF(24, 30) and LCM(24, 30)

24 = 2³ × 3     30 = 2 × 3 × 5

Prime In 24 In 30 For GCF (min) For LCM (max)
2 2³ 2¹ 2¹ 2³
3 3¹ 3¹ 3¹ 3¹
5 — 5¹ — 5¹
GCF = 2 × 3 = 6
LCM = 8 × 3 × 5 = 120

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Key rule: Take the MAXIMUM exponent for LCM
  • Include ALL primes from both factorizations
  • Contrast with GCF: Take the MINIMUM exponent, only common primes
  • Use a table to organize the comparison

⭕ Venn Diagram Method

How It Works

Place the prime factors of each number in a Venn diagram with overlapping circles. The GCF is the product of factors in the intersection. The LCM is the product of ALL factors in the entire diagram.

Build a Venn Diagram

Enter two numbers and click "Build Diagram"

Example: GCF and LCM of 12 and 18

12 = 2 × 2 × 3     18 = 2 × 3 × 3

GCF = Intersection: 2 × 3 = 6

LCM = All factors: 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 = 36

LCM(12, 18) = 36

📚 Teaching Tip

  • The intersection (overlap) gives the GCF
  • The entire diagram (all factors) gives the LCM
  • This visual method shows why GCF × LCM = a × b
  • Each factor appears exactly once in the entire diagram

🔗 GCF-LCM Product Method

The Fundamental Relationship

For any two natural numbers a and b:

GCF(a, b) × LCM(a, b) = a × b

Therefore:

LCM(a, b) = (a × b) ÷ GCF(a, b)
Calculate LCM Using GCF

Enter two numbers and click "Find LCM"

Example from Your Course Materials: LCM(731, 952)

Goal: Find LCM(731, 952)

Step 1: Find GCF(731, 952) using Euclidean Algorithm (it's easier!)

952 = 731 × 1 + 221
731 = 221 × 3 + 68
221 = 68 × 3 + 17
68 = 17 × 4 + 0
GCF(731, 952) = 17

Step 2: Use the formula

LCM(731, 952) = (731 × 952) ÷ 17 = 695,912 ÷ 17 = 40,936
LCM(731, 952) = 40,936
Verify the Relationship

Enter two numbers to verify GCF × LCM = a × b

📚 Teaching Tip

  • This method is most efficient when GCF is easy to find
  • Use Euclidean Algorithm for GCF of large numbers
  • The relationship explains why GCF and LCM are connected
  • Verify with students: GCF(24,30) × LCM(24,30) = 6 × 120 = 720 = 24 × 30 ✓

📐 Division by Primes Method

How It Works

Write both numbers side by side. Divide by the smallest prime that divides at least one number. Continue until all quotients are 1. The LCM is the product of all the divisors.

Find LCM Using Division by Primes

Enter numbers and click "Find LCM"

Example from Your Course Materials: LCM(12, 75, 120)
2 │ 12   75   120
2 │ 6    75   60
2 │ 3    75   30
3 │ 3    75   15
5 │ 1    25   5
5 │ 1    5    1
  │ 1    1    1

LCM = 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 5 × 5 = 600

LCM(12, 75, 120) = 600

📚 Teaching Tip

  • This method works great for finding LCM of 3+ numbers
  • If a prime doesn't divide a number, bring that number down unchanged
  • Continue until all numbers become 1
  • The LCM is the product of ALL divisors on the left
