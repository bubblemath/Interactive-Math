🎯 From Your Course Materials

The Greatest Common Divisor (GCD), also called the Greatest Common Factor (GCF), of two numbers is the largest positive integer that divides both numbers without a remainder.

📝 Definition

GCF(a, b) is the greatest number that is a factor of both a and b.

GCF(24, 30) = 6
GCF(108, 72) = 36
GCF(20, 32) = 4

⚡ Special Cases

Important cases to remember:

GCF(0, 13) = 13
GCF(a, 0) = a
GCF(a, a) = a
GCF(a, 1) = 1

🔗 Relatively Prime

Two numbers are relatively prime (or coprime) if their GCF is 1.

GCF(8, 15) = 1
8 and 15 are relatively prime
Four Methods to Find GCF
Method Description Best For
Listing Factors List all factors of each number, find the greatest common one Small numbers, building understanding
Prime Factorization Find prime factorizations, take common primes with smallest exponents Medium numbers, connecting to prime factorization
Euclidean Algorithm Repeated division until remainder is 0 Large numbers, efficient computation
Venn Diagram Visual representation of shared prime factors Visual learners, seeing the connection

🤔 For Your Teaching Practice

"When using prime factorization for GCF, we take the smallest exponent of each common prime. This is the opposite of LCM, where we take the largest exponent."

📋 Intersection-of-Sets Method

How It Works

List all factors of each number. Find the factors that appear in both lists. The greatest of these common factors is the GCF.

Find GCF by Listing Factors

Example from Your Course Materials: GCF(20, 32)

Factors of 20

1 2 4 5 10 20

Factors of 32

1 2 4 8 16 32

Common factors: {1, 2, 4}

GCF(20, 32) = 4

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Have students list factors in pairs: 1×20, 2×10, 4×5
  • Circle or highlight common factors in both lists
  • This method builds understanding but is slow for large numbers
  • Use colored rods (Cuisenaire rods) for a concrete approach

🔢 Prime Factorization Method

How It Works

Find the prime factorization of each number. The GCF is the product of all common prime factors, each raised to the smallest power it appears in either factorization.

Example from Your Course Materials

7 7² 7³ 7²

13 13¹ 13¹ 13¹
17 — 17¹ —
GCF = 2¹ · 7² · 13¹ = 2 · 49 · 13 = 1274

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Key rule: Take the MINIMUM exponent for GCF
  • If a prime doesn't appear in both, it's NOT in the GCF
  • Contrast with LCM: Take the MAXIMUM exponent
  • Use a table to organize the comparison

🔄 Euclidean Algorithm

How It Works

Let a and b be any two natural numbers with a ≥ b. To calculate GCF(a, b):

  • Step 1: Divide a by b to get quotient q and remainder r
  • Step 2: If r = 0, then GCF(a, b) = b
  • Step 3: If r ≠ 0, replace a with b and b with r, then repeat
Find GCF Using Euclidean Algorithm

Enter two numbers and click "Find GCF"

Example: GCF(731, 952)
1 952 = 731 × 1 + 221
2 731 = 221 × 3 + 68
3 221 = 68 × 3 + 17
4 68 = 17 × 4 + 0
✓ Remainder = 0, so GCF(731, 952) = 17

📚 Teaching Tip

  • This is the most efficient method for large numbers
  • The algorithm always terminates because remainders decrease
  • Named after the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid
  • Useful for finding LCM: LCM(a,b) = (a × b) ÷ GCF(a,b)

⭕ Venn Diagram Method

How It Works

Place the prime factors of each number in a Venn diagram with overlapping circles. Common factors go in the intersection (the overlap). The GCF is the product of factors in the intersection. The LCM is the product of ALL factors in the entire diagram.

Build a Venn Diagram

Enter two numbers and click "Build Diagram"

Example: GCF(12, 18)

12 = 2 × 2 × 3     18 = 2 × 3 × 3

GCF = Intersection: 2 × 3 = 6

LCM = All factors: 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 = 36

GCF(12, 18) = 6

📚 Teaching Tip

  • The intersection (overlap) contains the GCF's prime factors
  • The entire diagram (all factors) gives the LCM
  • If a factor appears multiple times in both, all shared instances go in the intersection
  • If no factors are common, the intersection is empty and GCF = 1
  • GCF × LCM = a × b (the product of the two numbers)
