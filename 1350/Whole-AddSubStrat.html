🔢 Counting On Strategy

Start with the greater addend, then count on the smaller addend.

4 + 2 = ?

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"A student keeps starting from 1 and counting all the blocks. How would you help them understand why starting from the larger number is more efficient?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Kinesthetic learners who benefit from physical counting motions. Have students "put the big number in their head" and count on with their fingers. This strategy is foundational—master it before moving to more abstract strategies.

👯 Doubles & Doubles +1/+2

Use known doubles facts to solve near-doubles problems.

3 + 4 = ?

3 + 3 = 6

→

3 + 4 = ?

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"A student knows that 6 + 6 = 12 instantly but freezes on 6 + 7. What question could you ask to help them make the connection?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Visual learners who can "see" the symmetry in doubles. Use real-world examples: "Two hands have 5+5 fingers." This strategy builds toward multiplication concepts—doubles are the foundation of the 2× facts!

🔟 Making 10 Strategy

Regroup to form a group of 10 and a leftover.

8 + 5 = 8 + 2 + 3 = 10 + 3 = ?

Ten Frame

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"Why is 10 such a 'friendly' number in our number system? How does understanding place value help students see why Making 10 works?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Visual learners who need to see the physical regrouping. The ten-frame is essential—it shows why our base-10 system makes this strategy powerful. This strategy directly prepares students for regrouping in multi-digit addition!

⬅️ Counting Back (One Less Than 10)

Because 9 is 1 less than 10, then 9 + 7 is one less than 10 + 7.

9 + 7 = (10 + 7) - 1 = 17 - 1 = ?

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"How does this strategy connect to the Making 10 strategy? When would you recommend one over the other?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Logical-mathematical learners who enjoy finding patterns and shortcuts. This strategy works best when one addend is 8 or 9. Note: We avoid subtraction notation here since students are still working with addition concepts.

🗑️ Take-Away Model

View subtraction as removing objects from a set.

8 - 3 = ?

Click blocks to remove them!

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"A student understands 'I have 8 cookies and eat 3' but struggles with '8 - 3' on paper. How would you bridge this gap between concrete and abstract?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Kinesthetic learners who need to physically manipulate objects. This is often the first subtraction model students encounter. Use real objects before pictures, and pictures before symbols (Concrete → Pictorial → Abstract).

🔍 Missing Addend (Counting Up)

Find the "gap" between numbers by counting up from the subtrahend.

12 - 9 = ? → Think: 9 + ? = 12

Start at 9, count up to 12!

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"How does the Missing Addend approach help a student connect subtraction back to the addition facts they already know?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Students who have strong addition skills but struggle with subtraction. This model emphasizes the inverse relationship between addition and subtraction—a crucial algebraic concept! It's especially useful for "making change" problems.

⚖️ Comparison Model

Determine "how many more" by matching and finding the difference.

8 - 5 = ?

Match the pairs to find the difference!

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"When would you use the Comparison Model instead of Take-Away? Give an example word problem for each."

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Logical-mathematical learners who struggle with "taking away" but excel at seeing relationships. This model is essential for word problems like "How many more does Sarah have than Tom?" It builds toward ratio and proportion concepts.

🔙 Back-to-Ten Strategy

Subtract in two steps: first get to 10, then subtract the rest.

13 - 5 = ?
Step 1: 13 - 3 = 10
Step 2: 10 - 2 = ?

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"How is Back-to-Ten related to the Making 10 addition strategy? Why is 10 our 'anchor' in both directions?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Students who have mastered Making 10 for addition. This is the subtraction counterpart! Emphasize number bonds to 10 (3+7, 4+6, etc.) as the foundation. This strategy prepares students for mental math with larger numbers.
