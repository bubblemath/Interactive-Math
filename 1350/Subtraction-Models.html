📖 Definition of Subtraction

For whole numbers a and b where a ≥ b, the difference a − b is the unique whole number c such that:

b + c = a

In other words: subtraction asks "what do I add to b to get a?"

⚠️ Important: Subtraction is NOT Closed

Unlike addition, subtraction is not closed on whole numbers. If a < b, then a − b is not a whole number.

5 − 8 = ? (not a whole number!)

This is why we require a ≥ b in our definition.

🔄 Inverse Relationship

Subtraction and addition are inverse operations. Every subtraction fact has a related addition fact:

12 − 5 = 7 ↔ 5 + 7 = 12

This relationship is the foundation for checking subtraction with addition and for the missing-addend model.

🎓 For Future Teachers

As future teachers, you will encounter students with diverse learning styles. This module presents multiple models and algorithms for subtraction—not because you need to learn subtraction, but because you need an arsenal of techniques to help students visualize how and why subtraction works.

📚 Teaching Tip: Choosing the Right Model

  • Take-Away: Best for "removal" situations (eating cookies, spending money)
  • Missing-Addend: Best for "how much more needed" situations (saving for a toy)
  • Comparison: Best for "how many more/fewer" situations (comparing collections)

Students should experience ALL models to build flexible thinking about subtraction!

⚠️ Borrow Across Zero

When borrowing from a column with 0, we must first borrow from the next column to the left. Try 403 − 156 to see this in action!

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"A student asks why we 'break apart' a long into units. How would you explain that we're not creating new value, just reorganizing?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Kinesthetic learners who need to physically manipulate objects.

  • Always use physical manipulatives before pictures or symbols
  • The "exchange" in concrete model = "borrowing" in standard algorithm
  • Ask: "Do we have enough ones to subtract? If not, what can we do?"
  • Common Error: Students may try to subtract the smaller digit from larger regardless of position

Break down numbers by place value, then subtract each place. This makes the regrouping process explicit and visible.

Best for: Students who need to see the "why" behind regrouping.

  • Great transition from concrete to standard algorithm

⚠️ Special Case: Borrowing Across Zero

Try 403 − 156 to see how we handle borrowing when there's a 0 in the tens place. We must first borrow from hundreds to tens, then from tens to ones.

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"A student consistently does 'smaller from larger' regardless of position (e.g., 52 − 38 = 26). What intervention would you use?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Efficiency once conceptual understanding is established.

  • Always connect "borrowing" back to concrete "exchanging"
  • The crossed-out number shows we "used" some of that place's value
  • Common Errors:
    • "Smaller from larger" error (52 − 38 = 26)
    • Forgetting to reduce after borrowing
    • Borrowing when not needed
  • Only introduce AFTER concrete and expanded algorithms
⚖️ Equal Additions Algorithm

Instead of "borrowing," we add the same amount to BOTH numbers. This doesn't change the difference! Common in many countries outside the US.

💡 Why It Works

If we add the same amount to both numbers, the difference stays the same:

12 − 5 = 7 → (12 + 10) − (5 + 10) = 22 − 15 = 7 ✓

🤔 How Would You Explain This?

"A student asks why adding to both numbers doesn't change the answer. How would you demonstrate this concretely?"

📚 Teaching Tip

Best for: Students who struggle with the "borrowing" concept.

  • No crossing out or reducing—just adding!
  • Use a number line to show the "gap" stays the same when both endpoints move
  • Common in British, European, and Latin American schools
  • Great alternative for students who make borrowing errors
  • Key Insight: We add 10 to the ones place of the top number, and add 1 to the tens place of the bottom number (which is also 10)
