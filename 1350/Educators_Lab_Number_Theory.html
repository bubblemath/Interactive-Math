🔍 Error Analysis

Diagnosing Student Errors

Understanding WHY students make mistakes is crucial for effective teaching. Common errors in number theory often stem from:

  • Confusing GCF and LCM
  • Misunderstanding prime factorization
  • Forgetting special cases (0, 1)
  • Procedural errors in algorithms
Diagnose the Error

🔴 Student Work

A student was asked to find GCF(24, 36).

24 = 2 × 2 × 2 × 3
36 = 2 × 2 × 3 × 3
GCF = 2 × 2 × 2 × 3 × 3 = 72

What error did the student make?

A. Wrong prime factorization
B. Confused GCF with LCM (took MAX instead of MIN exponents)
C. Forgot to include all primes
D. Arithmetic error in multiplication
Common Errors Reference
Error Type Example Intervention
GCF/LCM Confusion Taking MAX for GCF or MIN for LCM Use mnemonics: GCF = "Greatest" = smaller answer; LCM = "Least" but larger answer
1 is Prime Including 1 in prime factorization Emphasize: Prime has EXACTLY 2 factors; 1 has only 1 factor
Missing Factors 12 = 2 × 6 (not fully factored) Continue until ALL factors are prime
GCF(a,0) Error Saying GCF(5,0) = 0 GCF(a,0) = a because every number divides 0
Relatively Prime Confusion Thinking 8 and 15 can't be relatively prime because neither is prime Relatively prime means GCF = 1, not that the numbers are prime

Using Divisibility to Test Primality

From Your Course Materials

"To determine if a number is prime, you must check only divisibility by prime numbers less than √n"

Is 397 prime?

√397 ≈ 19.9, so check primes ≤ 19: {2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13, 17, 19}

• 397 is odd → not divisible by 2

• 3+9+7 = 19, not divisible by 3 → not divisible by 3

• Doesn't end in 0 or 5 → not divisible by 5

• 397 ÷ 7 = 56.7... → not divisible by 7

• 397 ÷ 11 = 36.1... → not divisible by 11

• 397 ÷ 13 = 30.5... → not divisible by 13

• 397 ÷ 17 = 23.4... → not divisible by 17

• 397 ÷ 19 = 20.9... → not divisible by 19

✓ 397 is PRIME!

🌍 Real-World Contexts

GCF vs LCM: When to Use Each

Students often struggle to identify whether a word problem requires GCF or LCM. Here's the key distinction:

  • GCF: DIVIDING or SPLITTING into equal groups
  • LCM: Finding when things COINCIDE or REPEAT together
Identify: GCF or LCM?

📋 Word Problem

Hot dogs are sold 10 to a package. Buns are sold 8 to a package. What is the least number of each you must buy to have an equal number of hot dogs and buns?

Real-World Applications Reference
GCF Applications LCM Applications
  • Splitting items into equal groups
  • Cutting materials into equal pieces
  • Simplifying fractions
  • Arranging items in equal rows
  • Dividing resources fairly
  • When events coincide (schedules)
  • Finding common denominators
  • Buying equal quantities (hot dogs/buns)
  • Repeating patterns
  • Synchronizing cycles
Key words: greatest, largest, divide equally, split, cut, simplify Key words: least, smallest, together again, same time, common

🤔 For Your Teaching Practice

"When teaching word problems, have students identify the KEY WORDS first. Then ask: 'Are we dividing/splitting (GCF) or finding when things happen together (LCM)?'"
