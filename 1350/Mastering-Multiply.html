🎯 Why Teach Strategies?

Memorizing multiplication facts through rote repetition is less effective than understanding the patterns and relationships between numbers. When students understand WHY facts work, they can:

  • Derive forgotten facts from known ones
  • Build number sense and flexibility
  • Connect multiplication to other operations
  • Develop confidence in mathematics

🔢 Skip Counting Strategy

The Concept

Skip counting is counting by a number other than 1. It's the foundation of multiplication!

Example: 4 × 5 means "count by 5s, four times" → 5, 10, 15, 20

Try Skip Counting

🤔 Teaching Connection

"How would you help a student who can skip count by 5s but doesn't connect it to multiplication? What questions would you ask?"

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Start with 2s, 5s, and 10s (easiest patterns)
  • Use songs and rhythms to make skip counting memorable
  • Connect to real objects: "Count the legs on 4 dogs" (4 × 4)
  • Use a number line to visualize the jumps

The Concept

The 2× facts are just doubles from addition! If students know their addition doubles, they already know their 2× facts.

Connection: 2 × 7 = 7 + 7 = 14

"After a student masters doubles, such as 3 + 3, then doubles plus 1 and doubles plus 2, etc. can be learned easily." — From your course materials

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Review addition doubles before introducing 2× facts
  • Use visual pairs (shoes, mittens, eyes) to reinforce
  • This strategy builds on prior knowledge from addition
  • Extend to "doubles plus 1" for 3× facts: 3×7 = 2×7 + 7

🖐️ Fives Facts Strategy

The Concept

The 5× facts have two powerful patterns:

  • Clock Connection: Minutes on a clock! 5×6 = 30 (6 on the clock = :30)
  • Ones Digit Pattern: Products always end in 0 or 5

🤔 Teaching Connection

"How would you help a student who knows 5×6=30 from the clock but can't figure out 5×13? What strategy would you teach them?"

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Use an actual clock as a teaching tool
  • Connect to money: 5 nickels = 25 cents
  • Tally marks come in groups of 5!
  • For larger numbers: 5×13 = 5×10 + 5×3 = 50+15 = 65

9️⃣ Nines Tricks

The Concept

The 9× facts have amazing patterns:

  • Finger Method: Hold up 10 fingers, put down the nth finger for 9×n
  • Digit Sum: The digits of any 9× product add up to 9
  • Tens Digit: The tens digit is always one less than the multiplier
Finger Method

Put down finger #7. Count fingers on each side!

Digit Sum Pattern

The digits of every 9× product add up to 9!

9×1 = 9 → 9
9×2 = 18 → 1+8 = 9
9×3 = 27 → 2+7 = 9
9×4 = 36 → 3+6 = 9
9×5 = 45 → 4+5 = 9
9×6 = 54 → 5+4 = 9
9×7 = 63 → 6+3 = 9
9×8 = 72 → 7+2 = 9
9×9 = 81 → 8+1 = 9

🤔 Teaching Connection

"Which nines trick would you teach first? Why might different students prefer different methods?"

📚 Teaching Tip

  • The finger method is kinesthetic—great for hands-on learners
  • Digit sum helps students check their answers
  • Another trick: 9×n = 10×n - n (e.g., 9×7 = 70-7 = 63)
  • Let students discover patterns themselves when possible

⬛ Square Numbers

The Concept

Square numbers are products where both factors are the same: n × n = n². They form actual squares!

Knowing square numbers helps derive nearby facts: 6×7 = 6×6 + 6 = 36 + 6 = 42

🤔 Teaching Connection

"How can knowing 7×7=49 help a student figure out 7×8? (Answer: 7×8 = 7×7 + 7 = 49 + 7 = 56)"

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Use graph paper to draw actual squares
  • Connect to area: "A 5×5 room has 25 square feet"
  • Square numbers appear on the diagonal of the multiplication table
  • Use squares as "anchor facts" to derive nearby products

📦 Distributive Property Strategy

The Concept

Break apart a harder fact into easier facts you already know!

Formula: a × (b + c) = (a × b) + (a × c)

Example: 7 × 8 = 7 × (5 + 3) = (7 × 5) + (7 × 3) = 35 + 21 = 56

Break Apart a Fact

Enter values and click "Show"

Common Break-Apart Strategies
Break into 5 + something:
7×8 = 7×5 + 7×3 = 35+21 = 56
Break into 10 - something:
7×9 = 7×10 - 7×1 = 70-7 = 63
Use doubles:
6×8 = 6×4 + 6×4 = 24+24 = 48
Use a square:
6×7 = 6×6 + 6×1 = 36+6 = 42

🤔 Teaching Connection

"The distributive property is the foundation for mental math strategies and multi-digit multiplication algorithms." — How would you help students see this connection?

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Let students choose HOW to break apart—there's no single "right" way
  • Use arrays to visualize the break-apart
  • This strategy is especially useful for 6×, 7×, and 8× facts
  • Connects directly to the area model and partial products algorithm

🔄 Commutative Property (Swap Strategy)

The Concept

The commutative property means a × b = b × a. This cuts the number of facts to memorize nearly in half!

Example: If you know 3 × 7 = 21, you automatically know 7 × 3 = 21

Visualize the Swap

Enter values and click "Show Both"

Facts You Get "For Free"

If you know these facts, you automatically know their swaps!

3×7 = 21 ↔ 7×3 = 21
4×6 = 24 ↔ 6×4 = 24
5×8 = 40 ↔ 8×5 = 40
6×9 = 54 ↔ 9×6 = 54
7×8 = 56 ↔ 8×7 = 56
4×9 = 36 ↔ 9×4 = 36

🤔 Teaching Connection

"How would you help a student who knows 5×8=40 but freezes on 8×5? What visual would you use?"

📚 Teaching Tip

  • Use arrays: rotate a 3×7 array to see it's also 7×3
  • Teach students to "swap to the easier fact"
  • Most students find smaller × larger easier (3×7 vs 7×3)
  • This reduces 100 facts to about 55 unique facts!

🎓 Educator's Lab

Teaching Scenarios

Practice matching strategies to student needs. Select the best approach for each situation.

Scenario 1: The Skip Counter

A student can skip count by 5s fluently (5, 10, 15, 20...) but doesn't connect this to multiplication. When asked "What is 4 × 5?" they start over and count all: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6...

What would you do?

Ask: "When you skip count by 5s four times, where do you land?"
Have them memorize the times table
Teach the finger method for 9s

Scenario 2: The Struggling Memorizer

A student has been drilling 7 × 8 for weeks but still can't remember it. They get frustrated and say "I'm just bad at math."

What strategy would you introduce?

More flashcard practice
Distributive property: 7×8 = 7×5 + 7×3 = 35+21
Skip this fact and move on

Scenario 3: The Visual Learner

A student learns best through pictures and diagrams. They're working on square numbers but struggling to see why 6 × 6 = 36.

What would help most?

Explain the definition of multiplication verbally
Have them write 6 × 6 = 36 ten times
Draw a 6×6 grid on graph paper and count the squares

Scenario 4: The Kinesthetic Learner

A student needs to move and use their hands to learn. They're struggling with 9× facts.

What strategy fits best?

Teach the finger method for 9s
Show them the digit sum pattern
Have them read about 9s in a textbook
