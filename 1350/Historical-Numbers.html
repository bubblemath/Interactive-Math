📊 Tally System

~30,000 BCE - Present
Additive (No Base)

The oldest counting method! Simple marks track quantities. Every 5th mark crosses the previous 4 for easy grouping. Still used today for counting votes, inventory, and games.

🏺 Egyptian Hieroglyphic

~3000 BCE
Base 10 (Additive)

Used picture symbols for powers of 10. Purely additive—symbols are repeated and summed. No symbol for zero. Could write numbers up to millions!

📜 Babylonian Cuneiform

~2000 BCE
Base 60 (Positional)

A positional system using base 60! Only two symbols (𒁹 for 1, 𒌋 for 10) but position matters. This is why we have 60 seconds/minute and 360° in a circle!

🏛️ Roman Numerals

~900 BCE - Middle Ages
Base 10 (Additive/Subtractive)

Uses letters I, V, X, L, C, D, M. Mostly additive, but uses subtraction for 4, 9, 40, 90, 400, 900 (IV, IX, XL, XC, CD, CM). Still used for clocks, outlines, and dates!

🌿 Mayan System

~200 CE
Modified Base 20 (Positional)

Uses dots (•=1), bars (—=5), and shells (𝟎=0). Vertical positional system. Third place is 18×20=360 (not 400) to align with their 360-day calendar!

⚠️ Special Rules to Remember

Roman Subtractive Rule

When a smaller value appears BEFORE a larger value, subtract it:

  • IV = 5 - 1 = 4
  • IX = 10 - 1 = 9
  • XL = 50 - 10 = 40
  • XC = 100 - 10 = 90
  • CD = 500 - 100 = 400
  • CM = 1000 - 100 = 900

Babylonian Place Values

Position determines value (like our system, but base 60):

  • 1st place: × 1 (60⁰)
  • 2nd place: × 60 (60¹)
  • 3rd place: × 3,600 (60²)
  • 4th place: × 216,000 (60³)

Mayan Modified Base 20

Vertical stacking with a calendar twist:

  • 1st level: × 1 (20⁰)
  • 2nd level: × 20 (20¹)
  • 3rd level: × 360 (18×20)
  • 4th level: × 7,200 (18×20²)

360 ≈ 365 days in a year!
