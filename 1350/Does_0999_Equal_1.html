The Big Question

0.9 = 1 ?

Is 0.999999... (repeating forever) equal to exactly 1?

📝 From Your Course Materials

Students will be able to understand and explain:

  • "That 0.9 = 1 and investigate various ways to show this is true"

🎯 The Answer: YES!

0.9 = 1 is a mathematical fact, not an approximation!

They are two different ways of writing the exact same number.

Why This Matters for Future Teachers

This concept challenges our intuition and is a perfect example of why mathematical proof is important. As future teachers, you'll encounter students who:

  • Believe 0.999... is "almost" but not "quite" equal to 1
  • Think there must be an "infinitely small" gap
  • Struggle with the concept of infinity

Understanding multiple proofs will help you address these misconceptions!

🔢 Algebraic Proof

📝 The Classic Algebraic Proof

This proof uses the same technique we learned for converting repeating decimals to fractions!

Proof: 0.9 = 1

Step 1: Let x = 0.999999...
Step 2: Multiply both sides by 10:
10x = 9.999999...
Step 3: Subtract the original equation:
10x - x = 9.999... - 0.999...
9x = 9
Step 4: Solve for x:
x = 9/9 = 1
Conclusion: Since x = 0.999... and x = 1, we have:
0.9 = 1 ✓
Why This Works

This proof relies on the fact that:

  • Multiplying by 10 shifts the decimal point one place right
  • The repeating part (999...) is the same in both 10x and x
  • When we subtract, the infinite repeating parts cancel exactly

This is the same technique we use to convert any repeating decimal to a fraction!

💬 Discussion Prompt for Your Classroom

"We used this same method to convert 0.3 to 1/3. Why does it give us exactly 1 when we apply it to 0.9?"

📊 Fraction Proof

📝 The Fraction Proof

This proof is often the most convincing for students because it uses familiar facts about fractions!

Proof Using 1/3

Step 1: We know that:
1/3 = 0.333... = 0.3
Step 2: Multiply both sides by 3:
3 × (1/3) = 3 × 0.333...
Step 3: Simplify the left side:
3/3 = 1
Step 4: Simplify the right side:
3 × 0.333... = 0.999...
Conclusion: Since both sides are equal:
1 = 0.9 ✓

∞ Limit/Series Proof

📝 The Infinite Series Approach

This proof uses the concept of an infinite geometric series. It's more rigorous but requires understanding of limits.

0.999... as an Infinite Sum
0.999... = 0.9 + 0.09 + 0.009 + 0.0009 + ...
= 9/10 + 9/100 + 9/1000 + 9/10000 + ...
= 9/10 + 9/10² + 9/10³ + 9/10⁴ + ...

Geometric Series Formula

Formula: For |r| < 1, the sum of an infinite geometric series is:
a + ar + ar² + ar³ + ... = a/(1-r)
Our series: a = 9/10, r = 1/10
Sum = (9/10) + (9/10)(1/10) + (9/10)(1/10)² + ...
Apply formula:
Sum = (9/10) / (1 - 1/10)
Sum = (9/10) / (9/10)
Result:
Sum = 1
Partial Sums Approach

Watch how the partial sums get closer and closer to 1:

Terms Sum Distance from 1
0.9 0.9 0.1
0.9 + 0.09 0.99 0.01
0.9 + 0.09 + 0.009 0.999 0.001
0.9 + 0.09 + 0.009 + 0.0009 0.9999 0.0001
... (infinitely many terms) 0.999... 0

🔑 Key Insight

As we add more terms, the distance from 1 becomes arbitrarily small. In the limit (with infinitely many terms), the distance is exactly 0.

💬 Discussion Prompt for Your Classroom

"If 0.999... is not equal to 1, what number would be between them? Can you name it?"

⚠️ Common Misconceptions

📝 Why Students Struggle

Understanding why 0.9 = 1 challenges our intuition about infinity and decimal representations. Here are common misconceptions and how to address them.

❌ Misconception 1: "0.999... is close to 1, but not quite"

Student thinking: "There's always a tiny bit missing"

Response: Ask "How much is missing? Can you write that number?" There is no number between 0.999... and 1. If two numbers have no number between them, they are the same number.

❌ Misconception 2: "The 9s never stop, so it never reaches 1"

Student thinking: Confusing the process with the result

Response: 0.999... is not a process of "adding more 9s" - it IS the complete infinite decimal. All infinitely many 9s are already there.

❌ Misconception 3: "There's an infinitely small gap"

Student thinking: Belief in "infinitesimals"

Response: In the real number system, there are no infinitely small positive numbers. If a positive number exists, you can always find a smaller one - but 0.999... leaves no room for any positive gap.

❌ Misconception 4: "0.999... and 1 are different representations"

Student thinking: "They look different, so they must be different"

Response: Many numbers have multiple representations! 1/2 = 2/4 = 0.5 = 0.50 are all the same number. Similarly, 0.999... = 1 are two ways to write the same number.

The "What's Between Them?" Argument

If 0.999... ≠ 1, there must be a number between them. What is it?

Any number you try to put there is either ≤ 0.999... or ≥ 1.
