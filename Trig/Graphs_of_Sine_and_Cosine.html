📖 Lesson 17: Applications of Radian Measure

🌟 Why Does This Matter?

Sine and cosine waves are everywhere in the real world:

  • 🎵 Sound Waves: Music and audio are sinusoidal—amplitude = volume, frequency = pitch
  • 🌊 Ocean Tides: Tidal patterns follow predictable sinusoidal curves
  • ⚡ Electricity: AC current is a sine wave (60 Hz in the US, 50 Hz in Europe)
  • ☀️ Daylight Hours: Seasonal variation in daylight follows a cosine curve
  • 💓 Heartbeats: ECG readings show periodic wave patterns
  • 📻 Radio Waves: All electromagnetic waves are sinusoidal

  • Cosine = the x-coordinate of the point

📊 Key Features of Sine and Cosine

y = sin(x)

  • Starts at (0, 0)
  • Maximum: 1 at x = π/2
  • Minimum: −1 at x = 3π/2
  • Period: 2π
  • Zeros: 0, π, 2π

y = cos(x)

  • Starts at (0, 1)
  • Maximum: 1 at x = 0, 2π
  • Minimum: −1 at x = π
  • Period: 2π
  • Zeros: π/2, 3π/2
💡 Key Insight: Both sine and cosine have the same shape—they're just shifted horizontally! Cosine starts at its maximum (1), while sine starts at zero and goes up. This is because cos(0) = 1 (x-coordinate at angle 0) and sin(0) = 0 (y-coordinate at angle 0).

🔊 Amplitude & Period Workshop

🌟 Why Does This Matter?

Amplitude and period control the "shape" of the wave:

  • Amplitude |A|: How tall the wave is (volume in sound, brightness in light)
  • Period 2π/|B|: How long one complete cycle takes (pitch in sound, color in light)

General Form

y = A · sin(Bx) or y = A · cos(Bx)

Amplitude = |A|     Period = 2π/|B|

📈 Interactive Graph Transformer

Negative A reflects the graph vertically

Larger B = shorter period (compressed)

Sound Wave Equation:

y = 0.5 · sin(2π · 440 · t)

440 Hz = A4 (concert pitch) | Period = 1/440 ≈ 0.00227 seconds

🎵 Musical Connection: A4 (440 Hz) is the standard tuning pitch. Doubling the frequency (880 Hz) gives A5—one octave higher. Halving it (220 Hz) gives A3—one octave lower!

🌓 Sine vs. Cosine Comparison

🌟 The Secret Relationship

Sine and cosine are the same wave, just shifted horizontally!

cos(x) = sin(x + π/2)

This means cosine "leads" sine by π/2 radians (90°).

📊 Side-by-Side Comparison

At θ = 0:    sin(θ) = 0.00   |   cos(θ) = 1.00
