📖 Lesson 14: Complex Numbers

🌟 Why Does This Matter?

Polar form transforms complex number operations from algebraic grinding into elegant geometry:

  • ⚡ Electrical Engineering: AC circuits use "phasors" (polar complex numbers) to represent voltage and current with magnitude and phase angle
  • 🎮 Computer Graphics: Rotations and scaling in 2D graphics are trivially easy in polar form—just multiply magnitudes and add angles!
  • 📡 Signal Processing: The Fourier Transform, which powers everything from MP3s to MRIs, relies on polar complex numbers
  • 🌀 Fractals: The Mandelbrot and Julia sets are generated by iterating complex numbers—polar form reveals their spiral structure
  • ⚛️ Quantum Mechanics: Wave functions are complex-valued, and their polar form separates amplitude from phase

🔄 The Polar Transformation Lab

🌟 Why Does This Matter?

Rectangular form (a + bi) is great for addition, but polar form makes multiplication, division, and powers trivially easy. Converting between forms is the key skill!

Two Ways to Write a Complex Number

Rectangular Form

z = a + bi

Polar Form

z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) = r cis θ

where r = |z| = √(a² + b²) and θ = argument (direction angle)

✨ Geometric Operations Visualizer

🌟 Why Polar Form is Powerful

In polar form, multiplication and division become geometric:

  • Multiplication: Multiply magnitudes, ADD angles
  • Division: Divide magnitudes, SUBTRACT angles

No more FOIL! No more conjugates! Just simple arithmetic on r and θ.

Product and Quotient Rules

Product: z₁ · z₂ = r₁r₂ cis(θ₁ + θ₂)

Quotient: z₁ / z₂ = (r₁/r₂) cis(θ₁ − θ₂)

⚖️ Why It's Easier: Side-by-Side Comparison

❌ Rectangular (FOIL)

(1 + √3i)(√3 + i)

= 1·√3 + 1·i + √3i·√3 + √3i·i

= √3 + i + 3i + √3i²

= √3 + i + 3i − √3

= 0 + 4i

5 steps, messy algebra

✓ Polar (Elegant!)

z₁ = 2 cis 60°

z₂ = 2 cis 30°

z₁ · z₂ = (2)(2) cis(60° + 30°)

= 4 cis 90°

= 4(cos 90° + i sin 90°)

= 4(0 + i) = 4i

Simple arithmetic!

⚡ De Moivre's Theorem: Powers & Roots

🌟 Why Does This Matter?

De Moivre's Theorem makes computing powers and roots of complex numbers incredibly simple:

  • Powers: Just raise r to the power and multiply θ by the power
  • Roots: Find n evenly-spaced points on a circle—beautiful geometry!

De Moivre's Theorem

Powers: [r cis θ]ⁿ = rⁿ cis(nθ)

Roots: ⁿ√z = ⁿ√r cis((θ + 360°k)/n) for k = 0, 1, 2, ..., n−1

🔢 Power Calculator

Raise a complex number to a power and watch it spiral!

z⁴ = [2 cis 30°]⁴

= 2⁴ cis(4 × 30°)

= 16 cis 120°

= 16(cos 120° + i sin 120°)

= −8 + 8√3i ≈ −8 + 13.86i
