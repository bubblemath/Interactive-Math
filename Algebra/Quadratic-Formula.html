🧬 Where Does the Quadratic Formula Come From?

The quadratic formula is not magic! It comes from completing the square on the general quadratic equation. Click through each step to see how it's derived.

\(\displaystyle x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)
1 Start with the general quadratic equation:
\(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\)
This is any quadratic equation where a, b, and c are constants and a ≠ 0.
2 Divide everything by a:
\(x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \frac{c}{a} = 0\)
We need the leading coefficient to be 1 before completing the square.
3 Move the constant to the other side:
\(x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x = -\frac{c}{a}\)
Subtract c/a from both sides to isolate the variable terms.
4 Complete the square — add \(\left(\frac{b}{2a}\right)^2\) to both sides:
\(x^2 + \frac{b}{a}x + \frac{b^2}{4a^2} = -\frac{c}{a} + \frac{b^2}{4a^2}\)
Half of b/a is b/(2a). Square it to get b²/(4a²). Add to BOTH sides!
5 Factor the left side as a perfect square:
\(\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \frac{b^2}{4a^2} - \frac{c}{a}\)
The left side is now a perfect square trinomial!
6 Simplify the right side with a common denominator:
\(\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^2 = \frac{b^2 - 4ac}{4a^2}\)
Multiply c/a by 4a/4a to get 4ac/(4a²), then combine fractions.
7 Take the square root of both sides:
\(x + \frac{b}{2a} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)
Don't forget the ± symbol! Square roots give both positive and negative values.
8 Solve for x:
\(x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)
Subtract b/(2a) from both sides and combine over the common denominator. This is the Quadratic Formula! 🎉

🔍 The Discriminant Explorer

The discriminant is the expression under the square root: \(D = b^2 - 4ac\). It tells us everything about the solutions BEFORE we solve!

Discriminant: \(D = b^2 - 4ac\)

📊 What the Discriminant Tells Us

Discriminant Value Number of Solutions Type of Solutions Graph Behavior
\(D > 0\) 2 solutions Two distinct real numbers Parabola crosses x-axis twice
\(D = 0\) 1 solution One repeated real number Parabola touches x-axis once (vertex)
\(D < 0\) 2 solutions Two complex conjugates Parabola never crosses x-axis

🧩 Factorability Test

Key Insight: A quadratic with integer coefficients is factorable over the integers if and only if:

  1. The discriminant \(D = b^2 - 4ac\) is a perfect square (0, 1, 4, 9, 16, 25, ...)
  2. AND the solutions come out to be rational numbers

Example: For \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\): \(D = 25 - 24 = 1\) ✓ Perfect square! So it factors as \((x-2)(x-3) = 0\)
