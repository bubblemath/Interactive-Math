Precalculus · Parent functions

Nine shapes, each pinned by an anchor and a few key points.

Every transformed graph you'll meet starts from one of these parents. Learn each one's shape, its anchor point — the reference you'll track when it shifts and stretches — and the small set of key points that lock in the curve. Master the patterns now and graphing later becomes bookkeeping.

What to learn for each parent

Shape — the overall look and end behavior.
Anchor — the vertex, cusp, inflection, or asymptote center.
Key points — the pattern that fixes the steepness.
Domain & range — what inputs and outputs are allowed.

Worked examples

Build the curve from its pattern.

Square root · the perfect-square pattern

\(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\)
Anchor
Starts at \((0,0)\) and only goes right — the domain is \(x\ge 0\).
Key points at perfect squares
\((1,1),\ (4,2),\ (9,3)\): take a perfect square in, get its root out.
Shape
A curve that rises quickly then flattens — half of a sideways parabola.

Cubic · the \(1, 8, 27\) pattern

\(f(x)=x^{3}\)
Anchor
Passes through \((0,0)\), its point of inflection where the curve switches bending direction.
Key points
\((1,1),\ (2,8)\) and, because it's odd, \((-1,-1),\ (-2,-8)\).
Shape
Flat at the origin, then steep both ways — an S lying along the line, rising left to right.

Reciprocal · the anchor that isn't on the graph

\(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}\)
Center, not a point
The "anchor" \((0,0)\) is where the asymptotes \(x=0\) and \(y=0\) cross — the graph never touches it.
Key points
\((1,1),\ (2,\tfrac12),\ (\tfrac12,2)\) and the odd reflections \((-1,-1),\ (-2,-\tfrac12),\ (-\tfrac12,-2)\).
Shape
Two branches in opposite quadrants, each hugging both axes.
