Sign charts with two kinds of boundaries.

A rational inequality is a sign chart with a twist. Get everything on one side as a single fraction — never cross‑multiply, the denominator’s sign is unknown — then mark the critical numbers. Zeros of the top can be included; zeros of the bottom are vertical asymptotes that are always excluded. Test one point per interval, then write the answer in interval notation.

01

The plan

One fraction, two kinds of critical numbers, test, brackets.

First get a single fraction on one side compared to 0. If the inequality has a number or another term floating around — like x/(x−2) ≥ 1 — subtract to one side and combine over a common denominator first. Then factor the top and bottom.

Don’t cross‑multiply
Multiplying both sides by the denominator is illegal here: you don’t know its sign, and multiplying an inequality by a negative flips it. Move everything to one side instead and let the sign chart do the work.

Now the critical numbers come in two kinds, and they behave differently at the endpoints:

Top zeros

Where the numerator is 0, the whole fraction is 0. For ≤ or ≥ these are included (closed [ ]); for <,> they’re open.

Bottom zeros (asymptotes)

Where the denominator is 0, the fraction is undefined — so these are always excluded (open ( )), even when the relation is ≤ or ≥.

That mix is the whole point: a ≤ answer can be half‑open, closed where the top is zero but open at an asymptote — like . Repeated factors still don’t flip the sign, which can leave an isolated point or an empty set.
