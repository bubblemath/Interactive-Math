Algebra · Setting up applied problems

Different stories, the same move: build a table, write one equation.

Money, mixtures, shared jobs, and travel look like four unrelated puzzles, but each one becomes a small table of quantities that collapses into a single equation. The skill is reading the story to spot which kind it is, laying out the table, and solving. Do that and the words stop being scary.

Skill 01 · Identify the type

Read for the signal, then name the type.

Each kind of problem announces itself. "Invested at … to earn" is interest. "%/pure/dilute" is mixture. "How long if both work together" is a shared job. "Same distance," "upstream/downstream," or "returns faster" is distance–rate–time. Spot the signal first; the setup follows from the type.

Which type is this?

Type · Interest & money

Split the money so the two interests add to the goal.

Let \(x\) be the amount at one rate; the rest, total minus \(x\), sits at the other. Each piece earns rate × amount, and those earnings add to the target. One variable, one equation.

Watch: use decimals for the rates (\(7\%=0.07\)), and make the second amount \((\text{total}-x)\), not a new variable.

Worked example · Candy's portfolio

Candy has $70,000 to invest for a $2,800 return (an overall 4%). Insured CDs pay 2%; corporate bonds pay 7%. How much in each?
Define
Let \(x=\) dollars in bonds (7%). Then \(70000-x\) is in CDs (2%).
Equation
\[0.02(70000-x) + 0.07x = 2800\]
Solve
\[1400 - 0.02x + 0.07x = 2800 \Rightarrow 0.05x = 1400 \Rightarrow x = 28000\]
Answer & check
$28,000 in bonds, $42,000 in CDs. Check: \(0.02(42000)+0.07(28000)=840+1960=2800.\) ✓

Type · Mixture & concentration

The pure stuff you start with plus what you add equals the pure stuff you end with.

Track the actual amount of juice (or acid, or salt). Each container holds volume × percent of it. Pure juice counts as 100%. The new total volume includes what you poured in.

Watch: pure juice is \(100\%=1\), and the final volume is \((\text{start}+x)\), not just the original.

Worked example · the orange soda

A soda is only 5% orange juice. To be called "natural" it must be at least 10% juice. How much pure juice must be added to 900 gal?
Define
Let \(x=\) gallons of pure juice added.
Equation
Juice in \(+\) juice added \(=\) juice out: \[0.05(900) + 1\cdot x = 0.10(900 + x)\]
Solve
\[45 + x = 90 + 0.10x \Rightarrow 0.90x = 45 \Rightarrow x = 50\]
Answer & check
Add 50 gal. Check: \(\frac{45+50}{900+50}=\frac{95}{950}=0.10.\) ✓

Type · Sharing a job

Add the rates, not the times.

If something takes \(a\) hours alone, its rate is \(\tfrac{1}{a}\) of the job per hour. Working together, the rates add to \(\tfrac{1}{t}\). Solve for \(t\) — and remember to flip back at the end, since the rate is \(\tfrac{1}{t}\), not \(t\).

Watch: the answer is always shorter than either alone. Adding the times (\(4+6\)) is the classic mistake.

Worked example · the spillways

Spillway A lowers the reservoir 1 ft in 4 hours; spillway B does it in 6 hours. With both open, how long?
Rates
A does \(\tfrac14\) per hour, B does \(\tfrac16\) per hour.
Equation
\[\tfrac14 + \tfrac16 = \tfrac{1}{t}\]
Solve
\[\tfrac{3}{12}+\tfrac{2}{12}=\tfrac{5}{12}=\tfrac1t \Rightarrow t=\tfrac{12}{5}=2.4\text{ h}\]
Answer
About 2 hours 24 minutes — faster than either alone. ✓

Type · Distance · rate · time

One row per leg, and \(d = r \cdot t\) holds in each.

Make a row for each leg of the trip with its rate, time, and distance. A current or wind adds to the speed one way and subtracts the other. Equal-distance trips set the two distances equal; total-time trips add the times.

Watch: upstream/into-the-wind is \((\text{speed}-\text{current})\); downstream/with-the-wind is \((\text{speed}+\text{current})\). Keep them on the right legs.

Worked example · the boat & the current

A boat goes upstream for 10 hours, then the same distance downstream in 7 hours. The current is 2 mph. Find the boat's speed in still water.
Set up
Let \(b=\) still-water speed. Upstream rate \(b-2\); downstream rate \(b+2\). Distances equal.
Equation
\[(b-2)\cdot 10 = (b+2)\cdot 7\]
Solve
\[10b-20 = 7b+14 \Rightarrow 3b = 34 \Rightarrow b = \tfrac{34}{3}\approx 11.3\text{ mph}\]

Worked example · the jet (total time)

A jet flies 4,200 km from New York to Los Angeles. The return is 100 km/h faster, and the whole trip takes 13 hours. Find the outbound speed.
Set up
Let \(r=\) outbound speed; return is \(r+100\). Each leg is 4200 km; the times add to 13.
Equation
\[\frac{4200}{r} + \frac{4200}{r+100} = 13\]
Solve
Clear denominators: \(13r^2 - 7100r - 420000 = 0\), giving \(r = 600\) km/h (return 700).
Check
\(\frac{4200}{600}+\frac{4200}{700}=7+6=13.\) ✓
