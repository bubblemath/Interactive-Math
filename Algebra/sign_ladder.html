The digits are the easy part. The sign is the decision.

Almost nobody misses 3 + 5. What people miss is −3 + (−5) — because somewhere along the way they learned that two negatives make a positive, and that is a multiplication rule. This ladder pulls the sign apart from the arithmetic and drills each call on its own: eleven rungs, one decision each, until naming a number and signing an answer both happen without thinking.

How it works

Split the hard call into easy ones

Nothing here is memorized whole. Every hard decision in this unit is two or three small decisions stacked, so the ladder teaches the pieces first and asks for the whole thing later.

01

Start anywhere

Eleven rungs run from a single yes/no call up to a full multi-step expression. Nothing is locked — climb in order, or jump straight to the rung you need. Each opens with its strategy and the reason it exists.

02

Earn each badge

Get four in a row on a rung to earn its badge. Answer with the mouse or press 1–6. One wrong answer resets the streak, so the goal is not to be right eventually — it is to stop deliberating.

03

Rules 6–8 refuse numbers

Three rungs will not let you type an answer. They ask which rule applies, or what sign is coming — nothing else. You cannot skip ahead to computing, which is exactly the habit that hides the error.

The engine of rungs 1–3

Two questions name any real number

Natural, whole, and integer look like three separate things to memorize. They are not. They are the same question asked with two switches: is there anything after the decimal point, and which side of zero are you on. Rung 1 teaches one switch, rung 2 teaches the other, rung 3 asks for both at once.

✋ Two negatives make a positive — when you multiply.

(−3)(−5) = 15, and that is true. −3 + (−5) = −8, and that is also true. The rule students carry from multiplication into addition is the single most expensive error in this unit, and it is why rungs 7 and 8 make you say which rule and what sign before you are allowed to compute anything.

Second trap, smaller but just as reliable: a radical is not automatically irrational. √16 is 4 — natural, whole, integer, rational, real. Rung 4 will test you on this on purpose.

Why this matters

Every later chapter assumes this one is automatic

Sign errors do not announce themselves

A wrong sign produces a perfectly reasonable-looking number. Nothing about 8 tells you it should have been −8. Every other kind of mistake in algebra eventually looks strange; this one never does.

The name picks the method

Whether a number is rational decides whether it has an exact decimal, whether it can sit under a radical unchanged, and whether an answer should be left as a fraction. Naming is not vocabulary. It is the first step of every problem.

It compounds immediately

Combining like terms, distributing, solving equations, slope — each one is integer arithmetic wearing a costume. A student who is slow here is slow in everything downstream, and it reads as being bad at algebra.

This unit looks like it is about definitions and it is not. It is about making two calls quickly and without doubt: what am I holding, and which way does it point. Everything else in the unit — the number-set diagram, the rules boxes, the sign chart — exists to make those two calls automatic. Once they are, the arithmetic was never the hard part.
