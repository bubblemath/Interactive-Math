Math 0232 · Unit B2 · linear inequalities

An inequality is a statement about order.

Solving one is Unit B1 with a single extra question attached to every move: did the order survive? Adding, subtracting and multiplying by a positive leave it alone. Exactly two things turn it around — multiplying or dividing by a negative, and swapping the two sides. This lesson also asks “which way?” three separate times: which way the sign points, which way you read it, and which way you shade.

The engine of the whole lesson

Try it on a statement you already believe

Your notes write 2 < 6 twice with blank space underneath, which is an invitation: multiply it by something positive, then by something negative, and watch what happens.

multiply both sides by2 < 6 becomesthe sign
Positivec > 0 3 6 < 18 stays <order untouched
Negativec < 0 −3 −6 > −18 turns to >order reversed

−6 really is bigger than −18. The sign did not flip because a rule said so — the order genuinely reversed, and the symbol had to change to keep telling the truth. There is a second, quieter reverser too: writing 2 < x as x > 2. Nothing was multiplied there either. You just walked around to the other side of the sign, and the sign turned to follow you.

✋ The sign turns for exactly two reasons — and a stray minus is not one of them.

Your lab pops up a red alert when you forget to flip. The quieter and more common error runs the other way: flipping when nothing called for it. x − 6 > −1 contains two minus signs and flips nothing at all, because you only ever add 6 to both sides. In 2 − 3x > 11 the flip happens once, at the very end when you divide by −3 — not when you subtract the 2.

Second: read the sign from x's side. −3 > x says x is less than −3. The mouth of the symbol opens toward the larger quantity, wherever that happens to be sitting, and the answer to “which way do I shade” follows from that and nothing else.

Third: three parts means all three parts. Subtracting 6 from the middle of −4 < 2x + 6 ≤ 12 means subtracting it from the −4 and the 12 as well. And if you divide a three-part inequality by a negative, both signs reverse — then rewrite it smaller-to-larger, or you are left with the correct but unreadable 1 ≥ x > −3.

Why this matters

Direction errors survive every check you might run

The number is usually right

A flipped sign gives you the correct boundary and the wrong half of the line. Everything about the arithmetic looks fine, so nothing prompts you to look again — the only defence is deciding the direction deliberately.

Testing one point settles it

Pick any number in your answer's shaded region and put it back into the original. If it does not make a true statement, the direction is backwards. Thirty seconds, and it catches every flip error you will ever make.

Domains and constraints are inequalities

Restricted domains, feasible regions, error bounds, tolerance ranges — the interesting mathematics later is mostly written in inequalities. Reading them fluently is not a detour.

Everything you learned about solving equations still applies here without a single change. Tidy each side, clear the fractions, gather the variable on one side, divide last. The one new thing is a question you now ask after every move — did that reverse the order? — and the honest answer is almost always no. Knowing precisely when it is yes is the whole lesson.
