Solving polynomial equations

Four ways to break a polynomial apart — and finish the equation.

Every equation here ends the same way: factor the left side, set it equal to zero, and read off the solutions. The hard part is spotting which tool the structure is asking for. This page drills that recognition, then lets you practice until it's automatic.

Technique 01 · Greatest common factor

Pull out the greatest common factor first — always.

Before reaching for any other method, check whether every term shares a common factor: a number, a power of the variable, or both. Factoring it out usually leaves something simpler (often a quadratic) that you can factor again. When the common factor includes a power of \(x\), one of your solutions will be \(x = 0\).

Spot it when: every term divides by the same monomial — e.g. each term has an \(x\), or all coefficients share a factor.

Form

\[ a x^{n} + b x^{n-1} + \dots = x^{k}\big(\text{remaining factor}\big) = 0 \]

After factoring, apply the Zero Product Property: if a product is \(0\), at least one factor is \(0\). Solve each factor separately.

Worked example

Solve \(\;2x^3 + 8x^2 + 6x = 0\)
Step 1 · Find the GCF
Each term divides by \(2x\). \[2x^3+8x^2+6x = 2x\,(x^2+4x+3)\]
Step 2 · Factor what remains
The quadratic factors cleanly: \[2x\,(x+1)(x+3) = 0\]
Step 3 · Zero product property
Set each factor to zero: \(2x=0,\;\; x+1=0,\;\; x+3=0\).
Solutions
\[x = 0,\quad x = -1,\quad x = -3\]

Technique 02 · Sum & difference of cubes

Recognize a perfect cube minus (or plus) a perfect cube.

When you see one cube added to or subtracted from another, two fixed formulas do all the work. The trick is the signs, and the mnemonic SOAP nails them: the binomial keeps the Same sign, the middle term of the trinomial takes the Opposite sign, and the last term is Always Positive. The trinomial factor almost never gives real solutions, so the real answer usually comes from the binomial alone.

Spot it when: two terms, both perfect cubes (1, 8, 27, 64, \(x^3\), \(8x^3\)…), joined by \(+\) or \(-\).

The two formulas · SOAP

\[ a^3 - b^3 = (a-b)(a^2+ab+b^2) \] \[ a^3 + b^3 = (a+b)(a^2-ab+b^2) \]

Same sign · Opposite sign · Always Positive last term.

Worked example

Solve \(\;8x^3 - 27 = 0\)
Step 1 · Identify the cubes
\(8x^3=(2x)^3\) and \(27=3^3\), so \(a=2x,\; b=3\) — a difference of cubes.
Step 2 · Apply the formula (SOAP)
\[(2x-3)\big((2x)^2+(2x)(3)+3^2\big)=0\]\[(2x-3)(4x^2+6x+9)=0\]
Step 3 · Solve each factor
\(2x-3=0 \Rightarrow x=\tfrac{3}{2}\). The trinomial has discriminant \(6^2-4(4)(9)<0\) → no real roots (two complex).
Real solution
\[x = \tfrac{3}{2}\]

Technique 03 · Factor by grouping

Four terms? Split into pairs and look for a shared bracket.

For polynomials of degree higher than two — typically four terms — group the terms in pairs, factor a GCF out of each pair, and watch for an identical binomial to appear. That shared binomial is what lets you factor again. The result often breaks down further (a difference of squares, say), giving you all the solutions.

Spot it when: you have four terms and no single GCF across all of them — but the pairs cooperate.

The pattern

\[ x^3 + ax^2 + bx + ab \;=\; x^2(x+a) + b(x+a) \;=\; (x+a)(x^2+b) \]

The repeated factor \((x+a)\) is the signal that grouping worked. If \(x^2+b\) is a difference of squares, factor it again.

Worked example

Solve \(\;x^3 + 2x^2 - 9x - 18 = 0\)
Step 1 · Group into pairs
\[(x^3+2x^2) + (-9x-18)=0\]
Step 2 · GCF of each pair
\[x^2(x+2) - 9(x+2)=0\]
Step 3 · Factor out the shared binomial
\[(x+2)(x^2-9)=0\]
Step 4 · Keep going — difference of squares
\[(x+2)(x-3)(x+3)=0\]
Solutions
\[x=-2,\quad x=3,\quad x=-3\]

Technique 04 · U-substitution to quadratic form

Hidden quadratics: rename a chunk as \(u\), solve, then substitute back.

An equation is in quadratic form when the exponent on the leading term is exactly twice the exponent on the middle term — like \(x^4\) and \(x^2\), or \(x\) and \(\sqrt{x}\). Let \(u\) stand for the smaller-power piece, and the equation collapses into an ordinary quadratic you already know how to solve. The step everyone forgets is the last one: substitute back and solve for the original variable.

Spot it when: three terms where the first exponent is double the second — \(x^4{-}5x^2{+}4\), \(x{-}7\sqrt{x}{+}12\), \(x^6{-}9x^3{+}8\).

The move

\[ x^4 - 13x^2 + 36 = 0 \;\xrightarrow{\;u=x^2\;}\; u^2 - 13u + 36 = 0 \]

Solve for \(u\) first. Then back-substitute \(u=x^2\) and solve each piece for \(x\). One \(u\) can give two \(x\)'s.

Worked example

Solve \(\;x^4 - 13x^2 + 36 = 0\)
Step 1 · Choose the substitution
Let \(u=x^2\). Then \(x^4=u^2\): \[u^2 - 13u + 36 = 0\]
Step 2 · Solve the quadratic in \(u\)
\[(u-4)(u-9)=0 \Rightarrow u=4,\; u=9\]
Step 3 · Back-substitute
\(u=x^2\), so \(x^2=4\) and \(x^2=9\).
Solutions
\[x=\pm 2,\quad x=\pm 3\]

Worked example · the \(\sqrt{x}\) case

Solve \(\;x - 7\sqrt{x} + 12 = 0\)
Step 1 · Substitute
Let \(u=\sqrt{x}\). Then \(x=u^2\): \[u^2 - 7u + 12 = 0\]
Step 2 · Solve for \(u\)
\[(u-3)(u-4)=0 \Rightarrow u=3,\;u=4\]
Step 3 · Back-substitute
\(\sqrt{x}=3 \Rightarrow x=9\); \(\;\sqrt{x}=4 \Rightarrow x=16\).
Solutions
\[x=9,\quad x=16\]

Cheat sheet

Keep this in view while you practice.

GCF

Factor the largest shared monomial out of every term first. A common \(x\) means \(x=0\) is a solution.

Cubes · SOAP

\(a^3\pm b^3=(a\pm b)(a^2\mp ab+b^2)\). Same, Opposite, Always Positive. Usually only one real solution.

Grouping

Four terms → pair them, GCF each pair, factor the repeated binomial. Then factor further if you can.

U-substitution

First exponent double the second → let \(u\) = the smaller piece, solve the quadratic, back-substitute.
