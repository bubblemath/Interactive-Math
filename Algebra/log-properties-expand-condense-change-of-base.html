Logarithms · the algebra of logs

Three rules turn one log into many — and back again.

Logarithms inherit three properties straight from the laws of exponents. They let you expand a single complicated log into a sum and difference of simple ones, condense a pile of log terms back into one, and — with change of base — rewrite any log so a calculator can finish it.

\begin{aligned}\log_b(MN)&=\log_b M+\log_b N\\[2pt] \log_b\frac{M}{N}&=\log_b M-\log_b N\\[2pt] \log_b\!\left(M^{p}\right)&=p\,\log_b M\end{aligned}

The toolkit

Three properties, each born from an exponent law

Every rule below is just an exponent law read through a logarithm. Multiplying powers adds exponents → the product rule. Dividing subtracts → the quotient rule. A power of a power multiplies → the power rule. The colours here are used consistently across the whole page.

Product rule
$\log_b(MN)=\log_b M+\log_b N$
A product inside becomes a sum outside. $\log_2(8\cdot4)=\log_2 8+\log_2 4=3+2=5$.
Quotient rule
$\log_b\dfrac{M}{N}=\log_b M-\log_b N$
A quotient becomes a difference. $\log_2\dfrac{8}{4}=3-2=1$.
Power rule
$\log_b\!\left(M^{p}\right)=p\,\log_b M$
An exponent drops to the front as a coefficient. $\log_2(4^{3})=3\log_2 4=3\cdot2=6$.
CARRY OVER FROM BEFORE

Two values you already know stay handy while simplifying: $\log_b 1 = 0$ and $\log_b b = 1$. They let stray pieces collapse — for instance $\log_5 5 + \log_3 x = 1 + \log_3 x$.

Expanding

Break one log into many

To expand, peel the outermost structure first: a fraction splits into a difference, a product splits into a sum, and finally each exponent steps out front. Work from the outside in and the expression unravels in a fixed order.

WORKED · $\log_b\dfrac{x^{2}y}{z}$
1
Quotient rule on the fraction: $\log_b\dfrac{x^{2}y}{z}=\log_b\!\left(x^{2}y\right)-\log_b z$.
2
Product rule on $x^{2}y$: $=\log_b\!\left(x^{2}\right)+\log_b y-\log_b z$.
3
Power rule drops the exponent: $=2\log_b x+\log_b y-\log_b z$. Fully expanded.

Try it yourself. Click through a random expression one property at a time — the flag shows which rule fired. Read the steps bottom-to-top and you're condensing instead.

Condensing

Collapse many logs into one

Condensing runs the rules backward, and order matters again — but reversed. First send every coefficient back up as an exponent (so each term is a single clean log), then fold sums into one numerator and differences into the denominator.

WORKED · $3\log_b x+\log_b y-2\log_b z$
1
Power rule (reverse) — coefficients become exponents: $\log_b\!\left(x^{3}\right)+\log_b y-\log_b\!\left(z^{2}\right)$.
2
Product rule (reverse) — the two added logs join as a product: $\log_b\!\left(x^{3}y\right)-\log_b\!\left(z^{2}\right)$.
3
Quotient rule (reverse) — the subtraction becomes a fraction: $\log_b\dfrac{x^{3}y}{z^{2}}$. One single log.
KEY HABIT

Coefficients must become exponents before you combine. Writing $2\log_b x$ as part of a product without first making it $\log_b x^{2}$ is the most common condensing slip. A leftover coefficient out front means you're not done.

Change of base

Rewrite any log so a calculator can finish it

Calculators only speak two logs natively: $\ln$ (base $e$) and $\log$ (base $10$). The change-of-base formula rewrites $\log_b x$ in either one:

$\log_b x=\dfrac{\log_c x}{\log_c b}=\dfrac{\ln x}{\ln b}=\dfrac{\log x}{\log b}$

The new base $c$ is yours to choose; $e$ and $10$ are simply the ones your calculator already knows. For example $\log_2 7=\dfrac{\ln 7}{\ln 2}\approx\dfrac{1.9459}{0.6931}\approx 2.807$.

Illegal moves

There is no rule for these

The three properties only fire on a product, quotient, or power inside a single log. Every tempting shortcut below breaks that and is false in general — plug in numbers and they fall apart.

✗  $\log_b(M+N)=\log_b M+\log_b N$
There is no rule for the log of a sum. The product rule splits multiplication, not addition. Check: $\log_2(4+4)=3$, but $\log_2 4+\log_2 4=4$.
✗  $\log_b(M-N)=\log_b M-\log_b N$
The quotient rule needs division, not subtraction. $\log_b M-\log_b N$ equals $\log_b\frac{M}{N}$, not $\log_b(M-N)$.
✗  $\dfrac{\log_b M}{\log_b N}=\log_b\dfrac{M}{N}$
Dividing two separate logs is the change-of-base pattern, not the quotient rule. The quotient rule is a difference: $\log_b M-\log_b N$.
✗  $\left(\log_b M\right)^{p}=p\,\log_b M$
The power rule needs the exponent on the argument inside: $\log_b(M^{p})=p\log_b M$. An exponent on the whole log stays put.
✗  $\log_b(MN)=\log_b M\cdot\log_b N$
A product inside becomes a sum, never a product of logs. $\log_b(MN)=\log_b M+\log_b N$.
✗  $\log_b\dfrac{M}{N}=\dfrac{\log_b M}{\log_b N}$
A quotient inside becomes a difference. The ratio of two logs is a different object entirely.

Drill every move
