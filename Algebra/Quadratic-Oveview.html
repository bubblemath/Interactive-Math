📚 Overview: Four Methods for Solving Quadratics

Every quadratic equation can be written in standard form: \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\) where \(a \neq 0\)

There are four main methods to solve quadratics. The key is choosing the most efficient method!

The Quadratic Formula: \(\displaystyle x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)

🔮 The Discriminant: Your Decision Helper

Discriminant: \(D = b^2 - 4ac\)

  • \(D > 0\): Two distinct real solutions
  • \(D = 0\): One repeated real solution
  • \(D < 0\): Two complex (imaginary) solutions

💡 Key Insight: If \(D\) is a perfect square (0, 1, 4, 9, 16, 25...), the quadratic is factorable over the integers!

🗺️ Method Selection Flowchart

1 Is there no middle term (\(b = 0\))?
Example: \(x^2 - 16 = 0\) → Use Square Root Method
2 Is it already in \((x + h)^2 = k\) form?
Example: \((x - 3)^2 = 25\) → Use Square Root Method
3 Is the discriminant a perfect square?
If yes → Try Factoring
4 Need vertex form or prefer step-by-step?
Use Completing the Square
5 Otherwise...
Use the Quadratic Formula — it always works!

🧩 Solving by Factoring (AC Method)

The AC Method Steps:

  1. Write in standard form: \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\)
  2. Multiply \(a \cdot c\)
  3. Find two numbers: product = \(ac\), sum = \(b\)
  4. Split the middle term
  5. Factor by grouping
  6. Use Zero Product Property to solve

Example 1: When \(a = 1\)

\(x^2 + 7x + 12 = 0\)
1 Identify: \(a = 1\), \(b = 7\), \(c = 12\)
Calculate \(a \cdot c = 1 \cdot 12 = 12\)
2 Find factor pairs of 12:
FactorsSum
1 × 1213
2 × 68
3 × 47 ✓
We need product = 12 and sum = 7. The numbers are 3 and 4!
3 Split middle term: \(x^2 + 3x + 4x + 12 = 0\)
Replace \(7x\) with \(3x + 4x\)
4 Group: \((x^2 + 3x) + (4x + 12) = 0\)
Group the first two and last two terms
5 Factor each group: \(x(x + 3) + 4(x + 3) = 0\)
Factor out GCF from each group
6 Factor out common binomial: \((x + 3)(x + 4) = 0\)
Both groups share \((x + 3)\)
7 Solve using Zero Product Property:
\(x + 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -3\)
\(x + 4 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -4\)

Solutions: \(x = -3\) or \(x = -4\)

Example 2: When \(a \neq 1\)

\(2x^2 + 7x + 3 = 0\)
1 Identify: \(a = 2\), \(b = 7\), \(c = 3\)
Calculate \(a \cdot c = 2 \cdot 3 = 6\)
2 Find factor pairs of 6:
FactorsSum
1 × 67 ✓
2 × 35
We need product = 6 and sum = 7. The numbers are 1 and 6!
3 Split middle term: \(2x^2 + 1x + 6x + 3 = 0\)
4 Group and factor: \(x(2x + 1) + 3(2x + 1) = 0\)
5 Factor out common binomial: \((2x + 1)(x + 3) = 0\)
6 Solve:
\(2x + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -\frac{1}{2}\)
\(x + 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = -3\)

Solutions: \(x = -\frac{1}{2}\) or \(x = -3\)

Special Case: Difference of Two Squares

Pattern: \(a^2 - b^2 = (a + b)(a - b)\)

\(x^2 - 25 = 0\)
1 Recognize the pattern: \(x^2 - 25 = x^2 - 5^2\)
Both terms are perfect squares with subtraction
2 Apply the formula: \((x + 5)(x - 5) = 0\)
3 Solve:
Solutions: \(x = 5\) or \(x = -5\)

√ Square Root Method

Key Principle: If \((expression)^2 = k\), then \(expression = \pm\sqrt{k}\)

⚠️ Always remember the ± symbol! Square roots give both positive and negative values.

Type 1: \(x^2 = A\)

\(x^2 = 49\)
1 The squared term is already isolated
2 Take the square root of both sides: \(x = \pm\sqrt{49}\)
3 Simplify:
Solutions: \(x = 7\) or \(x = -7\)

Type 2: \(x^2 - a = 0\)

\(x^2 - 16 = 0\)
1 Isolate the squared term: \(x^2 = 16\)
Add 16 to both sides
2 Take the square root: \(x = \pm\sqrt{16}\)
3 Simplify:
Solutions: \(x = 4\) or \(x = -4\)

Type 3: \((x - a)^2 = b\)

\((x - 2)^2 = 36\)
1 The squared binomial is already isolated
2 Take the square root: \(x - 2 = \pm 6\)
3 Solve both equations:
\(x - 2 = 6 \Rightarrow x = 8\)
\(x - 2 = -6 \Rightarrow x = -4\)
4
Solutions: \(x = 8\) or \(x = -4\)

Type 4: \((x - a)^2 + b = c\)

\((x + 5)^2 - 18 = 0\)
1 Isolate the squared term: \((x + 5)^2 = 18\)
Add 18 to both sides
2 Take the square root: \(x + 5 = \pm\sqrt{18}\)
3 Simplify the radical: \(x + 5 = \pm 3\sqrt{2}\)
\(\sqrt{18} = \sqrt{9 \cdot 2} = 3\sqrt{2}\)
4 Solve for x:
Solutions: \(x = -5 + 3\sqrt{2}\) or \(x = -5 - 3\sqrt{2}\)
(Approximately \(x \approx -0.76\) or \(x \approx -9.24\))

⬜ Completing the Square

The Magic Number: To complete the square, add \(\left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2\) to both sides.

This transforms \(x^2 + bx\) into the perfect square \(\left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2\)

\(x^2 + bx + \left(\frac{b}{2}\right)^2 = \left(x + \frac{b}{2}\right)^2\)

Example 1: \(a = 1\), Even \(b\)

\(x^2 + 8x + 7 = 0\)
1 Move the constant: \(x^2 + 8x = -7\)
2 Find the magic number: \(\left(\frac{8}{2}\right)^2 = 4^2 = 16\)
3 Add 16 to BOTH sides: \(x^2 + 8x + 16 = -7 + 16\)
4 Factor the perfect square: \((x + 4)^2 = 9\)
5 Take square root: \(x + 4 = \pm 3\)
6 Solve:
\(x = -4 + 3 = -1\) or \(x = -4 - 3 = -7\)

Solutions: \(x = -1\) or \(x = -7\)

Example 2: \(a = 1\), Odd \(b\) (Fractions!)

\(x^2 + 5x - 3 = 0\)
1 Move the constant: \(x^2 + 5x = 3\)
2 Find the magic number: \(\left(\frac{5}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{25}{4}\)
Odd \(b\) means we'll have fractions — that's okay!
3 Add \(\frac{25}{4}\) to both sides: \(x^2 + 5x + \frac{25}{4} = 3 + \frac{25}{4}\)
4 Simplify right side: \(\left(x + \frac{5}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{12}{4} + \frac{25}{4} = \frac{37}{4}\)
5 Take square root: \(x + \frac{5}{2} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{37}}{2}\)
6 Solve:
Solutions: \(x = \frac{-5 + \sqrt{37}}{2}\) or \(x = \frac{-5 - \sqrt{37}}{2}\)
(Approximately \(x \approx 0.54\) or \(x \approx -5.54\))

Example 3: \(a \neq 1\)

\(2x^2 + 12x + 5 = 0\)
1 Divide everything by \(a = 2\): \(x^2 + 6x + \frac{5}{2} = 0\)
We need the leading coefficient to be 1
2 Move the constant: \(x^2 + 6x = -\frac{5}{2}\)
3 Find the magic number: \(\left(\frac{6}{2}\right)^2 = 9\)
4 Add 9 to both sides: \(x^2 + 6x + 9 = -\frac{5}{2} + 9 = \frac{13}{2}\)
5 Factor and take square root: \((x + 3)^2 = \frac{13}{2}\)
\(x + 3 = \pm\sqrt{\frac{13}{2}} = \pm\frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}\)
6 Solve:
Solutions: \(x = -3 + \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}\) or \(x = -3 - \frac{\sqrt{26}}{2}\)
(Approximately \(x \approx -0.45\) or \(x \approx -5.55\))

🧪 The Quadratic Formula

\(\displaystyle x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)

The Discriminant \(D = b^2 - 4ac\) tells you what to expect:

  • \(D > 0\): Two distinct real solutions
  • \(D = 0\): One repeated real solution
  • \(D < 0\): Two complex (imaginary) solutions

Example 1: Two Real Solutions (\(D > 0\))

\(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\)
1 Identify: \(a = 1\), \(b = -5\), \(c = 6\)
2 Calculate discriminant: \(D = (-5)^2 - 4(1)(6) = 25 - 24 = 1\)
\(D = 1 > 0\) → Two real solutions! Also, 1 is a perfect square, so this is factorable.
3 Substitute: \(x = \frac{-(-5) \pm \sqrt{1}}{2(1)} = \frac{5 \pm 1}{2}\)
4 Solve both branches:
\(x = \frac{5 + 1}{2} = 3\) or \(x = \frac{5 - 1}{2} = 2\)

Solutions: \(x = 3\) or \(x = 2\)

Example 2: One Real Solution (\(D = 0\))

\(x^2 + 6x + 9 = 0\)
1 Identify: \(a = 1\), \(b = 6\), \(c = 9\)
2 Calculate discriminant: \(D = 6^2 - 4(1)(9) = 36 - 36 = 0\)
\(D = 0\) → One repeated solution! This is a perfect square trinomial.
3 Substitute: \(x = \frac{-6 \pm \sqrt{0}}{2(1)} = \frac{-6 \pm 0}{2}\)
4 Solve:
\(x = \frac{-6}{2} = -3\)

Solution: \(x = -3\) (repeated)

Example 3: Two Imaginary Solutions (\(D < 0\))

\(x^2 + 4x + 5 = 0\)
1 Identify: \(a = 1\), \(b = 4\), \(c = 5\)
2 Calculate discriminant: \(D = 4^2 - 4(1)(5) = 16 - 20 = -4\)
\(D = -4 < 0\) → Two complex solutions! The parabola doesn't cross the x-axis.
3 Substitute: \(x = \frac{-4 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2(1)} = \frac{-4 \pm 2i}{2}\)
\(\sqrt{-4} = \sqrt{4} \cdot \sqrt{-1} = 2i\)
4 Simplify:
Solutions: \(x = -2 + i\) or \(x = -2 - i\)
These are complex conjugates

🔄 Side-by-Side Comparison: Factoring vs. Quadratic Formula

When a quadratic IS factorable, both methods give the same answer. Let's compare!

Solve: \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = 0\)

🧩 Factoring Method

Step 1: Find factors of 6 that add to 5
→ 2 and 3 ✓
Step 2: Factor
\((x + 2)(x + 3) = 0\)
Step 3: Solve
\(x = -2\) or \(x = -3\)
⏱️ ~30 seconds

🧪 Quadratic Formula

Step 1: Identify \(a=1, b=5, c=6\)
Step 2: \(D = 25 - 24 = 1\)
Step 3: \(x = \frac{-5 \pm 1}{2}\)
Step 4: \(x = \frac{-4}{2} = -2\) or \(x = \frac{-6}{2} = -3\)
⏱️ ~60 seconds

💡 Key Insight: Both methods give \(x = -2\) or \(x = -3\), but factoring was faster!

When the discriminant is a perfect square, try factoring first. Save the quadratic formula for when factoring doesn't work.

🎯 Choosing the Best Method

Strategy Guide: Use these questions to pick the most efficient method:

  1. Is \(b = 0\)? → Square Root Method
  2. Is it already in \((x + h)^2 = k\) form? → Square Root Method
  3. Is the discriminant a perfect square? → Factoring
  4. Need vertex form? → Completing the Square
  5. None of the above? → Quadratic Formula
