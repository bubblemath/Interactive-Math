The missing factor was never missing.

Give a polynomial real coefficients and a complex root a + bi drags its conjugate a − bi along with it; give it rational coefficients and an irrational root p + q√d forces p − q√d. The degree tells you how many roots to expect, so you can spot the one that’s been left out — then multiply the conjugate pair into a tidy real quadratic and build the polynomial in standard form.

01

Why a partner is forced

Two conjugate theorems, one shortcut, and a degree count.

A polynomial with real coefficients can’t have a lone complex root, and one with rational coefficients can’t have a lone root like p + q√d. The imaginary part or the radical part has to cancel somewhere, and the only way that happens is if the conjugate is a root too.

Complex conjugates

If a + bi is a root (real coefficients), so is a − bi — its mirror image across the real axis.

Irrational conjugates

If p + q√d is a root (rational coefficients), so is p − q√d — symmetric about p on the real axis.

The conjugate-pair shortcut

Each pair multiplies to a real quadratic built from their sum and product — no need to FOIL with i every time.

Finally, the degree is a head count: a degree‑4 polynomial has four roots. List what you’re given, add the forced conjugate, and the total has to land on the degree — if it’s short, you’ve found what’s missing.

Match the polynomial

Which standard form has exactly these roots?

Building polynomials from conjugate roots, for precalculus & college algebra. With real coefficients, complex roots occur in a ± bi pairs; with rational coefficients, irrational roots occur in p ± q√d pairs. Supply the missing conjugate, collapse each pair into a real quadratic x² − 2ax + (a²+b²) or x² − 2px + (p²−q²d), and multiply through to standard form.
