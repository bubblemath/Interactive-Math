Why working by hand wins

The calculator skips the exact muscle algebra demands

A calculator turns every fraction into a decimal and every decimal into a black box. That's fine for a grocery receipt and fatal for algebra, because the moves you'll lean on later are the by-hand fraction moves:

Factoring lives inside reducing

Seeing the shared 6 in 18/24 is the same eye that spots the shared $(x-2)$ in $(x^2-x-2)/(x^2-5x+6)$. Decimals hide factors; by hand, you hunt them.

Common denominators become LCDs

Rebuilding $\tfrac12+\tfrac13$ as twelfths is exactly how you'll combine $\tfrac{1}{x-1}+\tfrac{1}{x+2}$. The habit transfers; the decimal answer doesn't.

Reciprocals power division

"Flip and multiply" on numbers is the identical move on $\tfrac{x}{x-1}\div\tfrac{x}{x+1}$. There's no decimal shortcut once a variable shows up.

Estimation matters too: a student who senses $\tfrac58$ sits just above $\tfrac12$ will catch a wrong answer a calculator would have rubber-stamped. That number sense is yours only if you build it.
