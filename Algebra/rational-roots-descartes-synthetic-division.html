01

Four tools, one pipeline

Each step narrows the search or shrinks the polynomial.

Finding the roots of a cubic, quartic, or quintic by hand isn’t guesswork — it’s a sequence of tools that hand off to one another. You build a finite list of rational candidates, prune it with a sign count, test the survivors with synthetic division, and each hit drops the degree by one until a quadratic is left.

Rational Roots
List every possible rational root: ±p/q, where p divides the constant and q the leading coefficient.
Descartes
Count sign changes in P(x) and P(−x) to bound how many positive and negative real roots to expect.
Synthetic Division
Test a candidate; a zero remainder means it’s a root, and you peel off that factor, leaving a smaller polynomial.
The Quadratic
Once degree 2 remains, factor it or use the quadratic formula — this is where irrational and complex roots appear.

The last step is the conjugate-pair idea in reverse: an irreducible quadratic with a negative discriminant hands you a complex pair a ± bi, and a positive non-square discriminant hands you an irrational pair p ± q√d.
