Modeling with exponentials & logs

Word problems: name the model, then solve for the unknown.

Applied exponential problems all follow one rhythm — recognize the situation, write the matching formula, list what's known, and isolate the unknown. Solving for an amount is plug-and-chug; solving for time or a rate is where logarithms earn their keep.

Choose a model

Four formulas cover almost everything

Before any algebra, match the scenario to one of these. The words give it away: compounded quarterly, compounded continuously, grows 4% per year, half-life.

Compound interest · periodic
$A=P\left(1+\dfrac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$
Interest added $n$ times per year. Knowns vary; solve for $A$, $P$, $t$, or $r$.
Continuous
$A=Pe^{rt}$
"Compounded continuously," or any steadily-compounding quantity.
Growth / decay
$A=A_0e^{kt}=A_0(1\pm r)^{t}$
Populations, cultures, values. $k=\ln(1\pm r)$ links the two forms.
Half-life / doubling
$A=A_0\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{t/h}=A_0\,2^{\,t/d}$
Radioactive decay, dating, doubling. $h$ = half-life, $d$ = doubling time.

Compound interest

Periodic compounding

Use $A=P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$. Write the rate as a decimal, and let $n$ be how many times per year interest is added: annually $1$, semiannually $2$, quarterly $4$, monthly $12$, daily $365$.

FUTURE VALUE
Invest \$3,000 at 5% compounded quarterly for 8 years.
1
P = 3000r = 0.05n = 4t = 8
2
$A=3000\left(1+\tfrac{0.05}{4}\right)^{4\cdot 8}=3000(1.0125)^{32}$.
3
$A\approx \$4{,}464.39$.
SOLVE FOR TIME · needs a log
How long for \$3,000 to grow to \$5,000 at 5% compounded quarterly?
1
$5000=3000(1.0125)^{4t}$, so $(1.0125)^{4t}=\tfrac{5}{3}$.
2
Take $\ln$: $4t\ln(1.0125)=\ln\tfrac{5}{3}$.
3
$t=\dfrac{\ln(5/3)}{4\ln(1.0125)}\approx \mathbf{10.28\ \text{years}}$.
SOLVE FOR RATE · needs a root
\$3,000 grows to \$5,000 in 8 years compounded quarterly. What annual rate?
1
$\left(1+\tfrac{r}{4}\right)^{32}=\dfrac{5000}{3000}=\dfrac{5}{3}$.
2
Take the 32nd root: $1+\tfrac{r}{4}=\left(\tfrac{5}{3}\right)^{1/32}$.
3
$r=4\!\left[\left(\tfrac{5}{3}\right)^{1/32}-1\right]\approx 0.0644=\mathbf{6.44\%}$.

Present value reverses step 1: solve $A=P(1+\frac{r}{n})^{nt}$ for $P=\dfrac{A}{(1+r/n)^{nt}}$.

Continuous compounding

When interest is "compounded continuously," use $A=Pe^{rt}$. Solving for the rate or the time brings in the natural log directly.

FUTURE VALUE
\$2,500 at 4.5% compounded continuously for 10 years.
1
$A=2500\,e^{0.045\cdot 10}=2500\,e^{0.45}$.
2
$A\approx \$3{,}920.78$.
SOLVE FOR TIME
When does \$2,500 reach \$4,000 at 4.5% continuous?
1
$4000=2500\,e^{0.045t}\Rightarrow e^{0.045t}=1.6$.
2
$0.045t=\ln 1.6\Rightarrow t=\dfrac{\ln 1.6}{0.045}\approx \mathbf{10.44\ \text{years}}$.

For the rate: $r=\dfrac{1}{t}\ln\dfrac{A}{P}$. Here \$2,500→\$4,000 in 10 years gives $r\approx 4.70\%$.

Exponential growth & decay

Two equivalent forms

The same curve can be written two ways. The continuous form $A=A_0e^{kt}$ uses a continuous rate $k$; the annual form $A=A_0(1+r)^t$ (growth) or $A_0(1-r)^t$ (decay) uses a per-year percent $r$. They describe the same process when $1+r=e^{k}$, i.e. $k=\ln(1+r)$.

CONTINUOUS · find rate, then time
A culture grows from 500 to 1,500 cells in 3 hours. When will it reach 5,000?
1
Find $k$. $1500=500e^{3k}\Rightarrow e^{3k}=3\Rightarrow k=\tfrac{\ln 3}{3}\approx 0.3662$.
2
Solve for $t$. $5000=500e^{kt}\Rightarrow e^{kt}=10$.
3
$t=\dfrac{\ln 10}{0.3662}\approx \mathbf{6.29\ \text{hours}}$.
ANNUAL · growth percent
A town of 40,000 grows 3.2% per year. Population in 15 years? When does it double?
1
$A=40000(1.032)^{15}\approx \mathbf{64{,}159}$.
2
Doubling: $2=(1.032)^t\Rightarrow t=\dfrac{\ln 2}{\ln 1.032}\approx \mathbf{22.0\ \text{years}}$.
3
Same as continuous $k=\ln 1.032\approx 0.0315$, i.e. a 3.15% continuous rate.

Half-life & doubling

Base-form decay and growth

For half-life problems use $A=A_0\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{t/h}$, where $h$ is the half-life. For doubling, $A=A_0\,2^{\,t/d}$. Solving for the exponent — the time — uses a log, just as before.

FUTURE AMOUNT & TIME
An isotope has a 28-year half-life; you start with 100 mg.
1
After 75 years: $A=100\left(\tfrac12\right)^{75/28}\approx \mathbf{15.62\ \text{mg}}$.
2
When is 25 mg left? $\tfrac{25}{100}=\left(\tfrac12\right)^{t/28}$.
3
$\dfrac{t}{28}=\dfrac{\ln 0.25}{\ln 0.5}=2\Rightarrow t=\mathbf{56\ \text{years}}$ (two half-lives).
PERCENT DATA · carbon dating
Burned wood at a Chilean dig holds about 1.67% of its original carbon-14. The half-life is 5,600 years — when was it burned?
1
Percent is $A/A_0$. $0.0167=\left(\tfrac12\right)^{t/5600}$.
2
Take $\ln$: $\dfrac{t}{5600}=\dfrac{\ln 0.0167}{\ln 0.5}$.
3
$t=5600\cdot\dfrac{\ln 0.0167}{\ln 0.5}\approx \mathbf{33{,}000\ \text{years ago}}$.

Solving for the half-life itself just swaps the unknown: $h=\dfrac{t\ln(1/2)}{\ln(A/A_0)}$.

Common pitfalls

Where applied problems go wrong

⚠ Six traps

  • Percent vs decimal. A rate of 5% goes into the formula as $0.05$, not $5$.
  • The exponent is $nt$. Quarterly for 8 years means $n=4$, $nt=32$ compounding periods — not $8$.
  • Rounding too early. Keep extra digits through the work; round only the final answer to the requested place. Early rounding can shift an answer by years.
  • Decay direction. Decay needs $k < 0$, factor $(1-r)$, or base $\tfrac12$ — never base $2$.
  • $\ln$ vs $\log$. Either base works when you solve, but pair $\ln$ with $e$; it keeps continuous problems clean.
  • Percent of original is $A/A_0$. "1.67% remaining" means $\frac{A}{A_0}=0.0167$ — a ratio, before you ever touch grams.
