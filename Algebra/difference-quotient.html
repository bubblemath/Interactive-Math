Precalculus · The difference quotient

Build \(f(x+h)\), subtract, and let the h cancel.

The difference quotient is the slope of the line through two points on a curve. You set it up by substituting \(x+h\) for every \(x\), subtracting \(f(x)\), and simplifying until the \(h\) in the denominator cancels. No limits yet — your answer keeps its \(h\), except for a line, where the \(h\) disappears and you're left with the slope.

The setup

\(\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\)

Three stages, each checked.

What it means

It's the slope of the line through two points.

The difference quotient is exactly the slope of the secant through \((x, f(x))\) and \((x+h, f(x+h))\): rise \(f(x+h)-f(x)\) over run \(h\). Drag the two points. For a line the slope never changes; for a parabola it shifts as \(h\) changes — which is why the answer still has an \(h\) in it.

f(x)

Drag either point along the curve. The run between them is \(h\).

Worked examples

One of each, all the way through.

Linear · f(x) = 3x − 2

f(x+h)
\(3(x+h)-2 = 3x+3h-2\).
subtract f(x)
\((3x+3h-2)-(3x-2) = 3h\).
divide by h
\(\dfrac{3h}{h} = 3\). The \(h\) cancels to nothing — for a line the difference quotient is just the slope.

Quadratic · f(x) = 2x² − x + 1

f(x+h)
\(2(x+h)^2-(x+h)+1 = 2x^2+4xh+2h^2-x-h+1\). The cross-term \(4xh\) comes from \((x+h)^2\).
subtract f(x)
\(\big(2x^2+4xh+2h^2-x-h+1\big)-\big(2x^2-x+1\big) = 4xh+2h^2-h\).
factor h, then divide
\(\dfrac{h(4x+2h-1)}{h} = 4x-1+2h\). The \(h\) stays in the answer.

Rational · f(x) = 1 / x

f(x+h)
\(\dfrac{1}{x+h}\).
subtract f(x), common denominator
\(\dfrac{1}{x+h}-\dfrac{1}{x} = \dfrac{x-(x+h)}{x(x+h)} = \dfrac{-h}{x(x+h)}\).
divide by h
Dividing by \(h\) means multiplying by \(\dfrac{1}{h}\): \(\dfrac{-h}{x(x+h)}\cdot\dfrac{1}{h} = \dfrac{-1}{x(x+h)}\).

Cheat sheet

Substitute, subtract, cancel the h.

Linear

\(f(x)=mx+b\) gives \(m\). The \(h\) cancels completely — that's the slope.

Quadratic

\(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\) gives \(2ax+b+ah\). Mind \((x+h)^2=x^2+2xh+h^2\).

Rational

\(f(x)=\tfrac{k}{x+c}\) gives \(\tfrac{-k}{(x+c)(x+h+c)}\). Use a common denominator.

Don't set h = 0

That's the limit, which comes later. Simplify until the \(h\) cancels in the denominator, then leave the rest.
