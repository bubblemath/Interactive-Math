Precalculus · Solving linear inequalities

Three notations, one solution set — plus the rule everyone forgets.

A linear inequality's answer is a set of numbers, and you can dress that set three ways: inequality notation, interval notation, and a number-line graph. Solving is mostly ordinary algebra — with one trap: multiply or divide by a negative and the symbol must flip. This page links the three notations and drills the flip.

Skill 01 · Translate between the three notations

Inequality, interval, and number line are one object in three outfits.

An open circle, a parenthesis, and a strict symbol (\(<,>\)) all say the same thing: "this endpoint is not included." A closed circle, a bracket, and \(\le,\ge\) all say "included." Skill 02 · Greater than vs less than — and the flip

Multiply or divide by a negative, and the symbol must reverse.

Adding and subtracting never change the symbol. But multiplying or dividing both sides by a negative number flips it: \(<\) becomes \(>\), \(\le\) becomes \(\ge\). Here's why — multiplying by \(-1\) reflects every number across zero, which reverses their order. Watch it happen.

Once \(x\) is alone on the left, the symbol points to the shading: \(x>\) or \(x\ge\) shades right (toward \(+\infty\)); \(x<\) or \(x\le\) shades left (toward \(-\infty\)).

Worked example · the flip in action

Solve \(\;-2x + 1 < 7\)
Step 1 · Subtract 1 (no flip)
\[-2x < 6\]
Step 2 · Divide by −2 → FLIP the symbol
\[x > -3\]Dividing by a negative reverses \(<\) into \(>\).
Step 3 · Interval notation
\[(-3,\ \infty)\] Shade right from an open dot at −3.
Skill 03 · Solve → a ray (one boundary)

Isolate \(x\); the solution is everything on one side of a boundary.

Collect the variable on one side and the numbers on the other, just like an equation — flipping only if you multiply or divide by a negative. The result, like \(x \le 5\), is a ray: a single boundary with everything to one side shaded.

The four rays

Worked example · no flip needed

Solve \(\;3x - 4 \le 11\)
Step 1 · Add 4
\[3x \le 15\]
Step 2 · Divide by 3 (positive → no flip)
\[x \le 5\]
Step 3 · Interval notation
\[(-\infty,\ 5\,]\] Closed bracket on 5 because of \(\le\).

Skill 04 · Compound → a bounded interval (two boundaries)

Two boundaries at once: do the same thing to all three parts.

A compound inequality like \(-1 \le 2x+3 < 7\) traps \(x\) between two bounds. Keep the variable in the middle and apply every step to all three parts simultaneously. The answer is a bounded interval with two endpoints — each open or closed depending on its symbol.

One operation, three parts. Subtract from all three, divide all three. If you divide all three by a negative, both symbols flip.

Worked example

Solve \(\;-1 \le 2x + 3 < 7\)
Step 1 · Subtract 3 from all three parts
\[-4 \le 2x < 4\]
Step 2 · Divide all three by 2 (positive → no flip)
\[-2 \le x < 2\]
Step 3 · Interval notation
\[[-2,\ 2)\] Closed at −2 (\(\le\)), open at 2 (\(<\)).

Skill 05 · No solution & all real numbers

When the variable cancels, the leftover statement decides everything.

Sometimes the \(x\)-terms vanish and you're left with a plain numeric claim. If it's true (like \(-3 \ge -8\)), every number works — the solution is all real numbers, \((-\infty, \infty)\). If it's false (like \(5 < 1\)), nothing works — no solution, \(\varnothing\). Don't panic when \(x\) disappears; just read the statement.

Worked example · no solution

Solve \(\;2x + 5 < 2x + 1\)
Step 1 · Subtract 2x from both sides
\[5 < 1\]
Step 2 · Read the statement
\(5 < 1\) is false for every \(x\).
Conclusion
No solution \((\varnothing)\).

Worked example · all real numbers

Solve \(\;4x - 3 \ge 4x - 8\)
Step 1 · Subtract 4x from both sides
\[-3 \ge -8\]
Step 2 · Read the statement
\(-3 \ge -8\) is true for every \(x\).
Conclusion
All real numbers: \((-\infty,\ \infty)\).

Direction
