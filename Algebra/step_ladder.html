Math 0232 · Unit B3 · coordinates, intercepts & slope

Every object here is a step.

So far across, so far up. A point is one step from the origin. An intercept is a step with a zero in it. Slope is the step you repeat forever along a line. Your notes present coordinates, intercepts and slope as three subjects, and they are one — with a single catch: a location writes the across part first, and a slope writes the up part first. Same step, opposite order, and that gap is where most of the errors in this lesson live.

The engine of the whole lesson

One step, written two ways

Walk three to the right and two up. That single movement is both a location and a slope — and the two notations put the numbers in opposite order.

As a location
where is it? (3, 2)  across first
As a slope
how steep is it?

Nothing in the notation warns you. (3, 2) and a slope of 2/3 describe the identical movement while looking unrelated, because a point answers “how far across, then how far up” and a slope answers “how far up, per unit across”. Rung 8 exists to make that swap automatic instead of accidental.

✋ A point on an axis is in no quadrant at all.

Three of the seven points on page 10 of your notes are like this — B = (0, 2), D = (−4, 0) and F = (0, −3) — and the origin makes four. Quadrants need both coordinates to have a sign, and zero has no sign. Being asked “which quadrant” does not guarantee the answer is one of the four.

Second: zero slope and undefined slope come straight out of the fraction. Slope is rise over run. A horizontal line has rise 0, and zero divided by something is zero. A vertical line has run 0, and zero on the bottom is undefined — the same rule from the integers and radicals units. Memorised as a pair, these two get swapped constantly; derived from the fraction, they cannot be.

Third: horizontal and vertical lines are each missing an intercept. y = 7 never crosses the x-axis, and x = −2 never crosses the y-axis. “None” is a real answer, and it appears on this ladder as often as it should.

Why this matters

This is the vocabulary the rest of the course speaks

Slope becomes rate of change

Cost per mile, degrees per hour, dollars per unit — every one is a slope with units attached. Reading a slope off a graph fluently is reading a rate off a graph fluently.

Two points are enough

Everything about a line is fixed by any two of its points. Intercepts are just the two easiest ones to find, which is exactly why your notes reach for them first.

The errors are silent

A swapped coordinate plots a real point. An inverted slope draws a real line. Nothing about either result looks wrong, so the only defence is getting the order right the first time.

The plane is not a new kind of mathematics — it is a way of writing down movement. Once you read every object here as “so far across, so far up”, the three topics on these four pages collapse into one, and the only thing left to keep straight is which of the two numbers the notation wants first.
