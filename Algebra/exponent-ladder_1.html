Exponent fluency · building toward radicals & rational exponents

An exponent counts factors. Every rule just counts them.

Multiply powers of the same base and the factors pile up, so you add the counts; divide and they cancel, so you subtract; raise a power to a power and you copy the pile, so you multiply. Climb from numeric powers up through radicals and rational exponents with variables — and learn to read $2^3\cdot 2^4$ as $2^7$ without ever reaching for $128$.

Why working by hand wins

The calculator gives you the number and hides the structure

Exponent rules look like a pile of separate tricks, but they're one idea — an exponent counts repeated factors — applied over and over. Evaluate everything to decimals and that idea never forms; keep things in exact, exponent form and the structure stays visible, which is exactly what you need once variables and roots appear.

The rule, not the number

$2^3\cdot 2^4 = 2^7$ comes from $3+4$, not from computing $128$. The rule is the transferable skill; the big number is a dead end.

A root is a power

$x^{1/2}=\sqrt{x}$ and $x^{2/3}=\sqrt[3]{x^2}$. Once a fractional exponent reads as a root, $8^{2/3}$ and $\sqrt{18x^3}$ stop being mysterious.

Simplify, don't evaluate

$\sqrt{50}=5\sqrt{2}$ is exact and structured; $7.07\ldots$ throws away the $\sqrt{2}$ you'll need to combine or cancel later.

Predicting add-subtract-or-multiply before you compute is the same instinct that flags a wrong answer instantly. A calculator can hand you a value; it can't hand you that instinct.
