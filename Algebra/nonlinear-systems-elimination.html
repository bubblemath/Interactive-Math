Nonlinear systems · elimination & substitution

Where two curves meet.

A nonlinear system pairs a line, parabola, circle, ellipse, or rational curve with another. Each solution is a point the two curves share — so "how many solutions" becomes "how many times do these curves cross." Line up a common term, eliminate it, and the system collapses to one equation you already know how to solve.

Solutions are intersection points

Every shared point satisfies both equations at once

Take a line and a circle. A solution $(x,y)$ has to sit on the line and on the circle — which is exactly a crossing point. Two curves like these can miss entirely (0 solutions), just touch (1, a tangency), or cross twice (2).

Some pairings reach further: a parabola and a circle, or a rational curve and a circle, can meet at up to 4 points. The algebra always mirrors the picture — a quadratic caps you at 2 real roots, a quartic at 4.

The elimination strategy

Cancel a shared term to drop to one variable

Nonlinear or not, the move is the same as for linear systems: get rid of one variable. Two reliable ways:

1 · Eliminate a lone variable by substitution. If one equation is already solved for $y$ (a line, a parabola $y=ax^2+bx+c$, or a rational $y=\tfrac{n}{x-h}+v$), substitute that expression into the other equation. One variable disappears.

2 · Eliminate a repeated term by subtracting. If both equations carry the same chunk — a lone $y$, or an $x^2$ — line them up and subtract so that chunk cancels. Two parabolas $y=\dots$ set equal cancel the $y$; substituting $x^2$ from $y=x^2$ into a circle cancels the $x^2$.

Either way you land on a single-variable equation — usually a quadratic (sometimes a quadratic in $x^2$). Solve it, back-substitute to recover the partner coordinate, and check every candidate in both original equations.

Count first. The degree of the reduced equation bounds the solutions: a quadratic gives at most 2 real roots, a quartic at most 4. If you expected 2 and found 4, or found a value that breaks a denominator, something needs a second look.

Worked examples

One elimination, every pairing
$\begin{cases} y = x^2 - 2x - 1 \\ y = x - 1 \end{cases}$
1
Substitute the line's $y=x-1$ into the parabola: $x-1 = x^2-2x-1$.
2
Collect on one side: $x^2 - 3x = 0$, then factor: $x(x-3)=0$.
3
So $x=0$ or $x=3$. Back-substitute into $y=x-1$.
Solutions: $(0,-1)$ and $(3,2)$.

$\begin{cases} x^2 + y^2 = 25 \\ y = -x + 7 \end{cases}$
1
Substitute $y=-x+7$ into the circle: $x^2 + (-x+7)^2 = 25$.
2
Expand and simplify: $2x^2 - 14x + 24 = 0$, i.e. $x^2 - 7x + 12 = 0$.
3
Factor $(x-3)(x-4)=0$, so $x=3,4$; then $y=-x+7$.
Solutions: $(3,4)$ and $(4,3)$.
circlelinesolution
$\begin{cases} y = x^2 - 1 \\ y = -x^2 + 2x + 3 \end{cases}$
1
Both give $y$, so set them equal (eliminating $y$): $x^2-1 = -x^2+2x+3$.
2
Collect: $2x^2 - 2x - 4 = 0$, i.e. $x^2 - x - 2 = 0$.
3
Factor $(x-2)(x+1)=0$, so $x=2,-1$; then $y=x^2-1$.
Solutions: $(2,3)$ and $(-1,0)$.

$\begin{cases} y = x^2 \\ x^2 + (y-3)^2 = 5 \end{cases}$
1
The parabola gives $x^2=y$. Substitute into the circle: $y + (y-3)^2 = 5$.
2
Expand: $y^2 - 5y + 4 = 0$, factor $(y-1)(y-4)=0$, so $y=1,4$.
3
Each $y$ gives $x=\pm\sqrt{y}$: $y=1\Rightarrow x=\pm1$, $y=4\Rightarrow x=\pm2$. (Keep both signs!)
Solutions: $(\pm 1, 1)$ and $(\pm 2, 4)$ — four points.
parabolacirclesolution
$\begin{cases} y = \dfrac{6}{x} \\[2pt] y = -x + 5 \end{cases}$
1
Set equal: $\dfrac{6}{x} = -x + 5$, then multiply by $x$: $6 = -x^2 + 5x$.
2
Rearrange: $x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0$, factor $(x-2)(x-3)=0$, so $x=2,3$.
3
Both are nonzero, so both are valid (recall $x\neq 0$). Then $y=-x+5$.
Solutions: $(2,3)$ and $(3,2)$.
rationallinesolution
$\begin{cases} y = \dfrac{2}{x} \\[2pt] x^2 + y^2 = 5 \end{cases}$
1
Substitute $y=\dfrac{2}{x}$: $x^2 + \dfrac{4}{x^2} = 5$, then multiply by $x^2$.
2
$x^4 - 5x^2 + 4 = 0$. Let $u=x^2$: $u^2-5u+4=0$, so $u=1,4$.
3
$x^2=1\Rightarrow x=\pm1$, $x^2=4\Rightarrow x=\pm2$; pair with $y=\dfrac{2}{x}$.
Solutions: $(1,2),\ (-1,-2),\ (2,1),\ (-2,-1)$.

Extraneous roots & irrational answers

Always check candidates — and don't fear a radical

Clearing a denominator or squaring can manufacture roots the original system never had. Every candidate must be verified in both starting equations; one that breaks a denominator is extraneous and gets thrown out.

EXTRANEOUS BY DOMAIN
$\begin{cases} y = x - 1 \\ y = \dfrac{x^2 - x - 2}{x + 1} \end{cases}$
1
Multiply by $x+1$: $(x-1)(x+1) = x^2 - x - 2$, i.e. $x^2 - 1 = x^2 - x - 2$.
2
This gives $x = -1$ — but $x=-1$ makes the denominator zero, so it's not allowed.
3
The rational curve is the line $y=x-2$ with a hole at $x=-1$; $y=x-1$ runs parallel above it.
No solution — the only candidate is extraneous.

Clean factoring is the exception, not the rule. When the reduced quadratic doesn't factor, the quadratic formula hands you exact irrational coordinates — those are perfectly good answers.

IRRATIONAL, BUT EXACT

Line + parabola. $y=x^2$ and $y=x+1$ give $x^2-x-1=0$, so $x=\dfrac{1\pm\sqrt5}{2}$ — the golden ratio and its companion. Two points, both irrational.

Line + circle. $y=x$ and $x^2+y^2=4$ give $2x^2=4$, so $x=\pm\sqrt2$ and the points are $(\sqrt2,\sqrt2)$ and $(-\sqrt2,-\sqrt2)$.

Rational + ellipse. $y=\dfrac2x$ and $\dfrac{x^2}{9}+\dfrac{y^2}{4}=1$ reduce to $x^4 - 9x^2 + 9 = 0$. As a quadratic in $x^2$: $x^2=\dfrac{9\pm3\sqrt5}{2}$, giving four points near $(\pm2.80,\,\pm0.71)$ and $(\pm1.07,\,\pm1.87)$.

⚠ Common slips

  • Dropping the $\pm$. $x^2=4$ means $x=2$ and $x=-2$. Forgetting the negative halves your solutions.
  • Solving for one variable only. After finding $x$, back-substitute to get $y$ — a solution is a point.
  • Skipping the check. Multiplying by $x$ or by $(x-h)$, or squaring, can add false roots. Test each candidate in both originals.
  • Forbidden $x$. For a rational, any candidate equal to the excluded value (where the denominator is $0$) is extraneous.
  • Stopping at the count. "Two solutions" isn't the answer — the points are.
