The ends are decided by the leading term.

For very large |x|, a polynomial behaves like its highest‑degree term and nothing else. So two facts settle everything: is the degree even or odd, and is the leading coefficient positive or negative? Those two bits pick exactly one of four end behaviors.

01

Only the leading term survives

Two yes/no questions, four possible behaviors.

As x grows huge in either direction, the highest‑degree term dwarfs every other term, so the polynomial’s arms follow . That makes end behavior a two‑question decision:

Even or odd degree? An even power keeps its sign when x is negative, so both ends point the same way. An odd power flips sign for negative x, so the two ends point opposite ways. Positive or negative leading coefficient? A positive coefficient sends the right end up; a negative one sends it down. Put the two answers together and you have the whole picture — lower‑degree terms never matter for the ends.
