Math 0232 · Unit B1 · solving linear equations

Two kinds of move. One of them always goes first.

Everything in Unit A was one expression. Now there are two sides and an equals sign between them, and every step you take is one of exactly two things: tidying a single side, or doing the same thing to both. Tidying rewrites a side without changing what it is worth. A both-sides move changes the value — which is the whole reason the other side has to follow. Knowing which kind you need is the skill. The engine of the whole lesson

Every step is one of these two rows

Your notes list five steps. Underneath them are only two kinds of action, and the difference between them is whether the other side of the equation has to react.

Distributing does not change what a side is worth. 3(8x+1) and 24x+3 are the same number written two ways, so the other side has no reason to move. Subtracting 2x genuinely makes a side smaller — and that is precisely why the other side must shrink by the same amount. The two properties in your notes are not arbitrary rules; they are the permission slip for the second row.

✋ “Both sides” means every term on both sides.

When you multiply ¼ + ⅓(5x+2) = −2 by 12 to clear the fractions, the −2 gets multiplied too. It becomes −24. Students clear the fractions they can see and leave the lone constant sitting there untouched, and the equation quietly stops being true. Same for dividing: if you divide by 3, every term is divided by 3.

Second: moving a term across flips its sign, and here is why. You are not really “moving” anything — you are subtracting it from both sides, and it vanishes from one side while appearing negated on the other. Learn the shortcut without the reason and the signs start disappearing the moment an equation runs past three terms.

Third: in 5 − (2x + 6) the minus reaches both terms, giving 5 − 2x − 6. That is the same error from the last unit, and example 5 on page 3 of your notes exists to catch it.

Why this matters

Every equation you meet from here is this one, dressed up

Inequalities are the same moves

The next lesson changes the equals sign to a less-than and adds exactly one new rule. Everything else — tidy first, do it to both sides, undo in reverse order — carries over untouched.

Literal equations are these moves without numbers

Solving P = 2l + 2w for l feels alien only until you notice it is subtract-then-divide, the same two steps as 3x + 5 = 20. The letters change nothing about the order.

Word problems fail here, not there

Most students can set up an equation from a story and then lose it in the solving. The setup is the interesting part; this is the part that has to be automatic so the interesting part gets your attention.

Solving is not a single skill — it is a sequence of small decisions, each of which is easy once you know which one you are making. Tidy while there is anything to tidy. Clear the fractions in one sweep across every term. Move variables to one side and numbers to the other. Divide last. The five steps on page 2 are that sequence, and the only reason equations feel hard is that nobody says out loud which step they are on.
