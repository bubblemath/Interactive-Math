Precalculus · Remainder theorem & Factor theorem

The remainder is f(c).

Divide a polynomial by x − c and the leftover number isn’t junk — it’s exactly f(c). That one fact powers both theorems: synthetic division evaluates a polynomial, and a remainder of zero means you’ve found a factor.

01

One fact, two theorems

Why the last cell of a synthetic division means so much.

Every division leaves the identity . The Remainder Theorem reads it at x = c: the (x − c) part becomes zero, so f(c) = r. The remainder you get from synthetic division is the value of the polynomial — no plugging in required.

The Factor Theorem is the special case r = 0. If the remainder is zero then f(c) = 0, which means (x − c) divides f(x) evenly — it’s a factor — and the bottom row of the synthetic division is the cofactor that goes with it.
