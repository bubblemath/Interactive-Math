Math 0232 · Unit A3 · integer exponents & order of operations

Every symbol reaches exactly as far as it is allowed to.

An exponent grabs one base and nothing else. A minus sign is either part of the base or a factor standing outside it, waiting its turn. Parentheses are the only thing that changes what gets grabbed. Order of operations is that same question at a larger scale — which operation reaches its neighbors first — which is why this lesson is really one idea, asked eleven ways.

The engine of rungs 1–3

When do the parentheses actually matter?

These are the eight cells from page 12 of your notes, arranged so the pattern in them can speak. Two switches: is the minus inside the parentheses or outside, and is the exponent even or odd.

even exponentodd exponent
Minus insidethe base is −2 (−2)4 = 16four negatives, even (−2)3 = −8three negatives, odd
Minus outsidethe base is 2 −24 = −16−(24), so −(16) −23 = −8−(23), so −(8)

Read the two columns. The odd column agrees and the even column does not. That is the whole reason this error survives: a student who only ever checks an odd exponent gets the right answer with the wrong understanding, and nothing corrects them until the exponent turns even.

✋ The exponent grabs the base, not the minus sign.

−24 is −16. The 4 reaches only as far as the 2, so this is −(2 · 2 · 2 · 2). The minus is a factor of −1 standing outside, and it applies last. Writing (−2)4 is the only way to make the exponent take the sign with it.

Second trap: PEMDAS is four tiers, not six. Multiplication and division are one tier, taken left to right; addition and subtraction are one tier, taken left to right. 42 ÷ 2 · 4 is 32, not 2 — and notice your notes draw this correctly, with M and A stacked over D and S. That stacked picture is the actual rule. The acronym is the thing obscuring it.

Third, smaller: a fraction bar and a pair of absolute value bars are grouping symbols. Everything above the bar resolves before anything divides, and everything between the bars resolves before the absolute value is taken.

Why this matters

A different species of error from the last two units

Not the wrong rule — the wrong reach

In the integers unit the error was borrowing a multiplication rule for addition. With fractions it was the same shape. Here the rule is correct and it is applied to the wrong amount of stuff, which means naming the operation does not help. Only asking what it touches does.

Scope is most of algebra

Distributing, squaring a binomial, negating a whole expression, differentiating later on — every one is a question about how far a symbol reaches. Students who read −x2 as (−x)2 will keep making that trade for years.

Calculators do not save you

A calculator applies these rules exactly as written, which means a scope error becomes a confidently wrong answer instead of an obvious one. The machine will not flag it. Reading the expression correctly is the only defence.

Every problem on page 13 of your notes is the same question in different clothing: what is attached to what? The brackets, the fraction bars, the absolute value bars, the parentheses around a negative base — all of them exist to answer it, and all of them are ignored by a student who starts computing from the left. Slow down for one second at the start, and the rest of the problem has already decided itself.
