Augmented matrices & Gauss-Jordan elimination

Strip a system down to numbers, then reduce.

A linear system carries a lot of repeated $x$'s, $y$'s, and $+$ signs. Pack the coefficients into an augmented matrix and all that's left is the arithmetic. Three row operations carry you to echelon form; a few more reach reduced echelon form, where the solution is simply read off.

Setting up the augmented matrix

Every coefficient gets a column; the constants sit past the bar

Line up the variables so each column holds one variable's coefficients. The vertical bar stands in for the equals signs — everything left of it is coefficients, everything right is constants.

$\begin{cases} 2x+3y-z=7 \\ x-y+2z=4 \\ 3x+2y+z=10 \end{cases}$
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2&3&-1&7 \\ 1&-1&2&4 \\ 3&2&1&10 \end{array}\right]$

A missing term means a $0$ in that column — write $x+2z=5$ as $1,\,0,\,2\mid 5$.

The three row operations

Three moves, each reversible, each preserving the solution

Every step of reduction is one of these three. None of them changes the system's solution — they just rewrite it. You'll type them in the reducer using the notation shown.

Swap
R1 ↔ R2
Exchange two rows. Useful to bring a nonzero (or simpler) entry into a pivot spot.
Scale
R2 → (1/2)R2
Multiply a row by a nonzero constant — typically to turn a pivot into a leading $1$.
Replace
R2 → R2 − 3R1
Add a multiple of one row to another. This is the workhorse that clears entries to $0$.

Whatever you do to the coefficients, do to the constant past the bar as well — the bar is bookkeeping, not a wall.

Echelon form, then back-substitution

Carve a staircase of leading 1s, then solve upward

Row-echelon form (REF) means a staircase: each row's leading entry is a $1$, sits to the right of the one above, with zeros beneath it. Work left to right, top to bottom. Once you're there, the bottom row almost solves itself.

REF + BACK-SUBSTITUTION
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1&1&1&6 \\ 1&-1&1&2 \\ 2&1&-1&1 \end{array}\right]$
1
Column 1 already leads with a $1$ in R1. Clear below it: R2 → R2 − R1 and R3 → R3 − 2R1.
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1&1&1&6 \\ 0&-2&0&-4 \\ 0&-1&-3&-11 \end{array}\right]$
2
Make the next pivot a leading $1$: R2 → −(1/2)R2.
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1&1&1&6 \\ 0&1&0&2 \\ 0&-1&-3&-11 \end{array}\right]$
3
Clear below that pivot: R3 → R3 + R2, then scale: R3 → −(1/3)R3.
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} \color{#c2620f}1&1&1&6 \\ 0&\color{#c2620f}1&0&2 \\ 0&0&\color{#c2620f}1&3 \end{array}\right]$
That's REF — three leading 1s in a staircase.
4
Back-substitute, bottom up. The last row gives $z=3$. The middle gives $y=2$. The top gives $x+y+z=6 \Rightarrow x=6-2-3=1$.  $(1,2,3)$.

Echelon vs reduced echelon form

Reduced form clears above the pivots too — so there's nothing left to solve

REF gets you a staircase and asks you to back-substitute. Reduced row-echelon form (RREF) goes further: each leading $1$ becomes the only nonzero entry in its column. Then each row literally reads "variable = number."

ECHELON FORM
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} \color{#c2620f}1&1&1&6 \\ 0&\color{#c2620f}1&0&2 \\ 0&0&\color{#c2620f}1&3 \end{array}\right]$

Leading 1s, zeros below. Still needs back-substitution to untangle the top rows.

REDUCED ECHELON FORM
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} \color{#7c3aed}1&0&0&1 \\ 0&\color{#7c3aed}1&0&2 \\ 0&0&\color{#7c3aed}1&3 \end{array}\right]$

Zeros above and below. Read it straight off: $x=1,\ y=2,\ z=3$.

Getting from the left to the right is just more clearing: R1 → R1 − R2 then R1 → R1 − R3. Driving all the way to RREF is exactly Gauss-Jordan elimination.

Gauss-Jordan & the three outcomes

Drive to RREF and read the answer — whatever kind it is

Gauss-Jordan reduces all the way to RREF. The final form tells you immediately which of the three cases you're in.

ONE SOLUTION
$\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1&2&5 \\ 3&-1&8 \end{array}\right]$
1
Clear below the first pivot: R2 → R2 − 3R1 gives $[\,0\ \ {-7}\ \ |\ {-7}\,]$.
2
Leading 1: R2 → −(1/7)R2, then clear above: R1 → R1 − 2R2.
3
$\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} \color{#7c3aed}1&0&3 \\ 0&\color{#7c3aed}1&1 \end{array}\right]$
A pivot in every variable column.  $x=3,\ y=1$.
NO SOLUTION
$\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1&2&4 \\ 2&4&10 \end{array}\right]$
1
R2 → R2 − 2R1, then R2 → (1/2)R2:
$\left[\begin{array}{cc|c} 1&2&4 \\ 0&0&\color{#be123c}1 \end{array}\right]$
2
The last row says $0x+0y=1$ — impossible.  No solution.
INFINITELY MANY
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1&1&1&6 \\ 1&-1&1&2 \\ 2&0&2&8 \end{array}\right]$
1
Reducing fully collapses the third row to zeros:
$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} \color{#7c3aed}1&0&1&4 \\ 0&\color{#7c3aed}1&0&2 \\ 0&0&0&0 \end{array}\right]$
2
No pivot in column 3, so $z$ is free. Let $z=t$: from the rows, $x=-t+4,\ y=2$.  $x=-t+4,\ y=2,\ z=t$.

The three tells in RREF: a pivot in every variable column → one solution; a row $[\,0\ \cdots\ 0\ |\ \text{nonzero}\,]$ → no solution; a missing pivot column with no contradiction → infinitely many, with that column's variable set to $t$.

⚠ Common slips

  • The bar isn't a wall. Every operation hits the constant column too — forget it and the constants drift out of sync.
  • Leading 1 before clearing. In Gauss-Jordan, scale a pivot to $1$, then use it to clear its column — above and below.
  • A zero row is fine. $[\,0\ 0\ 0\ |\ 0\,]$ just means a redundant equation (infinitely many), not "no solution." A nonzero constant past the bar is the contradiction.
  • Watch signs on replace. $R_2 \to R_2 - 3R_1$ subtracts $3\times$ every entry of $R_1$, including negatives.
  • Name the parameter. For a free column, write the family with $t$ — don't stop at "infinitely many."
