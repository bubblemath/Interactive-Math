The denominator is the unit.

Ninths plus ninths is ninths, the same way feet plus feet is feet. You cannot add feet to meters without converting first — and the least common denominator is that conversion. But multiplying does not care: feet times feet is fine, it just makes a different unit. Which operation you are doing decides whether the units have to match, and that one call routes every problem in this lesson.

✋ “Do the tops, do the bottoms” is a multiplication rule.

3⁄8 + 1⁄2 is not 4⁄10. Notice the shape of that error: a rule that is perfectly correct for multiplying, carried into addition where it does not belong. It is the same mistake as −3 + (−5) = 8 from the integers unit — different chapter, identical habit. Rung 1 exists to catch it before any numbers move.

Two smaller traps. You can only cancel factors, never terms: (3+6)⁄6 is not 3. And the L in LCD is doing real work — the least common denominator of 8 and 2 is 8, not 16. Multiplying the denominators together always gives a common denominator; it is just rarely the one you want.

Why this matters

Fractions are where algebra gets decided

Rational expressions are this, with letters

Every rule on this page returns later wearing variables. A student who cancels a term instead of a factor here will cancel across a plus sign there, and the error will look identical — only harder to spot.

The LCD becomes the common denominator

Solving equations with fractions, combining rational expressions, adding unlike radicals — all of them are “convert to a shared unit first.” The habit transfers even when the notation stops looking like a fraction.

Slow is the same as wrong

Fraction arithmetic shows up two or three times inside a single later problem. If each one costs thirty seconds of thought, the real problem never gets attention. Automatic is the whole point.

This lesson looks like five separate rules to memorize and it is really one question asked five times: what is the unit, and does it need to match? Reducing, multiplying, flipping, finding the LCD, building equivalent fractions — each one is a move you make because of the answer to that question. Once the question is automatic, the moves follow on their own.
