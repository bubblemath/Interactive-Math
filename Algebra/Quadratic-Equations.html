📐 The Standard Form

Quadratic Equation in Standard Form

\(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\)
where \(a \neq 0\) (if a = 0, it's not quadratic!)

Anatomy of a Quadratic Equation

\(\textcolor{#e74c3c}{a}x^2 + \textcolor{#27ae60}{b}x + \textcolor{#3498db}{c} = 0\)
a
Leading Coefficient
Coefficient of \(x^2\)
b
Linear Coefficient
Coefficient of \(x\)
c
Constant Term
The number with no \(x\)

Original Equation Standard Form a b c

📖 Key Vocabulary

Solutions / Roots / Zeros
The values of x that make the equation equal to zero. These terms are interchangeable!
x-intercepts
Where the parabola crosses the x-axis. Same as the solutions when graphed!
Parabola
The U-shaped curve that is the graph of a quadratic function.
Vertex
The highest or lowest point of the parabola (the turning point).
Discriminant
The expression \(b^2 - 4ac\) that tells us the nature of the solutions.
Perfect Square Trinomial
A trinomial that factors into \((x + k)^2\) or \((x - k)^2\).

🔮 The Discriminant: Predicting Your Solutions

The Discriminant Formula

\(D = b^2 - 4ac\)
This value tells you how many solutions to expect and what type they are!

📊 What the Discriminant Tells You

\(D > 0\)
Two different real solutions
Parabola crosses x-axis twice
\(D = 0\)
One repeated real solution
Parabola touches x-axis once
\(D < 0\)
Two complex solutions
Parabola doesn't cross x-axis

🛠️ Four Methods for Solving Quadratics

🔷 Method 1: Factoring (AC Method)

Best when: The equation factors nicely with integer solutions. Look for equations where you can find two numbers that multiply to \(a \cdot c\) and add to \(b\).

Example: \(x^2 - 5x + 6 = 0\) factors to \((x-2)(x-3) = 0\), giving \(x = 2\) or \(x = 3\)

√ Method 2: Square Root Method

Best when: There is no linear term (\(b = 0\)) or the equation can be written as \((something)^2 = number\).

Example: \(x^2 = 16\) gives \(x = \pm 4\)

Example: \((x-3)^2 = 25\) gives \(x - 3 = \pm 5\), so \(x = 8\) or \(x = -2\)

⬛ Method 3: Completing the Square

Best when: The leading coefficient \(a = 1\) and \(b\) is even. Also useful for deriving the vertex form.

Key idea: Transform \(x^2 + bx + c = 0\) into \((x + k)^2 = d\), then use the square root method.

🔢 Method 4: Quadratic Formula

Best when: The equation doesn't factor easily, or you want a method that ALWAYS works.
\(x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)

Remember: This formula works for ANY quadratic equation!

🎯 Method Selection Guide

If the equation looks like... Try this method first Why?
\(ax^2 + c = 0\) (no \(x\) term) Square Root Isolate \(x^2\), then take the square root
\((x + k)^2 = d\) Square Root Already in perfect square form!
\(x^2 + bx + c = 0\) with small integers Factoring Quick if it factors nicely
\(x^2 + bx + c = 0\) where \(b\) is even Completing Square Avoids fractions in the process
Doesn't factor easily / messy numbers Quadratic Formula Always works, guaranteed!
Need exact answers with radicals Quadratic Formula Gives exact irrational solutions
🎓 Remember: All four methods will give you the same solutions! The "best" method is simply the one that's fastest and easiest for that particular equation.

The Quadratic Formula

\(x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a}\)

Identify: \(a = 2\), \(b = 5\), \(c = -3\)
Find the coefficients (watch the signs!)
2
\(x = \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{5^2 - 4(2)(-3)}}{2(2)}\)
Substitute into the formula
💡 Be careful with signs! −b means −(5) = −5

\(x = \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{25 + 24}}{4}\)
Simplify inside the square root: 25 − (−24) = 25 + 24
4
\(x = \frac{-5 \pm \sqrt{49}}{4}\)
Add under the radical
5
\(x = \frac{-5 \pm 7}{4}\)
Simplify the square root
6
\(x = \frac{-5 + 7}{4} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}\) or \(x = \frac{-5 - 7}{4} = \frac{-12}{4} = -3\)
Split into two solutions and simplify

\(x = \frac{1}{2}\) or \(x = -3\)

🧠 Why Does Factoring Work?

🔍 Understanding the AC Method

1. The Connection to FOIL

When we multiply two binomials using FOIL, we get:

\((x + m)(x + n) = x^2 + nx + mx + mn = x^2 + (m+n)x + mn\)

Notice that:

  • The constant term is the product of m and n
  • The coefficient of x is the sum of m and n

2. Reversing the Process

Factoring is just FOIL in reverse! If we have \(x^2 + bx + c\), we need to find m and n where:

  • \(m \cdot n = c\) (product equals the constant)
  • \(m + n = b\) (sum equals the coefficient of x)

3. The Zero Product Property

Once factored, we use the Zero Product Property:

If \(A \cdot B = 0\), then \(A = 0\) or \(B = 0\)

This is why factoring helps us solve equations — once we have the product of factors equal to zero, at least one factor must be zero!

✓ Summary

The AC method works because:

  1. Factoring reverses the FOIL multiplication process
  2. The product a·c and sum b come from how binomials multiply
  3. Grouping reconstructs the original binomial factors
  4. The Zero Product Property lets us find solutions from factors
