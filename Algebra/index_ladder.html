The index is the group size.

That little number is not decoration. It says how many identical factors have to gather before one of them is allowed out. √49 = 7 because there are two 7s — a complete group of two. Everything else in this lesson is counting. Why an even root refuses a negative, which factor to pull out of √150, how many x's escape from under a radical — all the same question, asked about a different group size.

The engine of arcs one and two

When does a radical have a real answer?

This is the section on page 22 that your notes ask you to explain in your own words. Two switches: is the index even or odd, and is the radicand positive or negative.

positive radicandnegative radicand
Even index2, 4, 6 … √16 = 4two 4s, and 4·4 is positive √−16 — no real answertwo same signs never give a minus
Odd index3, 5, 7 … 3√8 = 2three 2s 3√−8 = −2three negatives stay negative

Three of the four work. The one that fails is the one where the group size is even — and that is not a new rule to memorize. It is the parity fact from the integers unit: an even number of negative factors multiplies to a positive, so no real number can fill an even-sized group and come out negative.

✋ Even index and a negative radicand: no real answer. Odd index: fine, and it keeps the sign.

√−49 has no real answer, but ∛−64 = −4 and √[5]{−32} = −2. Read the index before you read anything else — it is the only thing that decides which of those two worlds you are in.

Second trap: “largest” is doing real work. √72 = √4 · √18 = 2√18 is a completely true statement and a wrong answer, because there is still a perfect square hiding in the 18. Pull out the biggest group you can find the first time and you never have to notice this.

Third: you cannot split a sum. √(9 + 16) is √25 = 5, not 3 + 4 = 7. And notice the shape, because this is the fourth chapter running to ask it: a rule that is perfectly correct for multiplying, carried into addition where it does not belong.

Why this matters

Radicals are where the memorized facts start paying

Perfect powers are the vocabulary

Simplifying is fast if you recognise 49, 64, 81, 121, 144 on sight and slow if you have to search for them. The table on page 21 is not busywork; it is the lookup that every later step depends on.

The quadratic formula is next

Every quadratic you solve ends in a radical that needs simplifying, and half of them need you to notice there is no real answer. Both of those decisions are on this ladder.

Counting beats memorizing

Four separate-looking rules — even roots, odd roots, what comes out, what stays in — are one rule about group size. Carrying one idea is lighter than carrying four, and it does not fall apart under pressure.

Every question in this unit is the same question with a different number on the radical: how many identical factors have to gather before one gets out? Two for a square root, three for a cube root, and whatever the index says otherwise. Once that is automatic, the perfect-power table is the only thing left to know — and that you can simply learn.
