The big idea

Why the shorthand works — and when you’re allowed to use it.

When you divide a polynomial by a linear divisor, every long-division step does the same three things: divide the leading term, multiply back, and subtract. Because the divisor is just x minus a number, the “divide the leading term” step never changes the coefficient — so you can drop the x’s entirely and track only the numbers. That stripped-down bookkeeping is synthetic division.

The one sign change to watch: synthetic division uses the divisor’s root and adds, where long division writes x−c and subtracts. Flipping that sign is the whole reason the columns collapse so neatly. And it only works for linear divisors — that’s the price of the shortcut.
