Algebra / Precalculus · Function operations

Combine two functions — then ask what the originals allow.

Adding, subtracting, multiplying, or dividing two functions is just doing that operation to \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\), then simplifying. The domain is the catch: it comes from the original functions and the operation, never from the tidied-up expression. Intersect the two domains — and for division, also throw out the zeros of \(g\).

Domain of a combination

\(f+g,\ f-g,\ fg\) — domain is \(\text{dom}(f)\cap\text{dom}(g)\).
\(f/g\) — that intersection, and also \(g(x)\ne 0\).
Simplifying hides things. \(\sqrt{x-1}\,\sqrt{x-4}\) looks like two intervals but the domain is just \([4,\infty)\).
Cancellation doesn't restore an excluded point — the hole stays.

Worked examples

Four combinations, four domain stories.

Sum · linear + radical

\((f+g)(x)\) for \(f(x)=x+1,\ g(x)=\sqrt{x-2}\)
Add
\((f+g)(x)=x+1+\sqrt{x-2}\). Nothing combines — leave it.
Domain = intersection
\(\text{dom}(f)=(-\infty,\infty)\), \(\text{dom}(g)=[2,\infty)\). Intersection: \([2,\infty)\).
Answer
\(x+1+\sqrt{x-2}\), domain \([2,\infty)\).

Product · radical × radical (the two-interval trap)

\((fg)(x)\) for \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-1},\ g(x)=\sqrt{x-4}\)
Multiply & simplify
\((fg)(x)=\sqrt{x-1}\,\sqrt{x-4}=\sqrt{(x-1)(x-4)}\).
Domain from the originals
Need \(x\ge 1\) and \(x\ge 4\) — both must hold, so \([4,\infty)\).
Don't trust the simplified radicand
\(\sqrt{(x-1)(x-4)}\) alone looks defined on \((-\infty,1]\cup[4,\infty)\), but \((-\infty,1]\) was never in \(\text{dom}(g)\). Answer: \(\sqrt{(x-1)(x-4)}\), domain \([4,\infty)\).

Quotient · the cancelled point stays out

\(\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)(x)\) for \(f(x)=x-5,\ g(x)=\dfrac{2}{x-3}\)
Divide
\(\dfrac{f}{g}=(x-5)\div\dfrac{2}{x-3}=(x-5)\cdot\dfrac{x-3}{2}=\dfrac{(x-5)(x-3)}{2}\).
Domain from the originals
\(g\) is undefined at \(x=3\), so \(x=3\) is out — even though it vanished from the simplified expression.
Answer
\(\dfrac{(x-5)(x-3)}{2}\), domain \((-\infty,3)\cup(3,\infty)\). (Here \(g\) is never \(0\), so no extra exclusion beyond its own.)

Difference · rational − rational

\((f-g)(x)\) for \(f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x-2},\ g(x)=\dfrac{1}{x+1}\)
Common denominator
\(\dfrac{1}{x-2}-\dfrac{1}{x+1}=\dfrac{(x+1)-(x-2)}{(x-2)(x+1)}=\dfrac{3}{(x-2)(x+1)}\).
Exclude both originals' restrictions
\(\text{dom}(f)\) drops \(2\); \(\text{dom}(g)\) drops \(-1\). Intersection drops both.
Answer
\(\dfrac{3}{(x-2)(x+1)}\), domain \((-\infty,-1)\cup(-1,2)\cup(2,\infty)\).
Cheat sheet

Operate, simplify, but get the domain from the originals.

The four operations

\((f\pm g)(x)=f(x)\pm g(x)\), \((fg)(x)=f(x)g(x)\), \((f/g)(x)=f(x)/g(x)\). Simplify the result when you can.

Domain rule

Start with \(\text{dom}(f)\cap\text{dom}(g)\). For \(f/g\), also remove every \(x\) with \(g(x)=0\).

Radical × radical

\(\sqrt a\,\sqrt b=\sqrt{ab}\) only where both roots already exist. Keep the intersection of the two domains.

Cancellation

A factor that cancels still excludes its value. The hole is permanent; the simplified form just hides it.
