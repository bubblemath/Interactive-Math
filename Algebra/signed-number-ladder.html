Why working by hand wins

The calculator decides the sign so you never learn to

Signed-number rules feel small, but they're the load-bearing wall of algebra. A calculator silently resolves every sign for you, which is exactly why students who rely on it stall the moment a variable appears and there's nothing to type. The moves you'll lean on later are the by-hand sign moves:

Two negatives, one positive

Deciding the sign of $(-3)(-4)$ is the same decision as $(-2)(-x)$ or $\dfrac{-6x}{-3}$. By hand you make the call; on a calculator you never do.

The exponent grabs what it touches

$(-3)^2=9$ but $-3^2=-9$. That distinction sets the sign of every term when you evaluate $-x^2$ — and a calculator hides which one you meant.

The sign hits every term

$-(x-3)=-x+3$, not $-x-3$. Distributing a negative is pure sign-tracking, the habit you can only build by writing it out.

Predicting positive-or-negative before you compute is the same number sense that flags a wrong answer instantly. The calculator can hand you a value; it can't hand you that instinct.
