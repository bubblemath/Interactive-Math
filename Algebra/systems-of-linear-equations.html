Solving linear systems

Two methods, three possible outcomes.

A system asks where lines (or planes) meet. They might cross at a single point, never meet, or overlap completely. Substitution and elimination both find that answer — and tell you which of the three cases you're in by the equation you're left with at the end.

The three outcomes

Every linear system lands in one of three cases

Before picking a method, know what you're looking for. Two lines in a plane can do exactly three things:

One solution
Lines cross once — a single point $(x,y)$.
No solution
Parallel lines never meet — inconsistent.
Infinitely many
Same line — every point works, written with a parameter $t$.

The tell is the leftover equation. Reach a real value like $x=3$ → one solution. Reach a false statement like $0=5$ → no solution. Reach a true statement like $0=0$ → infinitely many.

Spot the case. Read each system and decide its outcome.

Substitution · 2 variables

Solve one equation for a variable, then substitute

Substitution is easiest when a variable is already alone, or has a coefficient of $1$ so isolating it costs nothing.

ONE SOLUTION
$\begin{cases} y=2x-1 \\ 3x+2y=12 \end{cases}$
1
The first equation is already solved for $y$.
2
Substitute into the second: $3x+2(2x-1)=12$.
3
$3x+4x-2=12 \Rightarrow 7x=14 \Rightarrow x=2$.
4
Back-substitute: $y=2(2)-1=3$.  Solution: $(2,3)$.

If the variables vanish and you're left with a false statement like $0=5$, there's no solution; if you're left with a true one like $0=0$, there are infinitely many.

Elimination · 2 variables

Scale equations so a variable cancels when you add

Multiply one or both equations by constants chosen to make a pair of opposite coefficients, then add.

ONE SOLUTION
$\begin{cases} x+2y=5 \\ 3x-y=8 \end{cases}$
1
Multiply the second by $2$: $6x-2y=16$.
2
Add to the first to cancel $y$: $7x=21 \Rightarrow x=3$.
3
Back-substitute: $3+2y=5 \Rightarrow y=1$.  $(3,1)$.
NO SOLUTION
$\begin{cases} x+2y=4 \\ 2x+4y=10 \end{cases}$
1
Multiply the first by $2$: $2x+4y=8$.
2
Subtract from the second: $0=2$.
3
A false statement — the lines are parallel.  No solution.
INFINITELY MANY · writing the parameter
$\begin{cases} x-3y=2 \\ 2x-6y=4 \end{cases}$
1
The second is exactly twice the first; subtracting $2\times$(first) gives $0=0$ — always true.
2
One independent equation remains: $x-3y=2$. Pick the free variable: let $y=t$.
3
Solve for the other: $x=3t+2$.  Solution: $x=3t+2,\ y=t$ for every real $t$.

Three variables · elimination & back-substitution

Eliminate one variable to drop to a 2-variable system

The plan: combine equations to remove a variable, solve the smaller system, then back-substitute upward. The same three outcomes appear — now the "infinitely many" case is a line of solutions in space.

ONE SOLUTION
$\begin{cases} x+y+z=6 \\ x-y+z=2 \\ 2x+y-z=1 \end{cases}$
1
Subtract eq.2 from eq.1 to kill $x$ and $z$: $2y=4 \Rightarrow y=2$.
2
Put $y=2$ into eq.1 and eq.3: $x+z=4$ and $2x-z=-1$.
3
Add those two: $3x=3 \Rightarrow x=1$, so $z=3$.
4
Solution: $(1,2,3)$. Check it in all three equations.
NO SOLUTION
$\begin{cases} x+y+z=6 \\ x+2y+3z=14 \\ 2x+3y+4z=18 \end{cases}$
1
Add eq.1 and eq.2: $2x+3y+4z=20$.
2
But eq.3 says $2x+3y+4z=18$. Subtract: $0=2$.
3
A contradiction.  No solution.
INFINITELY MANY
$\begin{cases} x+y+z=6 \\ x-y+z=2 \\ 2x+2z=8 \end{cases}$
1
Eq.3 is just eq.1 + eq.2 — it adds nothing new.
2
From eq.1 − eq.2: $2y=4 \Rightarrow y=2$; and eq.1 gives $x+z=4$.
3
One free variable. Let $z=t$:  $x=-t+4,\ y=2,\ z=t$.

Geometry & pitfalls

In 3-D, equations are planes

Each equation in three variables is a plane. Three planes can meet at a single point, share a whole line, or have no common point at all — the same trichotomy, one dimension up.

Meet at a point
Share a line (infinite)
No common point

Which method?

Reach for substitution when a variable already stands alone or has coefficient $1$ (isolating it is free). Reach for elimination when coefficients are tangled, or whenever you have three variables — systematically canceling one variable at a time is cleaner than nested substitutions.

⚠ Common slips

  • Scale the whole equation. Multiplying to eliminate means multiplying the right-hand side too, not just the variable terms.
  • Mind the signs when adding. Subtracting an equation flips every sign on that line — a frequent source of wrong answers.
  • $0=0$ is not "no solution." A true statement means infinitely many; a false one like $0=5$ means none.
  • Always back-substitute. Finding $x$ is half the job; feed it back to get the rest.
  • Name the parameter. "Infinitely many" isn't a complete answer — write the family with $t$, e.g. $x=-t+4,\ y=2,\ z=t$.
  • Verify in every equation. A point that fits two of three planes can still miss the third.
