A term has two parts. Only one of them is yours to change.

Every term is a coefficient and a variable part — the 6 and the x. When you add, you touch the coefficients and leave the variable part exactly as it was. When you multiply, you touch both. That single difference is the most expensive error in this unit, and your notes call it out by name on page 18. Eleven rungs, one decision each, until the two rules stop trading places.

6x + 7x = 13x, but 6x · 7x = 42x2. Same two numbers, same two variables, and the answers do not resemble each other — because the operation decided which parts were allowed to move.

✋ Adding never touches the exponent.

6x + 7x is 13x, not 42x2. And notice the shape of that mistake, because you have met it twice already: it is a multiplication rule carried into addition. In the integers unit it was −3 + (−5) = 8. With fractions it was 3⁄8 + 1⁄2 = 4⁄10. This is the same habit, third chapter running — and a student who sees the pattern once tends to stop making all three.

Second trap: a bare minus is a −1, and it has to reach every term. −(−2x + 9y − 7) is 2x − 9y + 7 — all three signs flip, not just the first. Subtracting polynomials is nothing but this, which is why rung 10 makes you write the line out before combining anything.

Third, smaller: x · x = x2 but x + x = 2x. The invisible exponent of 1 and the invisible coefficient of 1, doing opposite jobs in the same expression.

Why this matters

This is the last unit before everything is algebra

Combining like terms never stops

Solving equations, factoring, graphing, systems — every one of them ends with combining like terms, usually several times inside one problem. An error here does not stay here.

The properties are the permission

Every rearrangement you will ever make is licensed by one of four properties. Naming them feels like vocabulary now; later it is the difference between a step you can justify and a step you are guessing at.

Distributing is a reach problem

The factor outside has to touch every term inside, which is the same scope question the order-of-operations unit was built on. A missed term is invisible in the answer — nothing about it looks wrong.

This lesson looks like a list of property names followed by a pile of practice, and it is really one question repeated: which parts of this term am I allowed to change, and what gives me permission? The properties answer the second half. The operation answers the first. Once both are automatic, simplifying is bookkeeping — and bookkeeping is the part you can already do.
