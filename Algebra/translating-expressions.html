Pre-algebra · Words into expressions

Every phrase hides an operation — and sometimes a hidden order.

Translating is two jobs at once. First, spot the word that names the operation: "sum" means add, "quotient" means divide. Second, get the order right — because a few phrases like "less than" and "subtracted from" quietly flip it. Master both and any sentence becomes an expression.

Skill 02 · Getting the order right

A few phrases reverse what you write.

Addition and multiplication don't care about order — "3 more than a number" and "a number plus 3" are the same. But subtraction and division do, and three phrases flip the order on purpose: "less than," "subtracted from," and "goes into." Read them carefully.

The reversers. "5 less than x" is \(x-5\). "5 subtracted from x" is \(x-5\). "5 goes into x" is \(x\div5\). The number you hear first lands second.

Worked examples · order

"less than" flips
"5 less than a number" → the number comes first: \(x-5\) (not \(5-x\)).
"subtracted from" flips
"10 subtracted from a number" → \(x-10\). But "a number subtracted from 10" → \(10-x\).
"decreased by" keeps order
"a number decreased by 4" → \(x-4\). No flip — start at the number, take away 4.
"goes into" flips
"3 goes into a number" → \(x\div3 = \tfrac{x}{3}\). Whatever goes into the other is the divisor.

Skill 03 · Translating the whole phrase

Put the operation and the order together — and watch for grouping.

Some phrases bundle a piece together first. Words like "the sum of," "the difference of," or "the quantity" wrap things in parentheses. "Twice the sum of a number and 3" is \(2(x+3)\); but "the sum of twice a number and 3" is \(2x+3\). Where the grouping words sit tells you where the parentheses go.

"by" is not always times. "Increased by 5" adds (\(x+5\)); "multiplied by 5" multiplies (\(5x\)). The verb decides — "by" just points to the amount.

Worked examples · translation

Plain
"the sum of a number and 8" → \(x+8\).
Order trap
"12 less than a number" → \(x-12\).
Multiplication
"the product of 5 and a number" → \(5x\); "triple a number" → \(3x\).
Division
"the quotient of a number and 4" → \(\tfrac{x}{4}\); "8 divided by a number" → \(\tfrac{8}{x}\).
Grouping
"twice the sum of a number and 3" → \(2(x+3)\). The "sum" is grouped first, then doubled.
