📖 Key Vocabulary

Term Definition Example
Experiment Any occurrence where the outcome is uncertain A coin toss is an experiment
Sample Space (S) Set of all possible outcomes of the experiment For a coin toss: S = {Heads, Tails}
Event (E) Any subset of the sample space E = {Heads} is an event
Theoretical Probability P(E) = n(E) / n(S)
Probability is expressed as a number between 0 and 1		 P(Heads) = 1/2

The Probability Formula

P(E) = n(E) n(S) = Number of favorable outcomes Total number of possible outcomes

💡 Important Facts About Probability

  • Probability is always between 0 and 1 (inclusive)
  • P(E) = 0 means the event is impossible
  • P(E) = 1 means the event is certain
  • The sum of all probabilities in a sample space equals 1

🎯 Basic Probability Examples

🎲 Example: Rolling a Die

A six-sided die is rolled once. Find the probability of:

S = {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}    n(S) = 6

a) Probability of rolling a 2

E = {2}, so n(E) = 1

P(rolling a 2) = n(E)n(S) = 16

b) Probability of rolling a number less than 4

E = {1, 2, 3}, so n(E) = 3

P(less than 4) = 36 = 12

c) Probability of rolling a number greater than 7

E = { } (empty set - impossible!), so n(E) = 0

P(greater than 7) = 06 = 0

🃏 Example: Drawing a Card

You are dealt one card from a standard 52-card deck. Find the probability of:

n(S) = 52

a) Probability of drawing a Queen

There are 4 Queens in the deck, so n(E) = 4

P(Queen) = 452 = 113

b) Probability of drawing a Heart

There are 13 Hearts in the deck, so n(E) = 13

P(Heart) = 1352 = 14

c) Probability of drawing a card greater than 3 and less than 7

Cards: 4, 5, 6 (three values, 4 of each suit)

n(E) = 3 × 4 = 12

P(4, 5, or 6) = 1252 = 313

👨‍👩‍👧 Example: Family of 3 Children

You select a family with 3 children. S = {MMM, MMF, MFM, MFF, FMM, FMF, FFM, FFF}

n(S) = 8

a) Probability of having exactly one female child

E = {MMF, MFM, FMM}, so n(E) = 3

b) Probability of having at least one male child

E = {MMM, MMF, MFM, MFF, FMM, FMF, FFM} (all except FFF)

n(E) = 7

P(at least 1 male) = 78

📊 Probability with Permutations & Combinations

Review: Key Formulas

Permutation (ORDER MATTERS):
nPr = n! (n − r)!
Combination (ORDER DOESN'T MATTER):
nCr = n! r!(n − r)!
Probability:
P(E) = n(E) n(S)

📝 Step-by-Step Problem Solving Method

a

Is this a Permutation or a Combination?

Ask: "Does the ORDER matter?"

  • Order matters (line-ups, rankings, positions) → Permutation
  • Order doesn't matter (committees, groups, card hands) → Combination
b

Find the Total Number of Ways (Denominator)

Calculate ALL possible ways the selection/arrangement could happen.

This gives you n(S) for your probability formula.

c

Find the Number of Favorable Outcomes (Numerator)

Calculate the number of ways to get the SPECIFIC outcome you want.

This gives you n(E).

d

Calculate the Probability

P(E) = n(E)n(S)

Simplify your fraction if possible.

📖 Worked Example: Line-Up Problem

PERMUTATION

Problem:

Four boys and three girls line up at the classroom door. Find the probability that the first person is a girl and the last person is a boy.

a

Is this a permutation or a combination?

PERMUTATION - The order of the line matters!

b

Find the total possible ways the 7 children can be lined up

n(S) = 7! = 5,040
c

Find the number of ways with girl first AND boy last

• First position (girl): 3 choices

• Last position (boy): 4 choices

• Middle 5 positions: 5! = 120 ways

n(E) = 3 × 4 × 5! = 3 × 4 × 120 = 1,440
d

Calculate the probability

P(E) = 1,4405,040 = 27

📖 Worked Example: Committee Selection

COMBINATION

Problem:

A company has 7 women and 5 men. A group of 4 is selected at random. Find the probability of selecting 2 women and 2 men.

a

Is this a permutation or a combination?

COMBINATION - We're selecting a group, order doesn't matter!

b

Find the total number of ways to form a group of 4

n(S) = 12C4 = 12!4! × 8! = 495
c

Find the number of ways to select 2 women AND 2 men

n(E) = 7C2 × 5C2 = 21 × 10 = 210
d

Calculate the probability

P(E) = 210495 = 1433
