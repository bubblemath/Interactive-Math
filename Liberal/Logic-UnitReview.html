📝 Logic Unit Summary

⚡ Quick Reference: Truth Table Patterns

Key Patterns for Truth Tables Simple Reminders
Or (Disjunction) FF = F, otherwise T
And (Conjunction) TT = T, otherwise F
Conditional TF = F, otherwise T
Biconditional Same = T (TT = T, FF = T), otherwise false

🔤 Key Symbols & Connectives

~p - Negation: "Not p" (Opposite truth value)
p ∧ q - Conjunction: "p and q" (True ONLY when both are true)
p ∨ q - Disjunction: "p or q" (False ONLY when both are false)
p → q - Conditional: "If p, then q" (False ONLY when p is true and q is false)
p ↔ q - Biconditional: "p if and only if q" (True ONLY when p and q have the same truth value)

🔄 Variations of Conditional Statements (p → q)

Converse: q → p (Swap hypothesis and conclusion)
Inverse: ~p → ~q (Negate both)
Contrapositive: ~q → ~p (Swap AND negate both - Equivalent to original!)

⚖️ De Morgan's Laws

~(p ∧ q) ≡ ~p ∨ ~q
"Not (p and q)" = "Not p OR not q"
~(p ∨ q) ≡ ~p ∧ ~q
"Not (p or q)" = "Not p AND not q"

❌ Negation of a Conditional

~(p → q) ≡ p ∧ ~q
"Not (if p then q)" = "p AND not q"

📊 Quantifier Negations (The "Diagonal Rule")

Negation of "All A are B" → "Some A are not B"
Negation of "Some A are B" → "No A are B" (All A are not B)
Negation of "No A are B" → "Some A are B"
Negation of "Some A are not B" → "All A are B"

✅ Valid Argument Forms

Direct Reasoning (Modus Ponens): p → q, p ∴ q
Contrapositive Reasoning (Modus Tollens): p → q, ~q ∴ ~p
Disjunctive Reasoning (Form 1): p ∨ q, ~p ∴ q
Disjunctive Reasoning (Form 2): p ∨ q, ~q ∴ p
Transitive Reasoning: p → q, q → r ∴ p → r

❌ Invalid Argument Forms (Fallacies)

Fallacy of the Converse: p → q, q ∴ p
Fallacy of the Inverse: p → q, ~p ∴ ~q
Misuse of Disjunctive Reasoning (Form 1): p ∨ q, p ∴ ~q
Misuse of Disjunctive Reasoning (Form 2): p ∨ q, q ∴ ~p
Misuse of Transitive Reasoning (Form 1): p → q, q → r ∴ r → p
Misuse of Transitive Reasoning (Form 2): p → q, q → r ∴ ~p → ~r

🎯 Key Definitions

Tautology: A statement that is always TRUE

Self-Contradiction: A statement that is always FALSE

Valid Argument: An argument where the conclusion is true whenever all premises are true

Equivalent Statements: Statements with identical truth values for all combinations (symbol: ≡)
