≡ Lesson 9: Equivalent Statements & De Morgan's Laws

Variations, Negations & Logical Equivalence

📚 Key Concepts

≡ Equivalent Statements

Two compound statements are equivalent if they have the same truth values for all possible combinations of their simple statements.

The symbol ≡ is used to indicate equivalence.

If a statement is true, its equivalent statement is also true. If a statement is false, its equivalent statement is also false.

🔄 The Four Variations of a Conditional

Name Symbolic Form Description
Conditional p → q If p, then q
Converse q → p Swap p and q
Inverse ~p → ~q Negate both parts
Contrapositive ~q → ~p Swap AND negate both

✓ Key Equivalences

  • Conditional ≡ Contrapositive: p → q ≡ ~q → ~p
  • Converse ≡ Inverse: q → p ≡ ~p → ~q

✗ NOT Equivalent!

  • Conditional ≢ Converse: p → q ≢ q → p
  • Conditional ≢ Inverse: p → q ≢ ~p → ~q

🚫 Negating a Conditional

The negation of a conditional is NOT another conditional!

~(p → q) ≡ p ∧ ~q

"It is NOT true that if p then q" becomes "p AND not q"

⚡ De Morgan's Laws

Original Equivalent In Words
~(p ∧ q) ~p ∨ ~q "Not both" → "Not one OR not the other"
~(p ∨ q) ~p ∧ ~q "Not either" → "Not one AND not the other"

Remember: Flip the connective (∧↔∨) AND negate both parts!

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Mistake: Thinking the conditional equals its converse (p → q ≠ q → p)
  • Mistake: Thinking ~(p → q) = ~p → ~q (That's the inverse, not the negation!)
  • Mistake: Forgetting to flip the connective when applying De Morgan's
  • Mistake: Forgetting to negate BOTH parts when applying De Morgan's

⚠️ Common Mistake

The Converse is NOT equivalent to the Conditional!

Example: "If it rains, the ground is wet" does NOT mean "If the ground is wet, it rained" (someone could have used a hose!)

❌ Common Mistake: Confusing Negation with Inverse

~(p → q) ≠ ~p → ~q

The negation of "If it rains, the ground is wet" is:

  • ✓ Correct: "It rains AND the ground is NOT wet" (p ∧ ~q)
  • ✗ Wrong: "If it doesn't rain, the ground isn't wet" (~p → ~q) — This is the INVERSE!

🌍 Real-World Context

Statement: "If you build it, they will come."

p: You build it
q: They will come

Negation: "You build it AND they do NOT come."

This describes the ONE scenario where the original promise fails!

⚡ De Morgan's Laws

Watch the "Flip and Negate" transformation step-by-step!

De Morgan's "Flip & Negate" Animator

~(p ∧ q)
→
?
Step 1: Identify the connective inside the parentheses (∧ or ∨)
Step 2: FLIP the connective (∧ becomes ∨, or ∨ becomes ∧)
Step 3: NEGATE each component (p becomes ~p, q becomes ~q)
Step 4: Remove the outer negation — transformation complete!

📋 De Morgan's Laws Summary

Original Transformation Result
~(p ∧ q) Flip ∧→∨, Negate both ~p ∨ ~q
~(p ∨ q) Flip ∨→∧, Negate both ~p ∧ ~q

⚠️ Common Mistakes with De Morgan's

  • Forgetting to flip: ~(p ∧ q) ≠ ~p ∧ ~q (must change ∧ to ∨!)
  • Forgetting to negate both: ~(p ∧ q) ≠ ~p ∨ q (must negate BOTH p and q!)
  • Double negation: If you have ~(~p ∧ q), the ~p becomes ~~p = p

🔄 Symbol Correspondence

Logic Set Theory Meaning

∨ (OR) ∪ (Union) Either / In at least one
