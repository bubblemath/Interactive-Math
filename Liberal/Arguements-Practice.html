🎯 Lesson 10: Arguments

Valid Forms, Fallacies & Logical Reasoning

📚 Key Concepts

🎯 What is an Argument?

An argument consists of two parts:

  • Premises: Statements assumed to be true (the "given" information)
  • Conclusion: A statement that follows from the premises (marked with ∴)

The symbolic form is: [(Premise 1) ∧ (Premise 2)] → Conclusion

✓ Valid vs. ✗ Invalid Arguments

Type Definition Truth Table Test

✓ The Four Valid Argument Forms

Disjunctive Reasoningp ∨ q, ~p ∴ qProcess of Elimination
Transitive Reasoningp → q, q → r ∴ p → rChain Rule

✗ The Three Common Fallacies

NameFormWhy It's Invalid
Fallacy of the Conversep → q, q ∴ pAffirming the consequent doesn't prove the antecedent
Fallacy of the Inversep → q, ~p ∴ ~qDenying the antecedent doesn't disprove the consequent
Misuse of Disjunctivep ∨ q, p ∴ ~q"Or" is inclusive—both can be true!

⚠️ Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Confusing Modus Ponens with Fallacy of Converse: "p → q, p ∴ q" is VALID, but "p → q, q ∴ p" is INVALID!
  • Confusing Modus Tollens with Fallacy of Inverse: "p → q, ~q ∴ ~p" is VALID, but "p → q, ~p ∴ ~q" is INVALID!
  • Forgetting "or" is inclusive: In logic, p ∨ q means one OR the other OR BOTH can be true!

💡 How to Recognize Valid Forms

  • Modus Ponens: You have p → q AND you AFFIRM p → You can conclude q
  • Modus Tollens: You have p → q AND you DENY q → You can conclude ~p
  • Disjunctive: You have p ∨ q AND you ELIMINATE one → You can conclude the other
  • Transitive: You have a CHAIN of conditionals → You can connect the ends

⚠️ Why These Are Fallacies

  • Fallacy of Converse: Just because the ground is wet doesn't mean it rained — someone could have used a hose!
  • Fallacy of Inverse: Just because it didn't rain doesn't mean the ground isn't wet — there are other ways to wet the ground!
  • Misuse of Disjunctive: "I'll have coffee OR tea" doesn't mean I can't have both!

🔍 Spot the Difference!

In Modus Ponens, we affirm the antecedent (p) — the "if" part.
In the Fallacy of Converse, we affirm the consequent (q) — the "then" part.

The ground being wet doesn't prove it rained — someone could have used a sprinkler!

💡 Memory Tricks

  • Modus Ponens: "Ponens" sounds like "posit" — you POSIT (affirm) the antecedent
  • Modus Tollens: "Tollens" sounds like "toll" — you TOLL (deny) the consequent
  • Valid forms work with the FLOW: p → q, so affirm p or deny q
  • Fallacies work AGAINST the flow: Affirming q or denying p doesn't work!
