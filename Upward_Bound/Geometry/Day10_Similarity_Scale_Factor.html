🔑 The "Multiply by 1" Strategy

💡 Key Insight

Any fraction where the numerator equals the denominator is equal to 1. Multiplying by 1 doesn't change the value — it just changes the units!

Example: \(\frac{60 \text{ sec}}{1 \text{ min}} = 1\) because 60 seconds IS 1 minute.

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1: Convert 3.5 hours to seconds
1
Start with what you have: \(3.5 \text{ hours}\)
2
Multiply by conversion factors: \(3.5 \text{ hr} \times \frac{60 \text{ min}}{1 \text{ hr}} \times \frac{60 \text{ sec}}{1 \text{ min}}\)
3
Cancel units: hours cancel, minutes cancel, seconds remain
4
Calculate: \(3.5 \times 60 \times 60 = \mathbf{12{,}600 \text{ seconds}}\)
Example 2: Convert 100,000 inches to miles
1
Start: \(100{,}000 \text{ inches}\)
2
Chain conversions: \(100{,}000 \text{ in} \times \frac{1 \text{ ft}}{12 \text{ in}} \times \frac{1 \text{ mi}}{5{,}280 \text{ ft}}\)
3
Calculate: \(\frac{100{,}000}{12 \times 5{,}280} = \frac{100{,}000}{63{,}360} \approx \mathbf{1.58 \text{ miles}}\)
Example 3: Convert 10,000 feet to kilometers
1
Start: \(10{,}000 \text{ feet}\)
2
Chain: \(10{,}000 \text{ ft} \times \frac{1 \text{ mi}}{5{,}280 \text{ ft}} \times \frac{1.609 \text{ km}}{1 \text{ mi}}\)
3
Calculate: \(\frac{10{,}000 \times 1.609}{5{,}280} \approx \mathbf{3.05 \text{ km}}\)

⚖️ Ratio & Proportion Lab

A proportion is simply two ratios that are equal. Cross-multiplication is just efficient algebra!

📐 The Proportion

\(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}\)

Cross-multiply: \(ad = bc\)

🔍 Why Cross-Multiplication Works

It's not magic — it's just clearing denominators!

The Proof
1
Start with: \(\frac{a}{b} = \frac{c}{d}\)
2
Multiply both sides by \(bd\): \(bd \cdot \frac{a}{b} = bd \cdot \frac{c}{d}\)
3
Cancel denominators: \(d \cdot a = b \cdot c\)
4
Result: \(ad = bc\) — the "cross products"!

💡 Key Insight

Cross-multiplication is just clearing denominators — the same algebra from Day 2! We use it when we have ONE fraction equal to ANOTHER fraction.

📐 Similar Figures & Scale Factor

Similar figures have the same shape but different sizes. The scale factor is the "zoom level."

📐 Scale Factor

\(k = \frac{\text{New Side}}{\text{Original Side}}\)

New Side = k × Original Side

🔍 The "Zoom Level" Concept

Scale Factor (k) Effect Example
\(k = 2\) Enlargement (2× bigger) 5 → 10
\(k = 3\) Enlargement (3× bigger) 5 → 15
\(k = \frac{1}{2}\) Reduction (half size) 10 → 5
\(k = 1\) Congruent (same size) 5 → 5

💡 Memory Device

"Scale factor is the MULTIPLIER. If k = 2, every side doubles. If k = ½, every side halves."

📝 Worked Examples: Similar Polygons

Example 1: Similar Rectangles

Rectangle ABCD ~ Rectangle EFGH. AB = 6, EF = 9, BC = 4. Find FG.

1
Find scale factor: \(k = \frac{9}{6} = \frac{3}{2}\)
2
Apply to BC: \(FG = k \times BC = \frac{3}{2} \times 4 = 6\)
Example 2: Using Proportions

Pentagon ABCDE ~ Pentagon FGHIJ. AB = 8, FG = 12, CD = 10. Find HI.

1
Set up proportion: \(\frac{AB}{FG} = \frac{CD}{HI}\)
2
Substitute: \(\frac{8}{12} = \frac{10}{HI}\)
3
Cross-multiply: \(8 \cdot HI = 120\) → \(HI = 15\)

⚠️ Congruent vs. Similar

Symbol Meaning What's Equal
\(\cong\) Congruent Same shape AND same size
\(\sim\) Similar Same shape, PROPORTIONAL sizes

💡 Key Insight

Congruent figures are similar with scale factor k = 1. Similarity is the more general concept!

📜 Triangle Similarity Reference

⚠️ Reference Only: This section covers how to PROVE triangles are similar. These proofs are for reference and will NOT appear in the Practice Zone.

🔺 The Three Similarity Postulates

AA Similarity

Angle-Angle

What You Need:
Two pairs of congruent angles

If two angles of one triangle are congruent to two angles of another triangle, the triangles are similar.

SAS Similarity

Side-Angle-Side

What You Need:
Two pairs of proportional sides + included angle congruent

If two sides of one triangle are proportional to two sides of another, and the included angles are congruent, the triangles are similar.

SSS Similarity

Side-Side-Side

What You Need:
All three pairs of sides proportional

If all three pairs of corresponding sides are proportional, the triangles are similar.

📝 Proof Example 1: AA Similarity

Given: ∠A ≅ ∠D, ∠B ≅ ∠E

Prove: △ABC ~ △DEF

Statements Reasons
1. ∠A ≅ ∠D 1. Given
2. ∠B ≅ ∠E 2. Given
3. △ABC ~ △DEF 3. AA Similarity Postulate

📝 Proof Example 2: SAS Similarity

Given: \(\frac{AB}{DE} = \frac{AC}{DF}\), ∠A ≅ ∠D

Prove: △ABC ~ △DEF

Statements Reasons
1. \(\frac{AB}{DE} = \frac{AC}{DF}\) 1. Given
2. ∠A ≅ ∠D 2. Given
3. △ABC ~ △DEF 3. SAS Similarity Theorem

📝 Proof Example 3: SSS Similarity

Given: \(\frac{AB}{DE} = \frac{BC}{EF} = \frac{AC}{DF}\)

Prove: △ABC ~ △DEF

Statements Reasons
1. \(\frac{AB}{DE} = \frac{BC}{EF} = \frac{AC}{DF}\) 1. Given
2. △ABC ~ △DEF 2. SSS Similarity Theorem

⏱️ Why Time Yourself?

Shape Detectives, timing yourself serves two purposes:

  • Fluency Building: In geometry, you'll encounter proportions dozens of times per unit. Timing helps you move from "thinking through each step" to "automatic recognition" — the same way you don't have to think about spelling common words.
  • Exam Readiness: Standardized tests and classroom exams have time limits. Practicing under mild time pressure now builds confidence for when it counts.

Start with the timer OFF while learning. Turn it ON once you feel comfortable to build speed.
