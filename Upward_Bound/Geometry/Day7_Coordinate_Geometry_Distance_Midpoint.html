📍 Day 7: Coordinate Geometry — Distance & Midpoint

The Coordinate Grid Investigation

The Distance Formula is the Pythagorean Theorem in disguise!

Students who understand this connection never forget the formula. Today we unmask the disguise!

  • Distance Formula: It's just \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\) on a coordinate plane
  • Midpoint Formula: It's just finding the average — "meet your friend halfway!"
  • Coordinate Proofs: These formulas become your "evidence" in proofs
  • Real Applications: GPS, mapping, game design, architecture

Master these formulas today, and coordinate geometry becomes your superpower!

📐 The Pythagorean Theorem

\(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\)

In a right triangle, the sum of the squares of the legs equals the square of the hypotenuse.

📐 The Distance Formula: Unmasking the Disguise

The Distance Formula isn't a new formula to memorize — it's the Pythagorean Theorem applied to coordinates!

🎭 THE UNMASKING

Pythagorean Theorem: \(a^2 + b^2 = c^2\)
⬇️
In Coordinates: \((\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2 = d^2\)

where \(\Delta x = x_2 - x_1\) and \(\Delta y = y_2 - y_1\)

⬇️
Distance Formula: \(d = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}\)

💡 Key Insight

The horizontal distance \(|x_2 - x_1|\) is one leg (a), the vertical distance \(|y_2 - y_1|\) is the other leg (b), and the distance between the points is the hypotenuse (c)!

📝 Worked Example

Find the distance between A(1, 2) and B(4, 6).

1
Identify coordinates: \((x_1, y_1) = (1, 2)\) and \((x_2, y_2) = (4, 6)\)
2
Substitute: \(d = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (6-2)^2}\)
3
Simplify: \(d = \sqrt{3^2 + 4^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25}\)
4
Answer: \(d = 5\)

Find the distance between P(-2, 3) and Q(4, -1).

1
Identify coordinates: \((x_1, y_1) = (-2, 3)\) and \((x_2, y_2) = (4, -1)\)
2
Substitute: \(d = \sqrt{(4-(-2))^2 + (-1-3)^2}\)
3
Simplify: \(d = \sqrt{6^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{36 + 16} = \sqrt{52}\)
4
Simplify radical: \(\sqrt{52} = \sqrt{4 \times 13} = 2\sqrt{13}\)

📍 The Midpoint Formula: Meet Your Friend Halfway!

The Midpoint Formula is simply the average of the coordinates. Average the x's, average the y's!

📍 The Midpoint Formula

\(M = \left( \frac{x_1 + x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2} \right)\)

The midpoint is the average of the x-coordinates and the average of the y-coordinates.

💡 Memory Device

Midpoint = Middle Point = Mean of the coordinates

If you want to meet a friend halfway, you find the average of your locations!

📝 Worked Examples

Example 1: Find the midpoint of A(2, 8) and B(6, 4).

1
Average the x's: \(\frac{2 + 6}{2} = \frac{8}{2} = 4\)
2
Average the y's: \(\frac{8 + 4}{2} = \frac{12}{2} = 6\)
3
Midpoint: \(M = (4, 6)\)

Example 2: Find the midpoint of P(-3, 5) and Q(7, -1).

1
Average the x's: \(\frac{-3 + 7}{2} = \frac{4}{2} = 2\)
2
Average the y's: \(\frac{5 + (-1)}{2} = \frac{4}{2} = 2\)
3
Midpoint: \(M = (2, 2)\)

Example 3 (Fractions): Find the midpoint of A(1, 3) and B(4, 8).

1
Average the x's: \(\frac{1 + 4}{2} = \frac{5}{2}\)
2
Average the y's: \(\frac{3 + 8}{2} = \frac{11}{2}\)
3
Midpoint: \(M = \left(\frac{5}{2}, \frac{11}{2}\right)\) — Leave as fractions!

🔄 Finding a Missing Endpoint

Sometimes you know one endpoint and the midpoint, and need to find the other endpoint!

🔑 Strategy

If M is the average of A and B, then B must be "on the other side" of M from A.

Whatever change you make from A to M, make the same change from M to B!

Example: A(2, 5) and midpoint M(6, 8). Find B.

1
From A to M: x changes by +4, y changes by +3
2
From M to B: x changes by +4, y changes by +3
3
B = (6 + 4, 8 + 3) = (10, 11)

Algebraic Method:

\(\frac{2 + x}{2} = 6 \Rightarrow 2 + x = 12 \Rightarrow x = 10\)

\(\frac{5 + y}{2} = 8 \Rightarrow 5 + y = 16 \Rightarrow y = 11\)

B = (10, 11)

🧊 3D Sneak Peek: Adding the Z-Axis

Everything you learned today extends naturally to 3D! Just add a third coordinate.

🌐 Welcome to 3D Coordinates

In 3D, every point has three coordinates: \((x, y, z)\)

📐 The Formulas Extend Naturally

2D Distance Formula

\(d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}\)

3D Distance Formula

\(d = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2 + (z_2-z_1)^2}\)

2D Midpoint Formula

\(M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)\)

3D Midpoint Formula

\(M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2}, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2}, \frac{z_1+z_2}{2}\right)\)

💡 The Pattern

Notice the pattern? Just add another term for the z-coordinate! The same logic applies:

  • Distance: Add \((z_2 - z_1)^2\) under the radical
  • Midpoint: Add \(\frac{z_1 + z_2}{2}\) as the third coordinate

📝 3D Example

Find the distance between A(1, 2, 3) and B(4, 6, 7).

1
Apply the 3D formula: \(d = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (6-2)^2 + (7-3)^2}\)
2
Simplify: \(d = \sqrt{9 + 16 + 16} = \sqrt{41}\)

Find the midpoint of A(1, 2, 3) and B(4, 6, 7).

1
Average each coordinate: \(M = \left(\frac{1+4}{2}, \frac{2+6}{2}, \frac{3+7}{2}\right)\)
2
Simplify: \(M = \left(\frac{5}{2}, 4, 5\right)\)
