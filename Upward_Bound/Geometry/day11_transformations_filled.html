★ Why This Matters — You're Getting a Head Start:
Transformations = "Coordinate Commands" — programming rules that move, flip, turn, or zoom any shape on the coordinate plane. Each transformation is a rule: input (x, y) → output (x′, y′).
This is the opening unit of most 9th-grade geometry courses. You'll enter September having already seen it — that confidence is real.

Section 1 — Translation: The Slide
★ Discover before the formula:
To move a shape 3 units right, what happens to the x-coordinate? x increases by 3 (+3)
To move a shape 2 units up, what happens to the y-coordinate? y increases by 2 (+2)
To move a shape 4 units left: x − 4   5 units down: y − 5
➡️ GeoGebra — Translation Explorer geogebra.org/m/BDzKR5st
Task: Drag the translation vector. Predict the new coordinates before reading them.
  • Does the shape rotate or flip when you drag? No — shape stays identical. Only its position changes. Size, angles, and orientation are all preserved.
  • Try: translate left 3, down 4 — what is the rule in (x, y) → form? (x, y) → (x − 3, y − 4)
My rule: (x, y) → (x − 3, y − 4)
Translation Rule:
Coordinate Rule
(x, y) → (x + a, y + b)
+a: right    −a: left
+b: up      −b: down
Example: P(3, −2), translate left 6, up 5
New x: 3 + (−6) = −3
New y: −2 + 5 = 3
P′ = (−3, 3)
Section 2 — Reflection: The Flip
🪞 GeoGebra — Reflection Explorer geogebra.org/m/cFqBxS69 → Coordinate Reflections
Task: Reflect a point over each line. Look at coordinates before and after. Fill in the table BEFORE your teacher tells you the rules.
  • Over the x-axis: y changed (flipped sign); x stayed the same.
  • Over the y-axis: x changed (flipped sign); y stayed the same.
  • Over y = x: x and y swap positions. Prediction correct!

Over x-axis → (x, −y)
(4, −3) → (4, 3)
Over y-axis → (−x, y)
(4, −3) → (−4, −3)
Over y = x → (y, x)
(4, −3) → (−3, 4)

★ Mirror Mnemonic:
The coordinate ON the mirror line stays the same. The coordinate measuring distance from the mirror flips sign.
Orientation: Reflection reverses vertex order (like a mirror image). Translation and rotation preserve vertex order. All three are rigid motions — the image is congruent to the original.

Section 3 — Rotation: The Turn (CCW around the Origin)
🔄 GeoGebra — Rotation Explorer geogebra.org/m/FrsJMaqe
Task: Rotate a point 90°, 180°, and 270° CCW around the origin. Watch how the x and y coordinates change each time.
  • (3,2) after 90° CCW → (−2, 3). x became −y; y became the old x.
  • After 180°: both coordinates negate → (−3, −2). After 270° CCW → (2, −3).
  • Rotating 90° CCW four times → back to start (3, 2). Full 360°.
Complete the rotation rule table from your observations:
RotationRuleWhat happensQuadrant check
90° CCW (−y, x) x → −y, y → old x Q I → Q II
180° (−x, −y) Both signs flip Q I → Q III
270° CCW (y, −x) x → y, y → −old x = 90° CW
★ The "Lie on Your Side" Trick (90° CCW):
Tilt your head 90° to the LEFT and look at the axes.
What was pointing UP (pos. y) now points to your right (pos. x direction).
What was pointing RIGHT (pos. x) now points down (neg. y direction).
Result:   old y → new x  ·  old x → new −y
Rule: (x, y) → (−y, x) ✓
Rotate A(3, 2) by each angle — verify using quadrant check:
90° CCW: (x, y) → (−y, x)
(3, 2) → (−2, 3)
Check: landed in QII ✓ (Q I → Q II for 90° CCW)
180°: (x, y) → (−x, −y)
(3, 2) → (−3, −2)
270° CCW: (x, y) → (y, −x)
(3, 2) → (2, −3)
⚠ 180° ≠ Reflection! A(3, 4) after 180° rotation → (−3, −4). After reflection over x-axis → (3, −4). Different! Rotation preserves vertex order; reflection reverses it.
Section 4 — Dilation: The Zoom (The ONLY Non-Rigid Transformation)
🔎 GeoGebra — Dilation Explorer geogebra.org/m/drCj7kxE
Task: Drag the scale factor slider. Describe what you see for each range of k before reading any labels.
  • When k > 1: the shape gets bigger (enlarged) — moves away from origin.
  • When 0 < k < 1: the shape gets smaller (reduced) — moves toward origin.
  • When k = 1: no change — same size, same position.
  • What stays the same regardless of k? All angle measures — the shape is always similar to the original.

k = 1

Dilation Rule
(x, y) → (kx, ky)
k = scale factor (same k as Day 10 similarity!). Multiply BOTH coordinates by k.
★ Day 10 Connection:
Dilation CREATES similar figures. Yesterday's scale factor k IS today's dilation k. Rigid motions → congruent. Dilation → similar figures
Worked examples:
k = 3: A(2, −4)
(2,−4) → (6, −12)
k = ½: B(8, −6)
(8,−6) → (4, −3)
Find k: C(3,5)→C′(9,15)
k = 9/3 = 3   verify: 15/5 = 3 ✓
Area scaling from Day 9: If linear scale factor = k, then area scales by k². Dilate a shape by k = 3 → area multiplies by 9.

Section 5 — Composition: Applying Transformations in Sequence
★ Does Order Matter? — Watch Your Teacher Compute Both Sequences:
Same two transformations, same point — different order. What do you predict?
Sequence A: Translate THEN Reflect
Start: (2, 1)
Step 1 — Translate (x+3, y):   (5, 1)
Step 2 — Reflect over y-axis:   (−5, 1)
Final A: (−5, 1)
Sequence B: Reflect THEN Translate
Start: (2, 1)
Step 1 — Reflect over y-axis:   (−2, 1)
Step 2 — Translate (x+3, y):   (1, 1)
Final B: (1, 1)
Final A ≠ Final B  →  ORDER MATTERS! Same transformations, different order = different result
The Composition Rule:
Apply the transformation listed first in the sentence first. Each step uses the output of the previous step as its input.
Ex 1 — Reflect then Translate (guided)
Point (2, 3): First reflect over x-axis, then translate (x+1, y+2)
Step 1: Reflect x-axis → (x, −y)(2, 3) → (2, −3)
Step 2: Translate (x+1, y+2)(2, −3) → (3, −1)
Final: (3, −1)
Ex 2 — Rotate then Dilate (try it)
Point (4, 0): First rotate 90° CCW, then dilate k = 2
Step 1: 90° CCW → (−y, x)(4, 0) → (0, 4)
Step 2: Dilate k = 2 → (2x, 2y)(0, 4) → (0, 8)
Final: (0, 8)
Ex 3 — Double Reflection (discover the shortcut!)
Point (3, −2): First reflect over x-axis, then reflect over y-axis
Step 1: Reflect x-axis → (x, −y)(3, −2) → (3, 2)
Step 2: Reflect y-axis → (−x, y)(3, 2) → (−3, 2)
Final: (−3, 2)
Now apply a 180° rotation to (3, −2):
(3, −2) → (−3, 2)
Are these the same? → YES
Shortcut: reflect over x then y = 180° rotation
Ex 4 — Find k from a composition (try it)
Point P(4, 6) is dilated by k, then translated (x+1, y−3). Final point: (3, 0). Find k.
After dilation (before translate)Final x − 1 = 2   Final y + 3 = 3
Find kk = 2/4 = 1/2   Verify y: k × 6 = 3 ✓
k = 1/2
