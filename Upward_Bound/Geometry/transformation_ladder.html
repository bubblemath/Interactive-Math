Every transformation is one rule: (x, y) → something.

Translations, reflections, rotations, dilations — each is a single mapping you apply to a point. This ladder drills those rules from a simple slide up to composing two moves and reading a transformation backward. No calculator, no graph paper — just the rule, applied clean.

Why working by hand wins

The rule is faster than the picture

Rules, not pictures

You can graph every transformation, but the mapping (x, y) → (?, ?) is faster and never slips. Knowing each rule cold is the skill the graph paper hides.

Sign slips hide here

Reflections and rotations flip signs; one wrong sign sends the point to the wrong quadrant. By hand you catch it — a quick sketch can fool you.

Order matters in composition

Reflect-then-rotate is not the same as rotate-then-reflect. Doing the first move first, by hand, is the entire game.

There's a sanity check riding along: translations, reflections, and rotations are rigid — they slide, flip, and turn without changing size — while a dilation makes the figure bigger or smaller. Feeling whether a move should preserve the shape or resize it catches an error before it spreads, and that instinct is yours only if you work the rules by hand.
