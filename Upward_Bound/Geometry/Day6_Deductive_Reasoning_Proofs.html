⚖️ Day 6: Deductive Reasoning & Two-Column Proofs

The Detective's Closing Argument

Proofs are how detectives present their case in the court of Geometry.

Today we transition from "solving for x" to "justifying WHY a statement is true." This is the most intimidating content of 9th grade geometry — but we're going to de-mystify it!

  • Algebraic proofs first: You already know how to solve equations — now you'll learn to justify each step
  • Geometric proofs next: The vocabulary from Days 2-5 becomes your "evidence"
  • The "Because" test: Every statement needs a reason — "it looks equal" is NOT a valid reason!
  • Structure matters: Two-column format keeps your argument organized

Master the structure today, and proofs become conversations you can follow!

🌉 The Detective vs. The Witness

In a court of law, a detective can't just say "I think he did it." They need evidence. In Geometry, our "Reasons" are our evidence!

🚫 The Witness Says...

"They look like the same size."
"It seems like a right angle."
"The sides appear equal."
"I can tell they're congruent."

✅ The Detective Says...

"They are congruent because they are Vertical Angles."
"It measures 90° because it's marked with a square."
"The sides are equal because M is the midpoint."
"They are congruent because of the Isosceles Triangle Theorem."

💡 The "Because" Test

Can you complete this sentence? "This statement is true BECAUSE _______________."

If your answer is a theorem, definition, or postulate name — you're good!

If your answer is "it looks like it" — try again!

🔢 Algebraic Proofs: The Paper Trail

You already know how to solve equations — now you'll learn to justify each step with a property name.

🔑 Algebraic Properties (Your New Toolkit)

Addition Property: Add same to both sides
Subtraction Property: Subtract same from both sides
Multiplication Property: Multiply both sides by same
Division Property: Divide both sides by same
Distributive Property: a(b + c) = ab + ac
Substitution Property: Replace equal values
Combine Like Terms: Simplify expressions

📝 Practice: Select the Correct Reason

For each step, choose the property that justifies the transformation:

Prove: If 3x + 7 = 22, then x = 5

# Statements Reasons
1 3x + 7 = 22
2 3x + 7 - 7 = 22 - 7
3 3x = 15
4 3x / 3 = 15 / 3
5 x = 5

# Statements Reasons
1 2(x - 3) = 10
2 2(x - 3) / 2 = 10 / 2
3 x - 3 = 5
4 x - 3 + 3 = 5 + 3
5 x = 8

# Statements Reasons
1 4(x + 2) - 3 = 17
2 4x + 8 - 3 = 17
3 4x + 5 = 17
4 4x = 12
5 x = 3

📐 Geometric Proofs: The Closing Argument

Now we apply the same structure to geometric statements. The vocabulary from Days 2-5 becomes your "evidence."

Given: ∠1 and ∠2 are vertical angles, m∠1 = 65°

Prove: m∠2 = 65°

# Statements Reasons
1 ∠1 and ∠2 are vertical angles
2 m∠1 = 65°
3 ∠1 ≅ ∠2
4 m∠1 = m∠2
5 m∠2 = 65°

Given: M is the midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\)

Prove: 2(AM) = AB

# Statements Reasons
1 M is the midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\)
2 AM = MB
3 AM + MB = AB
4 AM + AM = AB
5 2(AM) = AB

Given: \(\overrightarrow{BD}\) bisects ∠ABC

Prove: 2(m∠ABD) = m∠ABC

# Statements Reasons
1 \(\overrightarrow{BD}\) bisects ∠ABC
2 m∠ABD = m∠DBC
3 m∠ABD + m∠DBC = m∠ABC
4 m∠ABD + m∠ABD = m∠ABC
5 2(m∠ABD) = m∠ABC

Given: ∠1 and ∠2 are supplementary, m∠1 = 65°

Prove: m∠2 = 115°

# Statements Reasons
1 ∠1 and ∠2 are supplementary
2 m∠1 + m∠2 = 180°
3 m∠1 = 65°
4 65° + m∠2 = 180°
5 m∠2 = 115°

💡 The Logic Lab: IF-THEN Reasoning

Deductive reasoning is like a machine: you put in a condition (IF), apply a rule, and get a conclusion (THEN).

🔧 THE LOGIC MACHINE

INPUT (Given):
"M is the midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\)"
⬇️
RULE (Definition):
Definition of Midpoint
⬇️
OUTPUT (Conclude):
"AM = MB"

💡 Key Insight

Every theorem, definition, and postulate is an IF-THEN statement in disguise:

  • IF M is the midpoint of AB, THEN AM = MB
  • IF angles are vertical, THEN they are congruent
  • IF angles are supplementary, THEN they add to 180°

🔄 The Converse: Flipping the IF and THEN

The converse of a statement swaps the IF and THEN parts. But be careful — the converse isn't always true!

⚠️ Converse Preview

📘 Original Statement
IF it is a square, THEN it is a rectangle.
✓ TRUE
📙 Converse
IF it is a rectangle, THEN it is a square.
✗ FALSE
📘 Original Statement
IF two angles are vertical, THEN they are congruent.
✓ TRUE
📙 Converse
IF two angles are congruent, THEN they are vertical.
✗ FALSE

(They could be corresponding angles, or just happen to be equal!)

📘 Original Statement
IF two sides of a triangle are congruent, THEN the base angles are congruent.
✓ TRUE (Isosceles Triangle Theorem)
📙 Converse
IF the base angles are congruent, THEN two sides are congruent.
✓ TRUE (Converse of Isosceles Triangle Theorem)

⚠️ Important

Just because a statement is true does NOT mean its converse is true. You must prove each separately!

This becomes crucial when proving lines are parallel — you'll use the converses of the angle theorems from Day 3.

🔍 Evidence Tampering: Spot the Invalid Reason

A detective must identify when evidence is invalid. Can you spot the flawed reasoning in these proofs?

🚨 Error Analysis #1: The "Looks Equal" Trap

Click on the line with the invalid reason:

Given: Lines m and n are parallel, cut by transversal t

Prove: ∠1 ≅ ∠5

1. m ∥ n Given
2. ∠1 ≅ ∠5 They look the same size

🚨 Error Analysis #2: Wrong Theorem

Click on the line with the invalid reason:

Given: M is the midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\)

Prove: AM = MB

1. M is the midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\) Given
2. AM = MB Segment Addition Postulate

🚨 Error Analysis #3: Missing Step

Click on the line with the invalid reason:

Given: ∠1 and ∠2 are supplementary, m∠1 = 70°

Prove: m∠2 = 110°

1. ∠1 and ∠2 are supplementary Given
2. m∠1 + m∠2 = 180° Definition of Supplementary Angles
3. m∠2 = 110° Subtraction Property

💡 Common Invalid Reasons to Watch For

  • "They look equal" — Appearance is not evidence!
  • Wrong theorem — Make sure the theorem matches the situation
  • Skipping steps — You can't jump to conclusions without showing the work
  • Using the converse incorrectly — Just because A→B doesn't mean B→A
