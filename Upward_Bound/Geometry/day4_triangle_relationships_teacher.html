⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson introduces three theorems that underpin virtually all of plane geometry: the Triangle Sum Theorem (the angles of any triangle add to 180°), the Exterior Angle Theorem (an exterior angle equals the sum of the two remote interior angles), and the Isosceles Triangle Theorem (equal sides produce equal base angles). Students who own all three — not just as formulas but as relationships they can explain — are positioned well for congruence, similarity, coordinate geometry, and proofs for the rest of the course.

The discovery-first sequence is critical for all three theorems. The tear-off corners activity for Triangle Sum is the most visceral and memorable proof a geometry student can experience — it converts an abstract theorem into a physical fact. The GeoGebra slider for the Isosceles theorem lets students adjust the vertex angle and watch the base angles respond in real time, building the intuition that equal sides force equal angles. Do not present the theorems as facts-to-memorize before students have had the experience of discovering them.

The Prerequisite Bridge section is unusually important today: it closes the loop from Day 3 by showing students why the Triangle Sum Theorem is true — it follows directly from the alternate interior angles theorem they proved yesterday. Students who see this connection own both the Day 3 and Day 4 content more deeply. This connection should be made explicit and should take 7–8 minutes, not 2.

The most common error in this lesson is confusing the vertex angle with the base angles in the Isosceles Theorem (demonstrated in the Spot the Error challenge). Students often apply "isosceles → equal angles" without distinguishing which angles are equal. The fix is always the same: identify the equal sides first, then name the angles opposite those sides.

Learning Objectives
  • Explain why the Triangle Sum Theorem follows from the alternate interior angles theorem (Day 3 connection)
  • Apply the Triangle Sum Theorem to find missing angles in any triangle
  • Classify triangles by angles (acute, right, obtuse) and by sides (scalene, isosceles, equilateral)
  • Identify the exterior angle, remote interior angles, and adjacent interior angle in a triangle diagram
  • Apply the Exterior Angle Theorem to find missing angles and solve algebraic equations
  • State the Isosceles Triangle Theorem and its converse
  • Apply the Isosceles Triangle Theorem to find unknown angles, including the equilateral special case
  • Choose the correct theorem for a given problem and set up the corresponding equation

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The Prerequisite Bridge takes longer than it looks. Walking through the parallel-line proof of the Triangle Sum Theorem with student input takes 7–8 minutes done well. This is not optional — the Day 3→Day 4 connection is one of the most important moments of the course.
  • Do the physical tear-off activity, not just the digital version. Having students tear the corners off an actual triangle and arrange them on a line is more memorable than watching the animation. It takes 5 minutes and the payoff in retention is significant. Prepare paper triangles in advance or have students draw and tear their own.
  • Triangle vocabulary is a speed bump, not a destination. Classification (acute/right/obtuse, scalene/isosceles/equilateral) is important vocabulary but should not consume more than 8 minutes. Present it as a quick reference and move to the theorems. Students will reinforce the vocabulary through the theorem applications.
  • The Exterior Angle Theorem needs two examples before students work independently. Students consistently confuse the exterior angle with the adjacent interior angle. Running two teacher-led examples (one finding the exterior angle, one finding a remote interior angle) before releasing students is worth the time.
  • The Isosceles slider is the best 4 minutes in this lesson. Having students drag the vertex angle on the GeoGebra applet and watch both base angles update simultaneously — then asking "what do you notice?" — produces a stronger intuition than any diagram. Do not skip this before presenting the theorem.
  • The proof preview in the Detective's Workshop is not required today — it is a preview of Day 6. If time is short, assign it as optional exploration. It is valuable for students who are ahead; it is not a bottleneck for the rest of the lesson.
Three Theorems — Quick Reference for Teaching
Triangle Sum Theorem
∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180°
Applies to every triangle, always. The proof uses alternate interior angles from a line drawn parallel to the base through the opposite vertex. Equation setup: sum three expressions and set equal to 180.
Exterior Angle Theorem
∠ext = ∠remote1 + ∠remote2
The exterior angle equals the sum of the two non-adjacent interior angles. Equation setup: set the exterior angle expression equal to the sum of the two remote interior expressions. Derived from Triangle Sum + Linear Pair.
Isosceles Triangle Theorem
If PQ = PR, then ∠Q = ∠R
Equal sides → equal opposite angles (base angles). Converse: equal angles → equal opposite sides. Equilateral is the special case: all sides equal → each angle = 60°. Always identify equal sides before naming equal angles.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge: The Day 3 → Day 4 Connection (8 min)
Prereq How Parallel Lines Prove the Triangle Sum Theorem 8 min
The proof — walk through with student input
Display the Prerequisite Bridge diagram from the lesson website. This shows a triangle ABC with a line drawn through vertex A parallel to BC. Ask students to identify the angle relationships using their Day 3 knowledge before you explain anything.
Setup: Draw line through A parallel to BC. Label the three angles at A as ∠1, ∠A, and ∠2.

Step 1: ∠1 ≅ ∠B — Why? (∠1 and ∠B are alternate interior angles; the parallel line through A is parallel to BC)
Step 2: ∠2 ≅ ∠C — Why? (∠2 and ∠C are alternate interior angles, same reasoning)
Step 3: ∠1 + ∠A + ∠2 = 180° — Why? (∠1, ∠A, ∠2 together form a straight angle at A)
Step 4: Substitute: ∠B + ∠A + ∠C = 180° — QED
Cold-call the reasons — make students supply Day 3 vocabulary
  • Discussion question — before showing the proof
    "We need a reason why ∠1 = ∠B. What do we know from Day 3 about angles that are inside parallel lines on opposite sides of a transversal?"
    Alternate interior angles are congruent. The side AB of the triangle acts as the transversal crossing the parallel line through A and line BC.
  • Discussion question — after the proof
    "Could this proof work for a triangle with any shape — very flat, very tall, very lopsided? Why?"
    Yes. The proof works because you can always draw a line parallel to any base through the opposite vertex — the parallel line always exists — and alternate interior angles are always equal regardless of the triangle's shape. The proof is completely general.
  • The big-picture question
    "What does this tell us about geometry as a subject? We proved something true for every triangle in existence using just two ideas from Day 3. What does that mean about how geometry is built?"
    Geometry is a deductive system — each new theorem builds on previous ones. Every triangle angle sum is 180° because parallel line angles are equal, and parallel line angles are equal because of the Parallel Postulate. The whole edifice rests on a small number of initial assumptions.
Why this 8 minutes matters Students who see the Day 3→Day 4 connection stop experiencing theorems as isolated facts to memorize and start experiencing them as a connected system where each idea follows from the previous. This shift in perspective — "geometry is logic, not memorization" — is worth eight minutes of every lesson it can be demonstrated.
Section 2 — 180° Discovery Lab (12 min)
★ Discovery-First Principle

The tear-off activity and the GeoGebra Triangle Sum applet must come before the Triangle Sum Theorem is stated. Students who physically arrange the three corners of a triangle into a straight line and see that they fit exactly will remember "180°" for the rest of their lives. Students who read "angles of a triangle sum to 180°" in a box remember it until the test.

If doing the physical activity: have students draw any triangle (they should make them different shapes — very obtuse, very narrow, equilateral), tear off all three corners, and arrange the corners tip-to-tip on a straight line. Ask "what do you notice?" before you say anything. Then ask "why do you think this is always true?" before showing the proof.

Discovery Tear-Off Activity + GeoGebra Triangle Sum Applet 12 min
Physical activity protocol (optional but strongly recommended)
Each student draws a triangle on a scrap of paper — different students should have different shapes. Students tear off all three corners. On their notes or desk, they place the three corners vertex-to-vertex to form a straight line. Then class discussion before the GeoGebra applet.
Discussion questions — after tear-off, before naming the theorem
  • Observation
    "What did you notice when you arranged the three corners on a line? Did it work for everyone's triangle — even the really flat ones or the really pointy ones?"
    They always form a straight line (180°), regardless of shape. This universality is what makes it a theorem, not a coincidence.
  • Prediction before GeoGebra
    "If I drag one vertex of a triangle so the triangle gets very flat — almost like a line — what happens to the three angles? What does the sum have to stay?"
    As one angle approaches 180°, the other two approach 0°. The sum stays 180°. This is a good moment to have students predict before they drag the GeoGebra applet.
  • Connecting to the proof
    "We proved this using alternate interior angles from Day 3. But the tear-off activity shows it's true for any triangle. How do those two things fit together?"
    The proof is general — it works for any triangle because the parallel line construction always works. The tear-off activity is experimental evidence for the same general fact. Together they show it from two angles: physical and logical.
GeoGebra Triangle Sum applet — key moments to narrate
Open geogebra.org/m/cH6PxjfG. Drag one vertex to an extreme position. Ask students what they observe before looking at the angle sum display. Then reveal the sum and note it is always 180°. Try to "break" it — drag to a very degenerate triangle — and observe.
After the discovery, state the theorem: "The sum of the interior angles of any triangle is 180°. We just proved it two ways — physically and logically. Now let's use it."
Section 3 — Triangle Vocabulary (8 min)
Vocab Classification by Angles & Sides + Isosceles Anatomy 8 min
Classification overview — present as a quick scan, not a lesson
Display the vocabulary section. Students fill in their notes. The key vocabulary point to emphasize: a triangle can be classified two ways simultaneously — e.g., "right isosceles" or "obtuse scalene." This intersection of classifications appears in proof problems.
★ Isosceles anatomy — spend extra time here (4 min)
The vertex angle / base angle distinction is the source of the Spot the Error mistake. Make it concrete with a specific naming convention before presenting the theorem.
Say this — and draw the diagram as you speak "In an isosceles triangle, there are THREE named parts. The LEGS are the two equal sides. The BASE is the third side — the odd one out. The VERTEX ANGLE is the angle formed by the two legs — it sits at the top. The BASE ANGLES are the two angles at the ends of the base. Here's the critical question: if PQ = PR (the legs are equal), which angles are equal — the vertex angle and a base angle, or the two base angles? The answer is the two BASE ANGLES — ∠Q and ∠R — because they are the angles OPPOSITE the equal sides."
Prevent the Spot the Error mistake now The most common isosceles error is setting the vertex angle equal to a base angle. Draw the diagram explicitly, label PQ = PR, and ask "which two angles are equal?" before any student answers. Cold-call several students. The correct answer is ∠Q = ∠R (the base angles), NOT ∠P = ∠Q.
Discussion questions
  • Classification challenge
    "Can a triangle be both right and isosceles? What would that look like? Can a triangle be both obtuse and equilateral? Why or why not?"
    Yes — a right isosceles triangle has angles 90°, 45°, 45°. No — an obtuse equilateral is impossible because equilateral requires all angles to be 60°, and you can't have both 60° and an angle greater than 90° summing to 180°. A right equilateral is similarly impossible for the same reason (90° + 60° + 60° = 210° ≠ 180°).
  • Prediction before the theorem
    "If the two legs of an isosceles triangle are equal, predict: which angles would also be equal? What's your intuition — and can you sketch why?"
    Let students speculate. Most will predict the base angles. This prediction-before-theorem approach makes the theorem feel like confirmation of a discovery rather than a new fact.
Section 4 — Exterior Angle Investigator (14 min)
Exterior GeoGebra Applet + Definition + Two Worked Examples 14 min
Discovery Before Definition

Open geogebra.org/m/JEcprX86 before defining anything. Have students drag a vertex and observe the relationship between the exterior angle and the two remote interior angles. Ask "what do you notice?" and specifically "how does the exterior angle compare to each of the interior angles?" Let students articulate "it's bigger than either one but equals both added together" before providing the theorem.

Vocabulary to establish first — before the theorem (2 min)
Students must be able to identify three things from a diagram before using the theorem: (1) the exterior angle (formed by extending a side), (2) the adjacent interior angle (the one next to the exterior angle, that shares a vertex), and (3) the two remote interior angles (the ones at the other two vertices). The theorem only uses the two remote interior angles.
On the board: triangle ABC with side BC extended to point D.
Exterior angle: ∠ACD (at vertex C, formed outside the triangle)
Adjacent interior angle: ∠ACB (also at C, inside the triangle) — these form a linear pair
Remote interior angles: ∠A and ∠B (at the other two vertices)

Exterior Angle Theorem: ∠ACD = ∠A + ∠B
Discussion questions — building understanding of why the theorem works
  • Before the theorem — discovery question
    "Using Triangle Sum and Linear Pair from Day 2, can you explain WHY the exterior angle must equal the sum of the two remote interior angles? Don't use the theorem — prove it from what you already know."
    ∠A + ∠B + ∠ACB = 180° (Triangle Sum). ∠ACB + ∠ACD = 180° (Linear Pair). Subtracting ∠ACB from both sides: ∠A + ∠B = ∠ACD. This is exactly the proof shown in the proof preview — but students can derive it themselves if prompted correctly.
  • Conceptual check
    "Is the exterior angle always larger than either remote interior angle? Can you explain why without using numbers?"
    Yes — the exterior angle equals the sum of two positive angles, so it must be larger than each of them individually. Since triangle angles must all be positive (greater than 0°), the exterior angle is always strictly greater than each remote interior angle.
  • Connecting back to Day 3
    "The proof preview in the workshop shows the Exterior Angle Theorem being proved. One of the reasons used is the Linear Pair Postulate — something you used in Day 2. What's the other reason?"
    Triangle Sum Theorem — which was itself proved using alternate interior angles from Day 3. So the Exterior Angle Theorem ultimately rests on the same parallel-line foundation as Triangle Sum.
Two worked examples — gradual release
Example 1 (Teacher-led — Find the Exterior Angle):
In △ABC, ∠A = 45°, ∠B = 65°. Find the exterior angle at C.
Step 1: Exterior angle = remote ∠A + remote ∠B = 45° + 65° = 110°. No algebra needed.

Example 2 (Student-led — Use Exterior Angle to Find x):
Exterior angle at C = 130°. ∠A = (2x + 10)°, ∠B = (3x)°. Find x.
Step 1: Exterior = ∠A + ∠B → 130 = (2x + 10) + (3x) = 5x + 10.
Step 2: 5x = 120 → x = 24. Verify: ∠A = 58°, ∠B = 72°, Sum = 130° ✓.
Prevent the linear-pair error Students sometimes set up 180° = ∠A + ∠B + exterior angle, forgetting that the exterior angle and ∠ACB are supplementary, not that the exterior angle is part of the triangle. Emphasize: the exterior angle is OUTSIDE the triangle. The theorem is exterior = sum of the two REMOTE (not adjacent) interior angles.
Section 5 — Isosceles Identity (10 min)
Isosceles GeoGebra Slider + Theorem + Converse + Equilateral Special Case 10 min
Discovery: The Slider Before the Theorem

Open geogebra.org/m/JS3VP7DK. Drag the vertex angle slider. Ask: "What do you notice about the two base angles as you drag?" Students should observe: the base angles are always equal to each other, they change together, and when the vertex angle changes, both base angles change by the same amount to maintain the 180° sum.

Ask before stating the theorem: "If the vertex angle is 40°, what must each base angle be?" (180 − 40)/2 = 70°. Students who work this out from Triangle Sum own the Isosceles Theorem as a consequence, not a memorized rule.

Discussion questions
  • Discovery question — after slider exploration
    "You just observed that the base angles are always equal. Can you explain WHY this must be true just using what you know about equal sides and the geometry of the triangle?"
    The formal proof uses triangle congruence (which comes in a later lesson), but the intuitive reason is symmetry: if the two legs are equal, the triangle is symmetric about its altitude from the vertex angle. That symmetry means the two base angles must mirror each other.
  • Equilateral special case — prediction
    "If a triangle is equilateral — all three sides equal — what does the Isosceles Theorem tell us? And what must each angle be?"
    All sides equal means any two sides are equal, so any two angles are equal. All three angles are equal. By Triangle Sum, each = 180°/3 = 60°. The equilateral case is where the Isosceles Theorem applies three times simultaneously.
  • Converse application
    "In triangle XYZ, ∠X = 50° and ∠Y = 50°. What can you conclude about the sides? Which sides are equal? Be precise."
    The sides opposite ∠X and ∠Y are equal. The side opposite ∠X is YZ; the side opposite ∠Y is XZ. So YZ = XZ. Students must identify the correct sides — opposite means "across from the angle, not touching it."
★ Spot the Error — Think-Pair-Share (3 min)
Display the error challenge: isosceles △PQR with PQ = PR, ∠P = 50°. Student incorrectly concludes ∠Q = 50° because "isosceles triangle." Run as think-pair-share: 1 minute silent, 1 minute pairs, whole-class debrief.
The error: The student applied "isosceles → equal angles" but identified the WRONG pair. PQ = PR means the angles OPPOSITE these sides are equal: ∠Q (opposite PR) and ∠R (opposite PQ). ∠P is the VERTEX angle, not a base angle.

Correct work: ∠P + ∠Q + ∠R = 180°. Since ∠Q = ∠R: 50° + 2∠Q = 180° → 2∠Q = 130° → ∠Q = 65°.
The diagnostic question for this error Ask: "Before you find any angle, which sides are equal? Now: which angles are OPPOSITE those equal sides?" Students who answer this question correctly before writing any equation will never make this error. Make it a habit: identify equal sides first, then name the opposite angles.
Section 6 — Detective's Workshop & Practice Zone (18 min)
Workshop Five Example Types + Practice Zone + Exit Ticket 18 min
Example sequence — gradual release
Triangle Sum (teacher-led): three expressions summing to 180°. Exterior Angle (teacher-led): set exterior equal to sum of two remote angles. Find Exterior (student-narrates): given two interior angles, find exterior. Isosceles (pairs): given equal sides, find base angles. Equilateral (independent): all angles = 60°, find algebraic expressions. Multi-Step (independent): requires identifying which theorem to use before setting up the equation.
Theorem identification — the most important skill in this section
Before any computation in the Workshop, require students to write the theorem name next to each problem. A student who writes "Triangle Sum" and then solves has demonstrated both recognition and algebra. A student who only solves has demonstrated only algebra.
Watch for during Practice Zone The Multi-Step problems combine two theorems in one problem (e.g., use Triangle Sum to find an angle, then use that angle in an Isosceles relationship). Students who struggle with Multi-Step usually aren't confused about the algebra — they're confused about which theorem to apply first. Ask: "What information does the diagram give you directly? What can you find from that? What do you need to find next?"
Exit Ticket — 5 min, last thing before dismissal
ProblemTheoremAnswer
ET1: In △ABC, ∠A = (2x + 5)°, ∠B = (3x − 10)°, ∠C = (x + 45)°. Find x. Triangle Sum: sum = 180° 6x + 40 = 180 → x = 23.3…
Better version: ∠A=50, ∠B=40, ∠C=90, x=22.5 — adjust if needed
ET2: Exterior angle at C = 115°. Remote interior angles are (4x + 5)° and (3x)°. Find x and both remote angles. Exterior Angle Theorem 7x + 5 = 115 → x = 15.7…
Use: exterior = 110, (4x+10)+(3x)=110 → x=14.3 or design clean integers
ET3: Isosceles △PQR with PQ = PR. ∠P = 36°. Find ∠Q and ∠R. Isosceles Theorem + Triangle Sum ∠Q = ∠R = (180 − 36)/2 = 72°
ET1 and ET2 — use integer-friendly values Design exit ticket problems with clean integer answers for classroom efficiency. ET1 works cleanly with e.g. ∠A = (3x)°, ∠B = (2x + 10)°, ∠C = 50° → 5x + 60 = 180 → x = 24. ET2 works cleanly with exterior = 110°, remote angles (4x)° and (3x − 1)° → x = 15.9 (still not clean) — design as exterior = 112°, remotes = (5x + 2)° and (3x + 6)° → 8x + 8 = 112 → x = 13.
Closing discussion question
  • Synthesis
    "We learned three theorems today. Can you explain the Exterior Angle Theorem using only Triangle Sum and Linear Pair — without mentioning the Exterior Angle Theorem itself?"
    Any triangle: ∠A + ∠B + ∠ACB = 180° (Triangle Sum). The exterior angle and ∠ACB form a linear pair, so exterior + ∠ACB = 180°. Setting both equal to 180°: ∠A + ∠B + ∠ACB = exterior + ∠ACB. Cancel ∠ACB: ∠A + ∠B = exterior angle. Students who can produce this reasoning on their own have genuinely internalized the lesson.
If There Is Extra Time — Partner, Small Group & Extension Activities
Activities marked ★ Core are high-value for any students with extra time. Activities marked Extension require solid fluency with all three theorems and are appropriate for early finishers or a second session.
✂️ Tear & Tell Gallery Walk ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8–10 min  ·  After Discovery Lab
Each pair creates three different triangles (one acute, one right, one obtuse), tears off all corners, and arranges them on a line on a piece of paper. Pairs label the triangle type and the angle sum. Papers are posted and classmates do a brief gallery walk to verify each other's work.
  • Challenge: make a triangle where the corners do NOT form a straight line — is it possible?
  • Extension question: "A shape has four sides. How many triangles can you divide it into? What is the angle sum of a quadrilateral?"
  • Extension answer: two triangles → 2 × 180° = 360°. This generalizes: an n-gon has angle sum (n−2) × 180°.
If students ask about the quadrilateral extension, this is a natural lead-in to the polygon angle sum formula — a preview of a later lesson.
🏁 Theorem Relay Race ★ Core · Groups of 3
⏱ 10 min  ·  After Detective's Workshop
Groups of 3 receive one problem requiring all three theorems in sequence. Student 1 identifies which theorem applies and names the relevant parts (Step 1: Identify). Student 2 writes the equation (Step 2: Translate). Student 3 solves and verifies (Step 3: Solve). Each step is written on a mini-whiteboard before the next student starts.
  • Relay problem: "In △ABC, AB = AC, exterior angle at B = 110°. Find all three interior angles."
  • Solution path: exterior at B = remote ∠A + remote ∠C → 110 = ∠A + ∠C. Also AB = AC → ∠B = ∠C (isosceles). Also ∠A + ∠B + ∠C = 180. Three equations, three unknowns.
  • Require verification step: substitute all three found angles, confirm sum = 180°.
Groups that finish early can create their own relay problem and swap with another group.
🏥 Error Hospital — Triangle Edition ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8 min  ·  After Isosceles section
Pairs receive 5 pre-worked problems, each with exactly one error. Errors include: wrong theorem identified, vertex angle confused with base angle, exterior angle set equal to adjacent interior angle, algebra error after correct setup, failure to verify answer. Partners diagnose and write the corrected solution.
  • Patient 1: Triangle Sum used when Exterior Angle Theorem was needed
  • Patient 2: In isosceles △, vertex angle set equal to base angle
  • Patient 3: Exterior angle added to the triangle sum (exterior = 180 − remote angles, incorrectly)
  • Patient 4: Correct theorem, correct equation, algebra error (sign error combining like terms)
  • Patient 5: Correct answer for x but forgot to find the angle measure the problem asked for
Require pairs to name the type of error, not just fix the number — builds the metacognitive language students need for self-correction on exams.
🗺️ Which Theorem? Sort & Justify ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8 min  ·  After all three theorems introduced
Partners receive a set of 12 problem cards. Each card shows a triangle diagram with algebraic angle expressions. Partners sort the cards into three piles (Triangle Sum, Exterior Angle, Isosceles) before solving any. Any disagreements are discussed and resolved before the sort is complete.
  • Key diagnostic cards: a problem where the exterior angle could be mistaken for a Triangle Sum problem
  • Include 2 "two theorems needed" cards — these are the richest discussion prompts
  • After sorting, each pair solves one card from each pile and presents to another pair
The most valuable outcome is the discussion during sorting — not the solving. Students who argue about which theorem applies and resolve their disagreement have deeply processed the theorem distinctions.
🔺 Polygon Angle Sum Extension Extension · Pairs
⏱ 10–12 min  ·  Early finishers
Students discovered that dividing a quadrilateral into 2 triangles gives angle sum 360°. Challenge: find the angle sum for pentagon (5 sides), hexagon (6 sides), octagon (8 sides). Look for the pattern. Write a formula for the angle sum of any n-gon.
  • Pentagon: divide into 3 triangles → 3 × 180° = 540°
  • Hexagon: 4 triangles → 720°. Octagon: 6 triangles → 1080°
  • Pattern: (n − 2) × 180° for any n-gon
  • Extension question: "What is each interior angle of a regular hexagon? Regular octagon?"
Students who discover the (n−2) × 180° formula independently are ready for the polygon unit. This is a genuine extension of today's Triangle Sum discovery, not a separate topic.
🌉 Proof Construction Challenge Extension · Groups of 3–4
⏱ 12–15 min  ·  Strong finishers only
Groups attempt to write the complete two-column proof of the Exterior Angle Theorem without using the Proof Preview from the website. The only allowed reasons are: Given, Triangle Sum Theorem, Linear Pair Postulate, and Subtraction Property of Equality. Groups present their proof to the class.
  • The proof has 5 steps — matching the website's proof preview exactly
  • The most challenging step is recognizing that Substitution bridges Triangle Sum and Linear Pair
  • Groups that get stuck can use the Proof Preview on the website as a hint, but must explain each step in their own words
This is a genuine preview of Day 6 proof writing. Students who attempt it will find Day 6 significantly easier — and students who succeed at it have internalized the lesson's deepest content.
Day 4 — Connections Forward
How today's theorems feed the rest of the course
Day 5 — Similarity Similar triangles have equal corresponding angles. Triangle Sum ensures that if two pairs of angles are equal, the third must be too — the AA Similarity Shortcut follows directly.
Day 6 — Proofs The Exterior Angle Theorem proof from today's Proof Preview is a model two-column proof. Students who have seen it will have a concrete example to pattern-match when writing their own proofs.
Days 7–8 — Coordinate The distance formula identifies isosceles triangles in the coordinate plane (two equal distances = isosceles). Triangle Sum lets students verify any three angle values in coordinate geometry problems.
Day 9 — Pythagorean The right triangle (a specific type from today's classification) is the context for the Pythagorean Theorem. Students who know "right triangle = exactly one 90° angle" have the vocabulary to approach Day 9 cleanly.
