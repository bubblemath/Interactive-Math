⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

Day 6 is the pivot point of the entire bridge course. Every lesson from Days 1–5 was building vocabulary and skills; today those tools become the evidence in a formal argument. The shift from "find x" to "justify why" is the most significant conceptual transition in the course, and it is genuinely difficult for most students. Plan for confusion — and plan to normalize that confusion.

The most important framing decision you make today is how you introduce what a proof is. The lesson uses a courtroom-and-detective metaphor: a witness says "it looks equal," a detective says "it is equal because of the Vertical Angles Theorem." This distinction — between observation and justified claim — is the entire lesson in one sentence. Return to it every time students struggle.

The algebraic proofs section comes first, and this sequencing is deliberate. Students already know how to solve equations. What they don't know is that each algebraic step they take has a name. Starting with algebra lets students experience the two-column format in familiar territory before the geometry vocabulary adds a second layer of complexity. A student who writes "Given → Subtraction Property → Combine Like Terms → Division Property → Simplify" for a linear equation is already writing proofs.

The error analysis section is one of the most valuable activities in the course. Students who can diagnose a flawed proof — "wrong theorem," "skipped a step," "used appearance as evidence" — have internalized proof structure at a deeper level than students who only write correct proofs. Budget 8–10 minutes for it and run it as discussion, not just individual seatwork.

Discovery note: Unlike Days 3–5, this lesson has less to discover and more to organize. The "discovery" here is the realization that students have already been reasoning deductively — they just haven't been naming the steps. The bridge from Days 2–5 vocabulary to formal proof reasons is the conceptual move that needs to land today. Use the Prerequisite Bridge section to surface this explicitly.

Learning Objectives
  • Explain the difference between an observation ("it looks equal") and a justified claim ("equal because of the Vertical Angles Theorem")
  • Apply the "Because Test" — complete "This is true BECAUSE ___" with a named theorem, definition, or postulate
  • Write a two-column algebraic proof, naming each equation-solving step with an algebraic property
  • Write a two-column geometric proof using reasons from Days 2–5 vocabulary
  • Translate a definition or theorem into an IF-THEN statement
  • Determine whether the converse of a given IF-THEN statement is true or false
  • Identify invalid reasons in a flawed proof (appearance, wrong theorem, missing step)

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The Prerequisite Bridge is the emotional setup for the whole lesson. Do not rush "Detective vs. Witness." Students who feel that proofs are just a naming game for things they already know will engage. Students who feel proofs are a new and foreign thing will resist. The first 8 minutes determines which group you have for the rest of the lesson.
  • Algebraic proofs take longer than expected. Students who can solve equations confidently are often surprised that they cannot immediately name each step. Give them time with the three interactive proofs on the website before moving to geometric proofs. The struggle to name steps is productive — do not narrate the answers for them.
  • The four geometric proofs are not equal in difficulty. The vertical angles proof is easiest (just cite the theorem). The midpoint proof and angle bisector proof require substitution, which students frequently don't recognize as a step. The supplementary proof requires the most steps. Sequence them in this order: vertical → supplementary → midpoint → bisector.
  • Logic Lab (IF-THEN and converses) takes less time than it looks. The concept transfers from everyday language quickly. Spend 5 minutes on it, not 12. If students get it fast, move to the error analysis. If they're confused about converse, use the rectangle-square example — it's universally understood.
  • The Error Analysis section must be run as discussion, not seatwork. The three errors (appearance, wrong theorem, missing step) are the three most common errors in student proofs for the next four years. Every minute spent on error analysis in discussion format pays dividends on every future proof students write.
  • The proof for "2(AM) = AB given M is the midpoint" requires substitution. Many students write AM + MB = AB (Segment Addition) and then stop — they don't realize they need to substitute AM for MB. Anticipate this and have a script ready for it.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge: The Detective vs. The Witness (8 min)
Bridge The "Because Test" + Reason Toolkit 8 min
★ Discovery Move: Let Students Feel the Problem First

Before displaying the lesson, ask this question cold: "In a geometry proof, can I write 'these two angles are equal because they look the same'?" Let students respond. Most will correctly say no. Then ask: "Why not? What's wrong with that reason?" This surfaces the intuition that appearance is insufficient before you've named the principle.

Then display the four "Witness Says" statements and ask: "What's unsatisfying about each of these? What information is missing?" Students will identify that the reason is missing from every witness statement — even though the conclusion might be correct. This is the discovery: the conclusion can be right, but without a named justification, it is not a proof.

Introduce the "Because Test" before the Reason Toolkit
The "Because Test" is a self-checking tool: can you complete "This statement is true BECAUSE ___" with the name of a theorem, definition, or postulate? If yes — valid reason. If not — invalid. Write this on the board and leave it up for the entire lesson.
Say this "Every step of a proof needs to pass the 'Because Test.' When I write that two segments are equal, I need to be able to say because — and fill in a specific named rule. Not 'because I can see it,' not 'because it seems right,' but because of the Vertical Angles Theorem, or the Definition of Midpoint, or the Segment Addition Postulate. The name is the evidence. If you have the name, you have the proof."
Reason Toolkit — connect to prior days
Display the Reason Toolkit from the lesson. Before students read it, ask: "Which of these reasons did we first use on Day 2? Day 3? Day 4? Day 5?" Let students point them out. This recasts the last four days as preparation for today — which is exactly what it was.
The emotional framing of this moment Many students feel that proofs are "new and hard" even before they start. If you can say — and have students agree — that every reason in the toolkit is something they already know from Days 2–5, the emotional landscape changes. Proofs aren't new content; they're organizing existing content into a new format. This reframe is worth 2 minutes of the opening.
Discussion questions
  • Opening question
    "The Witness says 'the sides appear equal.' The Detective says 'the sides are equal because M is the midpoint.' What is the Detective saying that the Witness is not?"
    The Detective is citing a named rule (Definition of Midpoint) that guarantees the conclusion. The Witness is citing an observation that could be wrong or could apply to a specific case only. The named rule is general — it applies to any midpoint, not just this one.
  • Why proofs matter beyond geometry
    "Can you think of a situation outside of math where someone might confuse 'it looks like' with 'I can prove it'? Why does the difference matter?"
    Law (eyewitness testimony vs. physical evidence), science (correlation vs. causation), engineering (looks sturdy vs. tested to specification). The difference matters because "looks like" depends on the observer and can be wrong; a proof works regardless of who is looking and cannot be wrong if the steps are valid.
Section 2 — Algebraic Proofs: The Paper Trail (20 min)
Algebraic 7 Properties + Three Interactive Proofs 20 min
★ Discovery Move: Name the Step Before Seeing the Name

Before presenting the 7 algebraic properties, write this equation solving sequence on the board without labels:

3x + 7 = 22  →  3x = 15  →  x = 5

Ask: "Between the first line and the second, what operation did you perform to both sides of the equation?" (Subtracted 7.) "What do you call that property?" Most students will say "subtraction property of equality" even if they've never formally written a proof. This confirms they already know the names — they just haven't been using them in written form.

Then present the 7 properties as vocabulary for what students already do, not as new operations they need to learn.

The 7 algebraic properties — present briefly, practice immediately
Addition Property of Equality: Add the same value to both sides → a = b means a + c = b + c
Subtraction Property of Equality: Subtract the same value from both sides
Multiplication Property of Equality: Multiply both sides by the same value
Division Property of Equality: Divide both sides by the same nonzero value
Distributive Property: a(b + c) = ab + ac
Substitution Property: Replace a quantity with an equal quantity
Simplify / Combine Like Terms: Arithmetic simplification within an expression
The hardest property for students: Substitution The Substitution Property is the most misunderstood. It doesn't mean "plug in x." It means: if AM = MB (two things are equal), you can replace MB with AM anywhere in the proof. Students who don't understand substitution will stall on Steps 3–4 of both the midpoint and bisector geometric proofs. Spend a 60-second extra explanation: "Substitution means: I know AM = MB, so wherever I see MB, I can write AM instead. Like swapping identical twins."
Three interactive proofs — think-pair-share protocol
Run each proof on the website as a class. Display the statement column only. Give pairs 60 seconds to discuss what reason belongs in each row before revealing the dropdown. Cold-call on any row where pairs disagree.
Proof 1 (Linear): Prove 3x + 7 = 22 → x = 5
Row 1: 3x + 7 = 22 → Given
Row 2: 3x + 7 − 7 = 22 − 7 → Subtraction Property of Equality
Row 3: 3x = 15 → Simplify / Combine Like Terms
Row 4: 3x ÷ 3 = 15 ÷ 3 → Division Property of Equality
Row 5: x = 5 → Simplify

Proof 2 (Two-Step): Prove 2(x − 3) = 10 → x = 8 — Note: students often write Division before Distributive. Ask "which step do we do first in this order? Why?"

Proof 3 (Distributive): Prove 4(x + 2) − 3 = 17 → x = 3 — Hardest algebraic proof. Anticipate: students want to skip the Distributive step and go straight to combining. Hold the line.
Discussion questions
  • The "why bother naming" question
    "I already know how to solve this equation. Why do I need to name each step? Isn't that just more work?"
    In algebra class, naming is optional because the goal is the answer. In geometry proofs, the goal IS the justification — the answer (the conclusion) is already given. You're proving that the conclusion follows from the given information, step by step. Naming the steps is not extra work; it is the work. Also: in more complex proofs, naming the steps is the only way to check that you haven't made a logical error.
  • The "skipping steps" question
    "In Row 2 of the linear proof, do I really need to show '3x + 7 − 7 = 22 − 7' as a separate line? Can I just jump to '3x = 15'?"
    In an informal proof or algebraic work, skipping one step is acceptable. In a formal two-column proof, each row corresponds to one application of one property. If you skip '3x + 7 − 7 = 22 − 7' and jump to '3x = 15', you're combining two properties (Subtraction + Simplify) into one row — which is technically not a valid single step. The level of formality expected depends on the context; for this course, one property per row is the standard.
Section 3 — Geometric Proofs: The Closing Argument (20 min)
Geometric Four Proofs — Vertical Angles · Supplementary · Midpoint · Bisector 20 min
Proof 1: Vertical Angles — Teacher-Led (5 min)
This is the simplest geometric proof. It has 5 steps and uses only: Given, Vertical Angles Theorem, Definition of Congruent Angles, and Substitution. It is the bridge from algebraic proofs to geometric proofs — the structure is the same, only the reasons change.
#StatementReason
1∠1 and ∠2 are vertical anglesGiven
2m∠1 = 65°Given
3∠1 ≅ ∠2Vertical Angles Theorem
4m∠1 = m∠2Definition of Congruent Angles
5m∠2 = 65°Substitution Property
Narrate row 3 carefully — this is where students first see the Day 5 congruence distinction pay off "Notice we wrote ∠1 ≅ ∠2 — using the congruence symbol — not m∠1 = m∠2. That's because the Vertical Angles Theorem says the angles themselves are congruent objects. Then in Row 4, we use the Definition of Congruent Angles to convert from the object statement (∠1 ≅ ∠2) to the number statement (m∠1 = m∠2). This is exactly the ≅ vs = distinction from Day 5 showing up in a proof."
Proof 2: Supplementary Angles — Student-Narrated (5 min)
Display the proof with the statement column visible. Ask students to supply each reason as you write it. The proof requires: Given (twice), Definition of Supplementary Angles, Substitution, Subtraction Property.
Common error in Proof 2 Row 4 is "65° + m∠2 = 180°" — the reason is Substitution Property (replacing m∠1 with 65°). Students often write "Definition of Supplementary Angles" again for Row 4 because they associate that row with supplementary angles. Ask: "We already said they're supplementary in Row 2. In Row 4, what's the new information we're using? We're replacing m∠1 with its value — that's substitution, not a new definition."
Proofs 3 & 4: Midpoint and Bisector — Pairs (10 min)
Both proofs have the same underlying structure: Given → definition → addition postulate → substitution → simplify. The substitution step in both is the critical one. Give pairs 4 minutes each, then debrief whole-class with attention to the substitution row.
#Midpoint Proof: Given M is midpoint of AB, Prove 2(AM) = ABReason
1M is the midpoint of AB̄Given
2AM = MBDefinition of Midpoint ← Students often miss this is the key step
3AM + MB = ABSegment Addition Postulate
4AM + AM = ABSubstitution Property ← Replace MB with AM (they're equal)
52(AM) = ABSimplify / Combine Like Terms
The substitution step — explicit narration required Row 4 is where most students stall. They have AM + MB = AB and they need to get to 2(AM) = AB, but they don't see how. Ask: "From Row 2, what do we know AM and MB have in common?" (They're equal.) "So in Row 3, wherever we see MB, can we replace it with something equal to MB?" (Replace MB with AM.) "That's Substitution Property. We're not doing arithmetic; we're swapping equal quantities." Write this narration on the board and leave it for the bisector proof.
Discussion questions
  • Connecting algebraic and geometric proofs
    "In the algebraic proof, you used Substitution Property to replace a number. In the midpoint proof, you used Substitution Property to replace a segment length. Is it the same property? How can you tell?"
    Yes — same property, different context. The Substitution Property says: if two quantities are equal, you can replace one with the other anywhere in the proof. In algebra it's "x = 5, so replace x with 5." In geometry it's "AM = MB, so replace MB with AM." The principle is identical.
  • The proof structure question
    "Every geometric proof we wrote today had the same pattern: Given → definition → postulate → substitution → simplify. Is this always the structure? Why might a proof be longer or shorter?"
    Not always — but for proofs involving a definition that gives us equal quantities, this structure recurs. A proof is longer when more steps are needed to connect the given information to the conclusion; shorter when a single theorem connects them directly. Proofs in later units (congruence, similarity) will involve more intermediate conclusions, each requiring its own justification chain.
Section 4 — Logic Lab: IF-THEN Reasoning (7 min)
Logic The Logic Machine + Converse — True or False? 7 min
The Logic Machine — 3 minutes
The "Logic Machine" (Input → Rule → Output) is the clearest way to describe how every theorem works: you put in the Given (IF), apply the rule, and get the conclusion (THEN). Use three examples from the lesson to demonstrate — midpoint, vertical angles, supplementary angles — before introducing the converse.
IF M is the midpoint of AB → THEN AM = MB (Definition of Midpoint)
IF angles are vertical → THEN they are congruent (Vertical Angles Theorem)
IF angles are supplementary → THEN they add to 180° (Definition of Supplementary Angles)
Converse — class vote (true or false?)
For each example, flip the IF and THEN and ask: "Is this still true?" Run as a class vote before revealing. The rectangle-square example is the most intuitive. Use it first.
This is the most important content in today's Logic Lab The converse distinction — "just because A implies B doesn't mean B implies A" — is essential for the parallel lines unit. The converse of the corresponding angles theorem (if corresponding angles are equal, then lines are parallel) IS true and is used to prove lines are parallel in proofs. Students who understand "converses must be proved separately" will be equipped for that. Students who don't understand this will write backwards implications in proofs.
Discussion questions
  • The rectangle-square example
    "IF it is a square, THEN it is a rectangle — TRUE. IF it is a rectangle, THEN it is a square — FALSE. Can you explain why the converse fails here?"
    A rectangle just needs four right angles and opposite sides equal — it can be any dimensions. A square requires ALL sides equal. Being a rectangle is a necessary but not sufficient condition for being a square. The converse takes a sufficient condition (being a square) and treats it as necessary (being a rectangle) — which overclaims.
  • The congruent-implies-vertical question
    "IF two angles are vertical, THEN they are congruent — TRUE. Is the converse true? If two angles are congruent, are they necessarily vertical angles?"
    No. Two angles can be congruent for many reasons: corresponding angles in parallel lines, base angles of an isosceles triangle, two separate right angles, two angles both measuring 45°. Congruent is a much weaker condition than vertical. Vertical angles are always congruent, but congruent angles are not always vertical.
Section 5 — Error Analysis: Spot the Invalid Reason (10 min)
Errors Three Flawed Proofs — Discussion Format 10 min
Run as Discussion, Not Seatwork

Display each flawed proof on the projector. Give students 30 seconds to identify the invalid line independently, then discuss as a class before revealing. The goal is not just to find the error — it is to articulate why it is wrong and what the correct reason should be. Students who can explain the error in words have a deeper understanding than those who can just click the wrong line on the website.

Error 1: "The Looks Equal Trap" — appearance used as evidence
Given: Lines m ∥ n, cut by transversal t. Prove: ∠1 ≅ ∠5
Row 1: m ∥ n — Given ✓
Row 2: ∠1 ≅ ∠5 — They look the same size ✗

Error: "They look the same size" is an observation, not a proof reason. The correct reason is the Corresponding Angles Postulate (∠1 and ∠5 are corresponding angles, and corresponding angles are congruent when lines are parallel).
  • Discussion question
    "What would have to be true for 'they look the same size' to be a valid reason in a proof? Is there any context in which appearances count as evidence?"
    If the diagram were drawn to precise scale and you could measure it with a protractor, appearance could be evidence in a practical context. But in a formal proof, diagrams are not drawn to scale — they are schematic representations of a general situation. A proof must work for every case, not just the specific diagram shown. That's why observation fails: the diagram might be slightly off, and the proof must be logically watertight regardless of how it's drawn.
Error 2: "Wrong Theorem" — correct conclusion, wrong justification
Given: M is the midpoint of AB̄. Prove: AM = MB
Row 1: M is the midpoint of AB̄ — Given ✓
Row 2: AM = MB — Segment Addition Postulate ✗

Error: The Segment Addition Postulate says AB + BC = AC when B is between A and C. It says nothing about midpoints. The correct reason is the Definition of Midpoint (if M is the midpoint, then AM = MB — that's literally what "midpoint" means).
  • Discussion question
    "The conclusion AM = MB is correct. The proof is still wrong. Why does the reason matter if the conclusion is right?"
    Because the reason is what makes the conclusion valid for all cases, not just this one. If you cite the wrong theorem, you've shown that the conclusion could be true in some other situation, but you haven't shown that it must be true in this one. The Segment Addition Postulate tells you AM + MB = AB — that doesn't force AM and MB to be equal. Only the Definition of Midpoint does that.
Error 3: "Missing Step" — jumped to conclusion without showing the work
Given: ∠1 and ∠2 supplementary, m∠1 = 70°. Prove: m∠2 = 110°
Row 1: ∠1 and ∠2 supplementary — Given ✓
Row 2: m∠1 + m∠2 = 180° — Definition of Supplementary Angles ✓
Row 3: m∠2 = 110° — Subtraction Property ✗

Error: Row 3 skips two steps. You can't jump from "m∠1 + m∠2 = 180°" to "m∠2 = 110°" in one step — you first need to substitute m∠1 = 70° (Substitution Property), and then apply the Subtraction Property to get m∠2 = 110°. Missing the substitution step is the most common error.
The missing substitution step appears in nearly every student's first proofs Emphasize: "Before you can subtract, you have to replace the variable with its value. That replacement is its own step — it's the Substitution Property. Every time you put a number in place of a variable or expression, you owe the proof a 'Substitution Property' row." This is the most important error-prevention message of the lesson.
Section 6 — Practice Zone & Exit Ticket (10 min)
Practice Mixed Practice + Exit Ticket 10 min
What to watch for during Practice Zone
Three patterns predict almost all proof errors: (1) citing a theorem that doesn't match the situation, (2) skipping the substitution step, and (3) confusing "Given" with "Therefore." Circulate specifically for these three. Students who make error #1 should be redirected to the Reason Toolkit. Students who make error #2 should hear the substitution narration again. Students who make error #3 may be writing the conclusion in the Given column — ask them which information the problem provided vs. what they derived.
Exit Ticket — 5 min
ProblemWhat it testsAnswer
ET1: Prove: if 2x + 5 = 13, then x = 4. Write the two-column proof (5 rows). Algebraic proof — all 7 steps named R1:Given, R2:Subtr.Prop, R3:Simplify, R4:Div.Prop, R5:Simplify
ET2: Given: BD bisects ∠ABC. Name the reason that justifies: m∠ABD = m∠DBC. Geometric proof reason recall Definition of Angle Bisector
ET3: A student writes: "∠1 = ∠2 because they look equal." Identify the error and write the correct type of reason needed. Error analysis recall Appearance is not a valid reason; need a named theorem, definition, or postulate (e.g., Vertical Angles Theorem if applicable)
If There Is Extra Time — Partner, Small Group & Extension Activities
Activities marked ★ Core are high-value for any students with extra time. Activities marked Extension are for students who are fully fluent on both algebraic and geometric proofs and ready for deeper challenge.
🔀 Reason Sort Race ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8 min  ·  After algebraic proofs section
Partners receive 10 cards, each showing a transformation of an equation (e.g., "3x = 15 → x = 5"). They must sort them into the correct algebraic property as fast as possible, then justify their sort verbally — one partner points to a card, the other says the property name and explains it.
  • Cards include: both directions of each property to catch students who confuse addition with subtraction
  • Include 2 "trick" cards: one that requires two steps (combine + divide) and one that is a correct use of Substitution (not arithmetic)
  • After sorting: pairs write a 3-row algebraic proof using any three of the sorted cards in sequence
The verbal justification requirement is the most important part — partners who can say "this is Division Property because we're dividing both sides by 3" have internalized the property, not just sorted a card.
📜 Proof Builder Challenge ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 10 min  ·  After geometric proofs section
Pairs receive a stripped two-column proof where all reasons are blank but statements are provided. They fill in the reasons using only the Reason Toolkit from the lesson. Three proofs of increasing difficulty: (1) vertical angles, (2) supplementary + given value, (3) midpoint + total length given.
  • Proof 3 (hardest): Given M is the midpoint of PQ and PQ = 36. Prove AM = 18. Requires: Given, Def. Midpoint, Segment Add., Substitution, Division Property
  • Require both the reason AND a one-sentence explanation of why that reason applies for each row
  • Pairs swap completed proofs with another pair to peer-review for missing steps
Proof 3 is a significant extension — it introduces a new type of given (total length provided) that students haven't seen. Use it selectively for pairs that finish Proofs 1 and 2 quickly.
🏥 Proof Hospital ★ Core · Groups of 3
⏱ 10 min  ·  After error analysis section
Groups of 3 receive 4 "patient proofs" — each with exactly one error. For each proof, the group must: (1) identify the specific row with the error, (2) name the error type (appearance/wrong theorem/missing step/wrong notation), and (3) write the corrected proof. Each group member takes one patient and presents to the group.
  • Patient 1: Used ≅ where = was needed (notation error from Day 5)
  • Patient 2: Stated the converse of a theorem as if it were the original theorem
  • Patient 3: Skipped the Substitution Property step in a midpoint proof
  • Patient 4: Used "it appears" as a reason for a corresponding angles step
The converse error (Patient 2) is the most sophisticated and most valuable for students to encounter before the parallel lines unit. Don't skip it.
🔄 Converse True/False Tournament ★ Core · Whole Class
⏱ 6–8 min  ·  After Logic Lab
Teacher reads an IF-THEN statement. Students write T or F for the original, then T or F for its converse, on mini-whiteboards. Class holds up boards on count of three. Any disagreement triggers a 60-second pair discussion before the class vote is resolved.
  • Round 1: "IF a triangle has all equal sides, THEN all angles are 60°" — T; Converse: "IF all angles are 60°, THEN all sides are equal" — T
  • Round 2: "IF two angles are supplementary, THEN they add to 180°" — T; Converse: "IF two angles add to 180°, THEN they are supplementary" — T (these are equivalent)
  • Round 3: "IF two angles are complementary, THEN each is less than 90°" — T; Converse: "IF each angle is less than 90°, THEN they are complementary" — F (e.g., 30° and 40°)
  • Round 4: "IF lines are parallel, THEN corresponding angles are equal" — T; Converse — T (this one IS a valid theorem — parallel lines converse)
Round 4 is the critical one — the converse of the Corresponding Angles Postulate IS true and is used to prove lines are parallel. Students who see this now will be better prepared for coordinate geometry and the proofs unit.
✍️ Write Your Own Proof Extension · Pairs
⏱ 12 min  ·  Strong finishers only
Given a set of three theorems from the Reason Toolkit, pairs construct their own Given-Prove problem and write the complete proof. The proof must use at least 4 steps and cite at least 2 different reasons. Pairs then swap proofs and check each other's work.
  • Suggested theorem combination: Triangle Sum Theorem + Exterior Angle Theorem → create a proof using both
  • Minimum requirements: a Given statement, a Prove statement, a 4+ row two-column proof, at least two different reasons
  • Stretch goal: write a proof where the converse of a theorem is needed, not the original
Students who can generate their own proofs — not just complete them — are at the highest level of mastery for this lesson. Even an imperfect self-generated proof shows more understanding than a perfectly completed worksheet.
🌐 Feromax Proof Practice Extension · Individual
⏱ 10–15 min  ·  Any time after algebraic proofs
Students use the Feromax "Prove It!" interactive proof tool (linked from the lesson website) to practice additional two-column proofs with immediate feedback. Feromax generates new proofs on demand and checks each step for validity.
  • Start with the "Angle Relationships" category to connect directly to today's lesson
  • Challenge: complete 3 proofs without any incorrect reason selections
  • Harder challenge: attempt the "Triangle Proofs" category, which uses reasons from Days 3–5
Feromax is one of the best free tools for proof practice — it lets students attempt proofs at their own pace and see immediate feedback. Recommend it as a homework extension even if time doesn't permit in class.
Day 6 — Connections Forward
How today's proof structure feeds the rest of the course
Days 7–8 — Coordinate The midpoint and distance formulas are proved using algebraic properties. Students who know the Substitution and Simplify steps will follow the derivations. Students who don't will just memorize formulas.
Days 9–10 — Pythagorean The Pythagorean Theorem is introduced with a proof. Students who understand IF-THEN structure and two-column format will engage with the proof rather than just memorizing the formula.
Day 11 — Transformations Transformation proofs use congruence and coordinate reasoning. The two-column format from today carries directly into proving that a transformation preserves distance or angle measure.
Full Geometry Course Every unit of high school geometry uses proof structure. Students who own the "Because Test" and the two-column format here will approach every geometry proof with a framework rather than starting from scratch. Day 6 is the most leveraged lesson in the course.
