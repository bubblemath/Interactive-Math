📈 Day 8: Slope and Parallel/Perpendicular Lines

The Slope Investigation

Slope is the "Directional Fingerprint" of a line.

Just as the Distance Formula tells us "how far," Slope tells us "which way." This connects directly to coordinate proofs!

  • Parallel lines: Same slope = same direction = never meet
  • Perpendicular lines: Negative reciprocal slopes = 90° angle
  • Coordinate proofs: Use slope to prove shapes are parallelograms, rectangles, etc.
  • Real applications: Ramps, roofs, roads, wheelchair accessibility

Master slope today, and coordinate proofs become straightforward!

🔢 The Fraction Forge: Reducing Fractions

Before we calculate slopes, you need to be comfortable reducing fractions. All slope answers must be in reduced form!

🔑 The Key Rule

To reduce a fraction, divide both the numerator and denominator by their Greatest Common Factor (GCF).

📝 Step-by-Step Examples

Example 1: Reduce \(\frac{12}{8}\)
1
Find the GCF of 12 and 8: Factors of 12 = {1, 2, 3, 4, 6, 12}, Factors of 8 = {1, 2, 4, 8} → GCF = 4
2
Divide both by 4: \(\frac{12 \div 4}{8 \div 4} = \frac{3}{2}\)
Example 2: Reduce \(\frac{-6}{9}\)
1
Find the GCF of 6 and 9: GCF = 3
2
Divide both by 3: \(\frac{-6 \div 3}{9 \div 3} = \frac{-2}{3}\)
3
Convention: Put the negative in front: \(-\frac{2}{3}\)
Example 3: Reduce \(\frac{15}{-25}\)
1
Find the GCF of 15 and 25: GCF = 5
2
Divide both by 5: \(\frac{15 \div 5}{-25 \div 5} = \frac{3}{-5}\)
3
Convention: \(-\frac{3}{5}\)

📈 Slope Discovery: The Directional Fingerprint

Slope tells us the steepness and direction of a line — it's the line's unique "fingerprint."

📐 The Slope Formula

\(m = \frac{\text{rise}}{\text{run}} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}\)

Slope measures how much the line rises (or falls) for every unit it runs horizontally.

🎭 Meet "Slope Man" — Your Visual Memory Aid

Remember the four types of slope by looking at Slope Man's face!

↗️
Positive Slope
Uphill (left to right)
\(m > 0\)
↘️
Negative Slope
Downhill (left to right)
\(m < 0\)
➡️
Zero Slope
Flat/Horizontal
\(m = 0\)
⬆️
Undefined Slope
Vertical (the "cliff")
\(m = \text{undefined}\)

💡 Memory Trick

"Start at the left and walk right. Are you climbing uphill (positive) or sliding downhill (negative)? Standing still on flat ground (zero) or hitting a wall (undefined)?"

📝 Worked Example

Find the slope between A(2, 3) and B(5, 9). Express as a reduced fraction.

1
Identify coordinates: \((x_1, y_1) = (2, 3)\) and \((x_2, y_2) = (5, 9)\)
2
Substitute: \(m = \frac{9 - 3}{5 - 2} = \frac{6}{3}\)
3
Reduce: \(\frac{6}{3} = 2\) (or \(\frac{2}{1}\))

🛤️ Parallel & Perpendicular Lines

The relationship between lines can be determined by comparing their slopes!

∥ Parallel Lines

🛤️

Same direction = Same slope

\(m_1 = m_2\)

Parallel lines never meet because they're going the same direction!

⊥ Perpendicular Lines

✚

The "90° Flip"

\(m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1\)

Perpendicular lines form right angles!

🔄 The Negative Reciprocal Rule

For perpendicular lines: Flip the fraction AND flip the sign!

If \(m_1 = \frac{2}{3}\)

↓

Then \(m_2 = -\frac{3}{2}\)

If \(m_1 = 4 = \frac{4}{1}\)

↓

Then \(m_2 = -\frac{1}{4}\)

If \(m_1 = -\frac{1}{5}\)

↓

Then \(m_2 = 5\)

💡 Quick Check

Multiply the two slopes together. If you get -1, they're perpendicular!

Example: \(\frac{2}{3} \times -\frac{3}{2} = -\frac{6}{6} = -1\) ✓

✍️ The Equation Builder: Point-Slope Form

When you have a point and a slope, Point-Slope Form is your best friend!

📐 Point-Slope Form (The Detective's Tool)

\(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\)

Just plug in your point \((x_1, y_1)\) and slope \(m\)!

💡 Why Point-Slope?

It's "plug and play" for detectives who have found a slope (the fingerprint) and a point (the location). Just substitute and simplify!

🔄 Converting to Slope-Intercept Form

Sometimes you need to convert to \(y = mx + b\) form. Here's the workflow:

Example: Write the equation of a line with slope 3 passing through (1, 4)

Step 1: Start with Point-Slope Form
\(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\)
Step 2: Substitute the point (1, 4) and slope m = 3
\(y - 4 = 3(x - 1)\)
Step 3: Distribute the slope
\(y - 4 = 3x - 3\)
Step 4: Add 4 to both sides (Slope-Intercept Form)
\(y = 3x + 1\)

📝 More Examples

Example 2: Line with slope \(-\frac{2}{3}\) through (6, -1)
1
Point-Slope: \(y - (-1) = -\frac{2}{3}(x - 6)\)
2
Simplify left side: \(y + 1 = -\frac{2}{3}(x - 6)\)
3
Distribute: \(y + 1 = -\frac{2}{3}x + 4\)
4
Slope-Intercept: \(y = -\frac{2}{3}x + 3\)
Example 3: Line parallel to \(y = 2x + 5\) through (3, 1)
1
Parallel means same slope: \(m = 2\)
2
Point-Slope: \(y - 1 = 2(x - 3)\)
3
Distribute: \(y - 1 = 2x - 6\)
4
Slope-Intercept: \(y = 2x - 5\)
Example 4: Line perpendicular to \(y = -\frac{1}{3}x + 2\) through (2, 5)
1
Perpendicular means negative reciprocal: \(m = 3\)
2
Point-Slope: \(y - 5 = 3(x - 2)\)
3
Distribute: \(y - 5 = 3x - 6\)
4
Slope-Intercept: \(y = 3x - 1\)

📋 The Two Forms: Quick Reference

Form Formula Best Used When...
Point-Slope \(y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)\) You have a point and a slope
Slope-Intercept \(y = mx + b\) You need to graph or find the y-intercept
