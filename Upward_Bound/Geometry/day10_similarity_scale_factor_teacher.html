⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

Day 10 is built around a single unifying idea: a ratio is a comparison, and proportional reasoning is the tool that scales that comparison to any size. Unit conversions, cross-multiplication, scale factors, and similar figures are all the same mathematical operation applied in different contexts. Students who see this unity will find the lesson coherent and memorable. Students who treat each topic as a separate procedure will struggle to transfer their skills.

The unit conversion section is a warm-up and prerequisite check, not a standalone lesson. Its purpose is to establish the "multiply by 1" framing — any conversion factor where numerator and denominator are equal quantities is a form of 1. This framing makes cross-multiplication feel less like magic and more like the algebra students already know from Day 2.

The most important conceptual move of the lesson is the derivation of cross-multiplication as "clearing denominators" — not a rule to memorize but a consequence of algebra. Students who understand WHY cross-multiplication works are far less likely to apply it incorrectly (e.g., cross-multiplying when quantities are being multiplied rather than set equal). The lesson website provides a formal derivation — use it. Do not skip it.

The scale factor section is where the lesson comes alive for most students. The "zoom level" framing — a scale factor is the number you multiply every dimension by — is intuitive and connects to familiar experiences like photo resizing, map scales, and model kits. The two GeoGebra applets (similar figures and similarity transformations) show students what similarity looks like visually before they encounter the algebra of proportions applied to geometric figures.

The Triangle Similarity Reference section is explicitly labeled as reference only — the lesson website says it will NOT appear in the Practice Zone. In an 80-minute lesson, this section should receive 8–10 minutes of brief presentation. Students should know the three postulate names (AA, SAS, SSS) and be able to read a proof using them, but the deep treatment of triangle similarity proofs is beyond the scope of this day.

Connection to prior lessons: Day 2's equation-solving skills appear directly in proportion solving. Day 6's proof structure (Given → Reason → Conclusion) is the scaffold for the AA, SAS, SSS similarity proofs. Day 9's scale factor insight (area scales as the square of the linear scale factor) can be connected if time permits — it is one of the most powerful applications of today's content.

Learning Objectives
  • Convert units using the "multiply by 1" strategy with single and chained conversion factors
  • Set up and solve proportions using cross-multiplication, and explain WHY cross-multiplication works (clearing denominators)
  • Solve proportions with algebraic expressions in the numerator, including those requiring distribution
  • Define similar figures and explain the scale factor as the "zoom multiplier" connecting corresponding sides
  • Find the scale factor between two similar figures and use it to find missing side lengths
  • Set up and solve proportions from similar polygon problems
  • Distinguish between congruent (k = 1) and similar (k ≠ 1) figures using correct notation (≅ vs ~)
  • Identify which triangle similarity postulate (AA, SAS, SSS) applies in a given situation
  • State the three triangle similarity postulates and explain what each requires as given information

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • Unit conversions are a warm-up gate — cap at 10 minutes. If students handle 3.5 hours → seconds in under 2 minutes, move on after 2–3 problems. The purpose is establishing the "multiply by 1" framing, not drilling every conversion. Students who struggle should be directed to the conversion cheat sheet, not re-taught from scratch.
  • The cross-multiplication derivation is NOT optional. Students who cannot explain why cross-multiplication works will apply it incorrectly when two fractions are being multiplied (not set equal). Spend 3 minutes on the "clearing denominators" proof before any proportion examples. This 3-minute investment prevents the most common proportion error in the entire curriculum.
  • Do not let proportion examples expand beyond 15 minutes. All three examples are important: Example 1 (simple numeric), Example 2 (expression in numerator, distribute), Example 3 (expressions on both sides). But Examples 2 and 3 are the ones students need most; if time is short, work Example 1 as a class and release Examples 2 and 3 to pairs.
  • The scale factor "zoom level" framing needs physical intuition before the formula. Open the GeoGebra Similarity applet (ZPVCZUAr) before writing any formula. Ask students to drag and observe before you define anything. Students who have seen scale factor as a visual "zoom" concept before encountering the formula k = New/Original will retain it far longer.
  • The Triangle Similarity Reference section is reference, not instruction. It is explicitly labeled as such on the website. Do not attempt to fully teach AA, SAS, SSS in the time remaining. Present the three postulate names, show one proof (AA is fastest), and direct students to the reference for the others. This should take 8–10 minutes, no more.
  • Scale factor connects to Day 9's area relationship. If time permits after the core content, the connection "linear scale factor k → area scales as k²" is one of the most powerful and commonly tested proportional reasoning facts in geometry. A 3-minute discussion of this connection is high-leverage if the class is moving quickly.

Section 1 — Unit Conversion Toolkit (10 min)
Conversions Multiply-by-1 Strategy + Three Chain Conversion Examples + Quick Practice 10 min
The "multiply by 1" framing — establish this before any examples
Write this on the board: "60 seconds/1 minute = 1. Why?" Students should be able to see that 60 seconds and 1 minute are the same duration — so the ratio equals 1. When you multiply any measurement by this "1," you change the units without changing the value. This is the entire conceptual basis for dimensional analysis/unit conversion.
Say this "I know 60 seconds and 1 minute are the same length of time — so their ratio is 1. Multiplying anything by 1 doesn't change its value, it only changes how we express it. Watch how we 'cancel' the units we don't want and keep the ones we do. This is the same fraction-canceling you've done in algebra — we're just using it on units."
Chain conversion worked examples — unit cancellation emphasis
3.5 hours → seconds:
3.5 hr × (60 min/1 hr) × (60 sec/1 min) = 3.5 × 60 × 60 = 12,600 seconds
Note: "hr" in numerator of 3.5 hr cancels with "hr" in denominator of (60 min/1 hr). "min" cancels similarly. Only "sec" remains.

100,000 inches → miles:
100,000 in × (1 ft/12 in) × (1 mi/5,280 ft) = 100,000/(12 × 5,280) ≈ 1.58 miles

Key habit: write units on every number. If a unit appears in both numerator and denominator, it cancels. Stop when only the target unit remains.
The unit cancellation habit prevents the most common conversion error Students who multiply instead of divide (e.g., converting minutes to hours by multiplying by 60 instead of dividing) are not thinking about unit cancellation — they're trying to remember a rule. The fix is writing units on every quantity: if "min" appears in both the numerator and denominator, it cancels; if it appears only in the numerator, you're going the wrong direction and need to flip the conversion factor.
Discussion questions
  • Connecting to proportions
    "A conversion factor like '60 seconds/1 minute' is a ratio equal to 1. How is that the same idea as setting up a proportion? What do proportions and conversion factors have in common?"
    Both are ratios. A proportion says two ratios are equal to each other. A conversion factor is a ratio that equals 1. In both cases, we're using the relationship between units or quantities to scale a value without changing its meaning. Unit conversion is what proportional reasoning looks like when applied to measurement units rather than lengths or areas.
  • Real-world context
    "A map has a scale of 1 inch = 50 miles. How would you use the 'multiply by 1' strategy to convert a 3.5-inch distance on the map to actual miles? What is the conversion factor?"
    Conversion factor: (50 miles / 1 inch). So 3.5 inches × (50 miles / 1 inch) = 175 miles. The "inch" units cancel, leaving miles. This is exactly unit conversion applied to a map scale — which is itself a ratio, connecting directly to the scale factor concept later in the lesson.
Section 2 — Ratio & Proportion Lab (18 min)
Proportions Cross-Multiplication Derivation + GeoGebra + Three Worked Examples 18 min
★ Discovery Move: Derive Before Displaying (3 min)

Write a/b = c/d on the board without any formula for cross-multiplication. Ask: "How would you solve for a in terms of b, c, d? Use the algebra you already know from Day 2." Students will suggest multiplying both sides by something. Lead them to multiply both sides by bd — the product of the denominators. This gives: d·a = b·c. That IS cross-multiplication. They derived it.

Then ask: "Now you know cross-multiplication isn't magic — it's just algebra. When is it valid to use it? What's the setup?" The answer: two equal fractions. Cross-multiplication only applies when you have a FRACTION = FRACTION equation. If fractions are being multiplied by each other (not set equal), cross-multiplication doesn't apply — this is the most common misuse.

GeoGebra Proportions applet — geogebra.org/m/zqWyhwC4 (3 min)
Open the applet before working examples. Ask students to manipulate the proportion and observe how the cross products relate. Key question: "If I double the value of a, what must happen to one of the other values to keep the proportion true?" This reinforces proportional reasoning as a relationship, not just a procedure.
Three worked examples — gradual release
Example 1: x/6 = 10/15 (Teacher-led)
Cross-multiply: 15x = 6 × 10 = 60 → x = 4
Verify: 4/6 = 10/15 → 2/3 = 2/3 ✓

Example 2: (x + 2)/8 = 15/20 (Student pairs)
Cross-multiply: 20(x + 2) = 8 × 15 = 120
Distribute: 20x + 40 = 120 → 20x = 80 → x = 4
Common error: distributing 20 only to x, not to 2. Require: 20(x + 2) written out before distributing.

Example 3: (3x − 1)/10 = (x + 5)/6 (Pairs, then cold-call)
Cross-multiply: 6(3x − 1) = 10(x + 5)
Distribute both sides: 18x − 6 = 10x + 50
Solve: 8x = 56 → x = 7
Verify: (3×7 − 1)/10 = 20/10 = 2. (7 + 5)/6 = 12/6 = 2. ✓
The distribution error in Example 2 — anticipate and pre-empt When students see 20(x + 2), many will write 20x + 2 instead of 20x + 40. This is the same distribution error from Day 9's quadratic expansion. Before students work Example 2, say: "You are about to distribute. The number outside the parentheses must multiply EVERY term inside. Watch for this — it's the most common error in proportion solving." Students who hear this warning make the error about half as often.
Discussion questions
  • When does cross-multiplication apply?
    "A student sees the equation (3/4)(2/5) = x/10 and cross-multiplies, setting 3 × 5 = 4 × 2x. What's wrong? When is cross-multiplication valid and when is it not?"
    Cross-multiplication is only valid when the equation has the form (fraction) = (fraction) — two ratios set EQUAL to each other. The student's equation has a fraction times a fraction equal to a fraction — a multiplication, not an equality of two fractions. The correct approach is to multiply the two fractions on the left: (3/4)(2/5) = 6/20 = 3/10, so x = 3. Cross-multiplication is a shortcut for the specific case of a proportion (equality of two ratios).
  • The verify habit
    "After solving x/6 = 10/15 and getting x = 4, how would you verify the answer? Why is verifying a proportion easier than verifying most other equations?"
    Substitute: 4/6 = 2/3. And 10/15 = 2/3. Equal. ✓ Proportions are easy to verify because you can reduce both fractions and check they're equal — a visual check that doesn't require much arithmetic. This makes the proportion an excellent equation type for building the checking habit: it's fast, visual, and unambiguous.
  • Connecting to Day 2
    "On Day 2 you learned to set up equations from geometric relationships. A similar figure problem gives you a proportion. How is 'two segments are complementary' from Day 2 structurally similar to 'these figures are similar' today?"
    Both give you a relationship that translates into an equation. "Complementary" → add to 90° → one equation. "Similar" → ratios of sides are equal → a proportion equation. In both cases, the geometric relationship is the instruction for what equation to write. Day 2 taught the pattern; today applies the same pattern to a different geometric relationship.
Section 3 — Similar Figures & Scale Factor (24 min)
Similarity "Zoom Level" Concept + GeoGebra + Two Methods + Congruent vs Similar 24 min
★ Discovery Move: GeoGebra Before Any Formula (5 min)

Open geogebra.org/m/ZPVCZUAr before writing anything. Ask students to drag the scale slider and observe. Key discovery questions to ask while the applet is live:

"What changes when I increase the scale factor? What stays the same? Look at the angles — do they change? Look at the side lengths — how do they change compared to the original?" Students should articulate: angles stay the same, side lengths change proportionally, the shapes look like zoomed versions of each other.

Then open geogebra.org/m/gsuknvyp (Similarity Transformations) and drag to show that similar figures can also be rotated, reflected, or translated — similarity is about the proportional relationship, not position or orientation. Ask: "Are these the same shape? Same size? What makes them similar?"

The "zoom level" framing — before the formula
Ask: "When you zoom in on a photo on your phone, what changes? What stays the same? If the photo goes from 2 inches wide to 6 inches wide, what is the zoom factor?" Students will say: shapes stay the same, sizes change; zoom factor is 3. Then: "In geometry, we call that the scale factor. It's the multiplier that takes every dimension of the original and produces the corresponding dimension of the copy."
After the applet and zoom analogy, now define "Scale factor k = New Side / Original Side. Every single side of the similar figure equals k times the corresponding side of the original. If k > 1, it's an enlargement. If 0 < k < 1, it's a reduction. If k = 1, the figures are congruent — same shape, same size."
Two methods for similar polygon problems — present side by side
Rectangle ABCD ~ Rectangle EFGH. AB = 6, EF = 9, BC = 4. Find FG.

Method 1 (Scale Factor):
k = EF/AB = 9/6 = 3/2
FG = k × BC = (3/2) × 4 = 6

Method 2 (Proportion):
AB/EF = BC/FG → 6/9 = 4/FG
Cross-multiply: 6 × FG = 36 → FG = 6

Both methods give the same answer. Method 1 is faster when k is obvious. Method 2 is more systematic for complex problems. Students should know both.
The "which is numerator?" trap in scale factor When students write k = New/Original, they must be consistent about which figure is "new" and which is "original." In any problem, the problem will specify which figure is the image (new) and which is the pre-image (original) — usually through the order of the similarity statement: △ABC ~ △DEF means A↔D, B↔E, C↔F, and AB/DE = BC/EF = AC/DF. If students lose track of corresponding vertices, they will write the wrong ratio. Require: always match vertices from the similarity statement before computing any ratio.
Congruent vs. Similar — the notation distinction
≅ (congruent): same shape AND same size. Example: △ABC ≅ △DEF means every side and angle is identical.
~ (similar): same shape, proportional sizes. Example: △ABC ~ △DEF means all angles equal, sides proportional.

Key insight: Congruence is a special case of similarity where k = 1. Every congruent pair is also similar, but not every similar pair is congruent.
★ Area scaling connection — Day 9 revisited (3 min if time permits)
Ask: "If I scale every dimension of a rectangle by k = 2, what happens to its area?" Students can compute: original area = ℓ × w. New area = (2ℓ) × (2w) = 4ℓw = 4 × original area. So doubling linear dimensions quadruples the area. The general rule: if linear scale factor = k, then area scale factor = k². This is one of the most frequently tested proportional reasoning facts on standardized tests.
Discussion questions
  • The zoom analogy extended
    "If a map has a scale of 1 inch = 50 miles, what is the scale factor of the map compared to reality? What type of transformation is a map — enlargement or reduction?"
    The map is a reduction of reality. The scale factor is 1 inch / (50 miles) — an extremely small number, since 50 miles must be converted to inches to find the ratio (50 × 5,280 × 12 ≈ 3,168,000 inches). So k ≈ 1/3,168,000 — an extreme reduction. This connects unit conversion (from Section 1) to scale factor (from this section): finding the numeric scale factor of a map requires unit conversion first. This is the lesson's biggest cross-section connection.
  • The area scaling surprise
    "A photo is 4 inches × 6 inches. You print a version that is 8 inches × 12 inches — double the dimensions. How much more printing material (area) does the larger photo use? Is it 2× more, or something else?"
    Linear scale factor k = 2. Area scale factor = k² = 4. Original area = 24 sq in. New area = 96 sq in. The larger photo uses 4× the printing material, not 2×. This surprises many people — doubling both dimensions quadruples the area. Real-world implication: if you want a photo that uses twice the paper, you should scale dimensions by √2 ≈ 1.41, not by 2.
  • Congruence as a special case
    "Every congruent figure is also similar. Is every similar figure also congruent? What would make a similar figure NOT be congruent?"
    No — similar figures are congruent only when k = 1. If k ≠ 1, the figures are similar but not congruent (different sizes). So congruence is a subset of similarity: the set of all congruent figures is contained within the set of all similar figures. This is why the similar symbol (~) is the more general relationship.
Section 4 — Triangle Similarity Reference (10 min)
Reference AA, SAS, SSS Postulates — Reference Only, Not Assessed in Practice Zone 10 min
Present as a survey — three names, three visuals, one proof
The lesson website flags this section explicitly as "reference only." In the remaining 10 minutes, the goal is: students know the three postulate names and the minimum information each requires. Do not attempt full derivations or additional proof practice — that would require a dedicated Day 13. Use the three-column summary on the lesson website as a quick-reference scan rather than a teaching target.
AA (Angle-Angle): If 2 pairs of corresponding angles are equal → triangles are similar.
Why only 2? Because if 2 angles are equal, the third must be too (Triangle Sum from Day 4).

SAS (Side-Angle-Side): 2 pairs of proportional sides AND the INCLUDED angle congruent → similar.
Note: The angle must be between the two sides (included). Order matters.

SSS (Side-Side-Side): All 3 pairs of corresponding sides proportional → similar.
Note: Sides proportional (ratios equal), not necessarily equal in length.
Work the AA proof together — it's the fastest (3 min)
Given: ∠A ≅ ∠D, ∠B ≅ ∠E. Prove: △ABC ~ △DEF.

Statement 1: ∠A ≅ ∠D → Reason: Given
Statement 2: ∠B ≅ ∠E → Reason: Given
Statement 3: △ABC ~ △DEF → Reason: AA Similarity Postulate

This is a 3-step proof — the simplest proof structure in the course. Ask students: "Why don't we need to say anything about ∠C and ∠F?" (Triangle Sum — if two pairs of angles are equal, the third pair must be too.)
Connecting to Day 3 The AA Similarity Postulate is one reason why the parallel lines angle relationships from Day 3 matter so much: if two parallel lines are cut by two transversals, the triangles formed often have two pairs of equal angles by the corresponding or alternate interior angles theorems. Day 3's vocabulary is what allows students to PROVE that triangles are similar using AA — without it, they'd have to measure angles. This is a powerful cross-day connection worth a 60-second mention.
Discussion questions
  • Why AA is sufficient
    "We need two pairs of equal angles for AA. Why don't we need to know anything about the side lengths to prove triangles are similar with AA?"
    Similar figures have the same angles and proportional sides. If we know the angles are equal (same shape), the sides must be in proportion — that's what "similar" means. You don't need to verify the side ratios separately because the angle information already guarantees similarity. This is different from congruence (SSS, SAS, ASA, AAS) where you need more information to prove the sizes are equal too.
  • The "included" requirement in SAS
    "In SAS Similarity, why does the angle need to be the INCLUDED angle (between the two sides)? What could go wrong if the angle were not included?"
    If the angle is not between the two proportional sides, it's possible to construct triangles where the two side proportions hold and a non-included angle is equal, but the triangles are not similar. The included angle ensures the shape is uniquely determined. This is analogous to the ambiguous case (SSA) in triangle congruence — not enough information without the angle being in the right place.

Exit Ticket
ProblemWhat it testsAnswer
ET1: Solve the proportion: (2x − 3)/7 = (x + 4)/5. Show all steps including the distribution. Algebraic proportion — cross-multiply, distribute both sides, solve 5(2x−3) = 7(x+4) → 10x−15 = 7x+28 → 3x = 43 → x = 43/3 ≈ 14.3. ET1 — Use integer-friendly values The proportion (2x − 3)/7 = (x + 4)/5 produces the non-integer x = 43/3. For a cleaner exit ticket, use (x + 2)/3 = (x + 8)/5 → 5x + 10 = 3x + 24 → 2x = 14 → x = 7. Both test the same skills (cross-multiply, distribute both sides, solve) but the integer answer is cleaner for class verification.
If There Is Extra Time — Partner, Small Group & Extension Activities
Activities marked ★ Core are high-value for any students with extra time. Activities marked Extension are for students fully fluent on all four lesson sections.
🗺️ Map Scale Challenge ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8–10 min  ·  After Conversions + Similarity
Pairs receive a simple hand-drawn map with a scale (e.g., 1 inch = 25 miles). They measure five distances on the map in inches and convert to actual miles using both the proportion method and the unit conversion method. Then they compute the scale factor and verify both methods give the same answer.
  • Distances: 2.5 in, 4 in, 1.75 in, 3.25 in, 0.5 in — all in the "real world" range of 12–100 miles
  • After computing: "What is the scale factor of this map?" (1/1,584,000 if using inches; students don't need to compute this — just identify it conceptually)
  • Extension: "If the map were 3 inches × 4 inches, what real-world area does it represent?"
This activity directly bridges Section 1 (unit conversion) and Section 3 (scale factor) and makes both feel applied and purposeful. The "what real-world area?" extension requires the k² area scaling relationship from the lesson discussion.
⚡ Proportion Speed Round ★ Core · Groups of 3
⏱ 8 min  ·  After Proportions section
Groups of 3 work as a relay: Student 1 sets up the proportion equation (cross-multiplication form). Student 2 distributes and simplifies. Student 3 solves for x and verifies by substitution. Rotate roles for three problems. Fastest group with all correct answers wins.
  • Round 1: x/4 = 15/20 (simple numeric) — x = 3
  • Round 2: (x + 5)/9 = (2x − 1)/15 → 15x + 75 = 18x − 9 → x = 28
  • Round 3: (3x)/8 = (x + 6)/4 → 12x = 8x + 48 → x = 12
Requiring verification in every round builds the checking habit. Groups that skip verification and produce a wrong answer learn the habit more durably than groups that verify correctly from the start.
📏 Scale Drawing Lab ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 10–12 min  ·  After Similar Figures
Pairs receive the dimensions of a "room" (6m × 9m with a 2m × 3m closet), choose a scale factor (e.g., 1 cm = 1 m), and draw the room to scale on graph paper. They compute all actual dimensions, apply the scale factor, and produce a labeled scaled drawing. Then they find the perimeter and area of both the drawing and the actual room, and verify the ratios.
  • Drawing: 6 cm × 9 cm room with 2 cm × 3 cm closet (scale factor 1/100 = 1 cm : 1 m)
  • Ratio check: Actual perimeter / Drawing perimeter = 100 (same as linear scale factor)
  • Area ratio check: Actual area / Drawing area = 10,000 = 100² (area scale factor = k²)
The area ratio verification is a powerful concrete demonstration of k² — students who compute (6×9)/(0.06×0.09) = 54/0.0054 = 10,000 have verified the k² relationship with their own numbers.
🔺 AA in the Wild ★ Core · Groups of 3
⏱ 8 min  ·  After Triangle Similarity Reference
Groups receive three diagram cards, each showing two triangles. For each card, they must: (1) identify which similarity postulate applies (AA, SAS, SSS, or "cannot determine"), (2) cite the reason from the diagram, (3) write the similarity statement in correct order (matching corresponding vertices).
  • Card 1: Two triangles sharing a vertical angle, with one pair of parallel sides marked → AA (vertical angles + corresponding angles from parallel lines)
  • Card 2: Two triangles with three pairs of side lengths labeled and proportional → SSS Similarity
  • Card 3: Two triangles with two side pairs proportional and the angle between them marked equal → SAS Similarity
Card 1's connection to Day 3 (parallel lines → corresponding angles) is deliberate: this is how Day 3 vocabulary enables Day 10 similarity proofs. Students who make this connection have integrated across the entire course.
📐 Shadow Proportion Problem Extension · Pairs
⏱ 10 min  ·  Strong finishers only
A 6-foot person casts a 4-foot shadow at the same time a tree casts a 22-foot shadow. How tall is the tree? This is a classic AA similarity problem: both the person and tree form triangles with the sun's rays at the same angle at the same time.
  • Set up the proportion: person height / person shadow = tree height / tree shadow
  • 6/4 = h/22 → h = 33 feet
  • Extension: Write an AA proof that the two triangles (person-shadow-sun ray and tree-shadow-sun ray) are similar, citing the reasons for each angle pair
  • Further extension: If you measured your shadow to be 5 feet at noon, what would the building across the street's actual height be if its shadow was 40 feet?
Shadow problems are a classic application of AA similarity that appears frequently on standardized tests. The connection to the parallel sun-ray assumption (making corresponding angles equal) ties to Day 3's parallel lines vocabulary.
📊 Coordinate Similarity Proof Extension · Individual
⏱ 12 min  ·  Any time after Similarity section
Given triangles A(0,0), B(4,0), C(0,3) and D(0,0), E(8,0), F(0,6), prove the triangles are similar using coordinate methods from Days 7–8 and today's similarity postulates.
  • Compute all side lengths using Distance Formula: AB=4, BC=5, AC=3; DE=8, EF=10, DF=6
  • Check ratios: DE/AB = 8/4 = 2, EF/BC = 10/5 = 2, DF/AC = 6/3 = 2 → all equal → SSS Similarity, k = 2
  • Extension: confirm using AA — both triangles are right triangles (check with slope product = −1 from Day 8); they share angle A = angle D = 90°; since the triangles sum to 180° and share one angle, the other angles must also be equal
This activity is the most complete synthesis in the course: Distance Formula (Day 7), slope and perpendicularity (Day 8), similarity and scale factor (today), and proof structure (Day 6). Students who can complete it independently are demonstrating comprehensive mastery of the coordinate geometry unit.

Day 12 — Synthesis Proportional reasoning appears in coordinate proofs (similar triangles), mixed measurement problems (scale factor and area), and applications (shadow problems, map scales). Today's skills are directly tested in Day 12.
All Future Tests Proportional reasoning (SAT, ACT, state exams) appears in every math assessment. Cross-multiplication, scale factor, and similar figures are among the most commonly tested geometry skills. The fluency built today has immediate, measurable payoff.
Trigonometry (Later) Trigonometry is built on similar triangles: the sine, cosine, and tangent ratios are constant for any two similar right triangles sharing the same angle. Students who deeply understand why similar triangles have proportional sides have the conceptual foundation for trig.
