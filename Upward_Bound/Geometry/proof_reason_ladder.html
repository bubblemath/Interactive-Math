Proof fluency · name the reason for every step

Every step needs a reason. Know which one.

A proof is only as strong as its justifications. This ladder builds the reason vocabulary from algebra's properties of equality up through geometry's definitions, postulates, and theorems — then puts it to work filling proofs and catching flawed logic. The skill is naming the why.

Why naming the reason matters

The reason is the proof

A claim without a reason isn't proof

In a two-column proof the right-hand column is the whole point. A correct statement with no justification proves nothing — naming the why is the work.

The Because Test catches bluffing

"They look congruent" fails every time. A valid reason names a property, definition, or postulate — appearance is never allowed, and learning to feel that is half of geometry.

The discipline transfers

Justifying each step is the same logic behind every argument you'll ever make — and the digital SAT quietly rewards the student who can spot the unjustified leap.

Algebra and geometry use the very same backbone: a chain of statements, each one earned by a stated rule. Build the reasons by name on the early rungs and the geometry proofs at the top stop feeling like a different subject — they're the same moves, wearing definitions and postulates instead of properties of equality.
