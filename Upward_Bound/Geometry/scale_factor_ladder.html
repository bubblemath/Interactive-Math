One scale factor. Three powers. Don't mix them up.

Similar figures are run by a single number — the scale factor k. Lengths scale by k, area by k², volume by k³. This ladder builds that from solving a proportion up to choosing the right power under pressure. No calculator: finding k and picking the power is the whole skill.

Why working by hand wins

The calculator can't pick the power

k, k², k³ — one per dimension

Length scales by k, area by k², volume by k³. The pattern is the whole subject — memorizing it as a calculator routine misses the idea entirely.

Find k first

Once you have the scale factor, every length, perimeter, area, and volume follows from it. A calculator can find a quotient, but it can't decide what to divide.

Corresponding parts, in order

Similar-figure proportions only work if matching sides line up the same way, top and bottom. That ordering is reasoning, not arithmetic.

Similarity is proportional reasoning, and the digital SAT leans on it hard — from map scales to "if the radius triples, what happens to the area?" A student who feels that area grows faster than length, and volume faster still, catches the trap before it costs a point. That instinct is built by hand, one power at a time.
