⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

Day 11 is the "confidence deposit" lesson of the course. The lesson itself says it explicitly: transformations are the opening unit of most 9th-grade geometry courses, and meeting them here first removes the "I've never seen this" panic that can derail a student in September. Frame the entire lesson around this gift: students are getting a preview of the material their classmates will encounter for the first time next year. This framing makes even the harder rules (rotation, y = x reflection, composition) feel like an advantage rather than a burden.

The unifying concept for the lesson is "coordinate commands." Every transformation is a rule that takes an input (x, y) and produces an output (x′, y′). Students who internalize this framing — transformation = function — will be prepared for function notation in Algebra 2, transformation of parent functions in Precalculus, and matrix representations in linear algebra. The coordinate rule table in the Rule Book section is the single most important reference to build and keep visible all lesson.

The discovery-first principle applies most powerfully to reflections and rotations. For reflections, students should manipulate the GeoGebra applet across the x-axis and y-axis and state the coordinate rule themselves before it's written. For rotations, the 90° CCW rule is the hardest to derive — use the physical gesture (rotate the coordinate axes 90°) or the "lie on your side" trick before showing the algebraic rule. Students who derive the rule remember it; students who copy it rarely do.

The composition section is the most conceptually demanding content of the lesson — and also the most fun to teach if you use the "different recipes" analogy. The two key insights are: (1) order matters, and (2) some compositions have shortcuts (reflect over x-axis then y-axis = 180° rotation). Both of these are highly tested on assessments and deeply satisfying to discover. Budget at least 12 minutes for composition.

Dilation connects directly to Day 10. The scale factor in dilation is the same k from similarity. Students who made that connection on Day 10 will feel the payoff today — dilation is what creates similar figures in the first place. Make this connection explicit: "Yesterday's scale factor is today's dilation rule."

Learning Objectives
  • Apply the translation rule (x + a, y + b) to move any point or figure and describe the direction and distance of the slide
  • Apply all four reflection rules (x-axis, y-axis, y = x, y = −x) to any point and explain WHY each coordinate changes
  • Apply all three rotation rules (90° CCW, 180°, 270° CCW) about the origin and identify the pattern in how coordinates transform
  • Apply the dilation rule (kx, ky) and connect k directly to Day 10's scale factor and similarity concepts
  • Distinguish rigid motions (translation, reflection, rotation) from non-rigid (dilation) and explain what is preserved in each
  • Apply a composition of two transformations in the correct sequential order
  • Demonstrate that changing the order of a composition produces a different result (non-commutativity)
  • Recognize that reflecting across x-axis then y-axis is equivalent to a 180° rotation
  • Name the transformation type from a description and write the coordinate rule from memory

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The overview framing is worth 3 minutes at the start. "Transformations are the opening unit of 9th-grade geometry — you are getting this preview first" is a confidence-building message that changes the emotional temperature of the room. Students who feel advantaged learn faster. Do not skip this framing even if time feels tight.
  • Translation is the easiest transformation — cap it at 6 minutes. Students intuit "slide" immediately. The only error to watch for is sign confusion: "translate left 3" means x − 3, not x + 3. One focused example and a 90-second check is sufficient. Do not spend 12 minutes on translation while leaving composition underserved.
  • Reflection rules require GeoGebra before the table. Students who see the applet before the rule will self-correct errors on the y = x and y = −x cases. Students who only see the table will confuse them. Invest 4–5 minutes in the reflection applet for at least x-axis and y-axis before displaying the full rule table.
  • Rotation rules are the hardest to memorize without a mnemonic. The 90° CCW rule (x, y) → (−y, x) is non-intuitive. Use the physical "lie on your side" demonstration: if you rotate the coordinate axes 90° counterclockwise, x becomes the new positive y direction and y becomes the new negative x direction. Students who do this physical gesture once remember the rule. Students who copy it from the board typically lose it by tomorrow.
  • The 180° rotation vs. reflection misconception must be addressed explicitly. Many students will correctly compute a 180° rotation and a reflection over the origin and see the same coordinates — but they are different transformations. The difference is orientation: rotation preserves vertex order (counterclockwise remains counterclockwise), while reflection reverses it. Name this explicitly during the rotation section, and return to it during composition when the double-reflection shortcut appears.
  • Composition is the highest-value section for assessment readiness. Both the SAT and state geometry exams include composition problems. The "order matters" demonstration with Sequence A and B from the lesson website is essential — run it live on the projector as a class rather than as individual exploration. Students who witness both sequences compute to different answers have an indelible memory of why order matters.
Coordinate Rules — Quick Reference for Teaching
➡️ Translation
(x, y) → (x+a, y+b)
+a = right, −a = left. +b = up, −b = down. Shape slides without rotating or flipping. All distances and angles preserved.
🪞 Reflections
4 lines: axis, y=x, y=−x
x-axis: (x,−y) · y-axis: (−x,y) · y=x: (y,x) · y=−x: (−y,−x). Orientation reverses. Distances preserved.
🔄 Rotations (CCW)
90°,180°,270° origin
90°: (−y,x) · 180°: (−x,−y) · 270°: (y,−x). Orientation preserved (unlike reflection). Distances preserved.
🔎 Dilation
(x, y) → (kx, ky)
Only transformation that changes SIZE. k > 1: enlargement. 0 < k < 1: reduction. k = Day 10's scale factor. Creates similar figures.

Section 1 — Translation: The Slide (6 min)
Translation Coordinate Command: (x, y) → (x + a, y + b) 6 min
★ Discovery: Physical Intuition Before the Rule

Before showing any notation, ask: "If I want to move a shape 3 units right and 2 units up, what happens to the x-coordinate of every point? What happens to the y-coordinate?" Students can answer this without any formula — moving right adds to x, moving up adds to y. The formula is just naming what they already know.

Then ask: "What if I want to move left? Down?" Students will say subtract. The formula (x + a, y + b) uses positive a for right and positive b for up — and negative values for left and down. This is the only conceptual point translation requires.

GeoGebra Translation Explorer — geogebra.org/m/BDzKR5st (2 min)
Drag the translation vector and ask students to predict what happens to the coordinates before reading them. Key observation: every point moves the SAME amount in the SAME direction — the shape doesn't change, only its position.
The sign error — address proactively
Translate A(3, −2) left 4, up 5: → (3 − 4, −2 + 5) = (−1, 3)
Common error: "left 4" written as x + 4 instead of x − 4.
Memory check: "If right is +, what is left? If up is +, what is down?" Students should answer from the number line, not from memory of the formula.
Discussion questions
  • What stays the same?
    "After a translation, what properties of the original figure are preserved? What, if anything, changes?"
    Size, shape, angle measures, side lengths, and orientation (the clockwise/counterclockwise order of vertices) are all preserved. Only position changes. This is why translation is a "rigid motion" — the figure is moved but not distorted. The image is congruent to the pre-image.
Section 2 — Reflection: The Flip (15 min)
Reflection Four Reflection Lines + Mirror Mnemonic + Discovery via Applet 15 min
★ Discovery Move: Applet First, Rules Second (5 min)

Open the Reflection Explorer (geogebra.org/m/cFqBxS69 — Coordinate Reflections tab, Mzfu57s8) before writing any rules. Ask: "I'm going to reflect this point over the x-axis. Look at the coordinates before and after. What changed? What stayed the same?" Students will observe: y changed to −y, x stayed the same. Write it together.

Repeat for y-axis: x changed to −x, y stayed the same. Then ask students to predict: "What do you think happens to the y = x reflection?" Some will say "both change" — let them test it. The swap (y, x) surprises many students and produces a memorable "aha" moment. The y = −x rule (swap AND negate) is the hardest — present it last, after students have the pattern for the others.

The mirror mnemonic — for axis reflections
For x-axis and y-axis reflections: "The coordinate ON the mirror line stays the same. The coordinate measuring DISTANCE from the mirror line flips sign." For x-axis reflection, the x-coordinate tells you where along the x-axis you are (on the mirror — stays same). The y-coordinate tells you how far above or below (distance from mirror — flips). This physical reasoning works for any axis reflection and generalizes to reflecting over y = k or x = k lines later in the year.
The swap insight for y = x — say this "The line y = x is the diagonal that says 'wherever x is, y has the same value.' When you reflect over it, the x and y coordinates literally trade places. A point that was 3 units from the y-axis and 5 units from the x-axis becomes 5 units from the y-axis and 3 units from the x-axis. They swap. This is why the rule is (y, x) — not (−x, −y), just a swap."
All four rules with worked examples
Reflect P(4, −3) over each line:
x-axis: (4, −(−3)) = (4, 3) — y flips
y-axis: (−4, −3) — x flips
y = x: (−3, 4) — swap x and y
y = −x: (−(−3), −4) = (3, −4) — swap AND negate both

Note: Verify y = x by checking: is the image (−3, 4) on the line y = x? No — y ≠ x. But the image is the reflection of (4, −3) in the sense that the midpoint of P and P′ lies on y = x: midpoint = ((4+(−3))/2, (−3+4)/2) = (1/2, 1/2). This IS on y = x. ✓
The y = −x rule — most common error Students frequently write (−y, −x) when they should write (−y, −x) for y = −x — wait, that IS the rule — but they confuse it with the y = x rule (y, x). The key difference: y = x just swaps. y = −x swaps AND negates. If a student asks "how do I remember which is which?", say: "The extra negative in y = −x gives you an extra negation in the rule. y = x (no negatives) → (y, x) (no negations). y = −x (one negative) → (−y, −x) (negate both after swapping)."
Discussion questions
  • Orientation reversal
    "After you reflect a triangle, does the clockwise order of the vertices stay the same or reverse? How is this different from translation?"
    Reflection reverses orientation — if vertices A, B, C are listed counterclockwise in the original, they will be listed clockwise in the reflection (or vice versa). Translation preserves orientation. This is why reflected text is "mirror writing" — the left-right order reverses. In transformations, we say reflection changes "handedness." This is what makes reflection fundamentally different from translation and rotation (both of which are orientation-preserving).
  • Real-world connection
    "Can you think of a real object that is related to its mirror image by a reflection? Are those two objects congruent? Are they the same?"
    A left hand and a right hand are mirror images (reflections of each other). They are congruent (same size and shape) but NOT the same — you cannot superimpose one on the other without lifting it out of the plane. This is the mathematical reason why left-handed and right-handed gloves exist: you can't turn a left glove into a right glove by sliding or rotating it. In 2D, a reflected figure is congruent to the original but not superimposable without flipping out of the plane.
Section 3 — Rotation: The Turn (14 min)
Rotation Three Rules + Physical Gesture + 180° ≠ Reflection Distinction 14 min
★ Discovery Move: GeoGebra Rotation + Physical Gesture (5 min)

Open the Rotation Explorer (geogebra.org/m/FrsJMaqe) and rotate a triangle 90° CCW. Ask: "Look at the coordinates before and after. What pattern do you see?" Students will observe the sign change and the swap, but may not articulate the exact rule. After 60–90 seconds of observation, introduce the physical gesture.

The "lie on your side" gesture (for 90° CCW): Tilt your head 90° to the left and look at the coordinate axes. What was pointing up (positive y) now points to your right (positive x direction from your perspective). What was pointing right (positive x) now points down (negative y from your perspective). So: old y becomes new x (with same sign), and old x becomes new negative y. Rule: (x, y) → (−y, x). Have every student do this gesture once.

Three rotation rules — present in order of difficulty
90° CCW: (x, y) → (−y, x) — Swap, negate the NEW first coordinate
Physical check: Rotate A(3, 2) → (−2, 3). Does this make sense? Original point is in Quadrant I. After 90° CCW, it should be in Quadrant II (x negative, y positive). ✓

180°: (x, y) → (−x, −y) — Both signs flip. Easier to remember: "double negate."
Note: Same as reflecting over the ORIGIN (not an axis).

270° CCW: (x, y) → (y, −x) — Swap, negate the NEW second coordinate
Shortcut: 270° CCW = 90° clockwise. Some students remember "clockwise" more easily.
The "swap which one?" confusion for 90° and 270° The most common rotation error: students write (y, −x) for 90° CCW instead of (−y, x). The difference is which term gets negated. Use the quadrant check: "Take any Quadrant I point. After 90° CCW, it should land in Quadrant II (x negative, y positive). If your rule gives x negative and y positive, you're right." This quadrant reasoning is a fast, reliable error-check that students can apply independently.
The 180° ≠ reflection misconception — address explicitly
Point A(3, 4):
180° rotation: (−3, −4) — origin is the center point
Reflection over x-axis: (3, −4) — different!
Reflection over y-axis: (−3, 4) — different!
Reflection over origin: (−3, −4) — same coordinates, but different transformation type!

The coordinates happen to match for a point, but the transformation is classified differently. Rotation (CCW) preserves the counterclockwise vertex order. Reflection reverses it. For an individual point this distinction doesn't matter; for a figure with vertex order, it does.
Discussion questions
  • Rotation pattern recognition
    "A point P(2, 5) is rotated 90° CCW to get P′. Then P′ is rotated 90° CCW to get P″. Then P″ is rotated 90° CCW to get P‴. What is P‴? What will happen if we rotate P‴ by 90° CCW one more time?"
    P(2, 5) → 90° → P′(−5, 2) → 90° → P″(−2, −5) → 90° → P‴(5, −2) → 90° → (2, 5) = P again! Four 90° CCW rotations return to the original point. This is the cyclic nature of rotation: 4 × 90° = 360° = full turn. Students who notice this pattern have discovered the "order 4" symmetry of 90° rotations — a preview of group theory.
  • Connecting to Day 8
    "On Day 8, we said perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals — if one slope is m, the other is −1/m. How does a 90° rotation connect to this?"
    A 90° rotation maps the vector (a, b) (the "slope vector" along a line) to (−b, a). The slope of the original is b/a; the slope of the rotated vector is a/(−b) = −1/(b/a) = the negative reciprocal. So the perpendicular slope rule from Day 8 is literally the 90° rotation rule applied to direction vectors. This is a beautiful cross-day connection that shows why the perpendicular relationship holds.
Section 4 — Dilation: The Zoom (12 min)
Dilation Scale Factor + GeoGebra + Day 10 Connection + Finding k 12 min
★ Discovery Move: Slider Before Definition (3 min)

Open the Dilation Explorer (geogebra.org/m/drCj7kxE) and drag the scale factor slider without explaining anything. Ask: "What happens to the shape when k > 1? When 0 < k < 1? When k = 1? Describe what you see before I give you any vocabulary." Students will describe enlargement, reduction, and no change. Then name what they described: "In geometry, those three situations have specific names."

Then connect directly to Day 10: "Yesterday you worked with scale factor k in similar figures. When you dilate a figure by k, you CREATE a similar figure. Dilation is the transformation that MAKES similarity happen. Scale factor from yesterday is literally the same k in today's dilation rule."

The "only non-rigid motion" distinction — make this explicit
The three earlier transformations (translation, reflection, rotation) are all RIGID MOTIONS — they preserve size and shape. The figure after the transformation is congruent to the original. Dilation is NOT a rigid motion — it changes size. The image is SIMILAR to the original (same shape, different size), but not congruent (unless k = 1). This distinction is the most important conceptual point of the dilation section.
Three worked examples — include the reverse (finding k)
Example 1 — Enlargement: Dilate A(2, −4) by k = 3 → A′(6, −12)
Example 2 — Reduction: Dilate B(8, −6) by k = ½ → B′(4, −3)
Example 3 — Find k: C(3, 5) → C′(9, 15). k = 9/3 = 3 (or k = 15/5 = 3) — verify both coordinates give the same k!

Key habit for Example 3: always verify k is consistent for BOTH coordinates. If x gives k = 3 but y gives k = 2, something is wrong with the given information or the calculation.
The negative scale factor case — skip or address briefly The lesson does not cover negative scale factors (which reflect AND dilate), and this is appropriate for 8th–9th grade. If a student asks, say: "Negative k creates a reflection as well as a scale change — we'll see that in later courses. For today, all our scale factors are positive."
Discussion questions
  • The area connection — Day 9 revisited
    "If you dilate a square with area 16 by k = 3, what is the area of the dilated square? Why?"
    Original square has area 16, so side = 4. After dilation by k = 3, side = 12. New area = 144 = 16 × 9 = 16 × k². This is the k² area scaling rule from Day 9: linear scale factor k → area scale factor k². Dilation makes this concrete: when you dilate by k, every side multiplies by k, so area multiplies by k². Students who connect this to Day 9 have a cross-lesson integration that will serve them throughout the course.
  • Connecting to Day 10
    "Yesterday you proved triangles were similar using AA, SAS, or SSS. Can every similar figure be produced by a dilation? Does dilation always produce a similar figure?"
    Yes — dilation of any figure always produces a similar figure (angles preserved, sides scaled by k). And any two similar figures are related by a dilation (possibly combined with a rigid motion). This means "similar figures" and "figures related by dilation (and rigid motions)" are equivalent descriptions. Dilation is the geometric mechanism behind similarity — this is why Days 10 and 11 are designed to follow each other.
Section 5 — Composition: Order Matters! (15 min)
Composition Sequence A vs B + "Different Recipes" + Shortcuts 15 min
★ Discovery: Sequence A vs B — Run Live on Projector

Before any explanation, write point (2, 1) on the board. Say: "I'm going to apply two transformations. The transformations are: (1) translate right 3, and (2) reflect over the y-axis. But I'm not going to tell you the order yet." Ask students to predict: "Does the order matter, or will we get the same answer either way?"

Then compute both sequences live. Sequence A (translate then reflect): (2,1) → (5,1) → (−5,1). Sequence B (reflect then translate): (2,1) → (−2,1) → (1,1). Different answers. Ask: "Why did we get different results? The transformations were the same." Let students articulate the reason before you explain it: each transformation acts on the OUTPUT of the previous one, so what happens first changes what the second transformation has to work with.

The "recipe" analogy — establish before more examples
Say this "Composition is like following a recipe. 'Bake then frost' gives you frosted cake. 'Frost then bake' gives you… a mess. Same ingredients, different order, different result. When we compose transformations, we always apply them left to right — or in the order listed. The notation T₂ ∘ T₁ means 'apply T₁ first, then T₂' — right to left in the notation, left to right in execution. This trips up many students. When in doubt: apply the transformation listed SECOND first? No — apply the one listed FIRST in the English sentence."
Three composition examples — escalating complexity
Example 1: Reflect over x-axis, then translate (x+1, y+2)
(2, 3) → reflect x-axis → (2, −3) → translate → (3, −1). Final: (3, −1)

Example 2: Rotate 90° CCW, then dilate by k = 2
(4, 0) → rotate 90° CCW → (−0, 4) = (0, 4) → dilate k=2 → (0, 8). Final: (0, 8)

Example 3 (The Shortcut): Reflect over x-axis, then reflect over y-axis
(3, −2) → reflect x-axis → (3, 2) → reflect y-axis → (−3, 2). Final: (−3, 2)
Compare to 180° rotation: (3, −2) → (−3, 2). Same result!
Shortcut: Reflect over x-axis THEN y-axis = 180° rotation
The double reflection shortcut — teach as a discovery, not a rule Do not announce the shortcut before students compute Example 3. Let them calculate the double reflection, then compute the 180° rotation, then notice they're equal. The moment of noticing is pedagogically far more valuable than being told the shortcut. Students who discover it themselves will use it on assessments; students who are told it frequently forget it under pressure.
Discussion questions
  • Why order matters — the algebra analogy
    "In algebra, does the order of operations matter? Does (3 + 4) × 2 give the same result as 3 + (4 × 2)? How is this similar to composition of transformations?"
    Yes — order of operations gives different results for (3+4)×2 = 14 vs 3+(4×2) = 11. Transformation composition is the same: each step takes the output of the previous step as its input, so changing the order changes the intermediate results and therefore the final output. In abstract algebra, this property is called "non-commutativity" — transformations (like most operations) don't generally commute. Only very special operations (like adding real numbers) always commute.
  • Other double-transformation shortcuts
    "We found that reflecting twice (x-axis then y-axis) equals a 180° rotation. What do you think happens if you rotate 90° CCW four times? If you apply any translation twice?"
    Four 90° CCW rotations = 360° = identity (back to original position). Two applications of any translation (a, b) = translation (2a, 2b) — they simply add. These are examples of transformation groups in abstract algebra. The set of all rotations about the origin forms a group; the set of all translations forms a group. Composition is the group operation.

If There Is Extra Time — Partner, Small Group & Extension Activities
Activities marked ★ Core are high-value for any students with extra time. Activities marked Extension are for students fully fluent on all five transformation types including composition.
🗺️ Coordinate Command Relay ★ Core · Groups of 4
⏱ 8–10 min  ·  After all four transformation types
Groups of 4 run a relay: Student 1 applies a translation, Student 2 applies a reflection, Student 3 applies a rotation, Student 4 applies a dilation — all to the same starting point, in that order. Each student must write the coordinate rule they used before passing to the next person.
  • Starting point: A(4, 2)
  • Student 1: Translate left 6, up 3 → (−2, 5)
  • Student 2: Reflect over y-axis → (2, 5)
  • Student 3: Rotate 90° CCW → (−5, 2)
  • Student 4: Dilate by k = 3 → (−15, 6)
  • All four rule sheets collected — teacher verifies the chain
Requiring the written rule before passing prevents the most common relay error: applying the right numbers to the wrong coordinates. Groups that get a wrong final answer can trace back through the chain to find the error.
🪞 Reflection Art ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 10 min  ·  After Reflection section
Pairs create a simple polygon (4–6 vertices) on a coordinate grid, then reflect it over all four lines (x-axis, y-axis, y = x, y = −x) in sequence. They label all five figures (original + 4 reflections) and check that each image is congruent to the original using the distance formula or by inspection.
  • Starting polygon must have vertices in Quadrant I (so reflections land in all four quadrants)
  • After plotting all five figures: "Which pairs of images are related by a rotation?"
  • Answer: reflecting over x-axis then y-axis = 180° rotation. Reflecting over y = x then y = −x = 180° rotation. Pairs discover multiple routes to the same transformation.
The "which pairs are rotations?" question turns a drawing activity into a discovery activity. Pairs who find all the rotation relationships have constructed the full dihedral group D₄ — without knowing it yet.
🔄 Rotation Pattern Hunt ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8 min  ·  After Rotation section
Pairs receive four coordinate rules on cards and must sort them into three piles: rotation, reflection, or "both work" (a rule that describes a valid rotation AND a valid reflection). Then they compute what each rule does to a test point (3, 4).
  • Rule A: (x, y) → (−x, −y) — is this rotation or reflection? (Both! 180° rotation = reflection over origin)
  • Rule B: (x, y) → (y, x) — is this rotation or reflection? (Reflection over y = x)
  • Rule C: (x, y) → (−y, x) — is this rotation or reflection? (Rotation 90° CCW only)
  • Rule D: (x, y) → (x, −y) — is this rotation or reflection? (Reflection over x-axis only)
Rule A's ambiguity (180° rotation and reflection over origin give the same coordinates for any point) is the hardest conceptual distinction. Pairs who identify this have genuinely understood the orientation-preservation distinction between rotation and reflection.
🧩 Composition Detective ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 10 min  ·  After Composition section
Pairs receive a "before" point and an "after" point and must figure out which composition of two transformations (from a given set) produced the result. Multiple valid answers may exist — that's part of the challenge.
  • Before: (2, 3) After: (−6, −3). What composition could produce this? (Translation right 4 then reflect y-axis? 180° rotation then translate right 8? Multiple solutions exist.)
  • Pairs must find at least 2 different valid two-step compositions that take (2,3) to (−6, −3)
  • Extension: find a SINGLE transformation (not a composition) that also maps (2,3) to (−6,−3)
This reverse-engineering format is significantly harder than forward computation — and much more like real problem-solving. Pairs who find multiple valid compositions have discovered that many different paths can lead to the same transformation outcome.

⏱ 12 min  ·  Strong finishers only
Pairs prove that reflecting a triangle across the x-axis and then the y-axis produces the same image as a 180° rotation — using general coordinates rather than a specific example. They work with triangle ABC where A = (a, b), B = (c, d), C = (e, f) and show both transformations produce the same result.
  • Double reflection: A(a,b) → reflect x → (a,−b) → reflect y → (−a,−b). Same for B and C.
  • 180° rotation: A(a,b) → (−a,−b). Same for B and C.
  • Both produce △A′B′C′ with the same coordinates → they are the same transformation. This IS a proof.
  • Extension: prove that reflecting across y=x then y=−x also equals 180° rotation
This is the first genuine algebraic proof involving transformations that most 8th–9th graders encounter. Students who complete it have connected Day 6's proof structure to today's transformation content — the most complete cross-day integration of the course.

How today's transformations open the 9th-grade geometry course
Day 12 — Synthesis Transformation proofs (show two figures are congruent via rigid motions, or similar via dilation) are part of the Day 12 capstone. Students who own the coordinate rules from today can complete these proofs efficiently.
Full Geometry Course Rigid motions (translation, reflection, rotation) are used to define congruence in modern geometry curricula. Two figures are congruent if and only if one can be mapped to the other by a sequence of rigid motions. This is the foundation of all congruence proofs in the full course.
Algebra 2 / Precalc Transformation of functions (f(x) → f(x + a), f(x) → −f(x), f(x) → k·f(x)) is the direct algebraic analog of today's coordinate transformations. Students who understand translation as (x + a) and vertical flip as negation will find function transformation immediately recognizable.
Linear Algebra / CS Composition of transformations is matrix multiplication. Every coordinate rule from today corresponds to a 2×2 matrix (rotation matrix, reflection matrix, dilation matrix). Composition = matrix product. Computer graphics, robotics, and animation all use this mathematical framework daily.
