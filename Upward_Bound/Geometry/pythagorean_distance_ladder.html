Right-triangle fluency · add or subtract, by hand

Same three numbers. One decision changes the answer.

Every Pythagorean problem hinges on one call: is the missing side the hypotenuse (add the squares) or a leg (subtract)? This ladder drills that decision — plus the triples, radicals, and distance work that make it fast — all the way up to a full mix. No calculator: the judgment is the skill, not the keypad.

Why working by hand wins

The calculator skips the one decision that matters

Add vs. subtract is a judgment

Hypotenuse missing → add the squares. Leg missing → subtract. Same three numbers, opposite operation — a calculator can't make the call, and on a test it can't catch a wrong one either.

Triples beat arithmetic

Seeing 9, 12, ? and knowing 15 on sight is faster than any keypad. The calculator makes you forget the pattern that wins time on the digital SAT.

Simplifying is factor-spotting

Turning √50 into 5√2 is the same eye that factors an expression. The decimal 7.07 hides every factor and the skill with it.

A built-in sanity check rides along the whole way: the hypotenuse is always the longest side, and a leg is always shorter. A student who feels that will catch a wrong answer a calculator would have rubber-stamped — and that instinct is yours only if you build it by hand.
