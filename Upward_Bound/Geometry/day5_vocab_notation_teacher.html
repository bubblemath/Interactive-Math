⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

Day 5 is a vocabulary-intensive lesson, but it should not feel like a vocabulary quiz. Every definition introduced today is one a student will need to cite as a reason in a proof starting Day 6. The goal is not that students can recite definitions — it is that they recognize which definition applies when they see a diagram, and that they understand why notation distinctions matter.

The three deepest conceptual moves in this lesson are: (1) the congruence-vs-equality distinction (the most cited notation error in all of high school geometry), (2) the "midpoint and bisector are commands to write equations" framing (which transforms abstract definitions into algebraic tools), and (3) the proof-preview matching game (which shows students that the vocabulary they just learned IS the proof vocabulary — the language of justification, not just description).

The discovery-first approach applies differently here than in Days 3–4. Rather than discovering a theorem, students are discovering why distinctions matter — through the "Can You Measure It?" rule, the ray directionality demonstration, and the applet explorations where they drag and observe before receiving a formal definition. Ask "what did you notice?" before you say what the definition is.

Pacing note: This lesson has more sections than any other in the course. The undefined terms section should move quickly (5 minutes). The notation decoder needs 10–12 minutes because the symbol distinctions must be practiced, not just read. The congruence vs. equality section should include the sorting challenge before you present the pitfalls table. The addition postulates, midpoint, and bisector sections each need 10 minutes. The proof preview is 7 minutes and is critically important — do not skip it as "extra."

Learning Objectives
  • Identify and name points, lines, line segments, rays, and planes using correct notation
  • Distinguish between \(\overline{AB}\) (segment as object) and \(AB\) (length as number)
  • Explain why \(\overrightarrow{AB} \neq \overrightarrow{BA}\) but \(\overleftrightarrow{AB} = \overleftrightarrow{BA}\)
  • Apply the "Can You Measure It?" rule to choose ≅ vs = correctly
  • Apply the Segment Addition Postulate and Angle Addition Postulate to set up equations
  • Define midpoint and angle bisector and immediately write the equation each implies
  • Match proof statements to the correct reason (Definition of Midpoint, Definition of Angle Bisector, Segment Addition Postulate, Angle Addition Postulate)

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • This lesson has more sections than any other day. Keep the undefined terms section to 5 minutes — it is conceptually important but not algebraically complex. The payoff in this lesson is at the midpoint/bisector and proof preview sections, and you must budget time to reach them.
  • The congruence vs. equality sorting challenge must come before the pitfalls table. If you show students the pitfalls table first, the sorting challenge becomes trivial and loses its instructional value. Let students make errors in the sort, then use the pitfalls table to confirm or correct them.
  • Do not rush past ray directionality. The distinction between \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BA}\) confuses students in proofs, coordinate geometry, and the definition of an angle bisector. The flashlight analogy (first letter = where you hold it) is effective and takes 90 seconds — use it.
  • "Midpoint and bisector are commands to write equations" is the key reframe of the lesson. Many students have encountered these words before without knowing what to do with them algebraically. The moment they realize "midpoint → AM = MB → set the expressions equal" is the moment the lesson clicks. Give it time.
  • The Proof Preview matching game is not optional extension content. It is the bridge to Day 6. Students who do the matching game and understand that "Definition of Midpoint" is a proof reason are prepared for Day 6 proofs. Budget 7 minutes for it.
  • GeoGebra applets: use one or two, not all four. The lesson has four GeoGebra applets. In 80 minutes, running all four slows the lesson significantly. Recommend: use the Segment Addition applet during the postulate section and the Midpoint applet during the midpoint section. The Angle Addition and Angle Bisector applets can be assigned as independent exploration.

Section 1 — Undefined Terms & Plane Vocabulary (5 min)
Vocab Points, Lines, Planes + Collinear / Coplanar 5 min
The circular logic demonstration — run this before any definitions (2 min)
Write "Define a point" on the board. Ask for a volunteer definition. Then trace the circularity: point → location → spot → position → point. This is why these terms are undefined — not because geometry is lazy, but because all definitions need to bottom out somewhere. These are the axioms the subject rests on.
Say this "Every dictionary definition uses other words. Eventually you go in circles. In geometry, we stop the circle on purpose — we accept three words without definition: point, line, and plane. Everything else in geometry is defined using these three. Today you'll see how far we can build from just three undefined starting points."
Discussion questions
  • Discovery question
    "Why do we need three non-collinear points to define a plane? What goes wrong if the three points are collinear?"
    Three collinear points lie on a single line — infinitely many planes contain that line, so the three points don't determine a unique plane. You need points that don't all line up: the tripod analogy works here. Two legs of a tripod → infinite planes through the line they make; three non-collinear legs → exactly one plane (the floor).
  • Real-world connection
    "A three-legged stool never wobbles. A four-legged table sometimes does. Why does this connect to today's geometry idea?"
    Three non-collinear points always determine exactly one plane — so three legs always lie flat. Four points may not be coplanar, meaning the fourth leg might not touch the floor.
Section 2 — The Notation Decoder (12 min)
Notation "Can You Measure It?" + Notation Family Tree + Ray Directionality 12 min
★ Discovery Before the Family Tree

Before displaying the notation table, write the following on the board and ask students to decide whether each is a number or an object: AB, ∠A, \(\overline{AB}\), 5 cm, m∠A, △ABC. Let students discuss in pairs for 60 seconds. Then introduce the "Can You Measure It?" rule as the principle that explains the answer to every item.

The rule: if you can put a ruler or protractor on it and get a number, it's a number (use =). If you're referring to the geometric object itself, it's an object (use ≅). This rule resolves every congruence-vs-equality question they will ever encounter.

Notation family tree — build with students, not at them
SymbolNameObject or Number?Use withKey feature
\(\overline{AB}\)SegmentObject (the physical piece)≅Has two endpoints; finite length
\(AB\)Length / DistanceNumber (a measurement)=Can equal 5 cm; can be in equations
\(\overrightarrow{AB}\)RayObject≅Starts at A, goes through B forever; direction matters
\(\overleftrightarrow{AB}\)LineObject≅Extends infinitely both ways; direction doesn't matter
★ Ray directionality — the flashlight demo (2 min)
Hold up an imaginary flashlight. "The first letter of a ray is where you hold the flashlight — that's the endpoint. The second letter is where you point it. \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) starts at A pointing toward B. \(\overrightarrow{BA}\) starts at B pointing toward A. These go in completely opposite directions." Draw both on the board and ask: "Are they the same ray?" (No.) "Is there any point that is on both rays but not at the endpoints?" (Only A and B themselves — and only if B is between A and wherever the ray ends.)
Why this distinction matters later The definition of an angle bisector uses ray notation: \(\overrightarrow{BD}\) bisects ∠ABC. If students confuse ray direction, they cannot correctly write the bisector definition or interpret angle diagrams in proofs. Spend 2 minutes here — it prevents a persistent error.
Whiteboard notation drill (3 min)
Rapid-fire whole class: teacher says a description, students write the correct symbol on mini-whiteboards. Do at least 8 rounds before moving to congruence vs. equality.
"The segment from P to Q" → \(\overline{PQ}\)  ·  "The length of PQ" → \(PQ\)  ·  "A ray starting at M going through N" → \(\overrightarrow{MN}\)  ·  "A line through A and B" → \(\overleftrightarrow{AB}\)  ·  "The measure of angle X" → \(m\angle X\)  ·  "The angle at vertex X" → \(\angle X\)  ·  "The ray starting at N going through M" → \(\overrightarrow{NM}\) (different from \(\overrightarrow{MN}\)!)
Discussion questions
  • Conceptual
    "A line is named \(\overleftrightarrow{AB}\) and also \(\overleftrightarrow{BA}\) — they're the same line. But \(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BA}\) are different rays. What is it about a line that makes order not matter, but a ray's order does matter?"
    A line has no starting point — it goes forever in both directions, so neither letter is more "first" than the other. A ray has a specific starting point (the endpoint), which gives it a direction. Changing which letter is first changes which end of the underlying line the ray starts from.
  • Application to angles
    "An angle is formed by two rays. When we write ∠ABC, what are the two rays that form it? Which letter names the vertex?"
    The two rays are \(\overrightarrow{BA}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BC}\). The middle letter (B) is always the vertex — the shared starting point of both rays. This is why angle names always put the vertex in the middle.
Section 3 — Congruence vs. Equality (10 min)
≅ vs = Object vs. Number Sorter → Pitfalls Table → Practice 10 min
★ Sorter First, Pitfalls Table Second — Do Not Swap This Order

Run the Object vs. Number Sorter from the lesson website as a whole-class activity before displaying the pitfalls table. Students who make errors in the sort and then see the table have a reason to engage with it. Students who see the table first treat the sort as a quiz with the answer key already open.

For the sorter, cold-call on every item that causes disagreement. The items most likely to produce disagreement: m∠A and m∠B (students sometimes treat the "m" as making it an object) and 5 cm and 5 cm (equal numbers — students sometimes say ≅ because they look the same). Both of these produce good discussion.

Discussion questions
  • The "m" question
    "What does the 'm' in \(m\angle A = 45°\) mean? Why do we write 'm' instead of just \(\angle A = 45°\)?"
    The "m" stands for "measure" — it converts the angle (an object) into a number (its degree measure). \(\angle A\) is the angle itself (an object); \(m\angle A\) is its measurement (a number). Writing \(\angle A = 45°\) is technically mixing an object with a number, which is the error in the pitfalls table.
  • Why does this matter in proofs?
    "You're writing a proof and you want to say two segments are the same length. You write '\(\overline{AB} = \overline{CD}\).' Your teacher marks it wrong. Why?"
    Because = is for numbers, and \(\overline{AB}\) is a geometric object (the segment itself), not a number. The correct statement is either \(\overline{AB} \cong \overline{CD}\) (the segments are congruent objects) or \(AB = CD\) (their lengths are equal numbers). In a proof, the distinction matters because each form justifies a different type of step.
  • Precision question
    "Two students write: Student A writes \(AB \cong CD\). Student B writes \(AB = CD\). Which one is correct? Are both wrong? Is one worse than the other?"
    Student A is wrong (AB is a length, a number — use =). Student B is correct. This is a useful calibration: ≅ is wrong for numbers; = is correct. Student A's error is using the object symbol for a number.
Section 4 — Segment & Angle Addition Postulates (15 min)
Postulates GeoGebra Explorations + "Whole = Sum of Parts" + Two Algebraic Examples 15 min
Discovery Before the Postulate Statement

Open geogebra.org/m/K9jNa7A5 (Segment Addition). Drag point B and watch the segment lengths update. Ask "what relationship always holds between AB, BC, and AC?" before displaying the postulate. Students should articulate "they always add up — AB + BC = AC" before you write the formal statement.

For the Angle Addition Postulate, the GeoGebra applet is optional given time constraints. If you skip it, ask students to predict the relationship before showing the example: "If ray BD is inside angle ABC, what equation connects the three angle measures?" Most students will correctly predict the addition relationship from the segment analogy.

The "whole = sum of parts" connection — make this explicit
Both postulates are the same idea applied to different objects: segments and angles. Writing the two postulates side by side reinforces this.
Segment Addition:   AB + BC = AC   (B is between A and C)
Angle Addition:      m∠ABD + m∠DBC = m∠ABC   (ray BD is in the interior of ∠ABC)

Both say: the piece on the left + the piece on the right = the whole thing.
Connection to Day 2 Students used the Segment Addition Postulate informally on Day 2 when they set up equations from diagrams. Today they're giving that process a formal name. Point this out explicitly: "On Day 2 you wrote AB + BC = AC without knowing the name of what you were doing. Now you know the name, and the name will appear as a reason in your Day 6 proofs."
Two examples — gradual release
Segment Addition (teacher-led):
B is between A and C. AB = (2x + 3), BC = (x + 5), AC = 20. Find x.
Equation: (2x + 3) + (x + 5) = 20 → 3x + 8 = 20 → x = 4. ✓ AB = 11, BC = 9, sum = 20.

Angle Addition (student-led after teacher sets up):
Ray BD is in the interior of ∠ABC. m∠ABD = (3x + 10)°, m∠DBC = (2x + 5)°, m∠ABC = 75°. Find x.
Equation: (3x + 10) + (2x + 5) = 75 → 5x + 15 = 75 → x = 12. ✓ Angles: 46° and 29°, sum = 75°.
Discussion questions
  • Connecting to prior knowledge
    "We used the Segment Addition Postulate on Day 2. We used something very similar to it in the Triangle Sum proof on Day 4 (∠1 + ∠A + ∠2 = 180° along a straight line). Where do you think that step connects to today's postulates?"
    The straight-angle step in the Triangle Sum proof (∠1 + ∠A + ∠2 = 180°) is the Angle Addition Postulate applied to a straight angle. A straight angle is 180°, and the three angles together fill it exactly. So that proof step is really an application of the Angle Addition Postulate.
  • Error prevention
    "In the Segment Addition Postulate, we wrote AB + BC = AC using lengths (numbers). What would be wrong with writing \(\overline{AB}\) + \(\overline{BC}\) = \(\overline{AC}\)?"
    You can't add geometric objects — addition only works on numbers. \(\overline{AB}\) + \(\overline{BC}\) is meaningless notation; AB + BC = AC (using lengths) is correct. This is exactly the congruence vs. equality distinction from Section 3 applied in context.
Section 5 — Midpoint & Bisector Lab (18 min)
Midpoint "Equal Splitter" Reframe + Four Examples + GeoGebra Explorations 18 min
★ The Key Reframe — Say This Before Any Definitions

"Today I'm going to teach you two words. These words are not just vocabulary — they are commands. When you see either word in a geometry problem, your brain should immediately start writing an equation. They tell you what to do, not just what to call something."

Then ask students to predict: "If I tell you M is the midpoint of \(\overline{PQ}\), what equation can you write immediately, before I define anything?" Most will correctly predict AM = MB or PM = MQ. This prediction confirms their intuition and frames the formal definition as naming what they already understood.

GeoGebra Midpoint applet — observe before defining (3 min)
Open geogebra.org/m/fcuedpk3. Drag the endpoints. Ask: "What is always true about AM and MB?" Students should observe they are always equal. Then drag the midpoint itself — does it stay equidistant? "What happens to the midpoint if you move an endpoint?" This observation directly motivates the definition: midpoint is the point that keeps the two halves equal.
The two definitions as equations — state these explicitly
Midpoint of \(\overline{PQ}\): M is the midpoint → PM = MQ (set expressions equal, solve for x)

Angle Bisector \(\overrightarrow{BD}\) of ∠ABC: \(\overrightarrow{BD}\) bisects ∠ABC → m∠ABD = m∠DBC (set expressions equal, solve for x)

In both cases: the word tells you two things are equal → your job is to write the equation and solve.
Two examples — teacher-led for midpoint, pairs for bisector
Midpoint (teacher-led):
M is the midpoint of \(\overline{PQ}\). PM = (3x + 2), MQ = (5x − 6). Find x and PQ.
Step 1: Midpoint → equal parts → 3x + 2 = 5x − 6.
Step 2: 8 = 2x → x = 4. PM = 14, MQ = 14. PQ = 28. ✓

Angle Bisector (student pairs):
\(\overrightarrow{BD}\) bisects ∠ABC. m∠ABD = (2x + 15)°, m∠DBC = (3x − 5)°. Find x and m∠ABC.
Step 1: Bisector → equal halves → 2x + 15 = 3x − 5.
Step 2: 20 = x. Each half = 55°. m∠ABC = 110°. ✓
Common midpoint error — verify step is critical Students who find x correctly but forget to then find PQ (or m∠ABC) leave the problem half-done. Make the verify step explicit: substitute x back into both expressions to confirm they're equal, and then find the full segment or angle. The problem asks for PQ, not x — catching this is part of the skill.
Discussion questions
  • Prediction before bisector definition
    "If a ray bisects an angle, what does 'bisect' mean? What equation would you write if you knew one half was (3x + 10)° and the other was (5x − 6)°?"
    Bisect means cut in half equally. Equation: 3x + 10 = 5x − 6. This is exactly the structure of the bisector example — letting students predict the equation before the definition is given makes the definition feel like confirmation.
  • Connecting midpoint and bisector
    "How are the midpoint and angle bisector similar? How are they different? Could you use the same equation setup for both?"
    Both mean "divides into two equal parts" — so the equation setup is the same (set the two expressions equal). The difference: midpoint applies to segments (lengths, numbers), angle bisector applies to angles. But the algebraic process is identical, which makes them easier to learn together.
  • Proof connection — planting the seed for Day 6
    "In a proof, you need a reason for every step. If your proof contains the step 'PM = MQ,' what is the reason? What if it says 'm∠ABD = m∠DBC'?"
    PM = MQ → reason: Definition of Midpoint (M is the midpoint of PQ). m∠ABD = m∠DBC → reason: Definition of Angle Bisector (BD bisects ∠ABC). These two reason-names are exactly what students will write in Day 6 proofs. This question directly previews the Proof Preview section.
Section 6 — Proof Preview (7 min)
Proof Reasoning Match Game + Quick Reference Table + Exit Ticket 7 min
Run the matching game whole-class
Display the four statement-reason pairs from the website one at a time. For each, ask: "What vocabulary word from today does this statement use?" Then ask: "What is the reason?" This is not a trick quiz — by the time students reach this section, they have covered every reason already. The game confirms consolidation.
AM = MB → Definition of Midpoint (M is the midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\))
AB + BC = AC → Segment Addition Postulate (B is between A and C)
m∠ABD = m∠DBC → Definition of Angle Bisector (\(\overrightarrow{BD}\) bisects ∠ABC)
m∠1 + m∠2 = m∠ABC → Angle Addition Postulate (ray BD is interior to ∠ABC)
The key insight — say this explicitly
Say this "Look at what just happened. The four reasons you matched — Definition of Midpoint, Definition of Angle Bisector, Segment Addition Postulate, Angle Addition Postulate — these are the exact words you will write in the 'Reasons' column of a two-column proof on Day 6. You already know the proof language. Day 6 is just learning to organize it into two columns."
Discussion questions
  • Synthesis
    "In a proof, why can't you just say 'because I can see it in the diagram'? What's wrong with that as a reason?"
    Diagrams can be misleading or inaccurate — they might not be drawn to scale, and they show one specific case rather than the general situation the theorem is supposed to cover. A proof must work by logic that doesn't depend on the visual. That's why every step needs a named definition, postulate, or theorem as its justification.
  • Looking forward
    "Looking at the quick reference table, which reason do you think you'll use most often in proofs? Why?"
    No one right answer — but the Segment Addition Postulate and Definition of Midpoint tend to be most frequent in introductory proofs, because many proof problems involve dividing a segment and using its parts. Angle Addition Postulate and Definition of Angle Bisector appear frequently in angle-based proofs.

If There Is Extra Time — Partner, Small Group & Extension Activities
Activities marked ★ Core are high-value for any students with extra time. Activities marked Extension are for students who are fully fluent on today's vocabulary and ready for deeper challenge.
🔤 Notation Speed Round ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 6–8 min  ·  After Notation Decoder
Partners take turns. Student A writes a description in words; Student B writes the correct notation symbol. Then swap. One round = 10 descriptions. Score 1 point per correct notation, including the symbol AND the correct choice of = vs ≅ when an equation is involved.
  • Description set: "the segment PQ," "the length of PQ," "a ray from R through S," "the line through A and B," "the measure of angle T," "the angle at vertex T"
  • Include two deliberate "traps": "the distance from P to Q" (answer: PQ, not \(\overline{PQ}\)) and "angle TUV equals 90°" (ask: is this written correctly?)
  • After 10 rounds, partners compare: which notations were hardest? Why?
The deliberate traps produce the richest discussion — students who correctly flag \(\overline{PQ} = 5\) as wrong (should be PQ = 5) have internalized the congruence-vs-equality distinction deeply.
🔗 Addition Postulate Relay ★ Core · Groups of 3
⏱ 8–10 min  ·  After Addition Postulates
Groups of 3 receive a single two-part problem (one Segment Addition, one Angle Addition). Student 1 identifies which postulate applies and writes the equation. Student 2 solves for x. Student 3 finds the segment or angle lengths and verifies. All work on a mini-whiteboard.
  • Relay problem A: B is between A and C. AB = (5x + 2), BC = (3x − 4), AC = 42. Find x, AB, BC.
  • Relay problem B: Ray BD is inside ∠ABC. m∠ABD = (4x − 5)°, m∠DBC = (2x + 11)°, m∠ABC = 60°. Find x and each angle.
  • After relay: groups swap problems and check each other's work
Require the verify step (Student 3): substitute x back, confirm parts sum to whole. Groups that skip this step should be sent back to check before presenting.
✂️ Midpoint & Bisector Mix ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8 min  ·  After Midpoint & Bisector
Pairs receive a set of 6 problems: 2 midpoint (segment), 2 angle bisector, and 2 where the problem gives them extra information (both a midpoint AND a total length, or both a bisector AND a total angle). For each problem, partners must: (1) write the equation, (2) solve for x, (3) find the full segment or angle.
  • Key skill: Problem 5 gives midpoint M of PQ and PQ = 36. PM = (2x + 4). Find x. (Different setup: use PQ/2 = PM, not set two parts equal.)
  • Key skill: Problem 6 gives bisector BD and m∠ABC = 88°. m∠ABD = (3x + 2)°. Find x. (Use half the total angle.)
  • These two problems preview the "given total, find the part" setup used frequently in proofs.
Problems 5 and 6 represent a harder setup that many students haven't seen yet. Use them as extension within this activity, not as the first problems.
📜 Build-a-Proof Challenge ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 10 min  ·  After Proof Preview
Pairs receive a stripped-down two-column proof with all statements filled in but all reasons blank. They fill in the reasons using only: Given, Segment Addition Postulate, Angle Addition Postulate, Definition of Midpoint, Definition of Angle Bisector, and Substitution. This is a direct preview of Day 6.
  • Proof: Given M is the midpoint of AC, and B is between A and M. Prove AB + BC = AC.
  • Statements: (1) M is midpoint of AC, (2) AM = MC, (3) AB + BM = AM, (4) AB + BM = MC, (5) AB + BM + MC = AC — students supply all five reasons
  • After completing, pairs compare with another pair — any disagreements become class discussion
Students who complete this successfully are genuinely ready for Day 6. Students who struggle usually get stuck on distinguishing Segment Addition (for part+part=whole) from Substitution (for replacing equal quantities). This distinction is worth 3 minutes of debrief.
🌍 Notation Error Hunt Extension · Individual
⏱ 8 min  ·  Early finishers
Students receive a "geometry textbook excerpt" (teacher-written) containing 8 notation errors: uses of = where ≅ is needed, uses of ≅ where = is needed, ray notation with wrong direction, and m∠A used as an object. Students find and correct all 8 errors, explaining each in a sentence.
  • Error 1: "\(\overline{AB} = \overline{CD}\) because they have the same length" → should be ≅
  • Error 2: "\(m\angle A \cong 45°\)" → m∠A is a number, use =
  • Error 3: "\(\overrightarrow{AB}\) and \(\overrightarrow{BA}\) name the same ray" → false; different starting points
  • Error 4: "\(AB \cong CD\) so the segments are equal" → AB is a length (number), use =
  • Errors 5–8: similar precision issues with plane/line/segment confusion
This activity is particularly effective for students who will go on to write proofs — it trains the precise notation habit that separates clean proofs from notation-error-filled ones.
🗺️ Geometry Vocabulary Map Extension · Pairs
⏱ 12 min  ·  Strong finishers
Pairs create a visual "vocabulary map" connecting all terms learned in Days 1–5. Each term is a node; connections are labeled with the relationship (e.g., "parallel lines" → "alternate interior angles" → "equal"). The map should show which terms are prerequisites for which other terms.
  • Suggested starting nodes: point, line, plane, segment, ray, angle, parallel lines, transversal, triangle, midpoint, bisector
  • Challenge: draw at least 15 connections with labeled relationships
  • Extension: mark which nodes are "undefined terms," which are "definitions," and which are "postulates"
The vocabulary map doubles as a study tool for the rest of the course. Students who build it have a clearer sense of geometry as a connected system rather than a list of isolated facts. This is one of the most valuable metacognitive activities in the course.
Day 5 — Connections Forward
How today's vocabulary feeds the rest of the course
Day 6 — Proofs Definition of Midpoint, Definition of Angle Bisector, Segment Addition Postulate, and Angle Addition Postulate are all proof reasons used on Day 6. Students who own these definitions can cite them without looking at a reference.
Days 7–8 — Coordinate Midpoint formula in coordinate geometry is the algebraic version of the Definition of Midpoint. The midpoint of \(\overline{AB}\) with coordinates produces exactly the equal-parts property defined today.
Days 10–11 — Similarity The congruence symbol ≅ appears in every similarity and congruence statement. Students who conflate = and ≅ write incorrect congruence statements throughout the similarity and triangle congruence units.
All Future Proofs Correct notation is not optional in formal geometry. Every proof reason cites a definition, postulate, or theorem by its exact name. Students who skip this today will write proof reasons like "because I can see it" instead of "Definition of Midpoint."
