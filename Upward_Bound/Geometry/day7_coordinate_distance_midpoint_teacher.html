⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

Day 7 has a single most important teaching goal: students must leave understanding that the Distance Formula is not a new formula — it is the Pythagorean Theorem applied to coordinate pairs. Students who make this connection never forget the distance formula, because they already have the Pythagorean Theorem internalized. Students who treat it as a separate formula to memorize will forget it by next week. Every teaching decision today should serve this connection.

The Midpoint Formula is the easier of the two concepts, and its conceptual foundation is even simpler: the midpoint is the average of the coordinates. Students who have encountered averages in statistics (which most 8th–9th graders have) already understand midpoints — they just don't know it yet. The "meet your friend halfway" framing from the lesson is effective precisely because it makes abstract averaging concrete.

The Radical Toolkit section is a prerequisite gate, not a standalone lesson. If students cannot simplify √50 to 5√2, they will produce unsimplified answers in every distance formula problem and spend mental energy on arithmetic instead of geometry. Ten minutes here is worth it. However, do not let this section expand to fill 20 minutes — its purpose is to enable the main lesson, not to become it.

The missing endpoint problem type is the highest-difficulty item in today's lesson and connects back to Day 5's Definition of Midpoint and Day 6's algebraic proof structure. Students who understand "midpoint = average → set up the equation and solve" from prior lessons have an advantage here. Spend time on both the intuitive method (same change from A to M as from M to B) and the algebraic method (set up the equation), because students vary in which approach clicks.

The 3D Sneak Peek is genuine extension content for students who are ahead. It should not be used as core instruction — it is a beautiful example of how the 2D formulas generalize, but introducing it as required content adds cognitive load to a lesson that already covers two significant formulas.

Learning Objectives
  • Derive the Distance Formula by connecting it to the Pythagorean Theorem on a coordinate plane
  • Simplify radical expressions by identifying the largest perfect-square factor
  • Apply the Distance Formula to find the distance between two points, including cases requiring radical simplification
  • Apply the Midpoint Formula by explaining it as the average of the coordinates
  • Find a missing endpoint given one endpoint and the midpoint, using both intuitive and algebraic methods
  • Identify when a distance answer is a Pythagorean triple (integer) vs. when it requires a simplified radical
  • (Extension) Extend the Distance and Midpoint Formulas to three dimensions

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The Pythagorean→Distance connection is the entire lesson in one insight. Do not rush past it. Students who see the right triangle drawn between two coordinate points and recognize a² + b² = c² with legs (x₂−x₁) and (y₂−y₁) have learned the distance formula conceptually. Everything after that is practice and simplification. Spend 5 full minutes on this connection before showing any formula.
  • The Radical Toolkit is a prerequisite gate — cap it at 10 minutes. Check comprehension quickly: √50 and √72 are the two most commonly tested radicals. If most students simplify them correctly in 90 seconds, move on. If half the class is stuck, spend 5 more minutes; if almost everyone is stuck, add a full 10 minutes and compress the practice zone.
  • Do not present both formulas back-to-back as memorization items. The lesson's sequencing is: discover distance from the Pythagorean connection → derive the formula → practice → then introduce midpoint as an averaging concept. Separating the two formulas by concept (derivation vs. averaging) prevents students from conflating them.
  • The GeoGebra Exploration 1 (right triangle forming) is more valuable than Exploration 2. Students who drag points and watch the right triangle form between two coordinate points, then measure the legs and hypotenuse, have a visual proof of the Distance Formula. If time is short, use Exploration 1 and skip Exploration 2.
  • The missing endpoint problem is significantly harder than the standard midpoint problem. Many students set up the equation wrong (they average the two known values instead of solving for the unknown). Budget an extra 3 minutes specifically for this problem type, and use both the intuitive and algebraic methods — different students will click on different approaches.
  • 3D Sneak Peek: skip during core instruction. It takes 5 minutes, adds cognitive load, and is not assessed in this bridge course. Assign it as optional exploration for early finishers or as a homework challenge.
Two Formulas — Quick Reference for Teaching
Distance Formula
d = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]
Origin: Pythagorean Theorem (a² + b² = c²) where a = |x₂−x₁|, b = |y₂−y₁|, c = d.
When answer is integer: the legs form a Pythagorean triple (3-4-5, 5-12-13, etc.).
Simplify: always check if the answer under the radical has a perfect-square factor.
Midpoint Formula
M = ( (x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2 )
Origin: Average of coordinates — the midpoint is the mean of each coordinate pair.
When answer is fraction: leave as fraction; don't convert to decimal unless context requires it.
Missing endpoint: set up the average equation and solve for the unknown, or use the "same change" method.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge: Pythagorean Theorem Review (10 min)
Prereq Four Quick Problems + Integer vs. Radical Answers 10 min
Warm-up protocol — independent, then debrief
Display all four problems. Students work independently for 4 minutes. Then debrief: note which answers are integers (3-4-5: c=5; 5-12-13: c=13) and which are radicals (1-1-√2; 2-4-√20 = 2√5). This distinction previews the radical toolkit and plants the seed that distance formula answers don't always simplify to integers.
a=3, b=4 → c=5 (Pythagorean triple — note this for later!)
a=5, b=12 → c=13 (Pythagorean triple)
a=1, b=1 → c=√2 (cannot simplify further — no perfect square factor)
a=2, b=4 → c=√(4+16)=√20=2√5 (requires simplification!)
The key discussion before the Distance Formula
After the warm-up, ask: "When you found c = √20 for the last problem, you should have simplified it to 2√5. How do you know √20 simplifies to 2√5?" This surfaces students who need the radical toolkit and those who don't — and sets up the Radical Toolkit section as needed content, not optional review.
Discussion questions
  • The unmasking question — plant this before moving on
    "You just used a² + b² = c² to find the length of a side. Now: if I gave you two points on a coordinate plane — say (1, 2) and (4, 6) — how would you find the distance between them? Can you think of a way to make a right triangle with those two points?"
    Draw the two points and sketch horizontal and vertical segments from each to create a right angle. The horizontal leg has length |4−1| = 3, the vertical leg has length |6−2| = 4. The hypotenuse is the distance between the points: c = √(3² + 4²) = √25 = 5. Students who arrive at this before being taught the formula have already derived the Distance Formula for themselves.
  • Pythagorean triples — connect to distance
    "When do you get a nice integer for c in the Pythagorean Theorem? Can you predict which distance formula problems will give integer answers and which will give radicals?"
    Integer answers occur only when the legs form a Pythagorean triple (3-4-5, 5-12-13, 8-15-17, etc.). For the distance formula: integer answers occur when (x₂−x₁) and (y₂−y₁) happen to be legs of a Pythagorean triple. Otherwise, expect a radical. You can't predict from the coordinates alone without computing — but recognizing the triple when it appears is efficient.
Section 2 — Radical Toolkit (10 min)
Radicals Perfect Squares Reference + Step-by-Step Examples + Quick Practice 10 min
★ Discovery Before the Rule

Before presenting the rule √(a·b) = √a · √b, ask: "Is it true that √(25 × 2) = √25 × √2? Try it — compute both sides." Students find that √50 ≈ 7.07 and 5√2 ≈ 5 × 1.414 ≈ 7.07. They're equal. Then ask: "What rule would explain why those are equal?" Students who derive the product rule themselves will remember it; students who just read it will forget it at the worst moment.

The key practical skill: identifying the largest perfect square factor. Students who pick a smaller factor (e.g., simplifying √72 as √(4×18) = 2√18 and stopping there) still have more to simplify. Emphasize: largest perfect square factor. Check: is the remaining radicand still reducible?

The largest perfect square factor — the key habit to build
Walk through √50, √72, and √45 with emphasis on finding the LARGEST factor, not just any perfect square factor.
√72: Mistake path → √(4×18) = 2√18 (still reducible, because 18 = 9×2)
        Correct path → √(36×2) = 6√2 ✓

Self-check: after simplifying, ask "is the number still inside the radical divisible by any perfect square?" If yes, keep simplifying.
The two types of radical error to watch for Error 1: Using a non-largest perfect square factor and not finishing (2√18 instead of 6√2). Error 2: Computing √72 ≈ 8.49 and writing a decimal instead of simplifying. Both errors will appear. For Error 1: say "always check if your remaining radicand has a perfect square factor." For Error 2: remind students that geometry problems usually expect simplified radical form, not decimal approximations — unless the problem says to round.
Quick check — mini-whiteboards
Four rapid-fire problems: √18, √32, √75, √98. Students write answers on whiteboards and hold up simultaneously. Any wrong answers get a 30-second pair correction before moving to the Distance Formula. Stop here; don't do all six practice problems from the website during class — those are for independent practice.
√18 = 3√2 (18 = 9×2)  ·  √32 = 4√2 (32 = 16×2)  ·  √75 = 5√3 (75 = 25×3)  ·  √98 = 7√2 (98 = 49×2)
Discussion questions
  • The "why simplify?" question
    "Why do we simplify radicals? If √72 and 6√2 are equal, why does the form matter?"
    Three reasons: (1) simplified form is conventional — math teachers, textbooks, and exams expect it; (2) simplified form makes comparison easier — you can immediately see that 6√2 and 4√2 both have the same √2 factor, while 6.48 and 5.66 look unrelated; (3) simplified form is exact, while decimal approximations lose precision. In proofs specifically, exact form is required.
  • Real-world connection
    "When would you actually need to know an exact distance rather than a decimal approximation? Why would a GPS or a map use coordinates?"
    Exact form matters in engineering (cutting a piece of material to precisely √50 cm is easier to mark than 7.071... cm), in game design (coordinates define exact positions), and in any geometric proof where the goal is to show two things are equal. GPS and mapping use coordinates because every location on Earth can be uniquely identified by two numbers (latitude and longitude) — exactly the coordinate pair system used in the distance formula today.
Section 3 — Distance Formula: Unmasking the Disguise (18 min)
Distance Pythagorean Connection + GeoGebra Exploration + Two Worked Examples 18 min
★ Discovery: Derive Before Displaying the Formula (5 min)

Plot A(1, 2) and B(4, 6) on the board. Do NOT show the formula yet. Ask: "How would you find the distance between these points? Can you use the Pythagorean Theorem somehow?" Give pairs 90 seconds to think before cold-calling.

Guide students to draw: a horizontal segment from A(1,2) to (4,2), then a vertical segment from (4,2) to B(4,6). These two segments are the legs of a right triangle. Their lengths are |4−1|=3 and |6−2|=4. The hypotenuse AB = √(3²+4²) = √25 = 5.

Then ask: "In the Pythagorean Theorem, what are a, b, and c? What did we just compute for each?" a = horizontal distance = |x₂−x₁|, b = vertical distance = |y₂−y₁|, c = distance between points. Now write the Distance Formula — as a direct consequence of what students just derived, not as a new formula.

GeoGebra Exploration 1 — geogebra.org/m/KUdVJFgk (6 min)
Project the applet. Drag points and ask students to watch the right triangle form. Have students call out the leg lengths before you display the formula result. Key questions to narrate while dragging.
  • During applet exploration
    "Watch the right triangle as I drag the points. Which leg is changing and which is staying the same? Now: if I move one point directly to the right, what happens to the vertical leg? What does that tell you about what controls each leg's length?"
    The horizontal leg length is controlled entirely by the horizontal separation (x₂−x₁); the vertical leg by the vertical separation (y₂−y₁). Moving one point horizontally only changes the horizontal leg. Moving one point vertically only changes the vertical leg. This confirms that the two legs are independent — which is what allows us to compute them separately and then combine with the Pythagorean Theorem.
  • After exploration — prediction
    "Without computing: if I move the two points so they are 3 units apart horizontally and 4 units apart vertically, what will the distance be? What if they're 5 and 12 units apart?"
    3 and 4 → c = 5; 5 and 12 → c = 13. Students who predict correctly using the Pythagorean triples from the warm-up have completed the connection between today's two sections. Celebrating this prediction builds confidence for the formula work that follows.
Two worked examples — Integer and Radical
Example 1: A(1,2) to B(4,6) — integer answer
d = √[(4−1)² + (6−2)²] = √[9 + 16] = √25 = 5 ✓ (students predicted this during exploration)

Example 2: P(-2,3) to Q(4,-1) — radical answer
d = √[(4−(−2))² + (−1−3)²] = √[6² + (−4)²] = √[36 + 16] = √52 = 2√13
Common errors: (1) subtracting negative numbers incorrectly — 4−(−2) = 6, not 2; (2) forgetting to simplify √52 (52 = 4×13).
The negative sign subtraction error is very common In Example 2, x₂−x₁ = 4−(−2) = 4+2 = 6. Students who write 4−2 = 2 (forgetting the double negative) will get √(4+16) = √20 = 2√5, which is wrong. Slow down on this step and ask "what does subtracting a negative mean?" before proceeding. This error appears in nearly a third of first attempts.
Discussion questions
  • Conceptual depth
    "Does it matter which point you call (x₁, y₁) and which you call (x₂, y₂)? Try swapping them in Example 1 — do you get the same answer? Why?"
    No, the order doesn't matter, because the formula squares the differences. (x₂−x₁)² = (x₁−x₂)² since squaring removes the negative. This is a useful property — you never have to worry about which point is "first" in the distance formula. (Note: in the midpoint formula, order also doesn't matter because addition is commutative.)
  • Connecting to Day 5
    "From Day 5, what does it mean for two segments to be congruent? How could you use the Distance Formula to prove two segments are congruent?"
    Two segments are congruent if they have the same length. To prove AB ≅ CD using coordinates: compute the distance AB and the distance CD using the formula. If the results are equal numbers, then the segments are congruent. This is a coordinate proof — using algebraic computation to prove a geometric relationship. This connects Days 5, 6, and 7 together.
Section 4 — Midpoint Formula: Meet Halfway (17 min)
Midpoint Average Framing + GeoGebra + Three Examples + Missing Endpoint 17 min
★ Discovery: Average Before Formula (2 min)

Before presenting the midpoint formula, ask: "If you and a friend live at different locations on a street that runs along the x-axis — you're at x = 2 and your friend is at x = 8 — where would you meet exactly halfway?" Most students answer 5. Then ask: "How did you get 5?" (Average: (2+8)/2 = 5.) "Now: what if you're at point (1, 3) and your friend is at (5, 7) — where do you meet halfway?" Let students predict before showing the formula.

Students who arrive at "average the x's, average the y's" before you write the formula have independently derived the Midpoint Formula. The formula then feels like naming what they already know, not learning something new.

GeoGebra Exploration — geogebra.org/m/zWFNqgUe
Drag endpoints and watch the midpoint always stay exactly in the middle. Key narration: "Notice the midpoint is always equidistant from both endpoints — it can never be closer to one than the other. Now watch the x-coordinate of the midpoint as I move one endpoint horizontally — what is it always equal to?"
Three worked examples + the fraction case
Example 1: A(2,8) and B(6,4) → M = ((2+6)/2, (8+4)/2) = (4, 6) — nice integers
Example 2: P(-3,5) and Q(7,-1) → M = ((-3+7)/2, (5+(−1))/2) = (2, 2) — negative numbers
Example 3: A(1,3) and B(4,8) → M = (5/2, 11/2) — fraction result; leave as fraction!

The fraction case: many students try to convert 5/2 to 2.5. In coordinate geometry, fractions are preferred. A decimal answer is technically correct but harder to work with in subsequent calculations.
★ Missing Endpoint — Two Methods (5 min)
Given A(2,5) and midpoint M(6,8), find endpoint B. Introduce both methods side by side — different students will prefer different approaches.
Intuitive Method ("same change"):
From A(2,5) to M(6,8): x changed by +4, y changed by +3.
From M(6,8) to B: same change → x: 6+4=10, y: 8+3=11. B = (10,11).

Algebraic Method (set up the average equation):
(2 + x)/2 = 6 → 2 + x = 12 → x = 10
(5 + y)/2 = 8 → 5 + y = 16 → y = 11
B = (10, 11).

Common error: students set up (6 + x)/2 = 2 (using M as an endpoint and A as the midpoint — the problem information is swapped).
The most common missing endpoint error Students swap what is given — they treat the midpoint as an endpoint and solve (M + B)/2 = A, which gives the wrong answer. Before setting up the equation, write: "The GIVEN is: endpoint A = (2,5), midpoint M = (6,8). The formula is: (A + B)/2 = M." Making the given information explicit before any algebra prevents this swap error.
Discussion questions
  • Connecting to Day 5
    "From Day 5, the Definition of Midpoint says AM = MB. How does the Midpoint Formula prove that the midpoint formula gives the point that satisfies AM = MB?"
    If M = ((x₁+x₂)/2, (y₁+y₂)/2), then compute the distance AM and the distance MB using the Distance Formula. Both distances equal (1/2)√[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²] — they're equal. This is a coordinate proof of the Definition of Midpoint using the Distance Formula — a beautiful connection between Days 5, 6, and 7.
  • Real-world connection
    "Two cell towers are located at coordinates (0, 0) and (10, 8) km on a map. A phone can connect to either tower. Where should the phone be located to be exactly halfway between the two towers? What is the maximum distance it could be from either tower?"
    Midpoint: ((0+10)/2, (0+8)/2) = (5, 4). Distance from either tower: d = √(5²+4²) = √41 ≈ 6.4 km. This is a direct application of both formulas in a realistic engineering context — and a preview of the kind of applied problem students will see in later math courses.
  • Algebraic proof connection — Day 6 revisited
    "From Day 6, the Definition of Midpoint was listed as a proof reason. How would you use the Midpoint Formula in a coordinate proof?"
    In a coordinate proof, you don't cite the Definition of Midpoint as a proof reason — instead, you compute the midpoint using the formula and show it equals the given midpoint, or show that AM = MB numerically. Coordinate proofs prove geometric facts by computation, not by citing theorem names. This is a different proof style from Day 6's two-column proofs, but it proves the same things.
Section 5 — Practice Zone & Exit Ticket (15 min)
Practice Distance · Midpoint · Missing Endpoint + Exit Ticket 15 min
Three errors to circulate for specifically
(1) Unsimplified radical: leaving √52 instead of simplifying to 2√13. Ask: "Is 52 divisible by any perfect square?" (2) Missing endpoint swap: student uses M where A should be (or vice versa) in the average equation. Ask: "Which point did the problem tell you was the midpoint?" (3) Subtraction of a negative: student computes 4−(−2) = 2 instead of 6. Ask: "What does subtracting a negative number mean?"
Exit Ticket
ProblemWhat it testsAnswer
ET1: Find the distance between A(−1, 3) and B(5, −5). Simplify your answer. Distance formula + radical simplification d = √[(5−(−1))² + (−5−3)²] = √[36+64] = √100 = 10
ET2: Find the midpoint of P(−4, 7) and Q(8, −3). Midpoint formula with negatives M = ((−4+8)/2, (7+(−3))/2) = (2, 2)
ET3: One endpoint is A(1, 4). The midpoint is M(5, 7). Find endpoint B. Missing endpoint — both methods valid B = (9, 10). Check: midpoint of (1,4) and (9,10) = (5,7) ✓
ET1 — this one produces a Pythagorean triple ET1 gives legs 6 and 8, which form a 6-8-10 triple (double of 3-4-5). Students who recognize this will compute quickly. Students who don't will still get the correct answer via the formula. After debrief: ask "what Pythagorean triple did you find? How does 6-8-10 relate to 3-4-5?" — this reinforces the pattern recognition from the warm-up.
If There Is Extra Time — Partner, Small Group & Extension Activities
Activities marked ★ Core are high-value for any students with extra time. Activities marked Extension are for students who are fully fluent on both formulas and ready for deeper challenge.
📐 Triangle Type Challenge ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8–10 min  ·  After Distance Formula
Pairs are given three coordinate points and must determine whether the triangle formed is scalene, isosceles, or equilateral by computing all three side lengths using the Distance Formula. They must show all computations and state which sides are equal (if any).
  • Triangle 1: A(0,0), B(4,0), C(2,3) — compute: AB=4, BC=√13, AC=√13 → isosceles
  • Triangle 2: A(0,0), B(6,0), C(1,4) — compute: AB=6, BC=√25=5, AC=√17 → scalene
  • Challenge: Find three coordinate points that form an equilateral triangle. (Hint: one vertex can be at the origin.)
This activity connects Day 4's triangle classification vocabulary to Day 7's coordinate tools — a direct bridge between units. The equilateral challenge is hard; don't require it for all pairs.
🗺️ Treasure Map Distance ★ Core · Groups of 3
⏱ 10 min  ·  After Distance + Midpoint
Groups receive a coordinate "map" with five labeled locations (school, library, park, store, home) at given coordinates. They must: (1) find the distance between three specific pairs, (2) find the midpoint between two locations (a "meeting point"), and (3) determine which two locations are closest to each other.
  • Make sure at least one distance requires radical simplification and one produces an integer
  • The "which two are closest?" question requires computing all distances and comparing — good practice for systematic application
  • Extension: find the location that is equidistant from three given landmarks (requires setting up Distance Formula equations)
The context makes the formulas feel purposeful rather than abstract. Students who resist "naked" formula practice often engage readily with the map context.
🏃 Midpoint Relay ★ Core · Groups of 4
⏱ 8 min  ·  After Missing Endpoint
Groups of 4 run a relay: Student 1 is given two endpoints and finds the midpoint → passes to Student 2 who uses that midpoint as one endpoint in a new problem → and so on for all 4 students. The final answer is verified by working backwards through the chain.
  • Start: A(2,4) and B(8,10) → midpoint M₁ = (5,7)
  • Round 2: M₁(5,7) and C(11,1) → midpoint M₂ = (8,4)
  • Round 3: M₂(8,4) and D(−2,6) → midpoint M₃ = (3,5)
  • Round 4: Given endpoint M₃(3,5) and midpoint (6,8), find the other endpoint E
The relay structure makes each student responsible for one clean computation — and errors propagate forward, so groups self-check at each handoff. Student 4's missing endpoint problem catches any errors made in the earlier rounds.
🧊 3D Extension Explorer Extension · Pairs
⏱ 10 min  ·  Early finishers
Pairs use the 3D Sneak Peek section from the lesson website to extend both formulas to three dimensions. They work through the provided 3D examples and then create their own 3D problem — find distance and midpoint between A(2,1,5) and B(6,4,9).
  • Distance: d = √[(6−2)²+(4−1)²+(9−5)²] = √[16+9+16] = √41
  • Midpoint: M = ((2+6)/2, (1+4)/2, (5+9)/2) = (4, 2.5, 7)
  • Discussion: "What does the 3D distance formula tell you about the Pythagorean Theorem? Is it still a² + b² = c², or something else?"
The 3D extension reveals that the pattern is truly general: the distance formula is a³+b²+c²=d² in 3D. Students who notice this see the Pythagorean Theorem as a special case of a more general principle, which is a beautiful mathematical insight for students ready for it.
📐 Coordinate Proof Challenge Extension · Pairs
⏱ 12 min  ·  Strong finishers only
Pairs receive two coordinate proof challenges that combine Distance Formula, Midpoint Formula, and vocabulary from Days 4–5.
  • Challenge 1: "Given A(0,0), B(4,0), C(2,4), prove that triangle ABC is isosceles." (Compute AB, BC, AC; show two are equal.)
  • Challenge 2: "Given A(1,3), B(7,3), C(7,9), D(1,9), prove that ABCD is a rectangle." (Show all four sides equal — actually a square — and confirm diagonals are equal.)
  • For each: write a brief proof argument stating Given, what you computed, and what the computation proves.
These coordinate proofs are the most direct preview of Day 8 content. Students who complete both with a written argument are ready for Day 8's coordinate geometry unit. The rectangle/square challenge is genuinely difficult — use it selectively.
🔢 Pythagorean Triple Hunt Extension · Individual
⏱ 8–10 min  ·  Any time after Pythagorean review
Students generate Pythagorean triples by exploring patterns. Start with the 3-4-5 family (6-8-10, 9-12-15 — multiples) and the primitive triples (5-12-13, 8-15-17, 7-24-25). Then find coordinate pairs that produce integer distances using the Distance Formula.
  • Pattern: what do all multiples of 3-4-5 have in common? (They're all divisible by 3-4-5.)
  • Challenge: find a pair of points (using only integer coordinates from −10 to 10) that produces a distance of 13 using the Distance Formula.
  • Answer: many solutions, e.g., (0,0) and (5,12) → d = √(25+144) = √169 = 13.
This activity connects number theory (Pythagorean triples), algebra (the distance formula), and geometry (right triangles) — exactly the kind of cross-domain thinking that characterizes mathematical maturity.

How today's formulas feed the rest of the course
Day 8 — Slope & Parallel/Perp. The Distance Formula is the tool for proving segments are congruent in coordinate proofs. The Midpoint Formula is used to find centers of segments — both appear constantly in Day 8 coordinate geometry.
Day 9 — Pythagorean Theorem Day 9 deepens the Pythagorean Theorem connection introduced today. Students who understand the Distance Formula as a² + b² = c² will engage more deeply with Day 9's applications and its proof.
Day 10 — Similarity The Distance Formula is used to find side lengths of triangles in coordinate proofs of similarity. Students who can compute side lengths quickly can verify side ratios — the key to the AA and SSS similarity tests.
All Future Courses The Midpoint Formula reappears in Algebra 2 (midpoint of arc, conic sections), Precalculus (midpoints of intervals), and Statistics (expected value as a midpoint). The Distance Formula appears in Physics (displacement), Statistics (Euclidean distance in data analysis), and Computer Science (game engine distance calculations).
