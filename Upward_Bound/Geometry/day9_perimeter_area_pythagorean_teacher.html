⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

Day 9 is intentionally a synthesis and consolidation lesson. Every skill introduced today has appeared in prior lessons — the Pythagorean Theorem from Day 7, algebraic equation solving from Day 2, and fraction/radical manipulation from Days 7–8. The new content is primarily organizational: formulas as a toolkit, composite strategies as a decision-making framework, and quadratic equations arising naturally from geometry problems. Students who feel capable of prior skills will find this lesson reinforcing and confidence-building. Students who are still shaky will get a structured second pass at key skills.

The AC Method refresher is the most algebraically demanding section and should be treated carefully. Many 8th–9th grade students have encountered factoring but not systematically. The Diamond/X-Box visual tool is effective — use it. The key teaching point is not the mechanics of factoring (students can look that up) but the judgment call: when does a Pythagorean Theorem problem produce a quadratic, and what do you do with the negative solution?

The Area Library section is the most formula-dense section in the course. The discovery-first principle applies here through the GeoGebra applets that show why the formulas work — parallelogram shearing into a rectangle, two triangles forming a parallelogram, circles "unrolling" into triangles. Students who see the geometric justification for each formula are far less likely to confuse them. Prioritize the three key insights (parallelogram = rectangle, triangle = ½ parallelogram, circle unrolling) over drilling all six formulas.

The Composite Area Lab is the highest-engagement section for most students — it's visual, strategic, and immediately applicable to real-world scenarios. The most important teaching move is slowing down to ask "which method?" before any calculation begins. Students who rush to compute without choosing additive vs. subtractive frequently get lost in the middle of a problem.

The Pythagorean Algebra section is the deepest content of the lesson — it requires students to recognize that geometry produces equations, set those equations up correctly, and then apply algebraic tools (including factoring) to solve them. The "always reject negative solutions" rule and the "hypotenuse check" (hypotenuse must be the longest side) are the two most important error-prevention habits to build here.

Learning Objectives
  • Factor quadratic expressions using the AC Method (5-step process and Diamond/X-Box visual)
  • Recognize when a geometry problem produces a quadratic equation and apply the AC Method to solve it
  • State area and perimeter formulas for rectangle, triangle, parallelogram, trapezoid, circle, and semicircle
  • Explain geometrically WHY each formula works (parallelogram→rectangle shearing, triangle = ½ parallelogram, circle unrolling)
  • Classify a composite figure as additive (add pieces) or subtractive (larger shape minus cutout)
  • Calculate the area of a composite figure by decomposing it into known shapes
  • Set up and solve Pythagorean Theorem equations when one or more side lengths are algebraic expressions
  • Reject negative solutions in geometric contexts and verify using the Pythagorean Theorem
  • Recognize the 3-4-5 (and 5-12-13, 8-15-17) Pythagorean triple patterns and their scaled multiples

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The AC Method section is a prerequisite gate, not a standalone lesson — cap it at 10 minutes. If students can factor x² + 5x + 6 correctly in 2 minutes, move on. The Diamond/X-Box visual is the most efficient tool for students who struggle — use it as a support, not the primary instruction. Do not let this expand to 20 minutes and crowd out the Pythagorean Algebra section, which is the most important content of the day.
  • Do not present all six area formulas verbally. The GeoGebra applets are the lesson. The parallelogram shearing applet (z3pQ7tmT) takes 2 minutes and produces an indelible conceptual image. Students who watch a parallelogram slide into a rectangle and then hear "A = bh" will never confuse parallelogram area with perimeter. Students who only read "A = bh" in a table often do.
  • The "why do formulas work" section is not optional. The three key insights (parallelogram = rectangle, triangle = ½ parallelogram, circle = thin triangles) are the most conceptually rich 5 minutes of the lesson. Students who understand WHY the formula works will never need to re-memorize it. These insights also directly answer the question "why is triangle area ½bh and not bh?" which students frequently ask.
  • The composite area "which method?" question must happen before any calculation. Students who don't consciously choose additive or subtractive often attempt to compute a component of the wrong size (e.g., computing the full rectangle including a section that's been cut out). Requiring a written "method: additive / subtractive" on every composite problem prevents this.
  • The Pythagorean Algebra section has three difficulty levels — use all three. Example 1 (linear equation) is accessible to all students. Example 2 (quadratic, AC method needed) is the core challenge. Example 3 (triple pattern + perimeter) is the synthesis. Skipping Example 3 is acceptable if time is short; skipping Example 2 is not — it is the direct connection between the AC Method refresher and the geometry content.
  • Always reject negative solutions — make this a written habit, not just a verbal reminder. Students who solve a quadratic and get x = 5 or x = −12 will sometimes choose x = −12 because it "looks like" a valid answer. Require students to write "reject x = [negative] because lengths must be positive" as a mandatory final step on every Pythagorean quadratic problem.
Formula Library — Quick Reference for Teaching
Rectangle
A = ℓw  ·  P = 2ℓ + 2w
Why it works: counting unit squares. Area is rows × columns. Perimeter is the fence around it. This is the base formula — all others derive from here.
Triangle
A = ½bh
Why: any triangle = ½ of a parallelogram with the same base and height. Two congruent triangles always tile a parallelogram. So triangle area = ½ × (parallelogram area) = ½bh.
Parallelogram
A = bh
Why: cut the triangle off one end and paste it onto the other — the parallelogram becomes a rectangle. Same base, same height, same area. The slant height (side length) does NOT equal h.
Trapezoid
A = ½(b₁+b₂)h
Why: the average of the two bases times the height. Think of it as the area you'd get if you replaced both unequal bases with one base equal to their average. Two congruent trapezoids form a parallelogram.
Circle
A = πr²  ·  C = 2πr
Why: "unroll" the circle into thin triangles. All triangles have height r (radius) and their bases sum to the circumference (2πr). Area = ½ × base × height = ½(2πr)(r) = πr².
Semicircle
A = ½πr²  ·  P = πr + 2r
Perimeter note: semicircle perimeter = curved arc (πr, half of 2πr) PLUS the straight diameter (2r). Students frequently forget the diameter and write P = πr only. Require both components.

Section 1 — AC Method Refresher: Factoring Prerequisite Gate (10 min)
AC Method Diamond/X-Box Visual + 5-Step Process + 4 Practice Problems 10 min
The Diamond/X-Box — lead with the visual (3 min)
Draw the diamond on the board before writing any steps. Top box = product (a·c), bottom box = sum (b), sides = the two numbers. The visual makes the "find factors that multiply to top and add to bottom" much clearer than prose. For x² + 5x + 6: top = 6, bottom = 5, sides = 2 and 3 (because 2×3=6 and 2+3=5).
x² + 5x + 6 = 0: a·c = 6, b = 5, find pairs of 6 that sum to 5 → {2, 3}
Split: x² + 2x + 3x + 6 = 0 → x(x+2) + 3(x+2) = 0 → (x+3)(x+2) = 0
x = −3 or x = −2

x² − 7x + 12 = 0: a·c = 12, b = −7, find pairs of 12 that sum to −7 → {−3, −4}
→ (x−3)(x−4) = 0 → x = 3 or x = 4

x² + 7x − 60 = 0: a·c = −60, b = 7, find pairs of −60 that sum to 7 → {12, −5}
→ (x+12)(x−5) = 0 → x = −12 or x = 5

2x² + 14x − 120 = 0: first divide by 2 → x² + 7x − 60 = 0 → same as above
The "divide by 2 first" trick — teach this explicitly The fourth problem (2x² + 14x − 120 = 0) can be reduced by dividing by 2 before factoring. Students who don't notice this will apply AC method to 2x² + 14x − 120 with a·c = 2×(−120) = −240, which is much harder. Always check: "Can I divide all terms by a common factor before I start?" This habit reduces AC method errors by about half.
Discussion question
  • Connecting factoring to geometry
    "Why do you think we're reviewing factoring in a geometry lesson? When would a geometry problem produce a quadratic equation?"
    The Pythagorean Theorem (a² + b² = c²) produces a quadratic whenever one of the sides is expressed as an algebraic expression with x in it. If a leg is x and another is (x + 7), then a² + b² becomes x² + (x+7)², which expands to a quadratic. This is exactly the connection we'll make in Section 4. The factoring we're doing now is the tool we'll need in 30 minutes.
Section 2 — Area Library: Why Formulas Work (22 min)
Area 6 Shapes + 3 Key Applets + "Why It Works" Insights 22 min
★ Discovery-First Approach for Area Formulas

The discovery move for area formulas is showing students the geometric transformation that justifies the formula, then asking them to articulate the rule before you write it. For the parallelogram: show the shearing applet and ask "how does the area of this shape compare to the rectangle it becomes?" For the triangle: show two congruent triangles forming a parallelogram and ask "if this parallelogram has area bh, what is the area of one triangle?" For the circle: show the unrolling animation and ask "if the circle unrolls into a triangle, what are the base and height of that triangle?"

Students who derive each formula from the visual — even approximately — own it permanently. Students who copy it from a table often misremember it under pressure. The investment of 5 extra minutes in the "why" section saves significant reteaching later in the course.

★ Three critical GeoGebra applets — use in this order
1. Parallelogram Shearing (geogebra.org/m/z3pQ7tmT)
Drag the slider to watch the triangle on one end of the parallelogram move to the other end, forming a rectangle. Before revealing the formula, ask: "What shape did we end up with? What does the base of the rectangle correspond to? What does the height correspond to? What can you conclude about the area formula?"
2. Triangle as Half Parallelogram (geogebra.org/m/x3VJ6uxf)
Shows two congruent triangles forming a parallelogram. Ask: "If the parallelogram has area bh, what is the area of one triangle? Why does ½bh make sense geometrically?" This is the most important formula derivation for student retention.
Discussion questions — for each shape formula
  • The parallelogram height trap
    "A parallelogram has a side length of 8 cm and a base of 5 cm. Its height is 6 cm. What is its area? Can you explain why the side length (8 cm) doesn't appear in the area formula?"
    A = bh = 5 × 6 = 30 cm². The side length doesn't appear because area is about the perpendicular distance between the two bases, not the slant distance along the side. Imagine pushing the parallelogram sideways more and more — the base and height stay the same, so the area stays the same, even as the sides get longer and more slanted. This is the shearing concept the GeoGebra applet demonstrates. The slant side is NOT the height.
  • The trapezoid average intuition
    "The trapezoid formula is ½(b₁ + b₂)h. That's the same as the average of the two bases times the height. Why would averaging the bases make sense as a way to find area?"
    The trapezoid is like a rectangle whose width changes from b₁ on one end to b₂ on the other. The "fairest" width to use is the average — (b₁ + b₂)/2. Then multiply by the height. This is also provable by cutting the trapezoid into two triangles: ½b₁h + ½b₂h = ½(b₁ + b₂)h. Either reasoning works.
  • Semicircle perimeter — the most common mistake
    "A semicircle has a radius of 5. What is its perimeter? Be careful — perimeter means the total boundary length. What pieces form the boundary of a semicircle?"
    P = curved arc + straight diameter = πr + 2r = π(5) + 2(5) = 5π + 10 ≈ 25.7 cm. The most common error is writing P = πr only (the arc) and forgetting the straight edge. The straight edge is the diameter (2r), not 2 radii — though they are the same thing, writing "diameter = 2r" helps students remember that the full straight edge is one piece, not two.
  • The circle unrolling insight
    "The area of a circle is πr². Can you explain where that formula comes from using the 'unrolling into triangles' idea?"
    Imagine cutting the circle into many thin triangular wedges like pizza slices. Each wedge is approximately a triangle with height = r (the radius) and a tiny base along the edge. All the tiny bases together = the full circumference = 2πr. Total area of all triangles = ½ × base × height = ½ × (2πr) × r = πr². The more slices you use, the more accurate this becomes — and in the limit (calculus), it's exact.
Which GeoGebra applets to use in class (time constraint) With 22 minutes total, use three applets in class and assign the rest as independent exploration: (1) Parallelogram shearing — essential, 3 min; (2) Triangle explorer — essential, 3 min; (3) Circle explorer — highly recommended, 3 min. The rectangle and trapezoid applets can be explored independently; their formulas are intuitive enough that the applet is supplementary rather than essential. The semicircle applet is worth 1 minute if time allows, primarily to establish the perimeter = arc + diameter distinction.
Section 3 — Composite Area Lab: Strategy First (18 min)
Composite Additive vs. Subtractive Methods + GeoGebra + 2 Worked Examples 18 min
★ "Which Method?" Must Come Before Any Calculation

The most important teaching habit for composite area is requiring students to choose and justify their strategy before any numbers are computed. Students who jump immediately to calculation often get halfway through a problem and realize they've computed the wrong component, or added when they should have subtracted.

Teach the decision rule explicitly: "Look at the diagram. Can you see the shape made of pieces added together? That's additive. Can you see a bigger shape with a hole or cutout? That's subtractive." After students choose, require them to write "Method: Additive" or "Method: Subtractive" on their notes before computing anything.

GeoGebra Composite Explorer — geogebra.org/m/bheumnkg (3 min)
Before the two worked examples, open the composite explorer. Ask: "For each figure in the applet, tell me: additive or subtractive? What are the component shapes?" Students who can correctly identify the strategy and components for two or three applet shapes are ready to work the examples.
Two worked examples + discussion
Example 1: Rectangle (10×6) with semicircular cutout (diameter 4) — SUBTRACTIVE
Step 1: Method → Subtractive (larger rectangle, hole removed)
Step 2: A(rectangle) = 10 × 6 = 60 cm²
Step 3: A(semicircle) = ½π(2)² = 2π cm² (diameter = 4, so radius = 2)
Step 4: A(total) = 60 − 2π ≈ 53.72 cm²

Example 2: Silo shape — rectangle (8×12) with semicircle on top — ADDITIVE
Step 1: Method → Additive (rectangle + semicircle attached)
Step 2: A(rectangle) = 8 × 12 = 96 ft²
Step 3: A(semicircle) = ½π(4)² = 8π ft² (width = diameter = 8, radius = 4)
Step 4: A(total) = 96 + 8π ≈ 121.13 ft²
The "diameter vs. radius" error in composite figures When a semicircle is attached to a rectangle, the dimension given is almost always the width of the rectangle, which equals the diameter of the semicircle. Students frequently use this dimension as the radius. Build the habit: whenever a semicircle or circle appears in a composite figure, write "radius = ___" as the first step before computing, and explicitly state how you found it from the given dimensions.
Discussion questions
  • Strategy selection
    "You have a shape that looks like a large L. Could you solve this using both additive AND subtractive methods? Which would be easier? Why?"
    Yes — an L-shape can be solved additively (two rectangles) or subtractively (large rectangle minus the missing corner piece). Both methods give the same answer. The better choice depends on which dimensions you know: if the missing corner dimensions are labeled, subtractive might be faster. If the two rectangle dimensions are labeled, additive might be faster. There is rarely only one correct method — the skill is choosing the more efficient one.
  • Real-world connection
    "A homeowner wants to tile a bathroom floor shaped like an L. The L-shape measures 10 ft × 8 ft with a 3 ft × 4 ft rectangle cut from one corner. How many square feet of tile are needed? Would you use additive or subtractive?"
    Subtractive is more natural here: A(large rectangle) = 10 × 8 = 80 ft². A(cut corner) = 3 × 4 = 12 ft². A(total) = 80 − 12 = 68 ft². If tile comes in boxes of 25 ft², you need 3 boxes (68 ÷ 25 = 2.72, round up to 3). This adds the practical layer of rounding up — you always buy more tile than the exact calculation requires.
Section 4 — Pythagorean Algebra Lab: Geometry Creates Equations (20 min)
Pyth. Algebra 3 Examples: Linear, Quadratic, Triple Pattern 20 min
★ The Big Idea: Geometry Produces Equations — Algebra Solves Them

Before Example 1, frame the entire Pythagorean Algebra section with this observation: "A geometric fact (a² + b² = c²) plus geometric information (the side lengths, including algebraic expressions) produces an equation. That equation is something you can solve. Today you're not doing geometry and algebra separately — you're watching them work together to find an unknown length that was never explicitly stated in the problem."

This framing prevents students from treating the Pythagorean Theorem as a formula to "plug into" and instead positions it as a modeling tool: geometry gives you the structure, algebra gives you the solution. The three examples escalate the algebraic demand while the geometric structure stays constant.

Example 1: One algebraic leg → linear equation (5 min)
Right triangle: legs x and 12, hypotenuse 13. Find x.
Step 1: Identify → right triangle → a² + b² = c²
Step 2: Translate → x² + 12² = 13²
Step 3: Solve → x² + 144 = 169 → x² = 25 → x = 5 (reject x = −5, lengths are positive)
Step 4: Check → 5² + 12² = 25 + 144 = 169 = 13² ✓
Note: this is the 5-12-13 Pythagorean triple!
Require this language at Step 3 "When we get x² = 25, we have two solutions: x = 5 and x = −5. We reject x = −5 because a length cannot be negative. x = 5. Write this on every single Pythagorean algebra problem from now on: 'reject x = [negative value] because lengths must be positive.'"
Example 2: Two algebraic legs → quadratic → AC Method (10 min)
Right triangle: legs x and (x + 7), hypotenuse 13. Find x.
Step 1: Identify → right triangle → a² + b² = c²
Step 2: Translate → x² + (x+7)² = 13²
Step 3: Expand → x² + x² + 14x + 49 = 169 → 2x² + 14x − 120 = 0
Divide by 2 → x² + 7x − 60 = 0
Step 4: AC Method → a·c = −60, b = 7 → factors: 12 and −5
→ (x + 12)(x − 5) = 0 → x = −12 or x = 5
Step 5: Reject x = −12 (negative length) → x = 5
Legs: 5 and 12. Hypotenuse: 13. This is the 5-12-13 triple! ✓
The expansion step — this is where most errors happen Expanding (x + 7)² is the most common error site. Students frequently write x² + 49 instead of x² + 14x + 49, omitting the middle term. This is the "FOIL" or "expand the binomial" skill. Slow down on the expansion: (x + 7)² = (x + 7)(x + 7) = x² + 7x + 7x + 49 = x² + 14x + 49. Require students to write the expansion step fully before combining like terms. Students who skip to x² + 49 will get a wrong quadratic and a wrong answer.
Discussion question — after Example 2
  • Connecting the AC Method to the geometry
    "We got x = 5 and x = −12. We rejected x = −12 for a geometric reason (negative length). But algebraically, x = −12 satisfies the equation. Can you explain what would happen if you substituted x = −12 back into the geometric problem?"
    If x = −12, the "legs" would be −12 and (−12 + 7) = −5. These are negative, so they don't represent real lengths. The equation x² + (x+7)² = 169 is satisfied by x = −12 algebraically (the squares make everything positive), but x = −12 doesn't correspond to a valid triangle. This is the key distinction: an equation can have solutions that don't satisfy the physical constraints of the original problem. "Reject" means "discard as geometrically invalid," not "wrong algebraically."
Example 3: Pythagorean triple pattern + perimeter (5 min)
Right triangle: legs 3x and 4x, hypotenuse 5x. Perimeter = 60. Find x and all sides.
Step 1: Pattern recognition → this is a 3-4-5 triple scaled by x! (Don't need full Pythagorean Theorem.)
Step 2: Perimeter = 3x + 4x + 5x = 12x = 60 → x = 5
Step 3: Sides = 15, 20, 25.
Check: 15² + 20² = 225 + 400 = 625 = 25² ✓ (also a 3-4-5 scaled by 5)
The pattern recognition insight — teach this explicitly Students who recognize 3-4-5, 5-12-13, and 8-15-17 triples and their multiples save enormous computation time. When a problem gives legs 3x, 4x, 5x — you don't need to verify the Pythagorean Theorem because the pattern guarantees it's satisfied for any x. The pattern is the proof. This is a legitimate and powerful problem-solving shortcut that appears frequently on standardized tests.
Discussion questions
  • The hypotenuse check
    "After solving for x in any Pythagorean algebra problem, how would you verify that your answer makes sense geometrically — beyond just plugging back into the equation?"
    Check that the hypotenuse is the longest side. In a right triangle, c must be strictly greater than either leg. If your solution produces a "hypotenuse" that is shorter than a leg, you've either set up the equation wrong (swapped a leg and hypotenuse) or made an algebra error. This visual check catches the most common setup error: mistaking which side is the hypotenuse. The hypotenuse is always opposite the right angle and is always the longest side.
  • Connecting to Day 7
    "On Day 7, we used the Pythagorean Theorem to derive the Distance Formula. Today we're using it in the opposite direction — starting from a geometric constraint and finding an unknown length. Are these the same theorem or different uses of it?"
    Same theorem, different directions of use. Day 7: given coordinates (sides), find the hypotenuse (distance). Day 9: given the hypotenuse and one leg (or algebraic expressions for all sides), find the missing leg. The theorem is the bridge between known and unknown lengths in any right triangle, regardless of whether those lengths are given as numbers, coordinates, or algebraic expressions.
  • Exit question
    "A right triangle has a hypotenuse of 26 and one leg of 10. Without computing: what Pythagorean triple family do you think this belongs to? What is the other leg?"
    This is a 5-12-13 triple scaled by 2: legs 10 and 24, hypotenuse 26. Students who recognize this avoid computing 26² − 10² = 676 − 100 = 576 = 24². Pythagorean triple pattern recognition is a powerful time-saving skill.

If There Is Extra Time — Partner, Small Group & Extension Activities
Activities marked ★ Core are high-value for any students with extra time. Activities marked Extension are for students fully fluent on all three sections.
🔺 Formula Justification Tournament ★ Core · Groups of 3
⏱ 8–10 min  ·  After Area Library
Each student in a group of 3 receives one formula to "defend" — they must explain WHY the formula is true using words, a sketch, or the GeoGebra applet. The other two students ask questions and vote: "Would a student who had never seen this formula understand your explanation?" Rotate until each student has presented one formula.
  • Assign formulas: Student A gets triangle (½bh), Student B gets parallelogram (bh), Student C gets trapezoid (½(b₁+b₂)h)
  • Presentations should include: the formula, a diagram, and a geometric argument for WHY the formula works
  • Extension: Student who finishes first takes the circle or semicircle formula
Students who teach a formula to peers almost always remember it longer than students who only receive it. This activity is one of the highest-retention approaches available for formula-dense content.
🏠 Dream Room Design ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 10–12 min  ·  After Composite Lab
Pairs design a room on graph paper (scale: 1 square = 1 ft). The room must be composite — at least two distinct shapes. They must calculate the total floor area (for carpeting), the total perimeter (for baseboard), and the area with one circular rug (radius 3 ft) subtracted. Written work must show "Method: additive/subtractive" for each calculation.
  • Minimum requirements: room must include at least one non-rectangular piece
  • Must calculate: total floor area, perimeter, area excluding the rug
  • Extension: if tile costs $3.50/ft², carpet costs $5.00/ft², how much does it cost to carpet the area NOT covered by the rug?
The cost extension adds the practical rounding-up skill — you must buy whole boxes of tile or whole rolls of carpet. Students who arrive at non-integer answers must decide how to round in a real-world context.
🔺 Triple Hunter ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 8 min  ·  After Pythagorean Algebra section
Pairs generate Pythagorean triples systematically using the formula: for any m > n > 0, (m²−n², 2mn, m²+n²) is a Pythagorean triple. They try m=2/n=1, m=3/n=1, m=3/n=2, m=4/n=1, m=4/n=3 and record the triples. Then verify 2–3 of them using the Pythagorean Theorem.
  • m=2, n=1 → (3, 4, 5); m=3, n=1 → (8, 6, 10) scaled 2×(3-4-5) — wait, actually (8, 6, 10)? Check: reorder → 6, 8, 10 ✓
  • m=3, n=2 → (5, 12, 13); m=4, n=1 → (15, 8, 17) → reorder: 8, 15, 17 ✓
  • Challenge: which triples from the table are "primitive" (no common factor) and which are multiples of a smaller triple?
The generating formula (m²−n², 2mn, m²+n²) is genuinely beautiful and surprises most students — it shows that there are infinitely many Pythagorean triples and gives a systematic way to find them all.
🧩 Area Equation Challenge ★ Core · Pairs
⏱ 10 min  ·  After both Area and Pythagorean sections
Pairs receive problems where a shape's area is given and a side length involves x — they must write and solve the equation. Combines area formula knowledge with algebraic equation solving.
  • Problem 1: A rectangle has area 48 and width (x+2). The length is (2x−1). Find x and the dimensions. [Set (x+2)(2x−1)=48; expand; AC Method]
  • Problem 2: A triangle has area 30 and base (x+3). Height is (2x−1). Find x. [Set ½(x+3)(2x−1)=30; expand; AC Method]
  • Problem 3: A circle has circumference 10π. Find the area. [C=2πr=10π → r=5 → A=25π]
Problem 1 and 2 demonstrate that area formulas also produce quadratic equations — not just the Pythagorean Theorem. This broadens students' understanding of "when do I need the AC Method?" to include any time two algebraic expressions multiply to a given value.
📐 Coordinate Area Challenge Extension · Pairs
⏱ 12 min  ·  Strong finishers only
Pairs find the area of a triangle whose vertices are given as coordinates, combining Day 7's Distance Formula with today's triangle area formula. Given A(0,0), B(6,0), C(2,5), find the exact area of triangle ABC.
  • Method 1 (shoelace formula): Area = ½|x₁(y₂−y₃) + x₂(y₃−y₁) + x₃(y₁−y₂)| = ½|0+30+0| = 15
  • Method 2 (base × height): AB is along x-axis, length 6. Height = y-coordinate of C = 5. Area = ½(6)(5) = 15 ✓
  • Challenge: find the area of triangle with vertices A(1,2), B(7,2), C(4,8) — use either method
The shoelace formula is a powerful coordinate geometry tool that connects Days 7, 8, and 9. Students who encounter it here will recognize it in more advanced courses (linear algebra, computational geometry). Even if they only use Method 2 for the first problem, introducing the shoelace formula as an alternative is worthwhile.
🌀 Circle Within Square Challenge Extension · Individual
⏱ 8 min  ·  Any time after Area section
A circle is inscribed in a square (the circle touches all four sides). The square has side length 2r. Find: (1) the area of the square, (2) the area of the circle, (3) the area of the four "corner" regions not covered by the circle, and (4) what fraction of the square is covered by the circle.
  • A(square) = (2r)² = 4r²; A(circle) = πr²; A(corners) = 4r² − πr²
  • Fraction covered = πr²/4r² = π/4 ≈ 0.785 = about 78.5% of the square
  • Challenge: If the circle has area 25π, what are the dimensions of the square and the areas of the corner regions?
  • r = 5; square side = 10; A(corners) = 100 − 25π ≈ 21.5 sq units
The π/4 result is genuinely surprising and beautiful — a circle inscribed in a square covers about 78.5% of the area, regardless of the size of the circle. Students who find this result and can explain why it's always the same fraction have made a genuine mathematical discovery.
