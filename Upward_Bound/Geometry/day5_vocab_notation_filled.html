Section 1 — Undefined Terms: The Building Blocks
These three terms cannot be defined using simpler geometry words — we accept them as understood. Everything else in geometry is built from these three.
Point
A location in space. No size, no length, no width.
Notation: point A or just A
Line
Straight path extending infinitely in both directions.
Notation: ↔AB or line ℓ
Plane
Flat surface extending infinitely in all directions.
Notation: Plane ABC or Plane P
Plane Vocabulary — fill in the blanks:
TermDefinition
CollinearPoints that lie on the same line
Non-collinearPoints that do NOT lie on the same line
CoplanarPoints / lines on the same plane
The tripod insight:
Three non-collinear points define exactly 1 plane.
Three collinear points define ∞ planes (infinitely many!).
Think: a 3-legged stool never wobbles — the three legs define exactly one flat surface.
Section 2 — The Notation Decoder
★ The "Can You Measure It?" Rule — Solves Every Notation Question
YES — you can put a ruler on it and get a number
→ It's a measurement / number
→ Use the symbol = (equal)
NO — you're talking about the geometric object itself
→ It's an object / shape
→ Use the symbol ≅ (congruent)
Notation Family — complete the Object/Number and Use With columns:
Symbol Name What it means Object or Number? Use with
AB̄ (with overline) Segment The piece of the line between A and B — a geometric object Object ≅
AB Length The measured distance between A and B — a number Number =
→AB (ray arrow) Ray Starts at A, passes through B, goes forever in one direction Object ≅
↔AB (line arrow) Line Extends through A and B forever in both directions Object ≅
⚠ Ray Directionality — the Flashlight Rule:
The first letter = where you hold the flashlight (the endpoint).
The second letter = where it points.
Are these the same ray?   →AB vs →BA   Circle:   YES   /   NO
→AB starts at A. →BA starts at B. Different endpoints = different rays.
Notation Drill — write the correct symbol:
"The segment from P to Q" → P̄Q (PQ with overline)
"The length of PQ" → PQ (no overline)
"A ray from M through N" → →MN
"The line through A and B" → ↔AB
"The measure of angle T" → m∠T
The angle name rule: In ∠ABC, the middle letter is always the vertex — the shared starting point of the two rays that form the angle. The two rays are ray BA (→BA) and ray BC (→BC).

Section 4 — Segment & Angle Addition Postulates
📏 GeoGebra — Segment Addition geogebra.org/m/K9jNa7A5
Your task: Drag point B between A and C. Watch AB, BC, and AC update. Look for a relationship between the three lengths.
  • What relationship is always true between AB, BC, and AC? AB + BC always equals AC — the parts always sum to the whole.
  • Can you move B so that AB + BC ≠ AC? Why or why not? No — as long as B is between A and C, the sum of the parts must equal the whole. That's what "between" means.
  • Write a formula for this relationship before reading the postulate below: AB + BC = AC
My formula: AB + BC = AC
Segment Addition Postulate
AB + BC = AC
If B is between A and C, the whole = sum of its parts.
Use when: B is between two other points.
Angle Addition Postulate
m∠ABD + m∠DBC = m∠ABC
If ray BD is inside ∠ABC, the whole = sum of its parts.
Use when: a ray divides an angle into two smaller angles.
Segment Addition — guided
B is between A and C. AB = (2x + 3), BC = (x + 5), AC = 20. Find x.
Step 1: WriteAB + BC = AC → (2x+3) + (x+5) = 20
Step 2: Solve3x + 8 = 20 → 3x = 12 → x = 4
x = 4   AB = 11   BC = 9   ✓ (11+9=20)
Angle Addition — try it
Ray BD is inside ∠ABC. m∠ABD = (3x + 10)°, m∠DBC = (2x + 5)°, m∠ABC = 75°. Find x.
Step 1: Writem∠ABD + m∠DBC = m∠ABC
(3x+10) + (2x+5) = 75
Step 2: Solve5x + 15 = 75 → 5x = 60 → x = 12
x = 12   m∠ABD = 46°   m∠DBC = 29°   ✓ (46+29=75°)
Both postulates follow the same idea — complete the sentence:
In geometry, "the whole equals the sum of its parts" — this applies to both segments (lengths) and angles (measures).

Task: Drag the endpoints. Watch AM and MB.
  • What is always true about AM and MB? AM always equals MB — the midpoint always divides the segment into two equal halves.
  • If you move the midpoint, what happens? The midpoint stays at the center — AM and MB remain equal as the endpoints change.
Observation: AM = MB   (always)
✂️ GeoGebra — Angle Bisector geogebra.org/m/yF8gjM7v
Task: Drag the bisector ray. Watch the two angle measures.
  • What is always true about the two angle halves? m∠ABD always equals m∠DBC — the bisector always creates two equal angles.
  • What happens to the angles if you change the total angle? Both halves change equally — each remains exactly half of the total angle.
Observation: m∠ABD = m∠DBC   (always)
