⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson transforms derivative rules from memorised formulas into understood results. Each rule is proven two ways — algebraically from the limit definition, and geometrically from the graph — before students drill the shortcut. Students who see both proofs remember the rules longer and apply them more flexibly than students who memorise from a table.

The pedagogical sequence is deliberate: algebraic proof first (the "why"), then geometric confirmation (the "what it looks like"), then the shortcut rule (the "how to use it"). This order matters. Students who learn the shortcut first and then see the proof experience it as reverse-engineering; students who discover the shortcut from the proof experience it as a natural consequence.

The geometric proof section is especially calculus-forward: the f(x) ↔ f′(x) connection table (where f has a max/min → f′ = 0; where f is increasing → f′ > 0) is one of the most tested concepts in AP Calculus. Students who own this connection can sketch derivative graphs without computing, and can read increasing/decreasing behaviour from derivative sign charts.

The six trig derivatives must be memorised — there is no shortcut to the shortcut here. But the geometric visualisation (sine's peak → cosine is zero; sine's steepest point → cosine is ±1) and the derivative cycle diagram give students memory anchors that survive across months.

Learning Objectives
  • Read and write derivative notation in all four forms: \(f'(x)\), \(\frac{dy}{dx}\), \(\frac{d}{dx}[f]\), \(y'\)
  • Prove the Constant Rule, Power Rule, and Sum/Difference Rule from the limit definition
  • Apply the Power Rule for any real \(n\) including negative and fractional exponents
  • Differentiate any polynomial using Constant, Power, and Sum/Difference rules
  • Connect the sign of \(f'(x)\) to the increasing/decreasing behaviour of \(f(x)\)
  • Identify where \(f'(x) = 0\) corresponds to a max or min on \(f(x)\)
  • State and apply all six trig derivatives from memory
  • Understand why \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x\) using the sine sum identity and special limits

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • Do not skip the algebraic proofs — even 10 minutes of proof discussion produces dramatically better retention than 10 more minutes of drill. Students who understand why the Power Rule works never confuse it with the exponential rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[a^x]\).
  • Animate each proof on the projector — don't copy from the board — the site's step-by-step animations are cleaner and better paced than live board work. Students follow on their notes while you narrate the animation.
  • The geometric proof section needs Desmos live — graphing \(y=x^2\) and \(y=2x\) simultaneously and pointing out "when \(x^2\) is at its minimum, \(2x=0\)" is more memorable than any diagram. This demo takes 3 minutes and is worth every second.
  • Six trig derivatives in 15 minutes is tight — present the sine proof in full (it shows the technique), state cosine without proof ("same method, similar result"), give the other four as a reference table. Students who want the remaining proofs can find them on the site.
  • The trig derivative cycle diagram is the most important mnemonic of the section — write it on the board, leave it up for the rest of class, and make students copy it as the first thing on their notes page.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge: Notation & Special Limits (10 min)
Prereq Four Notations + Two Special Limits + Rules Preview 10 min
Four Notations Quick Drill (3 min)
Write the four equivalent derivative notations on the board. Students copy all four onto their notes, then you drill: "I write one form, you write all three others." Fast, whole-class, no paper needed beyond the notes.
\(f'(x)\)  ←→  \(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\)  ←→  \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[f(x)]\)  ←→  \(y'\)

All four mean the same thing. In fall calculus you'll see all four on the same exam.
Special Trig Limits — Plant These Now (4 min)
These limits are needed for the sine derivative proof in Section 4. Introduce them now, accept them without proof (they follow from the Squeeze Theorem), and make students write them in the special box on their notes.
\(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\sin h}{h} = 1\)  ·  Confirmed numerically: try \(h = 0.1\): \(\frac{\sin(0.1)}{0.1} \approx 0.9983\). Try \(h = 0.01\): \(\approx 0.99998\). Approaches 1.

\(\displaystyle\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\cos h - 1}{h} = 0\)  ·  Confirmed: as \(h\to0\), \(\cos h \to 1\), so numerator \(\to 0\) faster than denominator.
Why introduce these now? Students who see the special limits cold during the trig proof get lost in unfamiliar notation right when they need to follow an algebraic argument. Introducing them separately, with numerical confirmation, means that when they appear in Step 5 of the sine proof, students already know what they are.
Rules Overview — Give Students the Destination (3 min)
Show the four rule boxes from the site. "These are the four things you'll be able to prove and use by the end of class. Let's build each one." Do not teach the rules yet — just show the names and formulas so students know where they're headed.
Section 2 — Algebraic Proofs (20 min)
Proofs Constant Rule → Power Rule → Sum/Difference Rule 20 min
📏 Constant Rule (5 min)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[c] = 0\)
Proof structure
\(f(x+h) = c\) (constant never changes). So \(f(x+h) - f(x) = c - c = 0\). Divided by \(h\): \(\frac{0}{h} = 0\). Limit: \(0\).

Geometric version: \(y=c\) is a horizontal line. Horizontal lines have slope 0. Derivative = slope of tangent = 0.
➕ Sum/Difference Rule (5 min)
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}[f \pm g] = f' \pm g'\)
Proof structure
Regroup \([f(x+h)+g(x+h)] - [f(x)+g(x)]\) as \([f(x+h)-f(x)] + [g(x+h)-g(x)]\). Split the fraction. Each piece is a difference quotient. Two limits = two derivatives.

Key insight: Differentiation distributes over addition — it is a linear operator. This is used constantly throughout calculus.
★ The Power Rule — Pattern Discovery (10 min)
Do not give the rule first. Show the three worked examples (\(x^2\), \(x^3\), \(x^4\)) on the projector, let students complete the pattern table, and ask "what rule do you notice?" before writing the general formula. This is the discovery experience the lesson title promises.
Function Limit Definition gives… Derivative Pattern observed
\(x^2\)\(\lim_{h\to0}(2x+h)\)\(2x^1\)exponent moved down, power −1
\(x^3\)\(\lim_{h\to0}(3x^2+3xh+h^2)\)\(3x^2\)exponent moved down, power −1
\(x^4\)\(\lim_{h\to0}(4x^3+\ldots)\)\(4x^3\)exponent moved down, power −1
\(x^n\)\(nx^{n-1}\) ← The Power RuleWorks for ALL real \(n\)
After the pattern — say this "This works for any real number \(n\) — not just positive integers. \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^{1/2}] = \frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2}\). \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^{-3}] = -3x^{-4}\). \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sqrt[3]{x}] = \frac{d}{dx}[x^{1/3}] = \frac{1}{3}x^{-2/3}\). The rule holds everywhere. That's what makes it powerful."
The \(x^3\) expansion — students often need help \((x+h)^3 = x^3 + 3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3\). Subtracting \(x^3\): \(3x^2h + 3xh^2 + h^3\). Dividing by \(h\): \(3x^2 + 3xh + h^2\). As \(h\to0\): \(3x^2\). The two \(h\)-containing terms vanish in the limit. Students who get confused by the \(3xh\) and \(h^2\) terms need to see explicitly that they disappear when \(h\to0\).
📈 Constant Multiple Rule — Mention Now
The site's cheat sheet includes \(\frac{d}{dx}[cf(x)] = c \cdot f'(x)\). This follows from the Sum Rule and Constant Rule together and means constants factor out of derivatives. Cover it here with one example: \(\frac{d}{dx}[5x^3] = 5 \cdot 3x^2 = 15x^2\). This is what makes differentiating polynomials term by term work.
Section 3 — Geometric Proofs & f ↔ f′ Connections (20 min)
Geometric Visual Confirmation + The Most Important AP Calculus Skill 20 min
Desmos Demo — \(y=x^2\) and \(y=2x\) (5 min)
Open Desmos. Graph both \(y=x^2\) and \(y=2x\) simultaneously. Walk through these four observations with students:
At \(x=-2\): \(x^2\) is decreasing → slope is negative. Check: \(2(-2)=-4\) ✓ negative
At \(x=0\): \(x^2\) is at its minimum → slope is zero. Check: \(2(0)=0\) ✓ zero
At \(x=1\): \(x^2\) is increasing → slope is positive. Check: \(2(1)=2\) ✓ positive
At \(x=3\): \(x^2\) increasing steeply → slope is large positive. Check: \(2(3)=6\) ✓
The geometric insight — say this "Every property of \(f'(x)\) is a statement about the slope of \(f(x)\). When \(f'(x) = 0\), the tangent to \(f\) is horizontal — that's a max or min. When \(f'(x) > 0\), \(f\) is going uphill. When \(f'(x) < 0\), \(f\) is going downhill. You can read the whole story of \(f\) from the graph of \(f'\) — and vice versa. This is one of the most tested skills in AP Calculus."
★ The f ↔ f′ Connection Table — Build with Students
Reading f from f′
  • →Where \(f'(x) = 0\): \(f\) has a max or min (critical point)
  • →Where \(f'(x) > 0\): \(f\) is increasing
  • →Where \(f'(x) < 0\): \(f\) is decreasing
  • →Where \(f'(x)\) changes sign: \(f\) has a local max (+ to −) or min (− to +)
Reading f′ from f
  • →Where \(f\) has a peak or valley: \(f'\) has a zero
  • →Where \(f\) rises: \(f'\) is positive (above x-axis)
  • →Where \(f\) falls: \(f'\) is negative (below x-axis)
  • →Where \(f\) is steepest: \(f'\) has its largest absolute value
This table is the most AP-tested content in the lesson AP Calculus AB and BC exam questions regularly show a graph of \(f'\) and ask students to sketch or describe \(f\), and vice versa. Students who own the four connections in the left column can answer these questions without any calculation. Spend 5 minutes building the table with student input before showing it complete.
📈 Calculus Connections — Optimisation and Curve Sketching
Critical points (\(f'(x) = 0\)) are where max/min occur — the foundation of all optimisation. Sign analysis of \(f'\) determines increasing/decreasing intervals — the foundation of curve sketching. Both are major calculus topics that build directly on today's geometric understanding.
Section 4 — Trig Derivatives (15 min)
Trig Six Derivatives — Sine Proof in Full + The Derivative Cycle 15 min
Write the Derivative Cycle First — Leave It Up All Lesson
The Trig Derivative Cycle — Memorise This
\[\sin x \;\xrightarrow{d/dx}\; \cos x \;\xrightarrow{d/dx}\; -\sin x \;\xrightarrow{d/dx}\; -\cos x \;\xrightarrow{d/dx}\; \sin x\]
The derivatives cycle with period 4. The signs alternate after the first step. After 4 derivatives, you're back to the original.
Full Proof: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x\) (7 min)
Step 1: \(\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{\sin(x+h)-\sin x}{h}\)

Step 2: Apply \(\sin(x+h) = \sin x\cos h + \cos x\sin h\):
\(\quad = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{\sin x\cos h + \cos x\sin h - \sin x}{h}\)

Step 3: Regroup:
\(\quad = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{\sin x(\cos h - 1) + \cos x\sin h}{h}\)

Step 4: Split into two limits:
\(\quad = \sin x\cdot\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\cos h - 1}{h} + \cos x\cdot\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\sin h}{h}\)

Step 5: Apply special limits (from the Prereq section!):
\(\quad = \sin x\cdot(0) + \cos x\cdot(1) = \cos x\) ✓
The "aha" in Step 5 Students who wrote down the special limits in Section 1 will recognise them here immediately. This is the payoff for introducing them early. Point to their notes: "You wrote these down at the start of class. Here's why." The \(\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\sin h}{h}=1\) was on their notes sheet — now they see it do real work.

Pattern for sec and csc — point this out Notice: \(\sec x\) and \(\csc x\) derivatives both keep the original function and multiply by the "other" function (\(\tan x\) or \(\cot x\)). The co-functions always pick up a negative sign (\(\cos\), \(\cot\), \(\csc\)). These two patterns cut the memorisation nearly in half.
Geometric Visualisation: Why \(\sin' = \cos\) (3 min)
Graph \(y = \sin x\) and \(y = \cos x\) in Desmos simultaneously. Point to each connection: at \(x = \frac{\pi}{2}\): sine is at its peak (horizontal tangent, slope = 0) → cosine = 0 ✓; at \(x = 0\): sine is crossing zero and climbing steeply (slope = 1) → cosine = 1 ✓; at \(x = \pi\): sine is crossing zero and falling steeply (slope = −1) → cosine = −1 ✓. This visual anchors the result in geometric intuition — the derivative of the height-on-the-circle is the horizontal-speed-on-the-circle.
