⚡   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson extends the derivative toolkit from individual functions to combinations of functions — the cases students will encounter constantly in calculus. Every real-world application of derivatives uses at least one of these three rules, and most use more than one in combination. The Chain Rule especially is non-negotiable: implicit differentiation, related rates, and the entire study of composite functions depend on it.

The pedagogical order here is deliberate: Product Rule first (easiest to visualise with the growing rectangle), then Quotient Rule (derived from the Product Rule — so the proof is short), then Chain Rule (the most used and most cognitively demanding). Students who see the Quotient Rule as a consequence of the Product Rule rather than a separate thing to memorise find both rules easier to retain.

The three most critical teacher moves in this lesson are: (1) making the mnemonic physical — have students say "Low d-High minus High d-Low" out loud and write it before they see the formula; (2) requiring students to label \(f\), \(g\), \(f'\), \(g'\) explicitly before applying any rule, at least for the first three examples of each; and (3) making the chain rule's inside-outside structure connect back to the transformation unit — students already understand inside/outside from parent function transformations, and the chain rule is that same structure applied to differentiation.

Learning Objectives
  • State why \((fg)' \neq f'g'\), \(\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' \neq \frac{f'}{g'}\), and \((f\circ g)' \neq f' \circ g'\)
  • Identify inner and outer functions in a composite expression
  • Apply the Product Rule to differentiate a product of two functions
  • Apply the Quotient Rule correctly, with the numerator in the right order
  • Apply the Chain Rule to composite functions with one or more layers
  • Choose the correct rule — or combination of rules — for a given function
  • Interpret the Chain Rule in Leibniz notation: \(\frac{dy}{dx}=\frac{dy}{du}\cdot\frac{du}{dx}\)
  • Apply the five standard chain rule patterns (\([g(x)]^n\), \(\sin(g)\), \(\cos(g)\), \(e^{g}\), \(\ln(g)\))

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The prerequisite bridge is the most important 10 minutes of the lesson — students who cannot identify the inner and outer functions of a composite cannot apply the Chain Rule. Do not rush this section. The composition review is not review; for many students it is new.
  • Always make students label f, g, f′, g′ before applying the Product or Quotient Rule — at least for the first three examples of each rule. Students who apply the rule without labelling make consistent errors in the second term. Labelling is the guard rail.
  • The Quotient Rule sign error is the single most common error in this lesson — write the wrong order (\(fg' - f'g\)) on the board and ask "what's wrong?" before presenting the correct version. Students who see the error first remember the correct order better than students who only see the correct version.
  • Chain Rule needs at least 20 minutes — it is the most used rule in all of calculus and the most cognitively demanding. If you are running short on time, cut one Quotient Rule example, not Chain Rule examples.
  • Partner activities are not optional enrichment — the "Which Rule?" identification activity and the Relay Race are the most effective ways to build the rule-selection fluency that the Practice Zone drills. Budget at least 15 minutes for structured partner/group work.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge (10 min)
Prereq Why Simple Rules Fail + Composition Review + Three Rules Preview 10 min
Why Simple Rules Fail — Show the Three Broken Shortcuts (3 min)
Write these three "rules" on the board and ask students to test them numerically with \(f(x)=x^2\) and \(g(x)=\sin x\):
Three Common Wrong Assumptions
✗ Product\((fg)' = f'g'\). Test: \(f=x^2, g=\sin x\). Wrong answer: \(2x\cos x\). Correct: \(2x\sin x + x^2\cos x\).
✗ Quotient\(\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'}{g'}\). Test: divide. Result is wrong every time.
✗ Chain\((f\circ g)' = f'\circ g'\). Test: \(\sin(x^2)\). Wrong answer: \(\cos(2x)\). Correct: \(2x\cos(x^2)\).
Correct Rules — Preview Only
✓ Product Rule\((fg)' = f'g + fg'\) — two terms, each uses one derivative
✓ Quotient Rule\(\frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\) — subtraction in numerator, denominator squared
✓ Chain Rule\(f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)\) — outer prime times inner prime
Composition Review — Label Inner and Outer (5 min)
The Chain Rule requires identifying which function is "inside" and which is "outside." Run this as a whole-class call-and-response before any Chain Rule instruction.
\(\sin(x^2)\): Outer = \(\sin(u)\), Inner = \(x^2\)  ·  \(e^{\cos x}\): Outer = \(e^u\), Inner = \(\cos x\)
\((3x+1)^5\): Outer = \(u^5\), Inner = \(3x+1\)  ·  \(\ln(\tan x)\): Outer = \(\ln(u)\), Inner = \(\tan x\)
\(\cos^3(2x)\): Three layers — outermost = \(u^3\), middle = \(\cos(v)\), innermost = \(2x\)
Connect to transformation unit Students already understand inside/outside from parent function transformations: \(f(x-h)\) transforms the inside, \(f(x)+k\) transforms the outside. The Chain Rule is that same structure applied to differentiation: inside functions get their own derivative multiplied in. Students who own the transformation inside/outside distinction learn the Chain Rule significantly faster.
Section 2 — Product Rule (20 min)
Product Rule The Growing Rectangle & "First d-Second Plus Second d-First" 20 min
The Growing Rectangle — Run the Animation (5 min)
Open the Growing Rectangle animation full-screen. Press "Grow the Rectangle" and narrate: both sides \(u\) and \(v\) expand simultaneously. The new area is the top strip (\(u \cdot dv\)), the right strip (\(v \cdot du\)), and a tiny corner (\(du \cdot dv \approx 0\)). As \(dx\to0\), the corner vanishes, leaving exactly \(u\,dv + v\,du\). This is the product rule, geometrically.
Narrating the animation "Watch what happens when we let the rectangle grow. The width goes from \(u\) to \(u + du\), and the height goes from \(v\) to \(v + dv\). The new area that appears is: this strip on top — that's \(u\,dv\). This strip on the right — that's \(v\,du\). And this tiny corner — that's \(du \cdot dv\), which is so small we ignore it in the limit. Add the two strips: \(u\,dv + v\,du\). Divide by \(dx\). That's the product rule."
The Algebraic Proof — Key Step is the Clever Trick (7 min)
Animate the 5-step algebraic proof. The critical step is Step 2: adding and subtracting \(f(x+h)g(x)\). Students often ask "where did that come from?" — the answer is that it's an algebraic device to create two recognisable difference quotients from one unfactored expression. It's not intuitive; it's a technique to be noted and accepted.
Step 2 — add and subtract \(f(x+h)g(x)\):
\(\displaystyle\frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - \underline{f(x+h)g(x) + f(x+h)g(x)} - f(x)g(x)}{h}\)

Regroup: \(\displaystyle f(x+h)\cdot\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h} + g(x)\cdot\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\)

As \(h\to0\): \(f(x+h)\to f(x)\), and the two fractions → \(g'(x)\) and \(f'(x)\).
Result: \(f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x) = f'g + fg'\) ✓
Worked Examples — Enforce the Labelling Protocol (8 min)
For every example, require students to write \(f=\), \(g=\), \(f'=\), \(g'=\) before substituting. This habit prevents the most common error: applying the rule to only one term.
Ex 1: \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^2\sin x]\)
\(f=x^2,\; g=\sin x,\; f'=2x,\; g'=\cos x\)
\(= f'g + fg' = 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x\) ✓

Ex 2: \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x\cos x]\)
\(f=e^x,\; g=\cos x,\; f'=e^x,\; g'=-\sin x\)
\(= e^x\cos x + e^x(-\sin x) = e^x(\cos x - \sin x)\) ✓

Ex 3: \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^3\ln x]\)
\(f=x^3,\; g=\ln x,\; f'=3x^2,\; g'=\frac{1}{x}\)
\(= 3x^2\ln x + x^3\cdot\frac{1}{x} = 3x^2\ln x + x^2\) ✓
Ex 3 — the ln x derivative This is students' first encounter with \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}\) in an applied context. Confirm they have it on their previous notes sheet. If not, write it on the board now: \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\).
📈 Calculus Connection — Integration by Parts
The Product Rule reverses to give Integration by Parts: \(\int u\,dv = uv - \int v\,du\). Students who understand the Product Rule proof will find Integration by Parts natural when they encounter it — it's the same clever-trick approach in reverse. Plant this now: "When we get to integration, we'll reverse this exact proof."
Section 3 — Quotient Rule (15 min)
Quotient Rule "Low d-High minus High d-Low" — Proof as Product Rule in Disguise 15 min
The Mnemonic — Say It Together First (2 min)
Before showing any formula, have the whole class say the mnemonic out loud: "Low d-High minus High d-Low, all over Low-Low." Then write the formula. Students who hear the mnemonic first and then see the formula connect the two; students who see the formula first and then learn the mnemonic never quite merge them.
\[\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{f}{g}\right] = \frac{\overbrace{f'g}^{\text{Low d-High}} - \overbrace{fg'}^{\text{High d-Low}}}{\underbrace{g^2}_{\text{Low-Low}}}\]
The Proof — Quotient Rule Follows from Product Rule (5 min)
This proof is short and elegant because it reuses the Product Rule students just proved. Animate the 6-step proof. The key insight is Step 3: \(\frac{d}{dx}[g^{-1}] = -g^{-2}g'\) — this is the Chain Rule applied to a power, previewing the chain rule before it's formally taught.
Why prove it this way? Students who see the Quotient Rule derived from the Product Rule understand both rules as part of a connected system rather than two separate things to memorise. The derivation also shows the Chain Rule lurking (\(-g^{-2}g'\)) — a natural lead-in to Section 4.
★ The Sign Error Trap — Demo First (3 min)
Write the wrong order on the board before the correct one:
❌ Wrong — This Loses Points
Wrong numerator order\(\dfrac{fg' - f'g}{g^2}\) — subtraction backwards
On every AP exam, roughly half of students who use the Quotient Rule get this wrong. The error is almost always the sign.
✓ Correct — Low d-High First
Correct numerator order\(\dfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\) — "Low d-High" is first
The mnemonic "Low d-High minus High d-Low" hard-codes the order: derivative of numerator first, then derivative of denominator second.
Worked Examples (5 min)
Ex 1: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{x^2}{\sin x}\right]\) — \(f=x^2, g=\sin x, f'=2x, g'=\cos x\)
\(= \dfrac{2x\sin x - x^2\cos x}{\sin^2 x}\) ✓

Ex 2: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[\tan x] = \dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}\right]\) — \(f=\sin x, g=\cos x, f'=\cos x, g'=-\sin x\)
\(= \dfrac{\cos x\cdot\cos x - \sin x\cdot(-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} = \dfrac{\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x}{\cos^2 x} = \dfrac{1}{\cos^2 x} = \sec^2 x\) ✓

Show this second example — it reproves the \(\tan x\) derivative and shows the Pythagorean identity in action.
Section 4 — Chain Rule (20 min)
Chain Rule "Peel the Onion" — Outer Prime Times Inner Prime 20 min
Visual: The Onion — Differentiate Outside In (4 min)
Open the Onion Peeling visualisation. Click each layer. Narrate: "Start at the outermost shell — differentiate it, keep the inside untouched. Then multiply by the derivative of what was inside. Peel one layer at a time, always moving inward." For multiple layers (\(\cos^3(2x)\)), three clicks demonstrate the principle better than any explanation.
The chain rule in plain English "The Chain Rule says: when a function is nested inside another function, you get two derivatives for the price of one. Differentiate the outer — but leave the inner exactly as it is. Then multiply by the derivative of the inner. That multiplication is the Chain Rule. It's the correction factor for the fact that the inside is also changing."
The Leibniz Proof — The \(\frac{\Delta u}{\Delta u}\) Trick (5 min)
Animate the 5-step proof. The pivotal step is Step 3: multiply by \(\frac{\Delta u}{\Delta u}\). As with the Product Rule's Step 2, this is a deliberate algebraic device — not intuitive, but mathematically valid. Students who understand that both proofs rely on "multiply by a clever 1" retain both proofs better.
\(\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x\to0}\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \lim_{\Delta x\to0}\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta u}\cdot\dfrac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} \xrightarrow{\Delta x\to0} \dfrac{dy}{du}\cdot\dfrac{du}{dx}\)
The \(\Delta u = 0\) subtlety — mention but don't dwell The proof as written has a minor gap: if \(\Delta u = 0\) for some \(\Delta x\), dividing by \(\Delta u\) is invalid. The rigorous fix (using an epsilon-delta argument) is beyond the scope of this course. Acknowledge it briefly — "there's a technical detail here that calculus textbooks address in more depth" — and move on. Students who ask deserve an honest answer; students who don't ask don't need to know.

Worked Examples — Increasing Complexity (7 min)
Ex 1 (one layer): \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x^2)]\) — outer \(\sin(u)\to\cos(u)\), inner \(x^2\to2x\). Answer: \(2x\cos(x^2)\) ✓

Ex 2 (one layer): \(\frac{d}{dx}[(3x+1)^5]\) — outer \(u^5\to5u^4\), inner \(3x+1\to3\). Answer: \(15(3x+1)^4\) ✓

Ex 3 (two layers): \(\frac{d}{dx}[\cos^3(2x)]\) — rewrite as \([\cos(2x)]^3\).
Layer 1 (outer): \(u^3 \to 3u^2\). Keep inside: \(3[\cos(2x)]^2\).
Layer 2 (inner): derivative of \(\cos(2x)\): \(-\sin(2x)\cdot2 = -2\sin(2x)\).
Answer: \(3\cos^2(2x)\cdot(-2\sin(2x)) = -6\cos^2(2x)\sin(2x)\) ✓

Ex 4 (chain + product): \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^2\sin(3x)]\) — Product Rule with chain inside second factor.
\(f=x^2, f'=2x\); \(g=\sin(3x), g'=3\cos(3x)\) (chain rule on \(g\)).
Answer: \(2x\sin(3x) + 3x^2\cos(3x)\) ✓
📈 Calculus Connections — Chain Rule is Everywhere
Related Rates: \(\frac{dV}{dt} = \frac{dV}{dr}\cdot\frac{dr}{dt}\) is the Chain Rule in Leibniz notation. Any related rates problem is a Chain Rule application. Implicit Differentiation: \(\frac{d}{dx}[y^2] = 2y\cdot\frac{dy}{dx}\) — the extra \(\frac{dy}{dx}\) factor is the Chain Rule treating \(y\) as a function of \(x\). U-Substitution: the Chain Rule reverses to give \(\int f(g(x))g'(x)\,dx = F(g(x))+C\).
Section 5 — Partner & Small Group Activities
The following six activities are designed for the final 15 minutes of class (Relay Race), mid-lesson breaks (Which Rule?), and extensions. Each targets a specific skill gap: rule identification, sign discipline, inside/outside labelling, and combined-rule fluency. Activities marked ★ Core should be used in every section of the class. Activities marked Extension are for early finishers or a second class period.
🃏 Which Rule? — Sort & Justify ★ Core  ·  Pairs
⏱ 8–10 min
👥 Pairs
📍 Any point in lesson
Partners receive a set of 12 function cards (pre-printed or written on the board). Each card shows one function. Partners sort the cards into three piles: Product Rule, Quotient Rule, Chain Rule — and discuss any that could require two rules. The goal is rule identification, not computation.
Suggested function set
  • \(x^3\cos x\) (Product)
  • \(\dfrac{e^x}{x^2}\) (Quotient)
  • \(\sin(x^3)\) (Chain)
  • \(x^2 e^{3x}\) (Product + Chain on second factor)
  • \(\dfrac{\ln x}{x}\) (Quotient)
  • \((x^2+1)^7\) (Chain)
  • \(\sqrt{x^3-2x}\) (Chain — rewrite as \((\ldots)^{1/2}\))
  • \(e^x\sin(2x)\) (Product + Chain on second)
After sorting: partners compare with another pair. Any disagreements become class discussion problems. Functions that require two rules are the richest discussion prompts.
🏁 Derivative Relay Race ★ Core  ·  Groups of 3
⏱ 10–12 min
👥 Groups of 3
📍 End of lesson
Each group gets one problem requiring all three rules in sequence. Student 1 identifies \(f\), \(g\), and which rule applies. Student 2 computes \(f'\) and \(g'\) (applying Chain Rule where needed). Student 3 assembles the final answer using the Product or Quotient Rule template. The relay structure forces every student to engage with a different step.
Suggested relay problems
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[x^2\sin(3x)\right]\) — Product; Chain on second factor
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{e^{2x}}{x^3+1}\right]\) — Quotient; Chain on numerator
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\cos^2(x^2)\right]\) — Chain ×2 (two layers)
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[e^x\ln(x^2+1)\right]\) — Product; Chain on second factor
Have groups write each step on a mini-whiteboard and hold it up before moving to the next step. This surfaces errors before the final answer is written.
🏥 Error Hospital — Find & Fix ★ Core  ·  Pairs
⏱ 8 min
👥 Pairs
📍 After Quotient Rule
Partners receive a set of 4–6 worked derivatives, each with exactly one error (wrong sign in Quotient Rule, missing chain rule factor, labelling wrong function as \(f\) vs. \(g\), forgetting to square the denominator). Partners diagnose the error and write the correct solution.
Error set (write on board or print)
  • "Patient 1": \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{\sin x}{x^2}\right] = \frac{x^2\sin x - 2x\cos x}{x^4}\) — ERROR: numerator order is backwards (should be \(\cos x \cdot x^2\) first)
  • "Patient 2": \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x^2)] = \cos(x^2)\) — ERROR: missing inner derivative \(\cdot 2x\)
  • "Patient 3": \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^3 e^x] = 3x^2 \cdot e^x\) — ERROR: only one term of Product Rule; missing \(+x^3 e^x\)
  • "Patient 4": \(\frac{d}{dx}[(x^2+1)^4] = 4(x^2+1)^3\) — ERROR: missing inner derivative \(\cdot 2x\)
After 6 minutes, call on pairs to explain each diagnosis — not just state the answer, but name the rule that was violated and why. This builds the metacognitive language ("I forgot the chain rule factor on the inner function") that helps students self-correct during exams.
📱 Desmos Verification Sprint ★ Core  ·  Individual/Pairs
⏱ 6–8 min
👥 Individual or pairs
📍 After any worked section
Students differentiate 4 functions by hand, then verify each one in Desmos using the \(\frac{d}{dx}\) notation. In Desmos, type \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)]\) to see the derivative graph, then compare it to their computed \(f'(x)\). If the graphs match, the derivative is correct. If not, students must find their error before checking with a partner.
How to use Desmos to verify derivatives
  • Type \(f(x)=x^2\sin(3x)\) in line 1
  • Type \(f'(x)\) in line 2 — Desmos computes it symbolically
  • Type your computed answer in line 3
  • If lines 2 and 3 overlap exactly — correct. If not — hunt the error.
This activity converts Desmos from an answer-checking tool into a debugging tool. Students who use it correctly develop self-correction skills rather than answer-copying habits.
🧅 Inside-Outside Labelling Race Extension  ·  Pairs
⏱ 6 min
👥 Pairs
📍 Chain Rule intro
Pairs race to correctly label the outer and inner functions of 10 composite expressions before the timer runs out. No derivatives — just identification. Speed matters here because fluent composition-reading is the bottleneck for Chain Rule fluency.
Expression set
  • \(e^{\tan x}\), \(\ln(\cos x)\), \(\sqrt{x^2+1}\), \(\sin^4 x\)
  • \(\tan(e^x)\), \((\ln x)^3\), \(\cos(\sqrt{x})\), \(e^{x^2+x}\)
  • \(\sin^3(2x)\) — three layers; \(\ln(\sin(x^2))\) — three layers
Score: 1 point per correct outer, 1 point per correct inner. Three-layer functions are worth 3 points each. After the round, pairs swap and mark each other's work.
🔥 Combo Challenge — All Three Rules Extension  ·  Groups of 3–4
⏱ 12–15 min
👥 Groups of 3–4
📍 End of class or Day 2
Each group receives four problems requiring combinations of all three rules. Groups must: (1) identify which rules are needed and in what order, (2) show all labelling, (3) produce a final answer, and (4) check with Desmos. One group presents each problem to the class with narration of each step.
Combo problems
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[x^3\cos(x^2)\right]\) — Product + Chain
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{e^{3x}}{\sin x}\right]\) — Quotient + Chain on numerator
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[(x^2+1)^4\ln(x)\right]\) — Product + Chain on first factor
  • \(\frac{d}{dx}\!\left[\frac{\cos^2(x)}{x^2+1}\right]\) — Quotient + Chain on numerator
Require all steps written out before Desmos check — no shortcuts. Groups that find a discrepancy with Desmos must present their error diagnosis as well as the corrected answer. Public error correction is the best learning activity in this lesson.
Exit Ticket & Answers
Close Exit Ticket — Five Problems 5 min
ProblemKey Steps / Rule UsedAnswer
ET1: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[x^4\cos x]\) Product. \(f=x^4,f'=4x^3; g=\cos x, g'=-\sin x\). Two terms. \(4x^3\cos x - x^4\sin x\)
ET2: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{e^x}{x^2+1}\right]\) Quotient. Low \(=x^2+1\), High \(=e^x\). Low d-High \(-\) High d-Low over Low². \(\dfrac{e^x(x^2+1) - 2xe^x}{(x^2+1)^2} = \dfrac{e^x(x^2-2x+1)}{(x^2+1)^2}\)
ET3: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[(3x^2+1)^5]\) Chain. Outer \(u^5\to5u^4\). Inner \(3x^2+1\to6x\). Multiply. \(30x(3x^2+1)^4\)
ET4: \(\dfrac{d}{dx}[e^x\sin(2x)]\) Product + Chain. \(f=e^x, f'=e^x\). \(g=\sin(2x), g'=2\cos(2x)\) (chain). \(e^x\sin(2x) + 2e^x\cos(2x) = e^x(\sin(2x)+2\cos(2x))\)
ET5: Identify all rules needed (no computation): \(\dfrac{d}{dx}\!\left[\dfrac{(x^2+1)^3}{\cos(x)}\right]\) Quotient Rule overall; Chain Rule on the numerator \((x^2+1)^3\). No chain needed on denominator (\(\cos x\) is already a base function). Quotient + Chain (on numerator)
