Learning Objectives
  • Students understand the purpose and structure of the course
  • Binders are set up with all 7 tabs correctly labeled
  • Teacher has diagnostic data on 4 skill clusters for each student
  • Students have articulated at least one personal math goal
  • Community tone is set — growth-focused, low-stakes, curious
Materials Needed
  • 3-ring binders (1 per student) + 7 tab dividers
  • Printed: Day 1 Welcome Packet (calcbridge_day1_goalcard.html)
  • Printed: Skills Pre-Assessment (calcbridge_preassessment.html)
  • Sticky notes or index cards (exit ticket)
  • Projector / screen for images + Desmos
  • Background music playlist (optional but recommended)
  • Markers for vocabulary wall (start today)
⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • Binder setup always takes longer than expected — have tabs pre-labeled or a student helper demonstrating at the front while others follow along.
  • Pre-assessment is the hard stop — 30 minutes regardless of completion. Unfinished problems are useful data too. Have filler activities ready for early finishers.
  • Goal cards need silence — not even music. Two minutes of quiet reflection signals from the start that this is a thinking space.
  • If you run short, skip the full debrief of the Notice & Wonder and go straight to course overview. The opening energy matters more than the activity completion.

"Calculus Everywhere" — Notice & Wonder
10 min
Project 3–4 images one at a time. Ask: what do these have in common? Students pair-share for 2 minutes, then 3–4 call-outs to the whole class.
  • 1Project image 1: a roller coaster curve photographed from the side
  • 2Project image 2: a car speedometer mid-acceleration
  • 3Project image 3: a world population graph from 1800 to now
  • 4Project image 4: a river bend seen from above (gradient of flow)
  • 5Pair-share 2 min: "What do all four of these have in common?"
  • 6Reveal: they all involve how fast something is changing — that's calculus
Teacher Note Don't over-explain the answer. Let students feel good about whatever they noticed. The goal is curiosity and buy-in, not correctness. Save the word "derivative" — it will land much harder in Week 2 after they've earned it.

Skills Pre-Assessment
30 min
Distribute the pre-assessment. Students work independently. Calm background music is appropriate here. Hard stop at 30 minutes regardless of completion.
Before distributing — say this "This is not a test. Nothing on here is graded and I am not judging you on it. This is a map — it tells both of us where we're starting so we can figure out where to focus. If you don't know something, skip it and move on. If something feels shaky even though you got an answer, circle it. Circles are just as useful as correct answers."
  • 1Distribute assessment face-down, turn over together on your signal
  • 2Remind: skip freely, circle shaky problems, no phones/notes
  • 3Play calm instrumental music at low volume
  • 4Circulate — don't answer math questions, do reassure anxious students
  • 5Call time at exactly 30 minutes — collect all papers
After class — scoring guide Don't mark right/wrong. For each student, make a quick 4-column grid (A/B/C/D) and note: ✓ solid, ~ shaky, ✗ blank/wrong. Look for class-wide patterns — if 80% miss A3 (factoring), that becomes a priority warm-up. Use the data to adjust Week 1 pacing.

Filler Activities
When to use these: Early finishers on the pre-assessment, any block that runs short, or if binder setup finishes faster than expected. Each activity is self-contained — no setup needed unless noted. Ranked within each prep tier by how well they fit the Day 1 tone.
Zero Prep
"What is Calculus?" Brainstorm
5 – 8 min Zero prep
Students write every word or phrase they associate with calculus for 90 seconds — anything heard, seen, feared, or guessed. Collect one word per person on the board to create a class word cloud.
How to run it
  • Say: "90 seconds, write every word that comes to mind when you think about calculus. No wrong answers."
  • Go around the room — one word per person on the whiteboard
  • Leave it up. Return to it on the last day.
"Which word surprised you the most when you heard someone else say it?"
Two Truths and a Lie — Math Edition
8 – 12 min Zero prep
Each student writes three statements about themselves and math — two true, one false. Share in pairs, then a few to the whole class. Builds community and reveals math identities quickly.
How to run it
  • Give 2 minutes to write three statements (math-related)
  • Pair-share: partners try to identify the lie
  • 3–4 volunteers share with the whole group
"I've always been good at algebra."  ·  "I failed a math test last year."  ·  "I actually enjoy graphing."
My Math Timeline
10 – 15 min Zero prep
Students draw a horizontal line on any blank paper and label it K through current grade. Mark high points (topics or years they felt strong) and low points (hard topics or years). Share with a partner.
How to run it
  • Draw the line and label — 1 minute
  • Mark their own highs and lows — 3 minutes (play music)
  • Share with a partner — 3 minutes
  • Optional: 2–3 volunteers share a low point and what helped
"What would have made that low point easier?"
Rate of Change in the Room
6 – 10 min Zero prep
Students look around the room and find three things that are changing right now — temperature, light, noise, their own focus level. For each one: how would you measure how fast it's changing? What units?
How to run it
  • Pose the question verbally — no paper needed
  • 1 minute quiet thinking, then pair-share
  • Whole-class share-out — list their answers on the board
  • Point out: every answer has a rate and a unit — that's calculus
"If you had to measure how fast your attention is changing right now, what unit would you use?"
Low Prep — needs projector
Slow Reveal Graph
8 – 12 min Low prep
Project a real-world graph — population, stock price, temperature — cropped so axes aren't visible. Reveal more in stages. Students guess what's being measured and what's happening. Forces graphical reasoning with incomplete information.
How to run it
  • Find a graph online (NYT Upshot, Our World in Data, FRED) and crop it
  • Show just the curve shape first — "What do you notice? What might this be?"
  • Reveal the y-axis — does that change their guess?
  • Reveal the x-axis — full context now. Were they right?
"What would you need to know to predict what happens next on this graph?"
"Estimate It" Chain
8 – 10 min Low prep
A chain of three escalating estimation questions. The third question is secretly an average rate of change — students solve a calculus concept without knowing it yet.
Questions to project
  • "How many floor tiles are in this room?" — estimate, pair-share
  • "How fast does human hair grow, in inches per year?" — estimate, pair-share
  • "A car goes from 0 to 60 mph in 4 seconds. What's the average rate of speed gain per second?" — this is \(\frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t}\)
"That last question — without knowing it, you just computed an average rate of change. That's the first idea in calculus."
Medium Prep — print once, reuse every year
Would You Rather… Math Edition
8 – 12 min Medium prep
Project or read a series of math dilemmas. Students vote and briefly defend. The last question usually sparks a real conversation about mathematical understanding.
Questions to project or print
  • "Solve 10 quadratic equations  or  factor 10 polynomials?"
  • "Calculus with no textbook  or  Calculus with no calculator?"
  • "Know the answer but not why  or  know why but not the answer?"
  • "Retake a test you failed  or  never have it count in the first place?"
The third question is genuinely worth 5 minutes of class discussion — it surfaces what students believe math is actually for.
Function or Not a Function? Card Sort
12 – 18 min Medium prep
Print 12–15 small cards: some showing functions (graphs, tables, mapping diagrams), some not. Students sort into two piles in pairs and defend their choices. Print, laminate, reuse every year.
Cards to include
  • A circle (not a function — fails vertical line test)
  • A vertical line (not a function)
  • A mapping diagram with two arrows from one input (not a function)
  • A parabola opening upward (function ✓)
  • A table with two rows having the same x-value but different y-values (not a function)
  • A table where x-values repeat but y-values match (function ✓)
"What's the one rule a relation has to follow to be a function?"
If You Have 15+ Minutes Left
Early Preview — Jump into Day 2 (Functions Deep Dive)
15 – 25 min Needs Desmos
If you have significant time left, don't fill it with games — use it. Launch directly into the first skill lesson from Day 2 as a preview. Frame it as bonus time, not extra class.
How to run it
  • Open Desmos on the projector. Type f(x) = x^2
  • "I want to show you something. Watch what happens when I change this number." Change the exponent, coefficient, and add constants — say nothing, just let them watch.
  • After 90 seconds: "What are you noticing? What's changing? What stays the same?"
  • Pair-share, then 3–4 call-outs. Start building the vocabulary of transformations organically.
  • Close with: "Tomorrow we go deeper on exactly this. You're already thinking about it."
"If I add a number outside the parentheses vs. inside the parentheses, does it matter? Make a prediction."
