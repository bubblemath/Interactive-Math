🔑   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson builds fluency with four equation types that appear constantly in calculus — radical, absolute value, exponential, and logarithmic equations. The unifying theme is the inverse operation strategy: every equation is solved by identifying what operation is being done and undoing it. That framing connects directly to how calculus students think about derivatives and integrals as inverse processes.

The most important skill of the day is not solving the equations — it's catching extraneous solutions. Every equation type here can produce ghost solutions, and a calculus student who doesn't habitually check solutions in the original equation will make systematic errors in optimisation, related rates, and differential equations. Build that checking habit explicitly today.

The interactive lesson at bubblemath.github.io provides animated step walkthroughs, a practice drill zone, and Desmos visualisations. Students should have the site open on their devices alongside the printed student notes.

Learning Objectives
  • Solve radical equations by isolating and squaring; identify extraneous solutions
  • Solve absolute value equations using the two-path (±) strategy
  • Solve exponential equations by taking \(\ln\) or \(\log\) of both sides
  • Solve logarithmic equations using log properties and converting to exponential form
  • Apply the extraneous solution check to all four equation types
  • Articulate how each equation type appears in calculus contexts

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • Section 3 (Exponential/Log) is the hardest to pace — log properties alone could fill 30 minutes. Keep to one worked example per property and move on. Students consolidate through the practice drill, not through more examples from you.
  • The extraneous solution section (Section 4) is non-negotiable — even 10 minutes here is worth protecting. Every other skill in this lesson is useless if students don't habitually check solutions.
  • Practice Zone (Section 5) works best as independent or paired work — circulate and watch for students who solve correctly but skip the check. That's your most important correction of the day.
  • Calculus connections are woven throughout — don't skip them. They're what makes this feel relevant rather than review.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge (10 min)
Prereq The Golden Rule: Undo Operations With Inverses 10 min
Opening Hook
Before any instruction, pose the Quick Check from the interactive site to the whole class: "To solve \(e^{2x} = 15\), you should — square both sides, take ln of both sides, take log₁₀, or square root?" Take a show of hands. Don't confirm the answer yet — use the disagreement to motivate the section.
Core Framing — Say This
Script "Every equation you'll ever solve in this course comes down to one question: what operation is being done to \(x\), and what undoes it? Square root undoes squaring. A logarithm undoes an exponential. That's it. Everything today is a variation on that idea."
Inverse Pairs to Cover
Display the five inverse pairs from the site. Students copy them onto their notes. Stress the domain restrictions — these are not optional footnotes, they are the reason extraneous solutions exist:
  • \(\sqrt{x}\) requires \(x \geq 0\) — output always \(\geq 0\)
  • \(\ln(x)\) requires \(x > 0\) — no log of zero or negatives
  • \(e^x > 0\) always — output is never zero or negative
📈 Calculus Connection
Setting \(f'(x) = 0\) to find critical points frequently produces radical, exponential, or log equations. Finding intercepts (\(f(x)=0\)) and integration bounds also require these skills. This is not just algebra review — these equations appear in every major calculus topic.
Teacher Note — now confirm the Quick Check Answer: take \(\ln\) of both sides. Ask a student to explain why — the correct reasoning is "ln and \(e^x\) are inverses, so \(\ln(e^{2x}) = 2x\)." Students who say "because ln cancels the e" are close but imprecise. Push for the inverse language.
Section 2 — Radical & Absolute Value (20 min)
Radical/AV Discovery Lab — Two-Path Strategy & Square-Both-Sides 20 min
Absolute Value — The Two-Path Strategy (7 min)
Key insight: \(|ax + b| = c\) means the expression is \(c\) units from zero — which happens in exactly two places on the number line (positive and negative). Work through the site's example live on the projector with students following on their notes sheet.
Solve \(|2x - 3| = 7\)
Path 1: \(2x - 3 = 7 \Rightarrow x = 5\)
Path 2: \(2x - 3 = -7 \Rightarrow x = -2\)
Solutions: \(x = 5\) and \(x = -2\)
Special Cases — Emphasise These \(|x| = 0\) → one solution only (\(x = 0\)). \(|x| = -5\) → no solution (absolute value is never negative). These two cases cause persistent errors on timed assessments. Write them on the board as warnings before moving on.
Radical Equations — Even vs. Odd Index (4 min)
Cover the critical distinction: even-index roots (square root, 4th root) require non-negative radicands and produce non-negative outputs only. Odd-index roots (cube root, 5th root) accept all real inputs and produce all real outputs. This distinction determines whether an equation can have no solution from the start.
The "Square Both Sides" Animation (9 min)
Run the animated walkthrough for \(\sqrt{x+3} = x-3\) on the projector — animate one step at a time and ask students to predict each step before clicking. This is the core procedure students need to own.
Solve \(\sqrt{x+3} = x-3\)
Step 1 — Radical is already isolated
Step 2 — Square both sides: \(x + 3 = (x-3)^2 = x^2 - 6x + 9\)
Step 3 — Rearrange: \(x^2 - 7x + 6 = 0 \Rightarrow (x-1)(x-6) = 0\)
Step 4 — CHECK: \(x=1\): \(\sqrt{4} = -2\)? NO ✗  |  \(x=6\): \(\sqrt{9} = 3\)? YES ✓
Answer: \(x = 6\) only
The check moment — make this dramatic "Look what happened — we got two solutions algebraically, and one of them is completely wrong. Not approximately wrong. Completely wrong. \(x=1\) gives \(\sqrt{4} = -2\), which is false. This is why we always check. Always. Every time."
✗ Common Error 1
\(\sqrt{x} = -4\) → square both sides → \(x = 16\)
Even root output is always ≥ 0. No checking needed — the equation has no solution by inspection.
✗ Common Error 2
Squaring before isolating: \(\sqrt{x+3} + 2 = 7\) → squared immediately
Must isolate the radical first: \(\sqrt{x+3} = 5\), then square. Squaring with terms on both sides creates a mess.
📈 Calculus Connection
Radical equations appear in implicit differentiation (\(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=5\)), related rates (distance formulas), and optimisation (solving \(f'(x)=0\) when \(f'\) contains a square root). The extraneous solution habit built today directly prevents errors in those contexts.
Section 3 — Exponential & Logarithmic Workshop (15 min)
Exp/Log The Fundamental Relationship & Log Properties 15 min
The Core Relationship (3 min)
This single equivalence is the foundation of the entire section:
\(b^x = y \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \log_b(y) = x\)
Practice the "Base Switcher" — students call out the log form of exponential statements and vice versa. \(2^5 = 32\) → \(\log_2(32)=5\). \(\log_3(81)=4\) → \(3^4=81\). Do 4 rapid-fire examples before moving to properties.
Log Properties — One Example Each (4 min)
Product: \(\log(xy) = \log x + \log y\). Quotient: \(\log(\frac{x}{y}) = \log x - \log y\). Power: \(\log(x^n) = n\log x\). Change of base: \(\log_b x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln b}\). One example per rule — students write them on their notes. Do not over-explain; the practice drill reinforces these.
The Biggest Log Mistake — Put This on the Board \(\log(a+b) \neq \log a + \log b\). There is no sum rule for logarithms. This error is catastrophic in calculus and appears constantly. Write it with a large ✗, say it twice, and ask students to add it to their Common Mistake Log.
Solving Exponential Equations (4 min)
Work through \(e^{2x} = 15\) using the animated walkthrough:
\(e^{2x} = 15\)
Take \(\ln\) of both sides: \(\ln(e^{2x}) = \ln(15)\)
Simplify: \(2x = \ln(15)\)
Solve: \(x = \dfrac{\ln(15)}{2} \approx 1.354\)
Solving Logarithmic Equations (4 min)
Work through \(\log(x) + \log(x-3) = 1\) — this example requires combining logs, converting to exponential form, solving a quadratic, AND checking for domain violations. It's the most complete example in the lesson.
Combine: \(\log(x(x-3)) = 1\)
Convert: \(x(x-3) = 10\)
Solve: \(x^2-3x-10=0 \Rightarrow x=5\) or \(x=-2\)
Check: \(x=-2\) → \(\log(-2)\) undefined ✗  |  \(x=5\) ✓
Answer: \(x = 5\) only
📈 Calculus Connections
Derivatives: \(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\) (the only function equal to its own derivative) and \(\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}\). Integration: \(\int \frac{1}{x}\,dx = \ln|x| + C\). Growth/decay: differential equations \(\frac{dy}{dt} = ky\) have exponential solutions. These are three of the most frequently tested calculus topics.
Section 4 — The Extraneous Solution Detective (10 min)
★ Detective The Most Critical Skill — Catching Ghost Solutions 10 min
Why Ghost Solutions Appear — Three Causes
Squaring both sides (makes negatives positive, creating false matches). Log domain violations (solution makes an argument ≤ 0). Absolute value cases (one path may produce a solution that fails the original). Use the interactive "Solution Burner" to demonstrate the check process for \(\sqrt{x+2} = x\).
The Verification Checklist
The site provides a 5-item checklist. Students add it to their notes sheet. It is worth reading every item aloud:
  • Did you isolate the radical/log/exponential first?
  • Did you apply the inverse operation correctly?
  • Did you solve the resulting equation?
  • Did you check every solution in the original equation?
  • Did you verify domain restrictions and reject all extraneous solutions?
Closing emphasis on checking — say this "In calculus, you'll be solving equations like these inside bigger problems — optimisation, related rates, differential equations. If you don't check solutions, you will sometimes report a solution that makes a function undefined, or that corresponds to a local minimum when you needed a maximum. The checking habit you build today is not review — it's calculus preparation."
📈 Calculus Connection
When finding critical points where \(f'(x)=0\), extraneous solutions are points outside the domain of \(f\) — they don't correspond to any actual feature of the graph. Continuity analysis also requires identifying where functions "break" — which is exactly what domain violations represent.
Section 5 — Practice Zone (15 min)
Practice Drill Zone + Spot the Error Challenge 15 min
How to Run the Practice Zone
Students work on the interactive drill at their own pace. Suggested sequence: Radicals first (most connected to today's animation), then Absolute Value, then Exponential/Log, then Mixed + Extraneous. The timer feature is optional — encourage students to start untimed and add the timer once they feel confident. The Streak counter is motivating for competitive students.
What to Watch For While Circulating
  • → Students who solve correctly but skip the check — correct this immediately and explicitly
  • → Students who write \(\log(a+b) = \log a + \log b\) — this error needs one-on-one correction
  • → Students stuck on absolute value — ask "how many places on the number line is the expression that many units from zero?"
  • → Students who set up \(\sqrt{x}=-4\) and try to solve — remind them: even root output is always ≥ 0, so by inspection this has no solution
Spot the Error Challenge (closing 3 min)
Use the "Spot the Error" challenge from the site as a whole-class close. Option A: \(\sqrt{x}=-3 \Rightarrow x=9\). Option B: No solution. Ask for a show of hands, then call on a student to explain why B is correct. The explanation should reference output restrictions, not just "the rule."
Exit Ticket & Transition
Close Exit Ticket & Connection Forward 5 min
Exit Ticket — Three Problems
ET1: Solve and check: \(\sqrt{2x-1} = x - 2\)
ET2: Solve: \(e^{3x} = 20\). Give exact and approximate answers.
ET3: Solve: \(\log(x+6) + \log(x-1) = 1\). Check for extraneous solutions.
Answers ET1: \(x^2-5x+5=0 \Rightarrow x=\frac{5\pm\sqrt{5}}{2}\); check both — \(x=\frac{5-\sqrt{5}}{2}\approx1.38\) is extraneous (gives negative right side), so \(x=\frac{5+\sqrt{5}}{2}\approx3.62\) only. ET2: \(x=\frac{\ln 20}{3}\approx0.999\). ET3: combine → \(\log(x^2+5x-6)=1\) → \(x^2+5x-16=0\) → \(x=\frac{-5\pm\sqrt{89}}{2}\); check: negative root makes \(x+6<0\), so only positive root.
📈 Connection Forward
All four equation types from today appear in the derivative rules students will learn in calculus. The chain rule produces equations with \(e^{f(x)}\) and \(\ln(f(x))\). The quotient rule often produces equations requiring the radical-solving skills from today. Students who own this lesson will recognise familiar structure in problems that look new.
