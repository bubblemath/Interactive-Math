☕ Cooling Curve Lab: Directions & Formulas

📋 Step-by-Step Directions

Prepare Your Materials

Get a thermometer (digital preferred), a hot beverage (coffee, tea, or hot water), a timer, and your data collection sheet. Find a location where the beverage won't be disturbed.

Record Initial Temperature

Measure and record the starting temperature of your beverage. Also note the room temperature (ambient temperature).

Collect Data Every Minute

Record the temperature every minute for 20 minutes. Keep the thermometer in the same position each time for consistency.

Calculate Average Rates of Change

Find the average rate of change over different intervals: [0,10], [0,5], [0,2], [0,1]. Notice how the rate changes as intervals shrink!

Estimate Instantaneous Rate

Use your shrinking intervals to estimate the instantaneous rate of change at t = 0. This is the derivative!

📐 Key Formulas

Average Rate of Change

\(\frac{\Delta T}{\Delta t} = \frac{T(t_2) - T(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}\)

The slope of the secant line between two points on the cooling curve.

Instantaneous Rate of Change

\(\frac{dT}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta T}{\Delta t}\)

The slope of the tangent line — what we estimate by shrinking intervals.

Newton's Law of Cooling

\(T(t) = T_{room} + (T_0 - T_{room})e^{-kt}\)

The exponential model for cooling. \(T_0\) is initial temp, \(k\) is the cooling constant.

Exponential Regression

\(y = ab^x\) or \(y = ae^{kx}\)

Use Desmos or a calculator to fit an exponential curve to your data.

🔗 Calculus Connection

This project previews the limit definition of the derivative. As your time intervals get smaller, your average rate of change approaches the instantaneous rate — exactly what happens when we take a limit!

🔍 Breaking Point Scavenger Hunt: Directions & Formulas

📋 Step-by-Step Directions

Find 3 Real-World Piecewise Functions

Look for pricing structures, tax brackets, shipping rates, parking fees, or any situation where the "rule" changes at certain thresholds.

Document Each Example

Take photos or screenshots. Record the exact values where the rules change (the "breaking points").

Write Formal Piecewise Notation

Convert each real-world example into proper mathematical piecewise function notation.

Analyze Continuity

Apply the 3-condition continuity checklist at each breaking point. Is the function continuous there?

Calculate One-Sided Limits

Find the left-hand and right-hand limits at each breaking point. Do they match?

📐 Key Formulas & Concepts

Piecewise Function Notation

\(f(x) = \begin{cases} \text{rule}_1 & x < a \\ \text{rule}_2 & x \geq a \end{cases}\)

Different formulas apply in different regions of the domain.

Left-Hand Limit

\(\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x)\)

The value f(x) approaches as x gets close to a from the LEFT (smaller values).

Right-Hand Limit

\(\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)\)

The value f(x) approaches as x gets close to a from the RIGHT (larger values).

Continuity Checklist

1. \(f(a)\) exists
2. \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x)\) exists
3. \(\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)\)

All three must be true for continuity at x = a.

⚠️ Common Examples to Look For

  • Parking garages: First hour $X, each additional hour $Y
  • Cell phone data: Free up to X GB, then $Y per GB
  • Tax brackets: Different rates at income thresholds
  • Shipping costs: Rates change based on weight or distance
  • Utility bills: Tiered pricing for electricity or water

☀️ Shadow Tracker: Directions & Formulas

📋 Step-by-Step Directions

Choose a Fixed Object

Find a vertical object with a known height (fence post, lamp post, stick). Measure and record its height.

Plan Your Measurement Times

Choose 4-5 times spread throughout the day (e.g., 9 AM, 11 AM, 1 PM, 3 PM, 5 PM). Record exact times.

Measure Shadow Lengths

At each time, measure the length of the shadow from the base of the object to the tip of the shadow.

Calculate Sun Angles

Use trigonometry: \(\tan(\theta) = \frac{\text{height}}{\text{shadow length}}\) to find the sun's angle.

Analyze Rate of Change

Calculate how fast the shadow length is changing at different times. When is it changing fastest?

📐 Key Formulas

Sun Angle from Shadow

\(\tan(\theta) = \frac{h}{s}\)

Where h = object height, s = shadow length, θ = sun angle above horizon.

Solving for Angle

\(\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{h}{s}\right)\)

Use inverse tangent to find the angle in degrees.

Slope of Shadow Line

\(m = \frac{h}{s} = \tan(\theta)\)

The slope from shadow tip to object top equals the tangent of the sun angle.

Rate of Shadow Change

\(\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t} = \frac{s_2 - s_1}{t_2 - t_1}\)

How fast is the shadow length changing? (feet per hour)

🔗 Calculus Connection: Related Rates

This project previews related rates — a major calculus topic! The sun angle, shadow length, and time are all connected. When one changes, the others change too. In calculus, you'll learn to find \(\frac{ds}{dt}\) using the chain rule!

🏀 Bouncing Ball Decay Lab: Directions & Formulas

📋 Step-by-Step Directions

Set Up Your Experiment

Find a ball (basketball, tennis ball, etc.) and a flat surface. Set up a measuring tape or mark heights on a wall. Have someone ready to record video.

Drop the Ball

Drop the ball from a measured height (e.g., 100 cm). Record video of the bounces so you can review and measure accurately.

Record Bounce Heights

Review your video and record the maximum height of each bounce for at least 5-6 bounces.

Calculate Rebound Ratio

Find the ratio of each bounce height to the previous bounce. This should be approximately constant!

Model and Predict

Write an exponential model. Predict the 10th bounce height. Analyze the limit as bounces approach infinity.

📐 Key Formulas

Rebound Ratio

\(r = \frac{h_{n+1}}{h_n}\)

The ratio of consecutive bounce heights. Should be constant (between 0 and 1).

Exponential Model

\(h_n = h_0 \cdot r^n\)

Height of bounce n, where \(h_0\) is initial drop height and r is rebound ratio.

Limit as n → ∞

\(\lim_{n \to \infty} h_0 \cdot r^n = 0\)

Since 0 < r < 1, the heights approach 0 (horizontal asymptote).

Total Distance (Bonus)

\(D = h_0 + 2h_0 \cdot \frac{r}{1-r}\)

Total distance traveled using geometric series (down + up for each bounce).

✅ What Makes a Good Rebound Ratio?

  • Basketball: r ≈ 0.75 - 0.85
  • Tennis ball: r ≈ 0.70 - 0.80
  • Golf ball: r ≈ 0.80 - 0.90
  • Super ball: r ≈ 0.85 - 0.95

If your ratios vary a lot, try to identify sources of error!

☕ Cooling Curve: Analysis & Reflection

🤔 Reflection Questions

1. As your time intervals got smaller ([0,10] → [0,5] → [0,2] → [0,1]), what happened to your average rate of change? What value does it seem to be approaching?

2. Why does the cooling rate slow down over time? Connect this to Newton's Law of Cooling.

3. What is the horizontal asymptote of your cooling curve? What does this represent in the real world?

4. How does this experiment connect to the limit definition of the derivative: \(\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\)?

🔗 Calculus Preview

In calculus, you'll learn that for Newton's Law of Cooling \(T(t) = T_{room} + (T_0 - T_{room})e^{-kt}\), the derivative is:

\(\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T_0 - T_{room})e^{-kt}\)

At t = 0, this gives the initial cooling rate. Compare this to your estimated instantaneous rate!

In calculus, you'll solve problems like: "A 6-foot person walks away from a streetlight at 4 ft/sec. How fast is their shadow growing?"

This uses the Chain Rule: \(\frac{ds}{dt} = \frac{ds}{d\theta} \cdot \frac{d\theta}{dt}\)

🔗 Calculus Preview: Infinite Series

The total distance formula comes from summing an infinite geometric series:

\(\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} ar^n = \frac{a}{1-r}\) when \(|r| < 1\)

This is a key topic in Calculus II!
