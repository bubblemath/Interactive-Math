🔑   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson builds the visual library that underlies all of calculus — the 15 parent functions students need to recognise on sight, plus a systematic method for reading any transformation from an equation. The unifying framework is H-S-V (Horizontal → Stretch → Vertical), applied in that order to the master formula \(y = a \cdot f(b(x-h)) + k\).

The most important conceptual insight of the day is the Inside-Outside rule: transformations inside the function argument work oppositely to what students expect, while transformations outside work as expected. This is not arbitrary — it has a genuine mathematical reason, and explaining it (rather than just asserting it) produces students who actually remember which direction is which.

The anchor point method is the practical skill that ties everything together. Rather than memorising where every transformed graph sits, students track 3–4 key points through the transformation. If you own your anchor points, you can sketch any transformed function in under 60 seconds — a skill that pays dividends in limits, derivative sign analysis, and integration bounds all year.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify all 15 parent functions by equation, shape, and key anchor points
  • State domain, range, symmetry, and asymptotes for each parent function
  • Apply the H-S-V method to read transformations from \(y = a \cdot f(b(x-h)) + k\)
  • Track anchor points through horizontal and vertical transformations
  • Explain why horizontal transformations work in the opposite direction
  • Sketch any transformed function by hand using the anchor point method

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • 15 parent functions is a lot of content — use the gallery as a reference, not a lecture. Don't teach each function from scratch. Students should build their reference card while scanning the gallery, stopping to discuss the memory hints and any function they haven't seen. Budget 15 minutes maximum for the gallery section.
  • The H-S-V method needs at least two complete worked examples with full anchor point tracking — one is not enough for this to stick. If the gallery runs short, give that time to the H-S-V worked examples.
  • "Opposite Land" is the most common confusion point — students who nod along during instruction and then consistently shift right when they should shift left have not genuinely understood why. The Inside-Outside explanation fixes this; make sure it lands before practice begins.
  • The Virtual Sketchpad is the most valuable practice activity — it requires students to actually produce a graph by hand, which is what calculus exams demand. Prioritise it over the match activity if time is tight.

Section 1 — Parent Function Gallery (15 min)
Gallery 15 Functions Across 5 Groups — Anchor Points Are the Key 15 min
How to Run the Gallery (not a lecture)
Students open the interactive lesson and scan the gallery while filling in their reference card. You circulate and highlight — only stop the class for: (1) the memory hints, which are genuinely useful mnemonics, (2) the trig functions, which most students know least, and (3) the exponential/log pair, whose inverse relationship is critical for calculus.
The 5 Group Memory Hints — Say Each One Aloud
Polynomials: "Degree Determines Direction" — even degree = U-turn (both ends same). Odd degree = S-curve (opposite ends).
Radicals: "Even Roots Have Starting Points, Odd Roots Go Through" — square root starts at a point; cube root passes through the entire real line.
Rationals: "Forbidden Zones and Mirror Behavior" — \(1/x\) is odd (opposite quadrants); \(1/x^2\) is even (same side, always positive).
Exp/Log: "Mirror Twins Across \(y=x\)" — \(e^x\) passes through \((0,1)\); \(\ln x\) passes through \((1,0)\). They are reflections of each other.
Trig: "Sine Starts at Zero, Cosine Starts at the Top" — \(\sin(0)=0\), \(\cos(0)=1\). Both have domain all reals, range \([-1,1]\), period \(2\pi\). Tangent has period \(\pi\) and vertical asymptotes at \(\frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi\).
Anchor Points — Why They Matter
Anchor points are the 3–4 characteristic points of each parent function that students track through transformations. Knowing them is what makes hand-sketching possible without a calculator. For each function, emphasise the points that are easiest to transform — usually integer outputs or the origin.
Which anchor points to prioritise For polynomials: the origin and \((\pm1, \pm1)\). For radicals: the starting point and where the output is 1, 2, 3. For rationals: \((1,1)\) and \((-1,-1)\) or \((-1,1)\). For exp/log: the y-intercept \((0,1)\) for \(e^x\) and the x-intercept \((1,0)\) for \(\ln x\). For trig: the five key points per period — zeros, peaks, troughs.
📈 Calculus Connection
Recognising parent functions on sight is essential for reading derivative graphs (\(f'\) of a parabola is a line; \(f'\) of \(e^x\) is itself), for setting up integration bounds (knowing where \(\sin x\) crosses zero), and for identifying asymptotic behaviour in limits. The trig group is especially important — \(\sin\), \(\cos\), and \(\tan\) appear in implicit differentiation, related rates, and throughout integration.
Section 2 — The H-S-V Transformation Method (25 min)
H-S-V The Master Formula: \(y = a \cdot f(b(x-h)) + k\) 25 min
The H-S-V Framework
Display the master formula. Introduce each component in order — the order matters because you apply H (horizontal) before S (stretch/reflect) before V (vertical) when sketching:
H
Horizontal
\((x-h)\) → shift \(h\) right
\(b\) → horizontal stretch by \(\frac{1}{b}\)
⚠ "Opposite Land" — \((x-2)\) shifts RIGHT not left
S
Stretch / Reflect
\(|a|>1\) → vertical stretch
\(0<|a|<1\) → vertical compression
\(a<0\) → reflect over x-axis
🧠 "Amplitude Adjuster"
V
Vertical
\(+k\) → shift up
\(-k\) → shift down
✓ Works as expected — "The Elevator"
Worked Example 1 — Reading Parameters (5 min)
\(y = 3\sqrt{x-4} + 2\)
Parent: \(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\)  ·  Anchor points: \((0,0), (1,1), (4,2), (9,3)\)
H: shift right 4  ·  S: vertical stretch by 3  ·  V: shift up 2
Transformed anchors: \((0,0)\to(4,2)\),  \((1,1)\to(5,5)\),  \((4,2)\to(8,8)\),  \((9,3)\to(13,11)\)
How to transform anchor points — the formula For \(y = a \cdot f(x-h) + k\): each anchor \((x,y)\) transforms to \((x+h,\; ay+k)\). Do this for every anchor point on the parent, then connect the resulting points in the same shape as the parent. This is the hand-sketching method.
Worked Example 2 — Full Anchor Tracking (7 min)
\(y = -2(x+1)^2 + 5\)
Parent: \(f(x) = x^2\)  ·  Anchor points: \((0,0), (1,1), (-1,1), (2,4)\)
H: shift left 1 (note: \((x+1) = (x-(-1))\), so \(h=-1\))  ·  S: vertical stretch ×2, reflect over x-axis  ·  V: shift up 5

Script — narrate each column "Watch what happens step by step. First, horizontal: every x-coordinate shifts left 1. Then stretch/reflect: every y-coordinate gets multiplied by negative 2 — that stretches it and flips it. Finally, vertical: every y-coordinate goes up 5. Each step is mechanical. If you know your anchor points and your H-S-V values, you never need to memorise where a transformed graph sits — you calculate it."
📈 Calculus Connection
Recognising transformed functions instantly is critical for: reading derivative graphs (the derivative of \(a \cdot f(x)\) is \(a \cdot f'(x)\) — the vertical stretch carries through), understanding limits at transformed asymptotes (a vertical asymptote at \(x=h\) comes from the horizontal shift), and sketching solution curves for differential equations.
Section 3 — Virtual Sketchpad Workshop (15 min)
Sketchpad Hand-Sketching Transformed Functions — Plot, Draw, Verify 15 min
How to Run the Workshop
Use the interactive site to generate challenges. Students sketch on paper first — not in Desmos — then verify by clicking the Verify button or opening Desmos. The sequence: (1) identify parent and anchor points, (2) read H-S-V parameters from the equation, (3) transform each anchor point, (4) sketch the curve through the transformed points, (5) verify.
Suggested Challenge Sequence
  • → Easy: \(y = (x-2)^2 - 3\) (quadratic, shift only)
  • → Easy: \(y = \sqrt{x+1}\) (square root, horizontal shift only)
  • → Medium: \(y = -|x+3| + 4\) (absolute value, reflect + two shifts)
  • → Medium: \(y = 2e^{x-1} - 3\) (exponential, stretch + two shifts)
  • → Hard: \(y = \frac{-1}{x+2} + 1\) (reciprocal, reflect + two shifts)
  • → Hard: \(y = 3\sin(x - \frac{\pi}{4}) + 1\) (sine, stretch + two shifts)
What to Watch For While Circulating
  • → Students shifting horizontal in wrong direction — redirect to "what value of x makes the inside equal zero?"
  • → Students sketching the shape correctly but missing the asymptote — asymptotes shift too, by the same h and k
  • → Students who apply V before H — the order matters for mental tracking; gently correct
  • → Students who verify in Desmos without sketching first — this defeats the purpose; ask them to close the laptop and sketch first
Section 4 — The Inside-Outside Decoder (10 min)
Decoder "Opposite Land" — The Mathematical Reason Why 10 min
The Inside-Outside Rule
⚠ Inside the function: \(f(b(x-h))\)
Transformations work opposite to expectation. \((x-h)\) shifts the graph to the right by \(h\) (not left). \(b\) causes a compression not a stretch.
✓ Outside the function: \(a \cdot f(x) + k\)
Transformations work as expected. \(+k\) shifts up. \(a>1\) stretches vertically. \(a<0\) reflects over x-axis.
The "Why" — The Substitution Explanation
Most students accept "it just works that way" and then consistently make the error. This explanation actually fixes it:
The substitution explanation — say this "Why does \(f(x-2)\) shift the graph to the right? Ask yourself: for what value of \(x\) does the input to \(f\) equal zero? It's \(x=2\), because \(2-2=0\). The old zero-point of the input, which was at \(x=0\), has moved to \(x=2\). The whole graph slid right. The subtraction pulled the zero-point to the right. That's 'Opposite Land' — subtracting moves right, adding moves left. It's not a trick; it's where the input is zero."
The Matching Activity — Run This Whole-Class
Project the 5-item matching activity from the site. Work through it as a class — for each match, ask a student to explain the reasoning, not just the answer. The answer key is:
✓   Answer Key — Inside-Outside Match
1→B  (\(f(x+3)\): inside, so shifts LEFT 3)  ·  2→A  (\(f(x)-5\): outside, down 5)  ·  3→D  (\(-f(x)\): outside, reflect x-axis)  ·  4→E  (\(f(\frac{1}{2}x)\): inside, horizontal stretch ×2)  ·  5→C  (\(4f(x)\): outside, vertical stretch ×4)
Match 4 — the tricky one Students most often miss match 4: \(f(\frac{1}{2}x)\) causes a horizontal stretch by 2, not a compression. The rule: \(b\) in \(f(bx)\) causes a horizontal change by factor \(\frac{1}{b}\). So \(b=\frac{1}{2}\) means the horizontal scale multiplies by \(\frac{1}{1/2}=2\) — a stretch. The answer \(E\) is \(f(\frac{1}{2}x)\) which appears to be a compression factor but actually produces a stretch. Spend 60 seconds here.
📈 Calculus Connection
The chain rule — one of the most important derivative rules — is precisely the inside-outside relationship in differentiation form. \(\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\). The "inside function" \(g(x)\) contributes its own derivative as a factor. Students who understand inside-outside in transformation terms are significantly better prepared for the chain rule.
Section 5 — Practice Zone (15 min)
Practice Four Drill Types — Build Fluency and Speed 15 min
Drill Sequence
Suggested order: Name That Parent (fastest — builds recognition) → Transformation Decoder (read H-S-V values) → Anchor Points (calculate transformed coordinates) → Write the Equation (hardest — requires working backwards). The timer feature is most useful for the first two drills; keep Anchor Points and Write the Equation untimed.
What to Watch For
  • → Students consistently reversing horizontal shift direction — use the "where does inside equal zero?" question
  • → Students confusing \(|a|\) (stretch factor) with \(a\) (which includes sign) — the reflection comes from the sign, not the magnitude
  • → Students who can decode but can't write the equation — "Write the Equation" drill is the most calculus-relevant because calculus requires producing function equations, not just reading them
Exit Ticket & Answers
Close Exit Ticket — Three Problems 5 min
Problems
ET1: Identify the parent function, state all H-S-V transformations, and list the domain and range: \(y = -2\sqrt{x+3} - 1\)

ET2: Transform the anchor point \((4, 2)\) of \(f(x)=\sqrt{x}\) through the transformation in ET1. Show each H-S-V step.

ET3: Explain in one sentence why \(f(x+3)\) shifts the graph to the LEFT and not the right.
Answers ET1: Parent \(\sqrt{x}\); H: left 3 (\(h=-3\)); S: vertical stretch ×2, reflect over x-axis (\(a=-2\)); V: down 1 (\(k=-1\)). Domain: \(x \geq -3\); Range: \(y \leq -1\). ET2: \((4,2)\): H → \((4+(-3), 2)=(1,2)\); S → \((1,-4)\); V → \((1,-5)\). ET3: \(f(x+3)\) shifts left because the input equals zero when \(x+3=0\), i.e. \(x=-3\) — the zero-point of the function moved left to \(x=-3\).
