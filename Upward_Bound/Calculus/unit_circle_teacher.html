🔑   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson builds the trig fluency that calculus demands constantly — not just knowing unit circle values, but solving equations that produce multiple solutions, identifying which quadrants give which signs, and avoiding the three systematic errors that cause students to lose points on trig problems throughout all of calculus.

The three Common Traps are the most important content of the day. The Division Trap (dividing by a trig function and losing solutions), the Forgetting Solutions Trap (finding one solution when two or more exist), and the Multiple Angle Trap (failing to expand the interval for \(\sin(2x)\) equations) each appear in AP Calculus problems. Make each one memorable — dramatic demonstration beats passive explanation.

The Unwrapping Animation on the site is one of the best visualisations in the entire interactive series. Run it live in full-screen before Section 2. Students who see the sine wave "unroll" from the unit circle remember the connection in a way that no diagram in a textbook produces. Budget 5 minutes for it even if time is tight.

Learning Objectives
  • Recall exact values of \(\sin\theta\) and \(\cos\theta\) for all standard angles on the unit circle
  • Use the reference angle strategy to find all solutions in \([0, 2\pi)\)
  • Apply ASTC quadrant signs to determine which quadrants yield positive or negative values
  • Avoid the Division Trap by factoring instead of dividing by trig functions
  • Find both solutions when an equation has two answers in \([0, 2\pi)\)
  • Handle multiple angle equations by expanding the solution interval correctly
  • Apply the Pythagorean, double-angle, quotient, and cofunction identities

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • Don't spend more than 10 minutes on unit circle value recall — students who know the values already are bored, and those who don't need practice, not lecture. The Practice Zone's "Unit Circle Values" drill is more efficient than reviewing the table from the front of the room.
  • The Unwrapping Animation is time-sensitive in both directions — run it for exactly 4–5 minutes. Less than 4 minutes and students don't have time to absorb it. More than 6 and it becomes passive screen-watching. Play it once automatically, then let one student drag the point manually.
  • The Division Trap demo must be dramatic to stick — write the wrong method in full on the board, let students agree it looks plausible, then reveal the missing solutions by checking. The moment of surprise is what makes students remember it.
  • Multiple Angle Trap needs its own worked example — students consistently forget to expand the interval. Budget 5 minutes for the \(\sin(2x)\) example with the substitution \(u=2x\) written out explicitly.
  • Practice Zone is highest ROI for the last 20 minutes — drill fluency is built through repetition, not more instruction. Set a timer and circulate; don't lecture through the practice block.

Section 1 — Unit Circle Review (10 min)
Prereq Reference Table, ASTC Signs & the Reference Angle Strategy 10 min
Opening Quick Check
Before any instruction, post three quick questions on the board. Give students 90 seconds to answer on paper. Then reveal and use the responses to calibrate how much time the review needs.
QC1:  \(\sin\!\left(\frac{\pi}{3}\right) = ?\)
QC2:  \(\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = ?\)
QC3:  In which quadrants is tangent positive?
Quick Check Answers QC1: \(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\). QC2: \(-\frac{1}{2}\) — the negative sign is what catches students; \(\frac{2\pi}{3}\) is in Q II where cosine is negative. QC3: Q I and Q III (tangent = sin/cos; both positive or both negative). If most students get QC2 wrong, the quadrant sign work needs more emphasis today.
ASTC — "All Students Take Calculus"
Display the ASTC quadrant grid from the site. Have students write the mnemonic on their notes and fill in which functions are positive in each quadrant. The mnemonic goes counter-clockwise starting from Q I: All positive → Sine only → Tangent only → Cosine only.

How to use ASTC to find solutions:
1. Find the reference angle (positive acute angle to nearest x-axis)
2. Check the sign of the value — which letter applies?
3. The two quadrants with that letter give the two solutions
4. Use the quadrant symmetry formula to convert reference angle → actual angle
Reference Angle Strategy — Worked Example
Solve \(\sin\theta = \frac{1}{2}\) on \([0, 2\pi)\)
Step 1 — Reference angle: \(\sin^{-1}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{\pi}{6}\)
Step 2 — Sign check: \(\frac{1}{2} > 0\), so sine is positive → Q I and Q II (the S in ASTC)
Step 3 — Q I solution: \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{6}\)
Step 4 — Q II solution: \(\theta = \pi - \frac{\pi}{6} = \frac{5\pi}{6}\)
Solutions: \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{6}\) and \(\theta = \frac{5\pi}{6}\)
Quadrant Symmetry Formulas — put these on the board Q I: \(\theta = \alpha\)  ·  Q II: \(\theta = \pi - \alpha\)  ·  Q III: \(\theta = \pi + \alpha\)  ·  Q IV: \(\theta = 2\pi - \alpha\)
where \(\alpha\) is the reference angle. Students copy these onto their notes and use them as a formula during practice. The Q III formula (\(\pi + \alpha\)) is the one most often forgotten.
📈 Calculus Connection
Solving \(f'(x) = 0\) for trig functions requires exactly this skill. Finding critical points of \(f(x) = \sin x\) means solving \(\cos x = 0\) — two solutions per period. Integration bounds for trigonometric integrals also come from solving trig equations. This is foundational.
Section 2 — The Unwrapping Animation (10 min)
Animation Where the Sine Wave Comes From — Circle to Graph 10 min
Running the Animation — Step by Step
  • → Open the Unwrapping Animation section on the projector in full-screen
  • → Set speed to 1× and press Auto Play — say nothing for the first full rotation. Let students watch.
  • → After one loop, pause and ask: "What were you watching? What was the sine wave showing?"
  • → Play again, this time narrating: "The y-coordinate of that point on the circle — that is sin(θ). We're literally reading off the height of the point and plotting it against the angle."
  • → Switch to Cosine. Play once more. "Same circle, same point — now we're reading the x-coordinate."
  • → Call one student to the projector to drag the point manually around the circle while others watch the wave respond.
Key Observations to Draw Out
After the animation, ask students to state each of these in their own words before writing them on the notes:
1. Sine starts at 0 (point is at the 3 o'clock position, y = 0)
2. Cosine starts at 1 (same position, x = 1)
3. Cosine is sine shifted left by \(\frac{\pi}{2}\): \(\cos\theta = \sin\!\left(\theta + \frac{\pi}{2}\right)\)
4. Both have period \(2\pi\) — one complete circle = one full wave
5. The domain of both is all reals; the range of both is \([-1, 1]\)
The key line about the wave "The sine wave is not an abstract curve someone invented. It is literally the shadow of a rotating point — the projection of circular motion onto a flat axis. Every time you see a sine wave in physics, biology, or engineering, something is rotating or oscillating. This animation is why."
📈 Calculus Connection
\(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin x] = \cos x\) and \(\frac{d}{dx}[\cos x] = -\sin x\). The visual intuition is immediate from the animation: when the sine wave is at its peak (at \(\frac{\pi}{2}\)), it's momentarily flat — slope zero — which is exactly when cosine is zero. When sine is crossing zero (at 0 and \(\pi\)), it's climbing or falling steeply — which is exactly when cosine is at its maximum or minimum. Students who saw the animation retain this connection.
Section 3 — Visual Equation Solver, Identity Toolkit & Three Traps (20 min)
Solver + Traps Solving Strategy, Four Identities & Three Errors That Lose Points 20 min
Visual Equation Solver — 3-Minute Demo
Use the site's solver to demonstrate \(\cos(x) = \frac{1}{2}\) visually. Students see both the unit circle view (two highlighted points) and the wave view (horizontal line intersecting the cosine curve twice). The visual makes the "two solutions" expectation intuitive before the algebra. Then demonstrate \(\sin(x) = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\) to show the Q III / Q IV pattern.
Identity Toolkit — One Line Each
IdentityFormulaWhen to Use
Pythagorean\(\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1\)When you see \(\sin^2\) or \(\cos^2\) — convert to single function
Double Angle\(\sin(2x) = 2\sin x\cos x\)When you see \(\sin(2x)\) or a product \(2\sin x \cos x\)
Quotient\(\tan x = \dfrac{\sin x}{\cos x}\)When mixing tangent with sine or cosine in one equation
Cofunction\(\sin x = \cos\!\left(\frac{\pi}{2}-x\right)\)When relating sine and cosine arguments
Pythagorean identity rearrangements — students need both \(\sin^2 x = 1 - \cos^2 x\) and \(\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x\). Students who only know the original form get stuck when they need to substitute. Write both rearrangements on the board alongside the original.
★ The Three Common Traps — Each Needs a Demonstration
🚨 Trap 1 — The Division Trap
✗ Wrong Method \(\sin x \cos x = \sin x\)
Divide both sides by \(\sin x\):
\(\cos x = 1 \Rightarrow x = 0\) only
✓ Correct Method Move to one side: \(\sin x \cos x - \sin x = 0\)
Factor: \(\sin x(\cos x - 1) = 0\)
\(\sin x = 0\) → \(x = 0, \pi\)
\(\cos x = 1\) → \(x = 0\)
Solutions: \(x = 0\) and \(x = \pi\)
Dividing by \(\sin x\) assumes \(\sin x \neq 0\). But \(\sin x = 0\) gives \(x = \pi\) — a valid solution that was thrown away.
🔢 Trap 2 — Forgetting Solutions
✗ Incomplete Answer \(\cos x = \frac{1}{2}\)
Reference angle \(\frac{\pi}{3}\), cosine positive in Q I:
\(x = \frac{\pi}{3}\) only — MISSING \(\frac{5\pi}{3}\)
✓ Complete Answer Cosine positive in Q I and Q IV
Q I: \(x = \frac{\pi}{3}\)
Q IV: \(x = 2\pi - \frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{5\pi}{3}\)
Both solutions required.
Rule: trig equations almost always have two solutions in \([0, 2\pi)\). A single answer should feel suspicious.
🔄 Trap 3 — The Multiple Angle Trap
✗ Common Error Solve \(\sin(2x) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) on \([0, 2\pi)\)
Reference angle \(\frac{\pi}{3}\), sine positive in Q I & II:
\(2x = \frac{\pi}{3}\) and \(2x = \frac{2\pi}{3}\)
\(x = \frac{\pi}{6}\) and \(x = \frac{\pi}{3}\) — ONLY 2 of 4 answers
✓ Correct Method — Expand the Interval Let \(u = 2x\). If \(x \in [0, 2\pi)\), then \(u \in [0, 4\pi)\)
Solve \(\sin u = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) on \([0, 4\pi)\):
\(u = \frac{\pi}{3}, \frac{2\pi}{3}, \frac{\pi}{3}+2\pi, \frac{2\pi}{3}+2\pi\)
Divide by 2: \(x = \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{\pi}{3}, \frac{7\pi}{6}, \frac{4\pi}{3}\)
The rule: if the argument is \(nx\), multiply your interval width by \(n\). For \(\sin(2x)\) on \([0,2\pi)\), work in \([0,4\pi)\). The number of solutions in the interval multiplies by \(n\) as well — expect 4 solutions, not 2.
Section 4 — Spot the Error Challenge (15 min)
Challenge Two-Method Vote — \(2\sin^2(x) - \sin(x) = 0\) 15 min
How to Run This
Project both methods from the site. Give students 2 minutes to decide individually which method is correct. Then take a class vote before any discussion. The false plausibility of Method A is the whole point — many students will vote for it. Use the vote to set up the explanation.
Method A — The Wrong One
✗ Division Trap in disguise \(2\sin^2(x) - \sin(x) = 0\)
Move: \(2\sin^2(x) = \sin(x)\)
Divide by \(\sin(x)\): \(2\sin(x) = 1\)
\(\sin(x) = \frac{1}{2} \Rightarrow x = \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{5\pi}{6}\)
Missing: \(x = 0, \pi\) (the solutions where \(\sin x = 0\))
Method B — The Correct One
✓ Factor — never divide by trig \(2\sin^2(x) - \sin(x) = 0\)
Factor: \(\sin(x)(2\sin(x) - 1) = 0\)
\(\sin(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0, \pi\)
\(2\sin(x) - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \sin(x) = \frac{1}{2} \Rightarrow x = \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{5\pi}{6}\)
All 4 solutions: \(0, \frac{\pi}{6}, \frac{5\pi}{6}, \pi\)
After the vote — make this memorable "Method A got the right-looking answer. It even got two of the four solutions. But it's wrong — and on an AP exam, a partially correct answer with missing solutions is a zero. Method B is exactly one step different: instead of dividing, you move everything to one side and factor. That single habit — factor, never divide — is worth points every time this type appears."
Section 5 — Practice Zone (15 min)
Practice Four Drill Types — Build Speed & Accuracy 15 min
Drill Sequence
Suggested order: Unit Circle Values (fastest — builds recall) → Reference Angles → Quadrant Analysis → Solve Equations (hardest — requires full strategy). Use the timer for the first two drills after students feel comfortable. Keep Solve Equations untimed — accuracy over speed here.
What to Watch For While Circulating
  • → Single solution to a two-solution equation — ask "Is cosine/sine positive or negative in only one quadrant?"
  • → Correct reference angle, wrong quadrant — redirect to ASTC: "Which letter applies to this sign?"
  • → Division of trig functions — any time a student divides by \(\sin x\) or \(\cos x\), stop them: "Move to one side first, then factor"
  • → Multiple angle equations solved with original interval — "What is \(u\) if \(u = 2x\)? What interval does \(u\) live in?"
Exit Ticket & Answers
Close Exit Ticket — Three Problems 5 min
Problems
ET1: Find all solutions in \([0, 2\pi)\):  \(2\cos\theta - \sqrt{3} = 0\)

ET2: Find all solutions in \([0, 2\pi)\):  \(\sin(2\theta) = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\)

ET3: A student solves \(\sin\theta\tan\theta = \sin\theta\) by dividing both sides by \(\sin\theta\) to get \(\tan\theta = 1\), giving \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{4}\) and \(\theta = \frac{5\pi}{4}\). What is wrong, and what are all the correct solutions?
Answers ET1: \(2\cos\theta = \sqrt{3} \Rightarrow \cos\theta = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\). Reference angle \(\frac{\pi}{6}\). Cosine positive in Q I and Q IV. \(\theta = \frac{\pi}{6}\) and \(\theta = \frac{11\pi}{6}\). ET2: Let \(u=2\theta\), \(u \in [0,4\pi)\). \(\sin u = -\frac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\), reference angle \(\frac{\pi}{4}\), sine negative in Q III and Q IV. \(u = \pi+\frac{\pi}{4}=\frac{5\pi}{4},\; 2\pi-\frac{\pi}{4}=\frac{7\pi}{4},\; \frac{5\pi}{4}+2\pi=\frac{13\pi}{4},\; \frac{7\pi}{4}+2\pi=\frac{15\pi}{4}\). Divide by 2: \(\theta = \frac{5\pi}{8}, \frac{7\pi}{8}, \frac{13\pi}{8}, \frac{15\pi}{8}\). ET3: Division by \(\sin\theta\) loses the solutions where \(\sin\theta=0\). Correct: move to one side: \(\sin\theta\tan\theta - \sin\theta = 0\); factor: \(\sin\theta(\tan\theta-1)=0\); so \(\sin\theta=0 \Rightarrow \theta=0,\pi\) AND \(\tan\theta=1 \Rightarrow \theta=\frac{\pi}{4},\frac{5\pi}{4}\). All 4 solutions.
