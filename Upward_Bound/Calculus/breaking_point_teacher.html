🔬   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This is a discovery-based lab, not a lecture. Students are "Function Detectives" who find where functions break by testing values before looking at graphs. The sequence is intentional: test values first → observe calculator output → then view the graph → name the feature. This order builds genuine intuition about why asymptotes and domain restrictions exist, rather than presenting them as rules to memorise.

The four station rotation is the structural heart of the lesson. Each station targets a specific aspect of function behaviour: holes vs. asymptotes (Station A), even vs. odd roots (Station B), the logarithmic limit preview (Station C), and piecewise continuity (Station D). Station D is the most calculus-forward — the piecewise function activity is a direct preview of the formal definition of continuity, which students will see in their first calculus unit.

The career connections section is worth 5 minutes of class time. The Tacoma Narrows Bridge story makes the resonance/asymptote connection visceral and memorable. Students who connect "vertical asymptote" to "bridge collapse" retain both the concept and its real-world stakes.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify the three types of function breaking points: Black Hole, Imaginary Wall, Logarithmic Void
  • Discover domain restrictions by testing values before graphing
  • Distinguish holes (removable discontinuities) from vertical asymptotes
  • Write domains in both interval notation and inequality notation
  • Understand why even roots restrict domain while odd roots do not
  • Connect piecewise function continuity to the formal limit definition
  • Connect function breaking points to calculus concepts: limits, continuity, derivatives

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The Test Values section is discovery, not lecture — students type calculator expressions themselves. Give them 8 minutes before any explanation. The "what did you discover?" questions are the lesson, not the setup for a lesson.
  • Station rotation: 10 minutes per station is tight — set a visible countdown timer and call "rotate" firmly. Groups that aren't finished should note their progress and hand it off — partial data from Station C (the limit table) is fine.
  • Station D (Piecewise Surgery) takes the most time — if groups are running late, give Station D an extra 2 minutes by shortening the debrief before it. This is the most calculus-critical station and should not be rushed.
  • The career section is 5 minutes maximum — the Tacoma Narrows video is compelling but can consume unlimited time. Show the first 90 seconds, then discuss. Don't play the full clip.
  • Practice Triage (4 problems) is best as exit work — students complete the first 2 during the last 10 minutes, not all 4. Problems 3 and 4 (the combined-restriction and hole/asymptote problems) are strong exit tickets.

Section 1 — The Three Breaking Points (10 min)
Overview Black Hole · Imaginary Wall · Logarithmic Void 10 min
🕳️Black Hole
Division by Zero
Denominator \(= 0\) → undefined. The function can approach but never reach this value.
\(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x-3}\),  breaks at \(x=3\)
Domain
\(x \neq 3\)  →  \((-\infty,3)\cup(3,\infty)\)
Graph shows: vertical asymptote at \(x=3\) (if factor doesn't cancel) or hole (if it cancels)
🚧Imaginary Wall
Negative Under Even Root
Even-index radical with negative radicand → not real. Graph simply doesn't exist to the left of the starting point.
\(g(x) = \sqrt{x+4}\),  breaks at \(x=-4\)
Domain
\(x \geq -4\)  →  \([-4,\infty)\)
Graph shows: starting point with a closed dot; no graph to the left
⚠️Logarithmic Void
Log of Zero or Negative
Logarithm only accepts positive inputs. At \(x=0\): vertical asymptote. For \(x<0\): function doesn't exist.
\(h(x) = \ln(x)\),  breaks at \(x \leq 0\)
Domain
\(x > 0\)  →  \((0,\infty)\)
Graph shows: vertical asymptote at \(x=0\) (approaches \(-\infty\) as \(x\to0^+\))
Opening hook — before showing the site "You're function detectives today. Every function in calculus has places it simply refuses to work — breaking points where the output either blows up to infinity, disappears into the imaginary, or just ceases to exist. Your job is to find those points before you ever look at a graph. We're going to test values first, like a scientist — form the hypothesis from the data, then confirm it visually."
📈 Calculus Connections — Introduce These Now
Black Hole → infinite limits and vertical asymptotes (\(\lim_{x\to3^+}\frac{1}{x-3} = +\infty\)). Imaginary Wall → domain restrictions on derivatives (you can't differentiate \(\sqrt{x}\) at \(x=0\) or to the left). Logarithmic Void → limits approaching boundaries (\(\lim_{x\to0^+}\ln x = -\infty\)). These three patterns appear in every unit of calculus.
Section 2 — Test Values: Discover the Breaks (12 min)
Test Values Investigations 1–3 + Challenge Function 12 min
How to Run the Test Values Section
Students type expressions into Desmos Scientific — not graphing, just the scientific calculator. This is deliberate: they discover the breaks algebraically before seeing the graph. Give them 8 minutes to work through Investigations 1–3 independently, then do a 2-minute whole-class share on the Challenge function. The "what did you discover?" reflection sentences are the most important output — circulate and check that students are writing them, not just recording numbers.

The graph is just confirmation."
Section 3 — Station Rotation (42 min total)
Stations A–D 10 Minutes Per Station · Groups of 3–4 42 min
A
Rational Ruptures: Holes vs. Asymptotes
Key question: Why does a cancelled factor produce a hole instead of an asymptote?
Function: \(\dfrac{(x-2)(x+3)}{(x-2)(x-4)}\)

At \(x=2\): factor \((x-2)\) cancels → hole at \((2, \frac{5}{-2}) = (2, -2.5)\)
At \(x=4\): factor \((x-4)\) remains → vertical asymptote
Simplified: \(\dfrac{x+3}{x-4}\),  \(x \neq 2, x \neq 4\)
Answer Key Hole at \(x=2\)  ·  Asymptote at \(x=4\)  ·  Simplified: \(\dfrac{x+3}{x-4}\)
Domain: \((-\infty,2)\cup(2,4)\cup(4,\infty)\)
Calculus connection This is the removable vs. non-removable discontinuity distinction that defines whether a limit exists at a point. At \(x=2\): the limit exists (equals \(-2.5\)) but the function is undefined — removable. At \(x=4\): the limit is \(\pm\infty\) — non-removable.
B
Radical Restrictions: Even vs. Odd Roots
Key question: Why does index parity determine whether a domain restriction exists?
Even roots (\(\sqrt{x}, \sqrt[4]{x}\)): require radicand \(\geq 0\) — negative inputs produce complex numbers

Odd roots (\(\sqrt[3]{x}, \sqrt[5]{x}\)): accept all reals — negative of a negative real number exists in real numbers (\(\sqrt[3]{-8} = -2\))
Answer Key Even roots require \(x \geq 0\) (domain restriction)  ·  Odd roots work for all real numbers
\(\sqrt{x}\): domain \([0,\infty)\)  ·  \(\sqrt[3]{x}\): domain \((-\infty,\infty)\)
Calculus connection Derivatives of even-root functions are undefined at the starting point — \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sqrt{x}] = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}\) is undefined at \(x=0\). This is a domain restriction on the derivative itself.
C
Logarithmic Limits: The Unreachable Horizon
Key question: What happens to \(\ln(x)\) as \(x\) approaches 0 from the right?
Using the slider to fill in values as \(x \to 0^+\):
\(a=1\): \(\ln(1)=0\)  ·  \(a=0.1\): \(\approx -2.303\)  ·  \(a=0.01\): \(\approx -4.605\)  ·  \(a=0.001\): \(\approx -6.908\)
Answer Key — the limit \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\ln(x) = -\infty\)
The output decreases without bound as the input approaches zero from the right.
Calculus connection — this station introduces one-sided limits This is students' first explicit encounter with a one-sided limit written in formal notation. The distinction between \(x \to 0^+\) (approaches from the right, from positive values) and \(x \to 0^-\) (doesn't exist for \(\ln\)) is important. Ask groups: "Why do we write \(0^+\) and not just \(0\)?"
D
Piecewise Surgery: Healing the Break
★ Most calculus-critical station — direct preview of the continuity definition
Function: \(f(x) = \begin{cases} x+k & x < 2 \\ x^2 & x \geq 2 \end{cases}\)

Finding \(k\) for continuity at \(x=2\):
Right-hand value: \(f(2) = 2^2 = 4\)
Left-hand limit: \(\lim_{x\to2^-}(x+k) = 2+k\)
For continuity: \(2+k = 4 \Rightarrow k=2\)
Answer Key \(f(2) = 4\)  ·  \(k = 2\)  ·  The function is continuous at \(x=2\) when \(k=2\)
★ The continuity moment — say this at Station D "What you just did algebraically is exactly what calculus formalises with limits. Continuity at a point requires three things: the function is defined there, the left-hand and right-hand limits both exist, and they equal each other AND the function value. You satisfied all three by finding \(k=2\). That's the definition of continuity — you just used it."
Practice Triage — Answer Key (4 Problems)
Answers Full Solutions for All Four Triage Problems Reference
ProblemWorkDomain (interval notation)
P1: \(\dfrac{x+2}{x^2-4}\) Factor denom: \((x+2)(x-2)\). Cancel \((x+2)\) → hole at \(x=-2\). \((x-2)\) remains → asymptote at \(x=2\). hole at \(x=-2\), asymptote at \(x=2\)
\((-\infty,-2)\cup(-2,2)\cup(2,\infty)\)
P2: \(\sqrt{9-x^2}\) Radicand \(\geq 0\): \(9-x^2 \geq 0 \Rightarrow x^2 \leq 9 \Rightarrow -3 \leq x \leq 3\) \([-3,3]\)
P3: \(\dfrac{\ln(x-1)}{\sqrt{4-x}}\) Log: \(x-1>0 \Rightarrow x>1\). Root in denom: \(4-x>0 \Rightarrow x<4\) (strict — denom can't be 0). Combined: \(1 < x < 4\). \((1,4)\)
P4: \(\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2-5x+6}\) Num: \((x+2)(x-2)\). Denom: \((x-2)(x-3)\). Cancel \((x-2)\) → hole at \(x=2\). \((x-3)\) remains → asymptote at \(x=3\). hole at \(x=2\), asymptote at \(x=3\)
\((-\infty,2)\cup(2,3)\cup(3,\infty)\)
P3 — the strictness of the radical denominator Students sometimes write \(\sqrt{4-x} \geq 0\) (allowing \(x=4\)) but since the radical is in the denominator, it cannot equal zero. The condition is \(\sqrt{4-x} > 0\), which means \(4-x > 0\), so \(x < 4\) strictly. This distinction — closed bracket vs. open bracket — is worth a 60-second class discussion because it appears on AP exams.

Close Exit Ticket — Two Problems 5 min
Problems & Answers
ET1: State the domain of \(f(x) = \dfrac{\sqrt{x+3}}{(x+3)(x-1)}\) in interval notation. Is the break at \(x=-3\) a hole or an asymptote?

Answer: Radical: \(x \geq -3\). Denom zeros: \(x=-3\) and \(x=1\). At \(x=-3\): the factor \((x+3)\) appears in both numerator (under the root — the root equals zero at \(x=-3\)) and denominator. This is a zero in the numerator, not a cancellation — so it's a hole only if the factor cancels. Here, \(\sqrt{x+3}\) and \((x+3)\) are different — they don't cancel. Result: \(x=-3\) is a point where the function is defined as 0/0 — undefined, giving a hole. Domain: \((-3,-1) \cup (-1,1) \cup (1,\infty)\) wait — at \(x=-3\): numerator is 0, denominator is 0 — undefined (hole). Domain: \((-3,1)\cup(1,\infty)\) with \(x>-3\) from radical and excluding \(x=-3\) and \(x=1\).

ET2: A piecewise function \(f(x) = \begin{cases} 3x-1 & x < 2 \\ x^2+k & x \geq 2 \end{cases}\) is continuous at \(x=2\). Find \(k\).

Answer: Left: \(3(2)-1=5\). Right: \(4+k\). Set equal: \(4+k=5 \Rightarrow k=1\).
