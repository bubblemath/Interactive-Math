🔑   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson builds the rational expression fluency that calculus demands constantly — in limits, the quotient rule, and partial fraction decomposition. The entire lesson is unified by one insight: you can only cancel factors, never terms. That single rule, understood deeply, prevents the most common algebra error in calculus.

The lesson's most important conceptual moment is the hole vs. asymptote distinction. Students who understand why a cancelled factor produces a hole (removable discontinuity) while a remaining factor produces a vertical asymptote are ready to understand \(\frac{0}{0}\) limits — the fundamental motivation for the factoring and cancellation work in calculus. Build toward that moment explicitly.

The interactive lesson at bubblemath.github.io provides animated step walkthroughs, the Giant One Discovery Lab, a Clear the Denominator simulator, and a practice drill. Students should have it open on devices alongside the printed notes sheet.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify domain restrictions of a rational expression before any simplification
  • Simplify rational expressions by factoring and cancelling common factors (the Giant One)
  • Distinguish between holes (removable discontinuities) and vertical asymptotes
  • Multiply, divide, add, and subtract rational expressions using LCD
  • Simplify complex fractions using the LCD method
  • Solve rational equations by clearing denominators; identify and reject extraneous solutions

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • Domain restrictions must be established before any simplification — if students identify them after, they'll miss restrictions that cancel out. Make "find restrictions first" a non-negotiable habit from the first example.
  • The Hole vs. Asymptote visualiser is the most calculus-critical moment of the day — don't rush past it. Students who see this connection understand why limits care about factoring. Budget 5 full minutes here, including the Desmos demo.
  • Addition/subtraction of rational expressions is the most time-intensive operation — if you're running short, cover multiplication fully and summarise addition with one guided example rather than rushing both.
  • Extraneous solution check is mandatory every time — circulate specifically to catch students who solve correctly but don't check. Build the habit explicitly by narrating your own check aloud during modelled examples.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge: Factoring Foundation (10 min)
Prereq The Golden Rule: Factoring is Everything 10 min
Opening Hook — Quick Check Before Instruction
Before teaching anything, post the Quick Check from the site: "Find the domain restrictions of \(\frac{x+5}{x^2-4}\)." Give students 60 seconds. Use the results to gauge how many already know to factor the denominator and how many need the foundational framing.
Core Framing
Script "Rational expressions are fractions with polynomials. Everything you do with them — simplify, add, solve — starts with one skill: factoring. If your factoring is sharp, this lesson is straightforward. If it isn't, every step is harder. We'll do a quick factoring review and then move into the new material."
Factoring Patterns to Review (fast — students know these)
Display the six patterns from the site. Students confirm them on their notes. Spend 30 seconds on GCF, difference of squares, and trinomials. Spend a full minute on sum/difference of cubes — these are the ones students most often forget. Do not re-teach from scratch; this is a reminder, not a lesson.
Domain Restrictions — The Most Important Habit of the Day
Work through the restriction example from the site explicitly. State the rule twice and make students write it on their notes sheet:
Before simplifying \(\dfrac{x+2}{x^2-9}\): factor denominator → \((x-3)(x+3)\)
Set each factor \(= 0\): restrictions are \(x \neq 3\) and \(x \neq -3\)
These restrictions persist even after simplifying.
Why restrictions persist — emphasise this now Students consistently drop restrictions after cancellation. The reason they must be kept: the original expression is undefined at those values, even if the simplified form appears defined. This matters enormously in calculus — it's the difference between a hole and an asymptote.
📈 Calculus Connection
The limit \(\lim_{x\to2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\) gives \(\frac{0}{0}\) by direct substitution — an indeterminate form. The solution is to factor and simplify first, exactly as in this section. Every limit problem of this type in calculus begins with the factoring skills reviewed today.
Section 2 — The "Giant One" Discovery Lab (20 min)
Giant One Simplifying Rational Expressions — Cancelling is Dividing by 1 20 min
The Giant One Concept (4 min)
The "Giant One" framing is the most memorable way to explain why cancellation works. Write on the board: \(\frac{x-a}{x-a} = 1\) (when \(x \neq a\)). "When you cancel a factor from numerator and denominator, you're dividing the expression by 1 — which doesn't change its value. That's all cancellation is." Run the animated simplifier for Example 1.
Simplify \(\dfrac{x^2-4}{x^2+4x+4}\)
Factor: \(\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{(x+2)^2}\)
Cancel the Giant One \(\dfrac{x+2}{x+2}\): \(= \dfrac{x-2}{x+2}\),  \(x \neq -2\)
Illegal Cancelling — The Most Important Error of the Lesson (3 min)
This error is the single most common algebra mistake in calculus. Write both versions on the board, cross out the wrong one dramatically:
✗ Illegal — Cancelling Terms
\(\dfrac{x+3}{x+5} = \dfrac{3}{5}\)
The \(x\) is being added — you cannot cancel something that is added. There is no "Giant One" here.
✓ Legal — Cancelling Factors
\(\dfrac{(x+3)(x-2)}{(x+5)(x-2)} = \dfrac{x+3}{x+5}\)
The \((x-2)\) is multiplied in both. \(\frac{x-2}{x-2} = 1\) — that's the Giant One.
Script — say this clearly "You can only cancel factors — things that are multiplied. You can never cancel terms — things that are added or subtracted. This one rule, if you internalize it, prevents the most common algebra error I see in calculus. \(\frac{x+3}{x+5}\) cannot be simplified. It is already in its simplest form."
★ Hole vs. Asymptote — The Calculus-Critical Distinction (5 min)
This is the most conceptually important 5 minutes of the lesson. Open Desmos and graph \(f(x) = \frac{(x-2)(x+3)}{(x-2)(x-1)}\). Students should see this on the projector and in their notes.
🕳 Hole (Removable Discontinuity)
The factor \((x-2)\) cancels from numerator and denominator. The simplified form is \(\frac{x+3}{x-1}\). At \(x=2\): the simplified form gives \(\frac{5}{1} = 5\), but the original is undefined. The graph shows a hole at \((2, 5)\) — a single missing point. The limit exists at this point. In calculus: \(\lim_{x\to2} f(x) = 5\).
📊 Vertical Asymptote
The factor \((x-1)\) remains in the denominator after cancelling. At \(x=1\): the denominator is zero and the factor does not cancel — the function "blows up." The graph shows a vertical asymptote at \(x=1\). The limit does not exist (or is \(\pm\infty\)). In calculus: \(\lim_{x\to1} f(x) = \pm\infty\).
The punch line — say this "Both \(x=2\) and \(x=1\) are restrictions — places where the original function is undefined. But they behave completely differently. The one that cancelled is just a missing point — the limit exists there and calculus can work with it. The one that didn't cancel blows up — the limit is infinite and the function has a genuine break. Knowing which you're dealing with is a calculus skill, and it starts with whether a factor cancels or not."
📈 Calculus Connection
Holes correspond to removable discontinuities — cases where \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) exists even though \(f(a)\) is undefined. This is precisely the situation that gives the indeterminate form \(\frac{0}{0}\) in limits, and the resolution is to factor and cancel. The Quotient Rule for derivatives also produces rational expressions requiring these simplification skills.
Section 3 — Operations Workshop (15 min)
Operations Multiply, Divide, Add, Subtract, Complex Fractions 15 min
Multiplication & Division — Factor First (5 min)
The rule: factor everything before multiplying, then cancel across the combined fraction. Never multiply out and then try to factor — that direction is much harder. Work through the site's example:
\(\dfrac{x^2-9}{x^2+5x+6} \cdot \dfrac{x+2}{x-3}\)
Factor: \(\dfrac{(x-3)(x+3)}{(x+2)(x+3)} \cdot \dfrac{x+2}{x-3}\)
Cancel: all four factors cancel → \(= 1\),  \(x \neq -3, -2, 3\)
For division: flip the second fraction (multiply by the reciprocal), then factor and cancel as above. Restrictions must be stated for the original denominators, including the flipped fraction's original numerator.
Addition & Subtraction — The LCD Challenge (7 min)
The most common error: adding/subtracting by combining numerators over the sum of denominators. Write this on the board before teaching the correct method:
✗ Never Do This
\(\dfrac{a}{b} + \dfrac{c}{d} = \dfrac{a+c}{b+d}\)
The denominators are not added. This would only be valid if \(b=d\) (same denominator already).
✓ LCD Method
\(\dfrac{3}{x-1} + \dfrac{2}{x+1}\)
LCD \(= (x-1)(x+1)\)
\(= \dfrac{3(x+1)+2(x-1)}{(x-1)(x+1)} = \dfrac{5x+1}{x^2-1}\)
Build the LCD from all distinct factors, rewrite each fraction, combine numerators.
Complex Fractions — LCD Method (3 min)
A fraction within a fraction. The fastest method: find the LCD of all inner fractions, then multiply every term in the main numerator and denominator by that LCD. All inner fractions clear simultaneously. This is faster than simplifying top and bottom separately and then dividing.
📈 Calculus Connection
The Quotient Rule produces expressions like \(\frac{u'v - uv'}{v^2}\) that often need to be simplified using LCD techniques. Partial Fraction Decomposition (an integration technique) directly reverses the LCD process — breaking a combined fraction back into simpler pieces.
Section 4 — Rational Equation Solver (20 min)
Solve Clear the Denominators + Extraneous Solution Detector 20 min
The Strategy: Clear the LCD (8 min)
Multiply every term — every term — by the LCD. This eliminates all fractions and produces a polynomial equation. Animate the site's worked example step by step:
Solve \(\dfrac{2}{x-3} + \dfrac{1}{x} = \dfrac{5}{x(x-3)}\)
LCD \(= x(x-3)\)
Multiply every term: \(2x + (x-3) = 5\)
Solve: \(3x - 3 = 5 \Rightarrow x = \frac{8}{3}\)
Check: \(x = \frac{8}{3}\) makes no denominator zero ✓
Stress "every term" The most common procedural error: multiplying some terms but not all. Write on the board: "Multiply EVERY term by LCD." Walk through the multiplication term by term during the animated example, narrating each cancellation aloud.
★ Extraneous Solution Detector (8 min)
Run the extraneous example from the site explicitly — this is not optional:
Solve \(\dfrac{x}{x-2} = \dfrac{2}{x-2} + 1\)
LCD \(= x-2\). Multiply: \(x = 2 + (x-2) = x\)
This gives \(x = x\) — true for all \(x\), but wait...
Solving: \(x - 2 = 2 \Rightarrow x = 2\)
Check: \(x=2\) makes denominator \(= 0\). Extraneous. No solution.
Make this stick "The extraneous values — the ones we reject — are exactly the domain restrictions we identified at the very start. The values that make the denominator zero were forbidden from the beginning. Multiplying by the LCD is powerful, but it can quietly smuggle in values that were never allowed. That's why we always check."
Solution Verification Checklist (2 min)
Read through the five-item checklist on the site with students. They add it to their notes. Particularly emphasise item 4: check each solution in the original equation — not the simplified version.
📈 Calculus Connection
Finding critical points of rational functions (setting \(f'(x)=0\)) produces rational equations. The extraneous solutions from those equations correspond to vertical asymptotes — values where the function is undefined and therefore cannot have a critical point. Domain awareness here directly prevents reporting a "critical point" that doesn't exist.
Section 5 — Practice Zone (15 min)
Practice Drill Zone + Spot the Error Challenge 15 min
How to Run the Practice Zone
Suggested sequence: Factor First (warm-up), then Simplify, then Operate, then Solve & Check. The Streak counter motivates students who are competitive. Use the timer feature selectively — it's most useful for the Factor First and Simplify drills where speed matters. Solve & Check should be untimed so students don't skip the checking step.
What to Watch For While Circulating
  • → Cancelling terms (the illegal cancel): \(\frac{x+3}{x+5} = \frac{3}{5}\). Correct on the spot with the Giant One framing.
  • → Missing restrictions after simplification: simplified form shown without stating \(x \neq\)... values. Remind: restrictions from the original denominator persist.
  • → Adding denominators: \(\frac{a}{b}+\frac{c}{d} = \frac{a+c}{b+d}\). Ask: "Did you build an LCD first?"
  • → Skipping the extraneous check: solution written without verification. Ask: "Does that value make any original denominator zero?"
Spot the Error — Closing 3 Minutes
Use the site's Spot the Error challenge as a whole-class close. Option A: \(\frac{x+3}{x+5}=\frac{3}{5}\). Option B: cannot be simplified. Ask for hands, then ask a student to explain precisely why Option B is correct — the explanation should reference factors vs. terms, not just "the rule."
Exit Ticket & Answers
Close Exit Ticket — Three Problems 5 min
Problems
ET1: Simplify and state all restrictions: \(\dfrac{x^2+x-6}{x^2-4}\)

ET2: Perform and simplify: \(\dfrac{x}{x+2} - \dfrac{3}{x-1}\)

ET3: Solve and check for extraneous solutions: \(\dfrac{x}{x+1} + \dfrac{2}{x} = 1\)
Answers ET1: Factor \(\frac{(x+3)(x-2)}{(x+2)(x-2)}\); cancel \((x-2)\); result \(\frac{x+3}{x+2}\), restrictions \(x \neq 2, x \neq -2\). ET2: LCD \(=(x+2)(x-1)\); \(\frac{x(x-1)-3(x+2)}{(x+2)(x-1)} = \frac{x^2-4x-6}{(x+2)(x-1)}\), restrictions \(x \neq -2, x \neq 1\). ET3: LCD \(=x(x+1)\); multiply: \(x^2 + 2(x+1) = x(x+1)\); \(x^2+2x+2 = x^2+x\); \(x = -2\); check: \(x=-2\) makes denominator \(x+2=0\) — extraneous. No solution.
