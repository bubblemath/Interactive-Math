∞   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson is the conceptual foundation of calculus — everything from derivatives to integrals depends on limits. Students arrive knowing how to compute average rates from the Marble Lab; today they formalise what they were already doing as limits, meet the three evaluation methods, and encounter the three-condition continuity checklist that governs differentiability and integration.

The single most important distinction of the day is approaching vs. arriving: the limit \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) describes what \(f\) approaches near \(a\), not what it equals at \(a\). Students who genuinely own this distinction can handle holes, asymptotes, piecewise functions, and the derivative definition with confidence. Students who confuse the two will make systematic errors throughout all of calculus.

The three-condition continuity checklist is the second major deliverable. Students who can apply all three conditions — existence, limit exists, limit equals function value — can diagnose any discontinuity and are prepared for the Intermediate Value Theorem, Mean Value Theorem, and integrability theorems they will encounter in their first calculus course.

The limit definition of the derivative section closes the loop from the Marble Lab: students see that what they observed numerically (shrinking intervals converging to a value) is exactly what the formula \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) computes algebraically.

Learning Objectives
  • State and interpret limit notation \(\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = L\) in plain English
  • Distinguish between the limit of a function and its value at a point
  • Evaluate limits using tables, graphs, and algebraic simplification
  • Recognise and resolve \(\frac{0}{0}\) indeterminate forms by factoring
  • Define and evaluate one-sided limits; apply the two-sided limit existence rule
  • Apply the three-condition continuity checklist to classify discontinuities
  • Distinguish removable (hole) from non-removable (asymptote, jump) discontinuities
  • Use the limit definition to compute \(f'(x)\) for \(f(x)=x^2\), \(x^3\), and \(2x+1\)

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The "approaching vs. arriving" distinction needs 5 full minutes — not a single sentence. Use the \(\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\) example, show that \(f(2)\) is undefined, then show \(\lim_{x\to2}f(x)=4\). Students who say "they're the same thing" haven't understood the distinction yet. Keep probing.
  • Three methods section: tables first, then graphs, then algebra — this order is not arbitrary. Tables build numerical intuition. Graphs provide visual confirmation. Algebra gives the tool for exact answers. Reversing the order produces procedural competence without conceptual understanding.
  • The continuity checklist must be applied to at least two examples — one where all three conditions pass (continuous function) and one where at least one fails. Students who only see the checklist described without applying it don't retain it.
  • The derivative section is a review and formalisation, not new content — students saw convergence in the Marble Lab. Move through the worked example at a steady pace; the goal is connecting notation to the numerical experience, not teaching the derivative for the first time.
  • Practice Zone drill priority: Algebraically first, then Continuity — these are the highest-value skills. From Tables and From Graphs are faster and build confidence; use them as warm-up, not as the main event.

Section 1 — Prerequisite Bridge: The Big Idea (10 min)
Prereq Approaching vs. Arriving — The Most Important Distinction in Calculus 10 min
Opening Hook — The Notation and What It Means
Write the limit notation on the board piece by piece, explaining each part as you write it:
\(\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L\)

\(\lim\) — "We are asking about approach, not arrival"
\(x \to a\) — "x gets infinitely close to a — but never equals a"
\(f(x)\) — "Watch what the output does as x approaches"
\(= L\) — "It approaches this value L"
The Critical Example — \(f(x)=\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\)
This example is the fulcrum of the lesson. Do it slowly.
Try direct substitution:  \(f(2) = \frac{4-4}{2-2} = \frac{0}{0}\) — undefined!

But the limit:  \(\lim_{x\to2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2} = \lim_{x\to2}\frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = \lim_{x\to2}(x+2) = 4\)

Key point: \(f(2)\) is undefined, but \(\lim_{x\to2}f(x) = 4\). These are different questions.
Script — say this "The function value asks: what does the function output at exactly \(x=2\)? Answer: nothing — it's undefined. The limit asks: what value is the function approaching as \(x\) gets close to 2? Answer: 4. These are two different questions. The limit doesn't care what happens at the point — only near it. This distinction is the heart of all of calculus."
📈 Why This Matters for Calculus
The derivative is defined as \(\lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\). Direct substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\) every time — the derivative is always of indeterminate form. If limits required the function to be defined at the point, the derivative wouldn't exist. The "approaching vs. arriving" distinction is what makes calculus possible.
Section 2 — Three Methods of Limit Evaluation (20 min)
3 Methods Tables → Graphs → Algebra 20 min
Method 1 — Tables
When to use
First approach to any limit; when algebraic simplification isn't obvious; with real data (the Marble Lab).

How: Build a table approaching from left and right. If both sides converge to the same value \(L\), the limit is \(L\).
Limitation
Can only estimate — can't confirm exact value. Sufficient for conjecture, not proof.
Method 2 — Graphs
When to use
Confirming table results; identifying discontinuity types visually; piecewise functions where algebra is complex.

How: Graph the function in Desmos. Trace toward the target from each side. If the y-value approaches the same number from both sides, that's the limit.
Limitation
Holes can be invisible at standard zoom — always confirm graphically with a table near the suspect point.
Method 3 — Algebra
When to use
When direct substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\) — the most important case. Factor, cancel, substitute.

How: Factor numerator and denominator. Cancel common factors (note the restriction). Substitute the target value.
The indeterminate form
\(\frac{0}{0}\) does NOT mean the limit is 0 or undefined. It means the method failed — try factoring.
Full Worked Example — Algebraic Method (animate on projector)
Find \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to2}\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\)

Step 1: Direct substitution → \(\frac{0}{0}\) — indeterminate form, must factor
Step 2: Factor numerator: \(\dfrac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2}\)
Step 3: Cancel \((x-2)\): \(= x+2\)  (valid when \(x\neq2\), and we never let \(x=2\) in a limit)
Step 4: Substitute: \(\lim_{x\to2}(x+2) = 4\) ✓
Three functions to demonstrate — vary the break type 1. \(\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\) — hole (factor cancels) → limit exists. 2. \(\frac{1}{x-2}\) — asymptote (factor doesn't cancel) → limit DNE (\(+\infty\) from right, \(-\infty\) from left). 3. \(\frac{|x|}{x}\) — jump (piecewise-like behaviour) → left limit \(-1\), right limit \(+1\), overall limit DNE. Each of these three tables will look different and produce different conclusions. All three are worth showing.
Section 3 — One-Sided Limits & the Continuity Checklist (20 min)
Sided + Continuity Left/Right Limits + Three-Condition Test 20 min
One-Sided Limits — The Existence Rule
\(\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x)\) — left-hand limit (approach from values \(< a\))
\(\lim_{x\to a^+}f(x)\) — right-hand limit (approach from values \(> a\))

The two-sided limit exists if and only if:
\(\lim_{x\to a^-}f(x) = \lim_{x\to a^+}f(x) = L\)

If the two sides disagree → the (two-sided) limit does NOT exist.
Running the Traveler Visualizer Open the Traveler Visualizer on the projector. Set it to a jump discontinuity (e.g., \(\frac{|x|}{x}\)). Drag the traveler from the left — watch the y-value approach \(-1\). Drag from the right — watch it approach \(+1\). Ask: "Can we say the limit exists?" No — the traveler arrives at different heights depending on which side it approaches from. The limit requires both travelers to meet at the same value.
★ The Three-Condition Continuity Checklist
A function \(f\) is continuous at \(x=a\) if ALL three conditions hold:
1
\(f(a)\) exists
The function is defined at the point
Fails at holes and vertical asymptotes — \(f(a)\) is undefined
2
\(\lim_{x\to a}f(x)\) exists
Both one-sided limits exist and are equal
Fails at vertical asymptotes (limit is \(\pm\infty\)) and jump discontinuities (sides disagree)
3
\(\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = f(a)\)
The limit equals the function value
Fails when there's a "defined hole" — the function is defined at the point but at the wrong value
Apply the checklist to both examples — do this explicitly Example 1 — Hole: \(f(x)=\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\) at \(x=2\). Condition 1: fails (\(f(2)\) undefined). Condition 2: passes (limit \(=4\)). Condition 3: cannot pass since Condition 1 failed. Classification: removable discontinuity. Example 2 — Asymptote: \(f(x)=\frac{1}{x-2}\) at \(x=2\). Condition 1: fails. Condition 2: fails (limit \(=\pm\infty\)). Classification: non-removable discontinuity. Students should fill in the checklist for both on their notes.
Hole vs. Asymptote — The Calculus Stakes
🕳️ Hole — Removable Discontinuity
Factor cancels from numerator and denominator. The limit exists. Condition 1 (and 3) fails — function undefined at the point.

Calculus: The limit exists, so we can define \(f(a)\) to plug the hole and make the function continuous. This is exactly what happens when we evaluate a derivative limit — the \(h\) cancels, the hole is "removed," and we substitute \(h=0\).
📊 Asymptote / Jump — Non-Removable
The limit doesn't exist (infinite or sided mismatch). Cannot be fixed by redefining a single point.

Calculus: Non-removable discontinuities are places where derivatives don't exist — the function isn't differentiable there. They're also relevant for integration bounds and the IVT/MVT theorems.
📈 Calculus Connections from Continuity
Differentiability requires continuity: if \(f\) is not continuous at \(a\), then \(f'(a)\) doesn't exist. Continuity is necessary but not sufficient (e.g., \(|x|\) is continuous but not differentiable at 0). Integrability: every function continuous on \([a,b]\) is integrable on \([a,b]\) — a key theorem. IVT: if \(f\) is continuous on \([a,b]\), it takes every value between \(f(a)\) and \(f(b)\). MVT: requires both continuity and differentiability on an interval.
Section 4 — Derivative Discovery: The Limit Definition (15 min)
Derivative Connecting Marble Lab Convergence to the Algebraic Formula 15 min
The Connection to the Marble Lab
Opening connection "In the Marble Lab you watched average rates converge to a single value as the interval shrank. What you were computing each time was \(\frac{d(t+h)-d(t)}{h}\) for smaller and smaller values of \(h\). Today we write that process algebraically. The limit definition of the derivative is the shrinking interval table, compressed into a formula."
Full Worked Example — \(f(x)=x^2\) (animate on projector)
\(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\)

Step 1 — Write the difference quotient:
\(\quad f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h}\)

Step 2 — Expand numerator:
\(\quad = \lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{x^2 + 2xh + h^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{2xh+h^2}{h}\)

Step 3 — Factor and cancel:
\(\quad = \lim_{h\to0}\dfrac{h(2x+h)}{h} = \lim_{h\to0}(2x+h)\)

Step 4 — Substitute \(h=0\) (now valid — the \(\frac{0}{0}\) form was resolved in Step 3):
\(\quad = 2x + 0 = 2x\)

Result: \(f'(x) = 2x\)
Why can we substitute h=0 in Step 4 but not at the start? At the start, \(\frac{0}{0}\) is indeterminate. After factoring and cancelling \(h\), the remaining expression \((2x+h)\) is a polynomial in \(h\) — no division by \(h\) remains. Now substituting \(h=0\) is valid. This is the same logic as the \(\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\) example: the problematic factor cancels, and then substitution works. The derivative formula always starts as indeterminate form and always resolves by cancellation.
Practice Derivatives — Two More on the Site
The site provides worked examples for \(f(x)=x^3\) and \(f(x)=2x+1\). For \(x^3\): the expansion \((x+h)^3 = x^3+3x^2h+3xh^2+h^3\) is the key step — students may need to see it. Result: \(f'(x)=3x^2\). For \(2x+1\): linear function, constant result \(f'(x)=2\). Note the pattern emerging: power rule \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n]=nx^{n-1}\). Plant this explicitly as a preview.
Exit Ticket & Answers
Close Exit Ticket — Four Problems 5 min
ProblemKey StepsAnswer
ET1: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to3}\frac{x^2-9}{x-3}\) Direct sub → \(\frac{0}{0}\). Factor: \(\frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3}\). Cancel. Substitute \(x=3\). 6
ET2: Is \(f(x)=\frac{x^2-9}{x-3}\) continuous at \(x=3\)? Apply all three conditions. Cond 1: \(f(3)\) undefined → fails. Cond 2: limit \(=6\) exists → passes. Cond 3: cannot hold since Cond 1 fails. Not continuous. Removable discontinuity (hole) at \(x=3\).
ET3: \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^-}\frac{|x|}{x}\) and \(\displaystyle\lim_{x\to0^+}\frac{|x|}{x}\). Does the two-sided limit exist? For \(x<0\): \(|x|=-x\), so \(\frac{-x}{x}=-1\). For \(x>0\): \(|x|=x\), so \(\frac{x}{x}=1\). Sides disagree. \(\lim^- = -1\), \(\lim^+ = 1\). Two-sided limit DNE (jump discontinuity).
ET4: Use the limit definition to find \(f'(2)\) for \(f(x)=3x^2\). \(\frac{3(2+h)^2-12}{h} = \frac{12+12h+3h^2-12}{h} = \frac{12h+3h^2}{h} = 12+3h \to 12\) \(f'(2) = 12\)
