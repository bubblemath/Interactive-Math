⚠️ Why We Need New Rules

The simple rules DON'T work for products, quotients, or compositions!

Common Mistake Why It's Wrong
\((fg)' = f' \cdot g'\) Product of derivatives ≠ derivative of product
\(\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'}{g'}\) Quotient of derivatives ≠ derivative of quotient
\((f \circ g)' = f' \circ g'\) Composition of derivatives ≠ derivative of composition

✖️ Product Rule: The "Growing Rectangle"

The Product Rule

\(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x) \cdot g(x)] = f'(x) \cdot g(x) + f(x) \cdot g'(x)\)
💡 "First times derivative of second, plus second times derivative of first"

📊 Geometric Proof: The Growing Rectangle

Imagine a rectangle with sides \(u\) and \(v\). As \(x\) changes, both sides grow!

\(u\) \(v\)
Original Area
\(u \cdot v\)
New Strip (top)
\(u \cdot dv\)
New Strip (right)
\(v \cdot du\)
Tiny Corner
\(du \cdot dv \approx 0\)

The Insight!

Change in Area = \(u \cdot dv + v \cdot du\) (ignoring the tiny corner)

\(\frac{d(uv)}{dx} = u\frac{dv}{dx} + v\frac{du}{dx}\)

📐 Algebraic Proof (Limit Definition)

1
Start with the limit definition
\(\frac{d}{dx}[f(x)g(x)] = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x)g(x)}{h}\)
2
Add and subtract \(f(x+h)g(x)\) (clever trick!)
\(= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h)g(x+h) - f(x+h)g(x) + f(x+h)g(x) - f(x)g(x)}{h}\)
3
Factor and split
\(= \lim_{h \to 0} \left[ f(x+h) \cdot \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} + g(x) \cdot \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h} \right]\)
4
Take the limit (as \(h \to 0\), \(f(x+h) \to f(x)\))
\(= f(x) \cdot g'(x) + g(x) \cdot f'(x)\)
5
The Product Rule!
\((fg)' = f'g + fg'\) ✓

✏️ Worked Examples

Find \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^2 \sin x]\)

Let: \(f = x^2\), \(g = \sin x\)

Then: \(f' = 2x\), \(g' = \cos x\)

Apply Product Rule:

\((fg)' = f'g + fg' = 2x \cdot \sin x + x^2 \cdot \cos x\)

Answer: \(2x\sin x + x^2\cos x\)

🔗 Calculus Connection

📈 Integration by Parts

The Product Rule reverses to give the integration technique "Integration by Parts"!

\(\int u \, dv = uv - \int v \, du\)

🎯 Physics Applications

Momentum = mass × velocity. If both change, you need the Product Rule!

\(\frac{dp}{dt} = m\frac{dv}{dt} + v\frac{dm}{dt}\)

⚠️ Common Error

Don't forget BOTH terms! The derivative of a product is NOT the product of derivatives.

➗ Quotient Rule: "Low d-High minus High d-Low"

The Quotient Rule

\(\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right] = \frac{f'(x) \cdot g(x) - f(x) \cdot g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}\)
💡 "Low d-High minus High d-Low, all over Low squared"

🎵 The Mnemonic Breakdown

\(\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{\text{High}}{\text{Low}}\right] = \frac{\text{Low} \cdot d(\text{High}) - \text{High} \cdot d(\text{Low})}{\text{Low}^2}\)

High = \(f(x)\)
The numerator
Low = \(g(x)\)
The denominator

🎵 "Low d-High minus High d-Low, all over Low-Low!"

📐 Algebraic Proof (Using Product Rule)

The Quotient Rule is actually the Product Rule in disguise!

1
Rewrite as a product
\(\frac{f}{g} = f \cdot g^{-1}\)
2
Apply Product Rule
\(\frac{d}{dx}[f \cdot g^{-1}] = f' \cdot g^{-1} + f \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[g^{-1}]\)
3
Use Chain Rule for \(g^{-1}\)
\(\frac{d}{dx}[g^{-1}] = -g^{-2} \cdot g' = -\frac{g'}{g^2}\)
4
Substitute back
\(= \frac{f'}{g} + f \cdot \left(-\frac{g'}{g^2}\right) = \frac{f'}{g} - \frac{fg'}{g^2}\)
5
Combine over common denominator
\(= \frac{f'g}{g^2} - \frac{fg'}{g^2} = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\)
6
The Quotient Rule!
\(\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)' = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\) ✓

⚠️ The Sign Error Trap!

The most common mistake is getting the subtraction order wrong!

❌ WRONG ✓ CORRECT
\(\frac{fg' - f'g}{g^2}\) \(\frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}\)

Remember: "Low d-High" comes FIRST!

✏️ Worked Examples

Find \(\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{x^2}{\sin x}\right]\)

High: \(f = x^2\), so \(f' = 2x\)

Low: \(g = \sin x\), so \(g' = \cos x\)

Apply Quotient Rule:

\(\frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} = \frac{2x \cdot \sin x - x^2 \cdot \cos x}{\sin^2 x}\)

Answer: \(\frac{2x\sin x - x^2\cos x}{\sin^2 x}\)

🔗 Chain Rule: "Peel the Onion"

The Chain Rule

\(\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\)
💡 "Derivative of outer (keeping inner) times derivative of inner"

Leibniz notation: \(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}\)

🧅 Visual: "Peel the Onion"

Composite functions have layers. Differentiate from outside in!

Outer
\(f(u)\)
Middle
\(g(v)\)
Inner
\(h(x)\)

Click each layer to "peel" it!

The Chain Rule Process

  1. Identify the outer function
  2. Differentiate the outer (keeping inner unchanged)
  3. Multiply by the derivative of the inner
  4. Repeat if there are more layers!

📐 Algebraic Proof (Limit Definition)

1
Let \(y = f(u)\) where \(u = g(x)\)
We want \(\frac{dy}{dx}\)
2
Write the limit definition
\(\frac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\)
3
Multiply by \(\frac{\Delta u}{\Delta u}\) (clever trick!)
\(= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x}\)
4
As \(\Delta x \to 0\), also \(\Delta u \to 0\)
\(= \lim_{\Delta u \to 0} \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x}\)
5
Recognize the derivatives!
\(\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}\) ✓

✏️ Worked Examples

Find \(\frac{d}{dx}[\sin(x^2)]\)

Outer: \(f(u) = \sin(u)\), so \(f'(u) = \cos(u)\)

Inner: \(u = x^2\), so \(u' = 2x\)

Apply Chain Rule:

\(f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x\)

Answer: \(2x\cos(x^2)\)

📋 Common Chain Rule Patterns

Function Derivative
\([g(x)]^n\) \(n[g(x)]^{n-1} \cdot g'(x)\)
\(\sin(g(x))\) \(\cos(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\)
\(\cos(g(x))\) \(-\sin(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)\)
\(e^{g(x)}\) \(e^{g(x)} \cdot g'(x)\)
\(\ln(g(x))\) \(\frac{g'(x)}{g(x)}\)
