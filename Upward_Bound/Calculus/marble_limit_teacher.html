🎱   Lesson Overview — Read Before Teaching

This lesson does something most calculus courses don't: it lets students physically discover the derivative before seeing the formula. The marble ramp produces real data, the shrinking interval table shows convergence happening in their own numbers, and the limit definition of the derivative arrives as the answer to a question students have already been asking — not as a formula imposed from outside.

The conceptual arc is carefully sequenced: big question (how can speed exist at a single instant?) → average rate as secant slope → the secant approaching the tangent as intervals shrink → real data confirming the pattern → the formal limit notation as a compact way to write what they've already seen. Every step earns the next one.

The single most important moment of the lesson is when students look at their shrinking interval table and realise that the average rates are converging to a single value. That convergence — not any formula — is the derivative. The formula is just the notation. Protect the time for that moment above all else.

Learning Objectives
  • Explain the difference between average rate of change and instantaneous rate of change
  • Compute average rate of change using \(\frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}\) between two data points
  • Interpret average rate as the slope of a secant line on a position-time graph
  • Observe that as two points approach each other, the secant slope approaches the tangent slope
  • Use the shrinking interval table to estimate instantaneous rate by convergence
  • Connect the limit definition \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) to the numerical pattern
  • Interpret the derivative \(v(t) = at\) as the physical velocity of the marble at any moment

⚑   Pacing Alerts
  • The opening question must stay open longer than feels comfortable — "How can speed exist at a single instant?" deserves 2–3 minutes of genuine student discussion before you offer any framing. Students who articulate their own confusion understand the problem better than students who receive the explanation.
  • The Virtual Marble Simulator secant visualiser is the most important tool in the lesson — open it full-screen and let students drag the points themselves. The moment they see the secant slope number changing as the interval shrinks is the lesson. Budget 8 full minutes for this, not 3.
  • The shrinking interval table is where the derivative is born — don't rush the data analysis section. If the lab ran long, skip 2 practice problems rather than abbreviating the convergence observation. Students need to see the pattern in their own numbers.
  • The limit definition formula arrives LAST — not partway through. Resist the urge to write \(f'(x) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}\) on the board before students have observed convergence in the table. The formula should feel like a reward, not a prerequisite.
  • If you are running the physical marble lab, add 15 minutes and shorten the practice zone. Real data is worth it. If the data is noisy (three trials won't perfectly match the model), that's actually pedagogically valuable — discuss why real measurements don't perfectly match the mathematical model.

Section 1 — The Big Question & Prerequisite Bridge (12 min)
Prereq How Can Speed Exist at a Single Instant? 12 min
Opening — Let the Question Breathe (3 min)
Write only this on the board: "A car's speedometer reads 47 mph at exactly 2:00pm. But speed = distance ÷ time. If it's a single instant, there's no distance travelled and no time elapsed. How is 47 mph even a real number?" Give students 90 seconds to think, then 90 seconds to discuss with a neighbour. Collect ideas without correcting any of them yet.
Opening framing "This question — how does speed exist at a single moment? — bothered mathematicians for almost two thousand years. Zeno of Elea argued in 450 BC that motion itself was impossible for exactly this reason. Newton and Leibniz finally solved it in the 1680s. The tool they invented is calculus. Today, you're going to discover their answer using a marble and a ramp."
Average vs. Instantaneous — The Core Distinction (5 min)
Average Rate of Change
Speed over an interval — you need two points.

\[\frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} = \frac{d_2 - d_1}{t_2 - t_1}\]
Geometrically: the slope of the secant line through two points on the position-time graph.

Example: drove 120 miles in 2 hours → average speed = 60 mph. Were you always going 60? No.
Instantaneous Rate of Change
Speed at a single moment — seems to require only one point.

\[\lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}\]
Geometrically: the slope of the tangent line at a single point on the graph.

This is what your car's speedometer reads. Today we find it with shrinking intervals.
The key analogy — use the car speedometer The speedometer reads an instantaneous rate. Students have trusted speedometers their entire lives without realising they were trusting calculus. The speedometer computes a very small \(\Delta d / \Delta t\) over a tiny interval and approximates it as instantaneous. That's exactly what students will do with the shrinking interval table.
Slope Review — Knowledge Check (4 min)
Run the knowledge check from the site: "A car travels 120 miles in 2 hours. What is its average speed?" Answer: 60 mph. Then connect to slope: average speed \(= \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}\) is exactly the slope formula \(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\) with distance and time as the axes. Students who understand slope understand average rate of change — they just haven't seen it in this context yet.
Section 2 — Virtual Marble Simulator (20 min)
Simulator Secant Line → Tangent Line — Watch Convergence Happen 20 min
The Physics Model
Introduce \(d(t) = \frac{1}{2}at^2\) — position as a quadratic function. For a ramp with acceleration \(a = 50\) cm/s², the model predicts: \(d(1) = 25\) cm, \(d(2) = 100\) cm. The graph is a parabola. Students will compute average rates between pairs of points on this parabola and watch what happens as the two points get closer.
Running the Secant Visualiser — Step by Step (8 min)
  • 1Open the simulator on the projector. Start a run at default angle (20°). Freeze frame.
  • 2Show the secant line between two points. Read the average rate from the display: \(\frac{d_2-d_1}{t_2-t_1} = 50\) cm/s.
  • 3Invite a student to the projector — drag Point 2 closer to Point 1. Ask the class: "What's happening to the slope?"
  • 4Drag Point 2 very close — within 0.01 seconds of Point 1. Ask: "What value is the average rate approaching?"
  • 5Key question: "If we could make the interval infinitely small — if we could let \(\Delta t = 0\) — what would the rate be? Can we actually do that?"
  • 6Plant the answer: "We can't divide by zero. But we can ask: what value does the rate approach as \(\Delta t \to 0\)? That's a limit. And the limit is the instantaneous rate."
The convergence moment — say this "Watch what happens as the interval shrinks. The secant line is rotating — it's getting closer and closer to that line that just barely touches the curve at one point. That's the tangent line. And its slope — that number the average rate is approaching — that's the instantaneous speed. The limit is how we capture the moment we can never quite reach."
Sample Shrinking Interval Calculation (5 min)
Using \(d(t) = \frac{1}{2}(50)t^2 = 25t^2\), compute a manual shrinking interval around \(t=1\):
Interval \([t_1, t_2]\) \(d(t_1)\) (cm) \(d(t_2)\) (cm) \(\frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t}\) (cm/s)
\([1.0,\ 2.0]\)25.00100.0075.00
\([1.0,\ 1.5]\)25.0056.2562.50
\([1.0,\ 1.1]\)25.0030.2552.50
\([1.0,\ 1.01]\)25.0025.502550.25
\([1.0,\ 1.001]\)25.0025.05002550.025
\(t \to 1\)instantaneous→ 50.000 cm/s
The formula confirms it \(v(t) = d'(t) = at = 50 \times 1 = 50\) cm/s at \(t=1\). Students who watched the table converge to 50 will recognise this not as a formula to memorise but as the value their table was approaching. That's the moment the derivative becomes real.
📈 The Big Calculus Reveal — Plant This Now
What students are computing manually is the limit definition of the derivative: \(v(t) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{d(t+h)-d(t)}{h}\). Each row of the shrinking interval table is one evaluation of \(\frac{d(t+h)-d(t)}{h}\) for a specific value of \(h\). The table doesn't reach \(h=0\) — we can't divide by zero — but we can see where the values are heading. That destination is the derivative.
Section 3 — Marble Lab: Physical or Virtual (23 min)
Lab Data Collection — Real Marble or Virtual Simulator 23 min
Physical Lab Setup (if running)
  • 1Foam pipe insulation (split lengthwise) makes an excellent cheap ramp — available at any hardware store
  • 2Prop one end 6–10 inches high. Keep angle consistent across all trials.
  • 3Mark masking tape every 10 cm: 0, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50 cm from the start
  • 4Phone perpendicular to ramp, same height, tripod or propped — parallax error if filming from above
  • 5iPhone: Settings → Camera → Record Slo-mo → 240fps. Android: Camera → More → Slow Motion → max fps
  • 6Record 3 trials. Find exact frame when marble crosses each tape mark. Average the three frame numbers.
  • 7Convert: Time (seconds) = Frame number ÷ fps. Use the site's Frame-to-Time Converter.
If data is noisy — use it Real data rarely matches the model perfectly. If a group's average rates don't converge as cleanly as the simulator's, that's a teaching moment: "Your data shows measurement uncertainty. The model assumes perfect conditions. In real physics, we always have both the data and the model — and we use both." Students who see messy convergence understand the concept more deeply than students who see perfect convergence.
Virtual Lab Option (if no physical materials)
Students use the simulator's data directly. Set ramp angle to 20°, run the simulation, and read off position at \(t = 0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\) seconds. These values go into the shrinking interval table. The virtual data is clean and will converge exactly — which is fine for the first encounter with the concept. Note: using real data produces richer discussion but the concept is equally accessible either way.
Section 4 — Data Analysis: The Shrinking Interval Table (15 min)
★ The Derivative Convergence — This Is the Moment the Derivative Is Born 15 min
How to Run the Data Analysis Section
Students enter their data (or the sample data) into the site's data analysis tab. They select a target distance (e.g., 50 cm) and the site generates the shrinking interval table automatically. Have students watch the table fill in and ask: "What do you notice about the average rates as the interval gets smaller?" Do not tell them the answer. Wait. Usually a student will say "they're all getting close to the same number." That's the moment.
★ The derivative reveal — say this at the right moment "Every row in that table is an average rate — a slope of a secant line. Watch the column on the right. The intervals are shrinking: one second, half a second, a tenth, a hundredth, a thousandth. And the rates are all closing in on one number. That number — the one all those averages are approaching — that is the instantaneous rate of change at that moment. That is the derivative. The formula we write for it is just shorthand for what you just watched happen in this table."
The Limit Definition of the Derivative — Earned, Not Given
\[f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\]
For the marble:  \(v(t) = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{d(t+h)-d(t)}{h} = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left[\tfrac{1}{2}at^2\right] = at\)
The formula says: compute the average rate over a tiny interval of size \(h\), then ask what value that approaches as \(h \to 0\). That's exactly what the table showed. The formula is the table, compressed into notation.
Connecting to the Marble: Velocity Is Linear
For \(d(t) = \frac{1}{2}at^2\), the derivative gives \(v(t) = at\). Verify against the table: at \(t=1\) with \(a=50\), \(v(1) = 50\) cm/s. At \(t=2\), \(v(2) = 100\) cm/s. Ask students: "Why does velocity increase linearly? What does that tell us about the marble's acceleration?" Answer: constant acceleration means velocity grows at a constant rate — a linear function.
📈 The Calculus Payoff — Three Connections to Name Explicitly
1. Average rate \(= \frac{\Delta d}{\Delta t} =\) slope of secant line.
2. Instantaneous rate \(= \lim_{h\to0}\frac{d(t+h)-d(t)}{h} =\) slope of tangent line \(=\) the derivative.
3. For \(d(t) = \frac{1}{2}at^2\): the derivative is \(v(t) = at\) — velocity increases linearly, confirming constant acceleration. Students who can articulate all three connections are ready for the formal derivative unit.
Section 5 — Practice Zone (10 min)
Practice Four Drill Types — Average Rate, Secant Slopes, Limit Intuition, Interpretation 10 min
Drill Priority
Limit Intuition is the most important drill — it requires students to predict where average rates converge, which is the core skill of this lesson. Run this one first if time is short. Average Rate and Secant Slopes build procedural fluency. Interpretation (stating what a rate means in context) is the highest-level skill and worth circulating to check.
Exit Ticket & Answers
Close Exit Ticket — Three Problems 5 min
Problems & Answers
ET1: A marble's position is given by \(d(t) = 25t^2\) cm. Compute the average rate of change from \(t=1\) to \(t=1.5\) seconds, and from \(t=1\) to \(t=1.1\) seconds. What value do these rates appear to be approaching?

Answer: \(\frac{d(1.5)-d(1)}{0.5} = \frac{56.25-25}{0.5} = 62.5\) cm/s.  \(\frac{d(1.1)-d(1)}{0.1} = \frac{30.25-25}{0.1} = 52.5\) cm/s. Approaching 50 cm/s (the instantaneous rate at \(t=1\)).

ET2: In one sentence, explain the relationship between a secant line and a tangent line on a position-time graph.

Answer: A secant line connects two points and gives the average rate between them; as the two points get closer together, the secant line approaches the tangent line, which gives the instantaneous rate at a single point.

ET3: The limit \(\lim_{h\to0}\frac{d(t+h)-d(t)}{h}\) gives the instantaneous velocity. For \(d(t)=25t^2\), what is the instantaneous velocity at \(t=2\)?

Answer: \(v(t) = at = 50(2) = 100\) cm/s. (Or verify: \(\frac{d(2+h)-d(2)}{h} = \frac{25(2+h)^2-100}{h} = \frac{25(4+4h+h^2)-100}{h} = \frac{100h+25h^2}{h} = 100+25h \to 100\) as \(h\to0\).)
