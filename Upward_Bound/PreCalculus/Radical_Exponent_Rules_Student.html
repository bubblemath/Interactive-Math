Section 3 - Negative Exponents: The "Staircase" Pattern

A negative exponent tells you to take the of the base.

\(x^3 = x \cdot x \cdot x\)  →  \(x^2 = x \cdot x\)  →  \(x^1 = x\)  →  \(x^0 = 1\)  →  \(x^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{x}\)  →  \(x^{-2} = \dfrac{1}{x^2}\)

Each step divides by \(x\). The pattern forces \(x^0 = 1\) and negative exponents to be fractions!

Section 4 - Rational Exponents: "Power Over Root"
The Transformer Formula
\(x^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{x^m} = \left(\sqrt[n]{x}\right)^m\)

Memory Trick: "Power over Root" — numerator is the power, denominator is the root!

Section 6 - Rationalizing the Denominator

Goal: Eliminate radicals from the of a fraction.

Monomials: Multiply by the Radical

\(\dfrac{5}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt{3}} = \dfrac{5\sqrt{3}}{3}\)

Try It:

\(\dfrac{7}{\sqrt{5}} =\)

Binomials: Multiply by the Conjugate

Conjugate of \((a + \sqrt{b})\) is \((a - \sqrt{b})\)

Try It:

Practice - Rationalize the Denominator

🔗 Calculus Connection: Why Rational Exponents Matter

In Calculus, you must convert radicals to rational exponents before finding derivatives!

Radical Form Calculus-Ready Form
\(f(x) = \sqrt{x}\) \(f(x) = x^{1/2}\)
\(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{x^3}\) \(f(x) = x^{-3}\)
\(f(x) = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt[3]{x^2}}\) \(f(x) =\)

The Power Rule \(\frac{d}{dx}(x^n) = nx^{n-1}\) only works with exponent form!

Section 7 - Summary Checklist
  • I can identify and avoid "illegal moves" (Freshman's Dream, Radical Split).
  • I add exponents when multiplying same bases: \(x^m \cdot x^n = x^{m+n}\).
  • I subtract exponents when dividing same bases: \(\frac{x^m}{x^n} = x^{m-n}\).
  • I multiply exponents for power-to-power: \((x^m)^n = x^{mn}\).
  • I move negative exponents to the other side of the fraction bar.
  • I use "Power over Root" for rational exponents: \(x^{m/n} = \sqrt[n]{x^m}\).
  • I can simplify radicals by extracting perfect powers.
  • My final answers have no radicals in the denominator.
