📚 KEY DEFINITIONS
Rational Equation An equation containing at least one fraction where the variable appears in a denominator.
\( \frac{2}{x-3} + \frac{1}{x} = \frac{5}{x(x-3)} \)
Extraneous Solution A "ghost" solution that emerges from the algebra but makes an original denominator equal to zero. Must be rejected!
If \(x = 3\) but denominator has \((x-3)\)...
💥 EXTRANEOUS!

Why Do Extraneous Solutions Appear?

When we multiply by the LCD, we're multiplying by an expression that could equal zero.

This "legal move" can introduce solutions that weren't valid in the original equation.

⚠️ ALWAYS CHECK YOUR SOLUTIONS!

🔧 THE 4-STEP SOLVING STRATEGY
1
FACTOR
Factor all denominators completely
2
FIND LCD
Identify restrictions & build LCD
3
MULTIPLY
Clear ALL denominators
4
CHECK
Test solutions in original!
  1. Factor every denominator to see the "building blocks." This reveals what values make each denominator zero.
  2. Find the LCD by listing all unique factors. Write domain restrictions: x ≠ ? for each factor.
  3. Multiply EVERY term (both sides!) by the LCD. Watch denominators vanish! Don't forget standalone numbers.
  4. Solve & Check: Solve the resulting equation, then test EVERY solution against your restrictions.
⚠️ COMMON PITFALLS & CORRECTIONS
❌ The Mistake Why It's Wrong ✅ The Correction
"Partial Multiplication"
Only multiplying fractions by LCD 		Whole numbers and terms without denominators ALSO need to be multiplied by the LCD! Multiply EVERY term on BOTH sides by the LCD, including constants like "1" or "3"
"The Ghost Solution"
Accepting all algebraic solutions 		A solution that makes a denominator zero is undefined—it's a "ghost" that doesn't really exist! Always perform the Solution Burner test: plug each solution into ORIGINAL denominators
"LCD Overkill"
Multiplying all denominators together 		Creates unnecessarily large expressions and more room for error Factor first! Use only the LEAST common denominator (unique factors, highest powers)
"Late Restrictions"
Finding restrictions after solving 		You might forget which values were restricted and accept an extraneous solution Write restrictions IMMEDIATELY after factoring, BEFORE any solving

Worked Example & Quick Reference

✏️ WORKED EXAMPLE: Solve \( \frac{2}{x-3} + \frac{1}{x} = \frac{6}{x^2-3x} \)
Step 1: Factor all denominators
• \(x - 3\) → already factored
• \(x\) → already factored
• \(x^2 - 3x = x(x-3)\) ✓
Step 2: Find LCD & Restrictions
LCD = \(x(x-3)\)

Restrictions:
x ≠ 0 x ≠ 3
Step 3: Multiply EVERY term by LCD
\( x(x-3) \cdot \frac{2}{x-3} + x(x-3) \cdot \frac{1}{x} = x(x-3) \cdot \frac{6}{x(x-3)} \)

Simplify each term:
\( 2x + (x-3) = 6 \)
Step 4a: Solve the equation
\( 2x + x - 3 = 6 \)
\( 3x - 3 = 6 \)
\( 3x = 9 \)
\( x = 3 \)
Step 4b: CHECK — The Solution Burner!
Test \(x = 3\) against restrictions:
• Is \(x = 3\) in our restriction list?
• YES! We have x ≠ 3

🔥 SOLUTION BURNER TEST 🔥

Testing x = 3
Denominator \((x-3)\):
\(3 - 3 = 0\)
💥 UNDEFINED!
❌ REJECTED — x = 3 is EXTRANEOUS
FINAL ANSWER: NO SOLUTION
(The only algebraic solution was extraneous)
✅ QUICK REFERENCE CHECKLIST
  • Did I factor ALL denominators completely?
  • Did I write domain restrictions BEFORE solving?
  • Did I identify the correct LCD (not just multiply all denominators)?
  • Did I multiply EVERY term by the LCD (including constants)?
  • Did I distribute correctly after multiplying?
  • Did I solve the resulting equation correctly?
  • Did I test EVERY solution against the original restrictions?
  • Did I reject any extraneous solutions?
🚀 CALCULUS PREVIEW: Why Extraneous Solutions Matter
📍

Vertical Asymptotes

Values that make denominators zero (and DON'T cancel) create vertical asymptotes—the graph shoots to ±∞!

🕳️

Removable Discontinuities

Values that make denominators zero but DO cancel create "holes"—points missing from the graph.

📐

Limits

Finding where functions are undefined is the first step in evaluating limits—the foundation of Calculus!
