Day 8: Special Logarithms

Section 4 - The Number \(e\) and Special Logs

Two logarithms are so common they get special names:

Common Logarithm

\(\log(x) = \log_{10}(x)\)

Base (calculator default)

Natural Logarithm

\(\ln(x) = \log_e(x)\)

Base \(e \approx\)

🌟 Why is \(e\) Special?

The number \(e \approx 2.71828...\) is called the "natural" base because:

  • It appears naturally in compound interest: \(\lim_{n \to \infty}\left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n = e\)
  • It's the ONLY base where the derivative of \(b^x\) equals itself!
  • It appears in population growth, radioactive decay, and countless natural phenomena
Section 5 - The "Big Three" Log Properties

These rules let you EXPAND (break apart) or CONDENSE (combine) logarithmic expressions:

Product Rule
\(\log_b(MN) = \log_b M + \log_b N\)
Multiply inside → Add outside
Quotient Rule
\(\log_b\left(\frac{M}{N}\right) = \log_b M - \log_b N\)
Divide inside → Subtract outside
Power Rule
\(\log_b(M^p) = p \cdot \log_b M\)
Exponent inside → Multiplier outside
Worked Example: Expand \(\log_2\left(\frac{x^3 y}{z}\right)\)

Step 1: Apply Quotient Rule: \(\log_2(x^3 y) - \log_2(z)\)

Step 2: Apply Product Rule to first term: \(\log_2(x^3) + \log_2(y) - \log_2(z)\)

Step 3: Apply Power Rule: \(\boxed{3\log_2 x + \log_2 y - \log_2 z}\)

Section 8 - Common Pitfalls
❌ The Trap ✅ The Truth
Log Addition Trap
\(\log(A + B) = \log A + \log B\)		 FALSE! Logs turn into addition, NOT addition into addition.
\(\log(A + B) \neq \log A + \log B\)
Negative Argument
Forgetting domain restrictions		 You CANNOT take the log of or a number!
Domain of \(\log_b(x)\): \(x > 0\)
Power Rule Misuse
\(\log(x + 3)^2 = 2\log(x+3)\)		 Power Rule only works when the ENTIRE argument is raised to the power.
Check your parentheses!

Day 8: Solving Exponential Equations

Section 9 - Solving Exponential Equations

Two main methods depending on the equation:

Method A: Same Base

If both sides can be written with the SAME base, set exponents equal.

Example: \(4^x = 32\)

\((2^2)^x = 2^5\)

\(2^{2x} = 2^5\)

\(2x = 5\) → \(x = \frac{5}{2}\)

Method B: Take the Log

When same base isn't possible, take \(\log\) or \(\ln\) of both sides.

Example: \(5^x = 12\)

\(\ln(5^x) = \ln(12)\)

\(x \cdot \ln 5 = \ln 12\)

\(x = \frac{\ln 12}{\ln 5} \approx 1.544\)

📋 The 4-Step Procedure

  2. Convert to exponential form using the Heart Method
  3. Solve the resulting equation
  4. CHECK for extraneous solutions! 🔥

Day 8: The Solution Burner & Practice

Worked Example: Solve \(\log_2(x) + \log_2(x-2) = 3\)

Step 1: Combine using Product Rule: \(\log_2[x(x-2)] = 3\)

Step 2: Convert to exponential: \(x(x-2) = 2^3 = 8\)

Step 3: Solve: \(x^2 - 2x = 8\) → \(x^2 - 2x - 8 = 0\) → \((x-4)(x+2) = 0\)

Solutions: \(x = 4\) or \(x = -2\)

Step 4: CHECK! Does \(x = -2\) work? \(\log_2(-2)\) is !

Final Answer: \(\boxed{x = 4}\) only

🚀 Calculus Connection: Why \(e\) is the "Magic" Base

In Calculus, you'll discover something amazing about \(e^x\):

\(\frac{d}{dx}[e^x] = e^x\)

The derivative of \(e^x\) is itself! This is the ONLY function with this property.
