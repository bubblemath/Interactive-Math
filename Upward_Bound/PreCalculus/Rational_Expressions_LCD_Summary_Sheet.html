📚 KEY DEFINITIONS
Rational Expression A fraction where the numerator and denominator are polynomials.
\( \frac{x^2 - 9}{x + 3} \)
Domain Restrictions Values of \(x\) that make the denominator zero. These are "illegal" inputs!
In \( \frac{5}{x-2} \), \( x \neq 2 \)
\( \frac{\text{Polynomial (Numerator)}}{\text{Polynomial (Denominator)}} \)
↑
Top
Can be zero
(creates x-intercepts)
↑
Bottom
CANNOT be zero
(creates restrictions)
Factor vs. Term Factor: Parts connected by multiplication
Term: Parts connected by addition/subtraction
\( \underbrace{(x+2)}_{\text{factor}} \cdot \underbrace{(x-3)}_{\text{factor}} \) vs. \( \underbrace{x}_{\text{term}} + \underbrace{2}_{\text{term}} \)
LCD (Least Common Denominator) The smallest expression that contains ALL factors from ALL denominators.
LCD of \(\frac{1}{x-2}\) and \(\frac{1}{x+3}\) is \((x-2)(x+3)\)
⚠️ THE "ILLEGAL CANCEL" WARNING

You can only cancel FACTORS (multiplication), never TERMS (addition/subtraction)!

❌ Common Mistake ✅ Correction Why?
\( \frac{x + 5}{x} = 5 \) Cannot simplify further The \(x\) in the numerator is a term, not a factor
\( \frac{x^2 - 9}{x - 3} = x^2 + 3 \) \( \frac{(x-3)(x+3)}{x-3} = x+3 \) Must factor completely BEFORE canceling
\( \frac{x+2}{x+2} = 0 \) \( \frac{x+2}{x+2} = 1 \) (where \(x \neq -2\)) Anything divided by itself equals 1, not 0
Forgetting restrictions after canceling Always keep original restrictions! The hole still exists even after simplification
🔧 PROCEDURE: Simplifying Rational Expressions
  1. Factor the numerator and denominator completely. (Use AC method, difference of squares, etc.)
  2. Identify Restrictions by setting each denominator factor \(\neq 0\). Write these FIRST!
  3. Cancel common factors that appear in BOTH the numerator and denominator.
  4. Rewrite the simplified expression, keeping ALL original restrictions.
✏️ WORKED EXAMPLE: Simplify \( \frac{x^2 + 5x + 6}{x^2 - 4} \)
Step 1: Factor completely
Numerator: \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x+2)(x+3)\)
Denominator: \(x^2 - 4 = (x+2)(x-2)\)
Step 2: Identify restrictions
\(x + 2 = 0 \Rightarrow x \neq -2\)
\(x - 2 = 0 \Rightarrow x \neq 2\)
Step 3: Cancel common factors
\( \frac{\cancel{(x+2)}(x+3)}{\cancel{(x+2)}(x-2)} \)
Step 4: Write final answer
\( \frac{x+3}{x-2} \), where \( x \neq -2, 2 \)

The "LCD Constructor" Method
Page 2 of 2
👨‍🍳 THE LCD RECIPE

To find the Least Common Denominator (LCD) for two or more fractions:

  1. Factor every denominator completely.
  2. List every unique factor that appears in ANY denominator.
  3. Assign the highest power (exponent) seen on each factor.
  4. Multiply these factors together to create your LCD.
(x-3) × (x+3) × (x+2) = LCD
✏️ WORKED EXAMPLE: Build the LCD

Find the LCD and build equivalent fractions for: \( \frac{1}{x^2-9} \) and \( \frac{1}{x^2+5x+6} \)

Step 1: Factor denominators
Denom A: \(x^2 - 9 = (x-3)(x+3)\)
Denom B: \(x^2 + 5x + 6 = (x+2)(x+3)\)
Step 2: List unique factors
(x-3) (x+3) (x+2)
Step 3: Construct LCD
LCD = \((x-3)(x+3)(x+2)\)
Step 4: "What's Missing?" Method

Compare each denominator to the LCD:

Fraction 1: \(\frac{1}{(x-3)(x+3)}\)
Missing: (x+2)
Multiply top & bottom by \((x+2)\):
\( \frac{1 \cdot (x+2)}{(x-3)(x+3)(x+2)} = \frac{x+2}{\text{LCD}} \)
Fraction 2: \(\frac{1}{(x+2)(x+3)}\)
Missing: (x-3)
Multiply top & bottom by \((x-3)\):
\( \frac{1 \cdot (x-3)}{(x+2)(x+3)(x-3)} = \frac{x-3}{\text{LCD}} \)
✅ QUICK REFERENCE CHECKLIST
  • Did I factor the denominator BEFORE looking for restrictions?
  • Did I list restrictions BEFORE canceling factors?
  • Am I only canceling FACTORS (×), not TERMS (+)?
  • Does my LCD include EVERY unique factor from all denominators?
  • Did I use the highest power of each factor in the LCD?
  • When building equivalent fractions, did I multiply BOTH top and bottom?
  • Did I keep ALL original restrictions in my final answer?
  • Did I check if the final answer can be simplified further?
🚀 CALCULUS PREVIEW: Why This Matters
🕳️

Holes vs. Asymptotes

Domain restrictions tell us where a function has a hole (factor cancels) or a vertical asymptote (factor doesn't cancel). This is critical for graphing rational functions!

📐

The Derivative

Finding the LCD is the first step in adding "Difference Quotients," which is how we calculate the derivative (instantaneous rate of change) — the foundation of Calculus!
