📚 KEY DEFINITIONS: Polynomial Behavior
End Behavior How the graph behaves as \(x \to +\infty\) and \(x \to -\infty\). Determined by the degree and leading coefficient.
\(f(x) = -2x^3 + 5x\): Odd degree, negative lead
↗ left, ↘ right
Multiplicity The number of times a zero (root) appears as a factor. Determines if the graph crosses or bounces at that zero.
\(f(x) = (x-2)^3(x+1)^2\)
Zero at 2 (mult. 3), zero at -1 (mult. 2)

📐 THE LEADING COEFFICIENT TEST

Degree Leading Coef. Left End (\(x \to -\infty\)) Right End (\(x \to +\infty\)) Visual
Even Positive (+) ↑ rises ↑ rises ↗ ↗
Even Negative (−) ↓ falls ↓ falls ↘ ↘
Odd Positive (+) ↓ falls ↑ rises ↘ ↗
Odd Negative (−) ↑ rises ↓ falls ↗ ↘

💡 Memory Trick: Even degree = "same direction" | Odd degree = "opposite directions"

🎯 MULTIPLICITY: Cross vs. Bounce

ODD Multiplicity
✖️
Graph CROSSES the x-axis
\((x-2)^1\), \((x+3)^3\), \((x-5)^5\)
EVEN Multiplicity
⤴️
Graph BOUNCES off the x-axis
\((x-2)^2\), \((x+3)^4\), \((x-5)^6\)

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES & CORRECTIONS
❌ The Mistake Why It's Wrong ✅ The Correction
"Degree Confusion"
Using highest exponent visible 		The degree is the highest power when the polynomial is in standard form. \(x(x+1)^2\) has degree 3, not 2! Expand or count: \(x(x+1)^2 = x^3 + 2x^2 + x\)
Degree = 3
"Elimination Error"
Forgetting to multiply ALL terms 		When multiplying an equation by a constant, EVERY term must be multiplied, including the constant on the right side. \(2(3x + y = 5)\) becomes
\(6x + 2y = 10\) (not \(6x + 2y = 5\))
"Bounce vs. Cross"
Even = crosses 		It's the opposite! Even multiplicity means the graph touches and turns around (bounces). Odd mult. = CROSSES
Even mult. = BOUNCES

🔧 PROCEDURE: Solving Systems by Elimination
  1. Align both equations in standard form: \(Ax + By = C\)
  2. Identify which variable to eliminate (look for opposites or easy multipliers)
  3. Multiply one or both equations so coefficients of one variable are opposites
  4. Add the equations to eliminate that variable
  5. Solve the resulting single-variable equation
  6. Substitute back to find the other variable
  7. Check your solution in BOTH original equations
✏️ WORKED EXAMPLE: Solve the System by Elimination
\(\begin{cases} 2x + 3y = 12 \\ 4x - 3y = 6 \end{cases}\)
Step 1: Check Alignment
Both equations are in standard form ✓
Step 2: Identify Variable to Eliminate
The \(y\) coefficients are already opposites!
\(+3y\) and \(-3y\) → Perfect for elimination
Step 3: Add the Equations
\(2x + 3y = 12\)
\(\underline{+ \; 4x - 3y = 6}\)
\(6x + 0y = 18\)
Step 4: Solve for x
\(6x = 18\)
\(x = 3\)
Step 5: Substitute Back
\(2(3) + 3y = 12\)
\(6 + 3y = 12\)
\(3y = 6\)
\(y = 2\)
Solution: \((x, y) = (3, 2)\)
Check: \(2(3) + 3(2) = 12\) ✓ and \(4(3) - 3(2) = 6\) ✓
🔧 PROCEDURE: Sketching Polynomial Graphs
  1. Find the degree and leading coefficient → Determine end behavior
  2. Find all zeros by setting \(f(x) = 0\) and solving (factor if needed)
  3. Determine multiplicity of each zero → Cross or bounce?
  4. Find the y-intercept by evaluating \(f(0)\)
  5. Plot zeros and y-intercept, then connect with smooth curve following end behavior
Example: \(f(x) = -x^3 + 4x = -x(x-2)(x+2)\)
Degree 3, negative lead → ↗ left, ↘ right | Zeros at \(x = 0, 2, -2\) (all mult. 1 → all cross)

🚀 CALCULUS PREVIEW: Limits & Local Behavior
∞

Limits at Infinity

End behavior IS the limit! When you write "as \(x \to \infty\), \(f(x) \to \infty\)", you're describing:

\(\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \infty\)
📉

Local Extrema & Derivatives

The "hills and valleys" between zeros are found using derivatives. A polynomial of degree \(n\) has at most \(n-1\) turning points!
