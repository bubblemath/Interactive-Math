Day 12: Introduction to Limits

Section 5 - What is a Limit?
🚶 The "Diner" Metaphor

Imagine walking toward a diner on Main Street. The LIMIT is the location you expect to reach when you get to the door.

Even if the diner has been demolished (a in the graph), your intended destination remains the same!

Key Insight: A limit asks "What value are we APPROACHING?" not "What value are we AT?"

📏 LIMIT NOTATION

\(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = L\)

"The limit of \(f(x)\) as \(x\) approaches \(c\) equals \(L\)"

Section 6 - Numerical Limits: The "Zoom-In" Method
📋 HOW TO FIND A LIMIT NUMERICALLY
  1. Choose values of \(x\) that get closer and closer to \(c\) from the LEFT (smaller values).
  2. Choose values of \(x\) that get closer and closer to \(c\) from the RIGHT (larger values).
  3. Calculate \(f(x)\) for each value.
  4. If both sides approach the same number, that's the limit!
Worked Example: Find \(\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\)

Section 7 - The "Two-Finger Trace" Method
👆👆 How to Find Limits on a Graph
  1. Put one finger on the graph to the LEFT of the point.
  2. Put another finger on the graph to the RIGHT of the point.
  3. Trace both fingers TOWARD the x-value.
  4. If your fingers meet at the same height, that's the limit!
  5. If they're at different heights, the limit Does Not Exist (DNE).
Section 8 - One-Sided Limits
Left-Hand Limit
\(\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x)\)
Approaching from the LEFT only
Right-Hand Limit
\(\lim_{x \to c^+} f(x)\)
Approaching from the RIGHT only
Two-Sided Limit
\(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\)
Both sides must AGREE!
🔑 THE LIMIT EXISTS IF AND ONLY IF...

\(\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to c^+} f(x) = L\)

Both one-sided limits must exist AND be equal!

Day 12: Limit vs. Function Value

Section 9 - Limit vs. Function Value
The Limit \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\)

What height are we APPROACHING?

Ignores what actually happens AT the point.

Can exist even if there's a hole!

The Value \(f(c)\)

What is the ACTUAL height at \(x = c\)?

Could be undefined (hole).

Could be different from the limit (jump).

Example: Reading a Graph with a Hole

If a graph has an open circle at (3, 5) and a solid dot at (3, 2):

\(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x) = \) (the height we're approaching)

\(f(3) = \) (the actual value at x = 3)

The limit and the value are NOT the same!

Day 12: Pitfalls & Practice

Section 10 - Common Pitfalls
❌ The Trap ✅ The Fix
"Undefined" = "DNE" Not the same! \(f(c)\) can be undefined while \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\) still exists (holes!).
Limit = Value The limit is where we're HEADED, not where we ARE. Check for holes and jumps!
Forgetting One Side Always check BOTH left and right. If they disagree, the two-sided limit DNE.
Ignoring Physical Domain In modeling, negative lengths don't exist! Always state realistic constraints.

Day 12: Summary & Exit Ticket

🚀 Calculus Connection: The Derivative is a Limit!

The Difference Quotient

The slope between two points on a curve:

\(\frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}\)

As \(h \to 0\), the two points get closer and closer...

The Derivative Definition

The instantaneous rate of change:

\(f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x + h) - f(x)}{h}\)

This is why limits are the foundation of Calculus!
