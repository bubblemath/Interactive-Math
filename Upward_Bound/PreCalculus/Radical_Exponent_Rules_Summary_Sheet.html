Rule Name Formula Visual Expansion Example
Product Rule \(x^m \cdot x^n = x^{m+n}\) \((xx)(xxx) = xxxxx\) \(x^2 \cdot x^3 = x^5\)
Quotient Rule \(\frac{x^m}{x^n} = x^{m-n}\) \(\frac{xxxxx}{xx} = xxx\) \(\frac{x^5}{x^2} = x^3\)
Power Rule \((x^m)^n = x^{m \cdot n}\) \((x^2)^3 = x^2 \cdot x^2 \cdot x^2\) \((x^2)^3 = x^6\)
Zero Exponent \(x^0 = 1\) \(\frac{x^n}{x^n} = x^{n-n} = x^0 = 1\) \(5^0 = 1\)
Negative Exp. \(x^{-n} = \frac{1}{x^n}\) Move across the fraction bar \(x^{-2} = \frac{1}{x^2}\)
Power of Product \((xy)^n = x^n y^n\) \((xy)(xy)(xy) = x^3y^3\) \((2x)^3 = 8x^3\)

The Negative Exponent Pattern

\(x^3\) = \(x \cdot x \cdot x\)
\(x^2\) = \(x \cdot x\)
\(x^1\) = \(x\)
\(x^0\) = \(1\)
\(x^{-1}\) = \(\frac{1}{x}\)
\(x^{-2}\) = \(\frac{1}{x^2}\)

Each step down = divide by x

⚠️ THE "ILLEGAL MOVES" — Common Mistakes
❌ The Mistake Why It's Wrong ✅ Correct Approach
"Freshman's Dream"
\((x+y)^2 = x^2 + y^2\) 		Exponents do NOT distribute over addition or subtraction! \((x+y)^2 = x^2 + 2xy + y^2\)
"Radical Split"
\(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2} = x + y\) 		Radicals cannot be split across + or −. Only works with multiplication! Leave as \(\sqrt{x^2 + y^2}\) (cannot simplify)
"Multiply vs. Add"
\(x^2 \cdot x^3 = x^6\) 		When multiplying same bases, ADD exponents (not multiply) \(x^2 \cdot x^3 = x^{2+3} = x^5\)
"Divide vs. Subtract"
\(\frac{x^6}{x^2} = x^3\) 		When dividing same bases, SUBTRACT exponents \(\frac{x^6}{x^2} = x^{6-2} = x^4\)
💎 RADICAL ↔ RATIONAL EXPONENT TRANSFORMER
\( x^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{x^m} = \left(\sqrt[n]{x}\right)^m \)
↑
m = power
(numerator)
↑
n = index
(denominator = root)
Radical Form
\(\sqrt[3]{x^2}\)
⟷
Rational Exponent
\(x^{2/3}\)

Memory Trick: "Power over Root" — the power goes on top, the root goes on bottom!

🔧 PROCEDURE: Simplifying Radicals
  1. Find Perfect Powers: Identify the largest perfect \(n\)-th power factor (where \(n\) is the index of the radical).
  2. Split Using Product Rule: \(\sqrt[n]{ab} = \sqrt[n]{a} \cdot \sqrt[n]{b}\) — separate perfect powers from leftovers.
  3. Extract Perfect Powers: Take the \(n\)-th root of perfect powers and move them outside the radical.
  4. Variable Rule: Divide the variable's exponent by the index. Quotient comes OUT, remainder stays IN.
  5. Combine: Multiply all extracted terms together, leaving non-perfect factors under the radical.
✏️ WORKED EXAMPLE: Simplify \(\sqrt{72x^5y^6}\)
Step 1: Factor the coefficient
\(72 = 36 \times 2\)
(36 is the largest perfect square factor)
Step 2: Split variables by index (2)
\(x^5 = x^4 \cdot x^1\) (4 ÷ 2 = 2 out, 1 stays)
\(y^6 = y^6\) (6 ÷ 2 = 3 out, 0 stays)
Step 3: Group into perfect & leftover
\(\sqrt{(36 \cdot x^4 \cdot y^6)(2 \cdot x)}\)
Step 4: Extract perfect squares
\(\sqrt{36} = 6\)
\(\sqrt{x^4} = x^2\)
\(\sqrt{y^6} = y^3\)
Step 5: Write final answer
\(\sqrt{72x^5y^6} = 6x^2y^3\sqrt{2x}\)
🔧 PROCEDURE: Rationalizing Denominators

Simple Radical (Monomial):

  1. Multiply top and bottom by the radical in the denominator.
  2. Simplify: \(\sqrt{a} \cdot \sqrt{a} = a\)
\(\frac{3}{\sqrt{5}} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{5}}{\sqrt{5}} = \frac{3\sqrt{5}}{5}\)

Binomial Denominator (Conjugate):

  1. Multiply by the conjugate: \((a + \sqrt{b}) \to (a - \sqrt{b})\)
  2. Use difference of squares to eliminate the radical.
\(\frac{2}{3+\sqrt{5}} \cdot \frac{3-\sqrt{5}}{3-\sqrt{5}} = \frac{2(3-\sqrt{5})}{9-5} = \frac{6-2\sqrt{5}}{4}\)

🚀 CALCULUS PREVIEW: Why Rational Exponents Matter
📐

The Power Rule

In Calculus, we use the Power Rule to find derivatives: \(\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}\). This ONLY works with exponents, not radicals!

Example: To differentiate \(\sqrt{x}\), first rewrite as \(x^{1/2}\), then apply the rule.

∫

Integration

The same conversion is essential for integration (finding areas). Converting \(\sqrt[3]{x^2}\) to \(x^{2/3}\) makes the antiderivative straightforward.

Master this now = smoother Calculus later!
