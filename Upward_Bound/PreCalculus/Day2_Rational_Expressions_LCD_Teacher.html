Lesson Overview - Read Before Teaching

This lesson builds the rational expression fluency that calculus demands constantly - in limits, the quotient rule, and partial fraction decomposition. The entire lesson is unified by one insight: you can only cancel factors, never terms. That single rule, understood deeply, prevents the most common algebra error in calculus.

The lesson's most important conceptual moment is the hole vs. asymptote distinction. Students who understand why a cancelled factor produces a hole (removable discontinuity) while a remaining factor produces a vertical asymptote are ready to understand \(\frac{0}{0}\) limits - the fundamental motivation for the factoring and cancellation work in calculus.

The interactive lesson at bubblemath.github.io provides the "Spot the Illegal Cancel" warm-up, LCD Constructor Lab, Simplification Station with restriction tracking, and leveled practice drills. Students should have it open on devices alongside the printed notes sheet.

Learning Objectives
  • Identify domain restrictions of a rational expression before any simplification
  • Simplify rational expressions by factoring and cancelling common factors (the Giant One)
  • Distinguish between holes (removable discontinuities) and vertical asymptotes
  • Find the Least Common Denominator (LCD) using the "Factor, List, Highest Power" recipe
  • Build equivalent fractions with a common denominator
  • Recognize and avoid illegal cancellation errors

Section 1 - Warm-Up: Spot the Illegal Cancel 10 min

Purpose:

This diagnostic warm-up surfaces the most common misconception immediately: students who try to cancel terms instead of factors. Use the results to gauge how much time to spend on the "Giant One" concept.

ILLEGAL - Cancelling Terms

\(\dfrac{x + 5}{x} = 5\)

The \(x\) in the numerator is part of a sum \((x+5)\), not a factor. There is no "Giant One" here.

LEGAL - Cancelling Factors

\(\dfrac{(x+2)(x-1)}{(x+2)(x+5)} = \dfrac{x-1}{x+5}\)

The \((x+2)\) is multiplied in both. \(\frac{x+2}{x+2} = 1\) - that's the Giant One.

Script - Say This
"Before we dive in, I want to see what you already know. Open Tab 1 on the interactive hub. You'll see four 'simplifications' - some are legal, some are illegal. Your job is to identify which is which. You have 3 minutes. Don't guess - think about WHY each one is or isn't valid."
Section 2 - The Giant One and LCD Recipe 15 min

The Giant One Concept

\(\dfrac{x-a}{x-a} = 1 \quad (x \neq a)\)

Cancelling a factor is dividing by 1 - it doesn't change the value!

The LCD Recipe (3 Steps)

  1. Factor all denominators completely
  2. List all unique factors
  3. Use the Highest Power of each factor
Example: Find the LCD

Denominators: \(x^2 - 9\) and \(x^2 + 5x + 6\)

Factor: \((x+3)(x-3)\) and \((x+2)(x+3)\)

List unique factors: \((x+3), (x-3), (x+2)\)

LCD: \((x+3)(x-3)(x+2)\)

Script - Domain Restrictions
"Before we simplify ANYTHING, we identify restrictions. Set each factor in the original denominator equal to zero. These restrictions persist even after simplifying - that's the difference between a hole and an asymptote, which we'll see in calculus."
Common Pitfalls and Teaching Strategies
1. The "Illegal Cancel"

Students try to cancel terms across addition: \(\frac{x+5}{x} = 5\)

Strategy: Use numerical substitution. "If \(x=2\), the left side is \(\frac{7}{2}\), but the right side is 5. They're not equal!" Emphasize: "We only cancel factors, never terms."
2. Forgotten Restrictions

Students simplify and forget the original domain restrictions.

Strategy: Use the "Restriction Tracker" sidebar in the Hub. Teach students to list restrictions BEFORE they "slash" any factors. The restriction persists even after the factor is gone.
3. LCD Over-Complication

Students multiply all denominators together rather than finding the LEAST common denominator.

Strategy: Use the "LCD Recipe" (Factor, List, Highest Power). Show that \((x+2)\) and \((x+2)^2\) require only \((x+2)^2\), not \((x+2)^3\).
4. Factoring Errors in Numerator

Students correctly factor the denominator but make sign errors when factoring the numerator.

Strategy: Always verify by expanding. "If \(x^2 - 9 = (x+3)(x-3)\), then \((x+3)(x-3)\) should FOIL back to \(x^2 - 9\)."
Partner and Small Group Activities
1. LCD "Speed Dating" Pairs

Give each student a card with a single factored denominator (e.g., \((x+3)\) or \((x-2)^2\)). Students pair up and must "build" their LCD together on a mini-whiteboard. Rotate every 2 minutes.

Materials: Index cards with factors, mini-whiteboards, timer
Reinforces: LCD must contain the highest power of every unique factor present
2. "Error Analysis" Gallery Walk Small Groups

Post 5 "worked problems" around the room that contain common errors (The Illegal Cancel, missing a restriction, sign error in LCD). Groups move from station to station to "diagnose" the error and provide the "cure" (correct steps).

Materials: 5 poster-sized worked problems with intentional errors, sticky notes for corrections
Reinforces: Critical analysis, error recognition, mathematical communication
3. "What's Missing?" Relay Teams of 4

Provide a starting fraction and a target LCD. Student 1 factors the denominator. Student 2 identifies the "missing factor." Student 3 multiplies numerator and denominator by that factor. Student 4 simplifies the final numerator.

Materials: Relay problem cards, answer verification sheet
Reinforces: Building equivalent fractions, collaborative problem-solving
4. "Factor Chip" Manipulatives Pairs

Give pairs physical or digital "factor chips" (e.g., chips labeled \((x+2)\), \((x-3)\), \((x+1)^2\)). Students draw two denominator cards and physically arrange chips to build the LCD.

Materials: Laminated factor chips, denominator problem cards
Reinforces: Visual/kinesthetic understanding of LCD construction
5. "Create the Expression" Challenge Pairs - Advanced

Challenge students to create a rational expression that meets specific requirements: "Create an expression with a hole at \(x=2\) and a vertical asymptote at \(x=-5\)." Partners verify each other's work.

Materials: Challenge cards with specifications, Desmos for verification
Reinforces: Deep understanding of holes vs. asymptotes, reverse engineering
6. "Simplification Showdown" Teams of 3

Teams race to simplify rational expressions correctly. One team member factors, one cancels, one states restrictions. All three must agree before submitting. First correct answer wins the round.

Materials: Problem cards (3 difficulty levels), team answer sheets, timer
Reinforces: Collaborative verification, speed with accuracy

Calculus Preview - Say This
"When you see \(\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\), direct substitution gives \(\frac{0}{0}\) - an indeterminate form. The solution? Factor and simplify first, exactly like we're doing today. The cancelled factor creates a 'hole' - a single missing point where the limit still exists. The skills you're building today are the exact skills you'll use to evaluate limits in calculus."
Formative Assessment Checkpoints
1 After Warm-Up: Can students identify the domain of \(\frac{1}{x^2-9}\) before simplifying? (Answer: \(x \neq 3, x \neq -3\))
2 During LCD Lab: Are students correctly identifying that \((x+2)\) and \((x+2)^2\) require the squared version for the LCD?
3 During Simplify Station: Are students remembering that a canceled factor still creates a "hole" in the graph? (Restriction persists!)
4 Before Exit Ticket: Can students explain in their own words why \(\frac{x+3}{x+5}\) cannot be simplified?
Exit Ticket Answers (Tab 6)

ET1: Simplify and state all restrictions: \(\dfrac{x^2+x-6}{x^2-4}\)

Answer: Factor \(\frac{(x+3)(x-2)}{(x+2)(x-2)}\); cancel \((x-2)\); result \(\frac{x+3}{x+2}\), restrictions \(x \neq 2, x \neq -2\)

ET2: Find the LCD: \(\dfrac{1}{x-3}\) and \(\dfrac{1}{x^2-9}\)

Answer: Factor second denominator: \((x+3)(x-3)\). LCD = \((x+3)(x-3)\)

ET3: Build equivalent fraction: Rewrite \(\dfrac{5}{x+2}\) with denominator \((x+2)(x-1)\)

Answer: \(\frac{5(x-1)}{(x+2)(x-1)} = \frac{5x-5}{(x+2)(x-1)}\)

ET4: Identify: Is \(\frac{x+4}{x+7} = \frac{4}{7}\) legal or illegal? Explain.

Answer: ILLEGAL. The \(x\) is a term being added, not a factor being multiplied. Cannot cancel terms.

ET5: For \(f(x) = \frac{(x-2)(x+1)}{(x-2)(x+3)}\), identify the hole and the vertical asymptote.

Answer: Hole at \(x = 2\) (cancelled factor). Vertical asymptote at \(x = -3\) (remaining factor).

Differentiation Strategies

For Struggling Students:

  • Provide "Factor Chips" for kinesthetic LCD building
  • Focus on monomial denominators before quadratics
  • Use color-coding: green for factors, red for terms
  • Allow calculator for arithmetic while focusing on process

For Advanced Students:

  • "Create the Expression" challenge (hole + asymptote specs)
  • Complex fractions with nested rational expressions
  • Prove why the "illegal cancel" fails using algebra
  • Connect to partial fraction decomposition preview
