The Discriminant: Your Factorability Detector

\(D = b^2 - 4ac\)
Discriminant Value What It Means Best Method Graph Shows
\(D > 0\) and Perfect Square Factorable over integers ✅ Factor (AC Method) 2 rational x-intercepts
\(D > 0\) but NOT Perfect Square Two real, irrational roots Quadratic Formula 2 irrational x-intercepts
\(D = 0\) One repeated root Factor or Formula Vertex touches x-axis
\(D < 0\) No real solutions Quadratic Formula (complex) No x-intercepts
📋 STEP-BY-STEP: The Efficiency Check
  1. Set to Zero: Rearrange so the equation equals 0. Get \(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\).
  2. Identify Coefficients: Find \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\). Watch signs carefully!
  3. Check for GCF: Factor out the Greatest Common Factor first. (The Golden Rule!)
  4. Calculate Discriminant: Compute \(D = b^2 - 4ac\) to determine your method.
  5. Choose Your Path:
    • If \(D\) is a perfect square → Factor using AC Method
    • If \(D \geq 0\) but not perfect square → Quadratic Formula
    • If \(D < 0\) → Complex solutions (Quadratic Formula)
  6. Solve for x: Set each factor equal to zero OR apply the formula.
  7. Verify: Plug solutions back into the original equation to check.
⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES & CORRECTIONS
❌ The "Zero Trap"
\(x^2 + 5x = 6\) → Identifying \(c = 6\)
✅ Correct Approach
First: \(x^2 + 5x - 6 = 0\) → Now \(c = -6\)
❌ The GCF Oversight
\(2x^2 + 4x + 2 = 0\) → Using AC with \(a=2\)
✅ Correct Approach
First: \(2(x^2 + 2x + 1) = 0\) → Then factor \(2(x+1)^2 = 0\)
❌ Sign Confusion
\(x^2 - 3x - 10\) → Looking for factors that add to \(+3\)
✅ Correct Approach
\(b = -3\), so find factors of \(-10\) that add to \(-3\): \((-5)(+2)\)
❌ Stopping at Factors
\((x-3)(x+2)\) → "Done!"
✅ Correct Approach
Set each factor = 0: \(x = 3\) or \(x = -2\) ← These are the solutions!

🔧 THE AC METHOD: Diamond/X-Box Procedure
  1. Multiply \(a \cdot c\) — Place this product at the TOP of the diamond.
  2. Place \(b\) at the BOTTOM of the diamond.
  3. Find the factor pair that MULTIPLIES to \(ac\) and ADDS to \(b\). Place on LEFT and RIGHT.
  4. Set up the X-Box: Place \(ax^2\) (top-left), \(c\) (bottom-right), and the two factors as middle terms.
  5. Factor by grouping: Find GCF of each row and column.
  6. Extract binomials: Read off the factors from the edges.

✏️ WORKED EXAMPLE: Solve \(2x^2 + 7x + 3 = 0\)
Step 1: Identify coefficients
\(a = 2\), \(b = 7\), \(c = 3\)
Step 2: Check discriminant
\(D = 7^2 - 4(2)(3) = 49 - 24 = 25\) ✅ Perfect square → Factorable!
Step 3: Set up Diamond
Top: \(a \cdot c = 2 \cdot 3 = 6\)
Bottom: \(b = 7\)
Find: Two numbers that multiply to 6 and add to 7
Answer: 1 and 6 ✓
Step 4: Build X-Box
\(2x^2\)
\(6x\)
\(1x\)
\(3\)
Step 5: Factor by grouping
Top row: \(2x^2 + 6x = 2x(x + 3)\)
Bottom row: \(1x + 3 = 1(x + 3)\)
Step 6: Extract factors
Common factor: \((x + 3)\)
Remaining: \((2x + 1)\)
\(2x^2 + 7x + 3 = (2x + 1)(x + 3)\)
Step 7: Solve for x
\(2x + 1 = 0\) → \(x = -\frac{1}{2}\)
\(x + 3 = 0\) → \(x = -3\)
Solution Set: \(x = -\frac{1}{2}\) or \(x = -3\)
✅ QUICK REFERENCE CHECKLIST
  • Is the equation in Standard Form (\(ax^2 + bx + c = 0\))?
  • Did I identify \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) with correct signs?
  • Is there a GCF to factor out first?
  • Did I calculate the discriminant?
  • Is the discriminant a perfect square? (If yes → factor)
  • Do my diamond numbers multiply to \(ac\) AND add to \(b\)?
  • Did I set up the X-Box correctly?
  • Did I factor by grouping properly?
  • Did I SOLVE for \(x\) (not just factor)?
  • Did I verify by plugging solutions back in?
🚀 CALCULUS PREVIEW: Why Factoring Matters
📍

Vertical Asymptotes

Zeros of the denominator that DON'T cancel become vertical asymptotes in rational functions.

🕳️

Holes (Removable)

Zeros that cancel between numerator and denominator create "holes" in the graph.

⛰️

Critical Points

Setting derivatives equal to zero and factoring finds maxima, minima, and inflection points.
