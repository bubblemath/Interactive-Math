🌉 Teacher Notes: Day 12

Function Modeling & Limits | Pre-Calculus Bridge Course | Week 3, Day 2
📖 Lesson Overview

The Big Picture: This lesson serves as the ultimate "Bridge to Calculus." We transition from static algebraic modeling to the dynamic concept of a Limit. Students see that modeling isn't just about finding a formula—it's about understanding how that formula behaves as inputs approach "interesting" values.

The Conceptual Anchor: 🚶 "Approaching the Edge" — A limit asks: "What value are we APPROACHING?" not "What value are we AT?" This distinction is the foundation of all Calculus.

The "Diner" Metaphor: "Imagine walking toward a diner. The Limit is the location you expect to reach when you get to the door. Even if the diner has been demolished (a hole in the graph), your intended destination remains the same!"

Calculus Connection: Modeling sets up optimization problems (finding max/min with derivatives). Limits are literally the definition of the derivative: \(\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}\).

🎯 Learning Objectives
  • Construct functional models from geometric and real-world verbal descriptions.
  • Identify independent and dependent variables in optimization-ready scenarios.
  • Define a limit intuitively as "intended height" vs. "actual height."
  • Evaluate limits numerically using tables of values approaching from both sides.
  • Evaluate limits graphically, including one-sided limits and holes.
  • Connect end behavior (from Day 9) to limits at infinity.

Day 12: Core Concepts & Scripts

📐 The Modeling Framework
🎯 The Two-Equation Setup

1. The CONSTRAINT Equation

What you're GIVEN (fixed total)

Example: "100 feet of fencing"

\(2L + 2W = 100\)

2. The GOAL Equation

What you want to OPTIMIZE

Example: "Maximize area"

\(A = L \times W\)

The Bridge: Use the constraint to eliminate one variable, creating a function of ONE variable!

🎤 Teacher Script: The Modeling Mindset
"In Calculus, we don't just want the area of a box—we want the MAXIMUM area. But before we can maximize, we must MODEL. Today, we are the architects building the equations that Calculus will later solve. Think of it this way: Algebra builds the machine, Calculus turns it on!"
🎯 The Limit Concept
Left-Hand Limit
\(\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x)\)
Approaching from the LEFT
Right-Hand Limit
\(\lim_{x \to c^+} f(x)\)
Approaching from the RIGHT
Two-Sided Limit
\(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\)
Both sides MUST agree!
🎤 Teacher Script: The "Diner" Metaphor
"Imagine you're walking toward a diner on Main Street. The LIMIT is the location you EXPECT to reach when you get to the door. Now, what if the diner has been demolished? There's a hole where it used to be! Your INTENDED destination is still the same spot—you just can't actually stand there. That's exactly what a limit does: it tells us where we're HEADED, even if we can't actually BE there."
📏 THE LIMIT EXISTS IF AND ONLY IF...

\(\lim_{x \to c^-} f(x) = \lim_{x \to c^+} f(x) = L\)

Both one-sided limits must exist AND be equal!

🎤 Teacher Script: The "Two-Finger Trace"
"Put one finger on the left side of the graph, one on the right. Now trace toward the point. If your fingers meet at the same height, the limit exists. If they're at different heights—like a jump discontinuity—the limit Does Not Exist. Your fingers are the left-hand and right-hand limits!"

Day 12: Pitfalls & Calculus Connections

⚠️ Common Pitfalls & Teaching Strategies
1. Variable Confusion in Modeling

Students struggle to write one variable in terms of another (e.g., expressing width in terms of length using perimeter).

Strategy — "The Substitution Bridge": Always write the CONSTRAINT equation first, solve it for one variable, THEN substitute into the GOAL equation. Make this a ritual: "Constraint first, then substitute!"
2. Limit = Function Value (Wrong!)

Thinking \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)\) always. This fails at holes and discontinuities!

Strategy — "The Hole Graph": Draw a graph with an open circle at (2, 4) and a solid dot at (2, 1). Ask: "What's f(2)?" (Answer: 1) "What's the limit as x approaches 2?" (Answer: 4). The limit ignores the actual point!
3. One-Sided Blindness

Forgetting that a two-sided limit only exists if BOTH one-sided limits agree.

Strategy — "The Two-Finger Trace": Physically trace the graph from both directions. If fingers meet at different heights, say "DNE" out loud. Make it kinesthetic!
4. Physical Domain vs. Mathematical Domain

In modeling, students forget that negative lengths or widths don't make sense.

Strategy — "The Reality Check": After writing the model, always ask: "What values make PHYSICAL sense?" A box can't have negative dimensions, even if the algebra allows it.

Day 12: Assessment & Differentiation

✅ Formative Assessment Checkpoints
  • The Constraint Check: Can students identify the difference between the "Goal" equation and the "Constraint" equation?
  • The Table Test: Given a table approaching \(x = 2\) from both sides, can they predict the limit?
  • The Jump Check: Looking at a jump discontinuity, can they explain why the general limit DNE?
  • The Hole vs. Value: Can they distinguish between \(f(c)\) and \(\lim_{x \to c} f(x)\)?
📝 Exit Ticket & Answers
Exit Ticket Problems
  1. A farmer has 100 feet of fencing to enclose a rectangular pen against a barn (only 3 sides needed). Write the area as a function of the width \(x\).
    Answer: Constraint: \(2x + L = 100 \Rightarrow L = 100 - 2x\). Goal: \(A(x) = x(100 - 2x) = 100x - 2x^2\)
  2. From the graph, find \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x)\) if there's a hole at (3, 4) and a solid dot at (3, 2).
    Answer: \(\lim_{x \to 3} f(x) = 4\) (The limit is the "intended" height, not the actual value!)
  3. Use the table to estimate \(\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}\):
    | x | 1.9 | 1.99 | 1.999 | 2.001 | 2.01 | 2.1 |
    | f(x) | 3.9 | 3.99 | 3.999 | 4.001 | 4.01 | 4.1 |
    Answer: \(\lim_{x \to 2} = 4\) (Both sides approach 4)
  4. If \(\lim_{x \to 5^-} f(x) = 7\) and \(\lim_{x \to 5^+} f(x) = 3\), what is \(\lim_{x \to 5} f(x)\)?
    Answer: DNE (Does Not Exist) — The one-sided limits don't agree!
🎚️ Differentiation Strategies
🔴 For Struggling Students
  • Provide a "Modeling Template" with blanks for Constraint and Goal
  • Use color-coding: RED for constraint, GREEN for goal
  • Start with numerical limits only (tables) before graphs
  • Give pre-drawn graphs with clear holes marked
  • Use the "Two-Finger Trace" physically on paper
🟢 For Advanced Students
  • Challenge: Find the domain of the model function (physical constraints)
  • Introduce algebraic limit evaluation: factor and cancel
  • Explore limits at infinity: \(\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^2 + 1}{x^2 - 5}\)
  • Preview: "What does the limit have to do with slope?"
  • Connect to Day 9: End behavior IS a limit at infinity!

Day 12: Partner & Small Group Activities

👥 Partner & Small Group Activities
1. The "Box Builder" Relay 📦
Groups of 3
Each group gets a sheet of paper representing a flat piece of cardboard. Student A: "Cuts" the corners (algebraically) and defines the dimensions in terms of x. Student B: Writes the Volume function V(x). Student C: Determines the physical domain (what values of x make sense?). Groups present their models to the class.
📦 Materials: Paper squares, scissors (optional), modeling worksheets
✨ Reinforces: Geometric modeling, constraint identification, domain restrictions
2. Limit "Speed Dating" 💕
Rotating Pairs
Students sit in two rows facing each other. Each pair receives a laminated graph card with various "interesting" points (holes, jumps, asymptotes). They have 90 seconds to identify the limit at a specific x-value, then one row rotates. Track correct answers for friendly competition!
📦 Materials: Laminated graph cards (20+), timer, answer key, scorecard
✨ Reinforces: Graphical limit evaluation, one-sided limits, quick recognition
3. The "Zoom-In" Numerical Challenge 🔍
Pairs
One student uses a calculator to approach a value from the LEFT (e.g., 1.9, 1.99, 1.999), while their partner approaches from the RIGHT (2.1, 2.01, 2.001). They compare results to "discover" the limit. If the values converge, they've found it! If not, they discuss why.
📦 Materials: Graphing calculators, function cards, recording sheets
✨ Reinforces: Numerical limits, two-sided approach, calculator fluency
4. "Who's My Limit?" Matching Game 🃏
Small Groups (3-4)
A card-sort activity where students match four representations: (1) A function like \(\frac{x^2-4}{x-2}\), (2) Its table of values near x = 2, (3) Its graph with the hole, and (4) The limit value. Include "trap" sets where the limit DNE!
📦 Materials: Card sets (5 complete sets per group), answer key
✨ Reinforces: Multiple representations, connecting algebraic/numerical/graphical
5. The "Forbidden Zone" Modeling 🚫
Pairs
Students receive real-world scenarios (ladder against wall, box from cardboard, fenced area) and must determine the "Physical Domain." They discuss why the math might allow certain numbers (negative lengths, widths larger than the material) that the physical world does not.
📦 Materials: Scenario prompt cards, domain recording sheets
✨ Reinforces: Domain constraints, mathematical vs. physical reasoning
6. Continuity Gallery Walk 🖼️
Small Groups (3-4)
Large graphs are posted around the room showing various discontinuities. Groups rotate through stations, identifying where "the diner is demolished" (hole), "the road breaks" (jump), or "the road goes to infinity" (vertical asymptote). They write limit notation for each point of interest.
📦 Materials: Large poster graphs, markers, station worksheets
✨ Reinforces: Limit notation, types of discontinuities, mathematical communication
7. Build-a-Limit Challenge 🔧
Pairs (Advanced)
Students are given a limit statement (e.g., \(\lim_{x \to 5} f(x) = 10\) but \(f(5) = 3\)) and must CREATE a function and graph that satisfies those conditions. They must include a hole at the specified point. Share and compare creative solutions!
📦 Materials: Whiteboards, markers, limit requirement cards
✨ Reinforces: Reverse engineering, deep understanding of limit vs. value
